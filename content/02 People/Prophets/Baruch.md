# Baruch

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Jeremiah's secretary and loyal companion; recorded, read, and preserved Jeremiah's prophecies
- **Family:** Son of Neriah; grandson of Mahseiah; brother of Seraiah (a royal official)
- **Key Books:** [[Jeremiah]]

## Biography
Baruch son of Neriah is among the most important supporting figures in the Hebrew prophetic tradition — the faithful scribe who made the written preservation of [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]]'s prophecies possible. His brother Seraiah served as "quartermaster" (or "staff officer") for King Zedekiah (Jeremiah 51:59), indicating a family with connections to the royal court. Baruch himself served [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] in what appears to have been a deep relationship of mutual trust and loyalty across decades of ministry during the most dangerous period in Judah's history.

His first major appearance comes in Jeremiah 32, when he witnessed and sealed the deed of the field [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] purchased from his cousin Hanamel during the Babylonian siege — a prophetic act of confidence in the future restoration of Judah. Baruch was called to put the deed in a clay jar for safekeeping, a gesture symbolizing that normal life would eventually return. This moment reveals Baruch as more than a scribe — he was a witness to and participant in [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]]'s prophetic symbolic actions.

The most consequential episode comes in Jeremiah 36, when [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] dictated his prophecies to Baruch, who wrote them on a scroll. Since [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] was barred from entering the temple, Baruch read the scroll publicly in the temple complex, then before the princes, and finally before King Jehoiakim himself. The king responded by cutting the scroll into pieces and burning it section by section in the fire — an act of defiance against the prophetic word. Baruch and [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] went into hiding, but at God's command, Baruch wrote a second, expanded scroll. This episode makes Baruch the instrument of the first "edition" of Jeremiah's written prophecies.

Jeremiah 45 contains a personal oracle addressed specifically to Baruch, acknowledging that he had expressed lament and weariness: "Woe is me! For the LORD has added sorrow to my pain. I am weary with my groaning, and I find no rest" (Jeremiah 45:3). God's word to him is sobering but compassionate — in a time of disaster, Baruch was told not to seek great things for himself, but his life would be preserved as a "prize of war" wherever he went. This chapter functions as a pastoral word to an exhausted servant, embedded within a massive collection of prophecies about nations.

Baruch accompanied [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] through the fall of Jerusalem, the murder of Gedaliah, and the forced migration to Egypt against their advice (Jeremiah 43). He is last seen in Egypt, having remained faithful to his prophet through imprisonment, persecution, national catastrophe, and exile.

## Key Events
- Witnesses and seals the deed of [[Jeremiah]]'s land purchase during the siege (Jeremiah 32:12–16)
- Writes [[Jeremiah]]'s dictated prophecies on a scroll (Jeremiah 36:4)
- Reads the scroll publicly in the temple (Jeremiah 36:8–10)
- Reads the scroll before the princes; they advise him and [[Jeremiah]] to hide (Jeremiah 36:11–19)
- The scroll is burned by King Jehoiakim; Baruch writes a second expanded scroll (Jeremiah 36:20–32)
- Receives a personal oracle from God through [[Jeremiah]] (Jeremiah 45)
- Accompanies [[Jeremiah]] to Egypt after the fall of Jerusalem (Jeremiah 43:3–7)

## Relationships

- Seraiah - brother; royal official who carried [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]]'s oracle against Babylon (Jeremiah 51:59–64)
- Jehoiakim - king who burned the scroll Baruch wrote

## Notes
- Without Baruch, the written prophecies of [[Jeremiah]] may not have been preserved — he is literally the reason Jeremiah's book exists in written form
- Jeremiah 45 is a rare case in Scripture where a prophetic oracle is addressed specifically to a prophet's assistant, acknowledging the emotional and spiritual cost of such service
- His brother Seraiah received [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]]'s scroll against Babylon with instructions to sink it in the Euphrates (Jeremiah 51:59–64), suggesting the whole family was drawn into prophetic ministry
