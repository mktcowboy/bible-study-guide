# Daniel (Prophet)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Faithful exile, interpreter of dreams, court official, apocalyptic prophet
- **Family:** From Judah, likely of noble or royal background
- **Key Books:** [[Daniel]], [[Ezekiel]], [[Matthew]], [[Revelation]]

## Biography

Daniel was taken from Judah to Babylon as a young exile during the Babylonian ascendancy. Given the Babylonian name Belteshazzar, he was trained for service in the king's court along with [[Hananiah (Shadrach)]], [[Mishael (Meshach)]], and [[Azariah (Abednego)]]. From the beginning, Daniel resolved not to defile himself with the king's food, showing that faithfulness in exile begins with small acts of covenant loyalty.

Daniel becomes a model of wisdom under foreign rule. God gives him knowledge, understanding, and the ability to interpret dreams and visions. He interprets Nebuchadnezzar's dream of the great image, revealing that human empires rise and fall under the rule of the God of heaven, whose kingdom will never be destroyed. Later, he interprets the writing on the wall for Belshazzar, announcing Babylon's fall.

Daniel's personal faithfulness climaxes in the lions' den. Under Darius, jealous officials manipulate the law to target Daniel's prayer life. Daniel continues praying toward Jerusalem, is cast into the den, and is delivered by God. His deliverance becomes public testimony that the living God rules and saves.

The second half of Daniel contains visions of beasts, kingdoms, heavenly judgment, the Son of Man, seventy weeks, conflict, resurrection, and the end. Jesus refers to the abomination of desolation spoken of by Daniel, and [[Revelation]] draws heavily on Daniel's imagery of beasts, kingdoms, thrones, and final judgment.

Daniel's life holds together practical holiness, wisdom in public service, courage under pressure, and apocalyptic hope. He is faithful in Babylon without becoming Babylonian in ultimate allegiance.

## Notes
- Daniel is both a narrative model of faithfulness and a prophetic source for later apocalyptic hope.
- The book teaches that empires are real and frightening, but temporary under God's rule.
- Daniel's prayer life is not private piety detached from history; it is resistance, hope, and covenant memory.
- Jesus' use of "Son of Man" draws deeply from Daniel 7.
