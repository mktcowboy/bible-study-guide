# Jeremiah (Prophet)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Prophet to Judah before and during the Babylonian exile; priest from Anathoth
- **Family:** Son of Hilkiah, of the priests in Anathoth
- **Key Books:** [[Jeremiah]], [[Lamentations]], [[2 Kings]], [[2 Chronicles]]

## Biography

Jeremiah was called before birth and appointed as a prophet to the nations. He ministered from the days of Josiah through the fall of Jerusalem, speaking across the reigns of Judah's final kings. His ministry stretched through reform, relapse, siege, destruction, exile, and the painful aftermath of national collapse.

Jeremiah's call included both tearing down and building up. The LORD touched his mouth and put his words there, appointing him to root out, pull down, destroy, throw down, build, and plant. Jeremiah was young and reluctant, but God made him a fortified city against kings, priests, and people who would resist his message.

Jeremiah warned that Judah's covenant infidelity, idolatry, injustice, and false confidence in the temple would bring judgment. He opposed false prophets who promised peace without repentance and repeatedly announced that Babylon would become the instrument of judgment. Because of this, he was mocked, beaten, imprisoned, accused of treason, and thrown into a cistern.

Yet Jeremiah is also a prophet of hope. He wrote to the exiles, telling them to seek the welfare of Babylon while waiting for God's future restoration. He bought a field at Anathoth as a sign that houses and fields would again be possessed in the land. Most importantly, he announced the [[New Covenant]], in which God would write his law on the heart, forgive iniquity, and remember sin no more.

Jeremiah's life is intensely personal. His grief over Judah is not detached analysis; he suffers with the message he carries. The tradition associating him with [[Lamentations]] fits the same prophetic burden: judgment is deserved, but God's mercies are not consumed.

## Notes
- Jeremiah's grief is part of his prophetic authority; he does not announce judgment coldly.
- His conflict with false prophets is central: hope severed from truth becomes deadly.
- The new covenant promise is one of the most important bridges between Old Testament prophetic hope and New Testament fulfillment.
- Jeremiah shows that faithful ministry can look like apparent failure when a generation refuses to hear.
