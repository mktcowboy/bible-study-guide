# Gamaliel

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Respected Pharisee teacher; Paul's rabbi in Jerusalem; counseled the Sanhedrin to leave the apostles alone
- **Family:** Grandson of Hillel the Elder; father of Simeon ben Gamaliel
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]]

## Biography
Gamaliel I (known as Gamaliel the Elder or Rabban Gamaliel I) was one of the most respected Jewish legal scholars of the first century AD. He was a Pharisee and member of the Sanhedrin, and his honorary title "Rabban" (our master) placed him above the ordinary title of "Rabbi." He was the grandson of the legendary Hillel the Elder, founder of the Hillelite school of legal interpretation that was generally more lenient than the competing Shammaite school. According to the Mishnah (Sotah 9:15), "Since Rabban Gamaliel the Elder died, the glory of the Torah ceased."

His most significant appearance in the New Testament comes in Acts 5, following the apostles' arrest and their miraculous release from prison by an angel. When the Sanhedrin debated what to do with men who refused to stop preaching in Jesus' name, Gamaliel stood and delivered a measured counsel. He offered two historical precedents — Theudas and Judas the Galilean, both of whom led movements that collapsed when their leaders died — and argued that if the apostles' movement was merely human, it would fail on its own, but "if it is of God, you will not be able to overthrow them" (Acts 5:38–39). The Sanhedrin accepted this reasoning, had the apostles flogged, and released them.

Gamaliel's second appearance is in Acts 22, where Paul identifies himself as having been "educated at the feet of Gamaliel" (Acts 22:3), trained "according to the strict manner of the law of our fathers." This discipleship under Gamaliel may explain Paul's deep knowledge of Torah, his rhetorical sophistication, and the range of his scriptural arguments. The irony is considerable: the rabbi's most famous student became the most effective opponent of the Jewish establishment that Gamaliel himself had defended by restraint.

Jewish tradition does not record Gamaliel as a Christian believer, though some later church tradition speculated about his sympathies. His counsel in Acts 5 was pragmatic wisdom, not theological affirmation of the resurrection. Yet his intervention preserved the early church at a critical moment, and later Christian writers sometimes cited him as a friend to the movement.

## Key Events
- Rises in the Sanhedrin to counsel restraint toward the apostles (Acts 5:34–39)
- Invokes the examples of Theudas and Judas the Galilean (Acts 5:36–37)
- Named by Paul as his teacher in Jerusalem (Acts 22:3)

## Notes
- Gamaliel's "wait and see" argument (Acts 5:38–39) has become a recurring pattern in discussions of how to evaluate religious movements — not all pragmatic counsel is faithful discernment, but his reasoning preserved the early church at a critical moment
- The fact that Paul, trained under the most respected Pharisaic teacher of the era, became Christianity's greatest theologian is one of the most striking ironies in church history
- The Mishnah's tribute — "Since Rabban Gamaliel died, the glory of the Torah ceased" — shows his standing in normative Judaism independent of his New Testament appearances
