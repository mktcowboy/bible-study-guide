# Amos (Prophet)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Prophet
- **Book(s):** [[Amos]]
- **Period:** ~760–750 BC — during the reigns of Uzziah of Judah and Jeroboam II of Israel; "two years before the earthquake" (Amos 1:1), confirmed archaeologically at ~760 BC

## Profile

Amos of [[Tekoa]] is the great social prophet of the Old Testament and the prototype of the lay prophet — a man with no professional credentials, no prophetic family lineage, and no institutional standing who was nevertheless seized by God and sent on a specific mission. He says of himself: "I was no prophet, neither was I a prophet's son; but I was an herdman, and a gatherer of sycomore fruit: And the LORD took me as I followed the flock, and the LORD said unto me, Go, prophesy unto my people Israel" (7:14–15). His occupation was a combination of livestock herding (*noqed*, a word also used of Mesha king of Moab in 2 Kings 3:4) and dressing or cultivating sycamore figs (*boles shiqmim*) — a fruit requiring deliberate incision to ripen, grown in the Shephelah lowlands rather than the highlands where Tekoa sits, suggesting Amos had diversified agricultural work. He was a man of the land, not the temple or the court.

His commissioning takes him north — from Tekoa in Judah, a hilltop village ten miles south of Bethlehem, into the northern kingdom of Israel during the reign of Jeroboam II. This was the most prosperous period in the north's history: expanded borders, military success, growing trade — and, behind that prosperity, systemic exploitation of the poor and a worship life that had been entirely severed from covenant ethics. Amos arrives with the full weight of the Mosaic tradition behind him. He knows Deuteronomy's care for the poor and the stranger (Deuteronomy 10:18; 15:7–11); he knows what covenant faithfulness looks like; and he can see how far Israel has departed from it. His rhetoric is sophisticated — the eight oracles of chapters 1–2 are a masterpiece of rhetorical entrapment — and his prose is vivid, lyrical, and unsparing.

The confrontation with [[Amaziah]] the priest of [[Bethel]] (7:10–17) reveals the character of Amos under pressure. When Amaziah tells him to go home to Judah and prophesy there — implying that Bethel is no place for a southern troublemaker — Amos does not retreat. He does not claim prophetic authority on institutional grounds; he claims it on the basis of direct divine commission. He then pronounces a personal judgment oracle against Amaziah, demonstrating that the same prophetic authority that indicted the nation can address an individual who attempts to silence it. The book that ends with the restoration of "the tabernacle of David that is fallen" (9:11) — a promise James quotes at the Jerusalem Council in Acts 15 — is thus framed by one man's radical obedience to a commission he received by a sycamore tree.
