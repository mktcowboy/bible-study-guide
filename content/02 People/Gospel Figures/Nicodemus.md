# Nicodemus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Pharisee and member of the Sanhedrin who came to Jesus by night; Jesus taught him about being born again; later defended Jesus before the council and helped bury him
- **Family:** Jewish ruler; a Pharisee; no family details given
- **Key Books:** [[John]]

## Biography
Nicodemus was a Pharisee and a member of the Jewish ruling council (Sanhedrin) — a man of religious education, social standing, and real power in first-century Jerusalem. He appears three times in the Gospel of John, and across those three appearances his relationship to Jesus can be traced on a quiet arc from cautious private inquiry to open, costly discipleship.

His first appearance (John 3) is the famous night meeting with Jesus. The timing — at night — may indicate caution about being seen with this controversial teacher, or it may simply reflect a busy teacher's schedule. Nicodemus addresses Jesus with genuine respect: "Rabbi, we know that you are a teacher come from God, for no one can do these signs that you do unless God is with him" (John 3:2). Jesus' response bypasses the compliment and moves directly to the confrontational: "Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born again he cannot see the kingdom of God" (John 3:3). The conversation probes the deepest theological questions — spiritual rebirth, the work of the Spirit, the nature of salvation — and includes the most quoted verse in the New Testament (John 3:16). Whether Nicodemus came to full faith at this meeting is not stated.

His second appearance (John 7:50–51) is brief but significant. When the Sanhedrin discussed arresting Jesus, Nicodemus raised a procedural point: "Does our law judge a man without first giving him a hearing and learning what he does?" He was sharply rebuked: "Are you from Galilee too? Search and see that no prophet arises from Galilee." His intervention was not a full public confession, but it was a willingness to risk the council's contempt on Jesus' behalf — a meaningful step.

His third appearance comes at the crucifixion. After [[Joseph of Arimathea]] requested the body of Jesus from [[Pilate]], Nicodemus came bringing a mixture of myrrh and aloes — about 75 pounds — for the burial (John 19:38–42). This extravagant provision, combined with his public participation in the burial, placed him openly in the company of Jesus' disciples at the most dangerous moment possible. Many scholars see this as John's indication that Nicodemus had moved from night-time inquiry to daylight commitment.

## Key Events
- Comes to Jesus by night; receives the teaching on being born again (John 3:1–21)
- Defends Jesus before the Sanhedrin on procedural grounds (John 7:50–51)
- Provides 75 pounds of burial spices for Jesus' burial alongside [[Joseph of Arimathea]] (John 19:39–42)

## Notes
- John's three-stage presentation of Nicodemus — night inquiry, cautious defense, open burial participation — constitutes one of the Gospel's most carefully developed character arcs
- The 75 pounds of spices Nicodemus brought for the burial was an extravagant quantity, befitting a royal burial; the gesture signals his recognition of Jesus' dignity even in death
- John 3:16–21, embedded in the Nicodemus dialogue, is often extracted as a standalone text, but reading it as Jesus' direct challenge to a religious expert who "knows" but has not believed adds its full force
