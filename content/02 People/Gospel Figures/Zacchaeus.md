# Zacchaeus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Chief tax collector at Jericho; wealthy; sought to see Jesus; repented and made restitution
- **Family:** Not specified
- **Key Books:** [[Luke]]

## Biography
Zacchaeus is introduced in Luke 19 as the chief tax collector (Greek: *architelōnēs*) at [[Jericho]], which made him the highest-ranking tax official in a wealthy customs district controlling trade routes. He was rich. He was also short, and when he heard Jesus was passing through he could not see him over the crowd — so he ran ahead and climbed a sycamore tree.

Jesus stopped beneath the tree, looked up, and called Zacchaeus by name: "Zacchaeus, make haste, and come down; for today I must abide at thy house." Zacchaeus came down "joyfully" (19:6) — the word used elsewhere for response to the gospel. The crowd grumbled that Jesus had gone to be a guest of a sinner.

Zacchaeus stood before Jesus and made a public declaration: half his goods he would give to the poor, and if he had taken anything falsely he would restore it fourfold — far beyond what the Law required (which was principal plus 20%). Jesus declared: "This day is salvation come to this house, forsomuch as he also is a son of Abraham." The story illustrates Luke's running theme that Jesus seeks the lost, the outcast, and those written off by religious society.

## Notes
- Zacchaeus's fourfold restitution goes beyond the legal minimum, demonstrating the generosity that genuine repentance produces.
- Jesus calling him "son of Abraham" reclaims him as a member of the covenant community — the same phrase used in Luke 13:16 of the bent woman.
- The story immediately precedes the Parable of the Minas (19:11), connecting personal repentance with faithful stewardship in anticipation of the kingdom.
