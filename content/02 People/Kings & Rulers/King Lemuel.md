# King Lemuel

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** King of an unidentified nation; recipient of his mother's wisdom instruction
- **Family:** Identity uncertain; traditionally associated with [[Solomon]] by some rabbinical sources
- **Key Books:** [[Proverbs]]

## Biography
King Lemuel appears only in Proverbs 31:1-9, where he records wisdom "which his mother taught him." The passage is unusual in the Hebrew Bible for being attributed to a woman's instruction to a king. His mother warned him against three dangers: women who would destroy kings, intoxicating drink, and neglect of justice for the poor and oppressed.

The name "Lemuel" means "devoted to God" or "belonging to God." His identity is uncertain — some Jewish and Christian traditions identified him with Solomon (whose mother Bathsheba was known for her strong influence), while others see him as a non-Israelite king, noting the oracle is described as from Massa, possibly an Arabian tribe descended from Ishmael.

The wisdom given to him by his mother concludes with a call to "open thy mouth, judge righteously, and plead the cause of the poor and needy" — an ethic that the following poem about the virtuous woman (Proverbs 31:10-31) then embodies in domestic life.

## Notes
- One of the few explicit examples in Scripture of a woman imparting royal wisdom and moral instruction.
- The oracle's concern for justice to the poor and marginalized is consistent with the broader prophetic and wisdom traditions of the Old Testament.
- The identity question (Lemuel = Solomon?) cannot be resolved with certainty; the text is primarily interested in the wisdom itself, not the king's genealogy.
