# Asa

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** King of Judah; reformer who removed idols; trusted God against a vast Cushite army but later relied on Aram instead of God
- **Family:** Son of Abijah; grandson of [[Rehoboam]]; great-grandson of [[Solomon]]
- **Key Books:** [[1 Kings]], [[2 Chronicles]]

## Biography
Asa was the third king of Judah (approximately 910–869 BC) and one of the few kings given an explicitly positive evaluation in the biblical record. His 41-year reign was the longest of any Judahite king up to that point. He is remembered primarily as a reformer who restored Yahwistic worship and removed foreign religious influences that had taken root during the reigns of his father and grandfather.

His religious reforms were extensive. He removed the "foreign altars and the high places," broke down the pillars, cut down the Asherim, and commanded Judah to seek the LORD and obey his law (2 Chronicles 14:3–4). Most remarkably, he even removed his grandmother Maacah from her position as queen mother because she had made an obscene image for Asherah (2 Chronicles 15:16). This willingness to confront family relationships in the name of covenant faithfulness set him apart.

The defining military moment of Asa's reign came when the Cushite Zerah led an army of one million men and three hundred chariots against Judah — a force vastly superior to anything Asa could field. Rather than seeking alliances, Asa cried out to God: "LORD, there is none like you to help, between the mighty and the weak. Help us, O LORD our God, for we rely on you, and in your name we have come against this multitude" (2 Chronicles 14:11). God struck the Cushites and they fled; it was one of the most complete military victories in Judah's history.

The contrast makes Asa's later failure all the more stark. When Baasha king of Israel threatened Judah by fortifying Ramah, Asa turned not to God but to Ben-hadad of Aram, stripping the temple and royal treasuries to hire him. The prophet [[Azariah (Abednego)]] (also called Hanani the seer) rebuked him: "Because you relied on the king of Syria, and did not rely on the LORD your God, the army of the king of Syria has escaped you" (2 Chronicles 16:7). Asa's response revealed how far he had drifted — he imprisoned the prophet and began to oppress some of the people. Even in his final illness, "he did not seek the LORD, but sought help from physicians" (2 Chronicles 16:12). His reign ends as a tragedy of partial faithfulness, illustrating how a strong beginning does not guarantee a faithful end.

## Key Events
- Removes foreign altars, pillars, and Asherim; commands Judah to seek the LORD (2 Chronicles 14:3–5)
- Deposes grandmother Maacah for her idolatry (1 Kings 15:13; 2 Chronicles 15:16)
- Prays for deliverance and defeats the Cushite army of one million (2 Chronicles 14:9–15)
- Makes a covenant renewal with all Judah following Azariah's prophecy (2 Chronicles 15)
- Hires Ben-hadad of Aram instead of trusting God against Baasha of Israel (2 Chronicles 16:1–6)
- Imprisons Hanani the seer who rebuked him (2 Chronicles 16:7–10)
- Dies of a foot disease after not seeking God in his illness (2 Chronicles 16:12–14)

## Notes
- Asa's reign illustrates the contrast between early faith and later self-reliance — the same king who trusted God against a million soldiers later could not trust him against a regional neighbor
- 2 Chronicles 16:9 contains one of the great summary statements of divine providence: "The eyes of the LORD run to and fro throughout the whole earth, to give strong support to those whose heart is blameless toward him"
- His imprisonment of a prophet marks a boundary that Judah's better kings did not cross — the suppression of prophetic rebuke is a consistent marker of spiritual decline in the Chronicler's framework
