# David

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Second king of Israel; shepherd, warrior, psalmist, worship organizer, and covenant king
- **Family:** Son of [[Jesse]]; father of [[Solomon]] and [[Absalom]]; husband of [[Bathsheba]], Michal, Abigail, and others
- **Key Books:** [[1 Samuel]], [[2 Samuel]], [[1 Chronicles]], [[Psalms]], [[Matthew]], [[Luke]]

## Biography

David began as the youngest son of Jesse, a shepherd from Bethlehem. When [[Saul]] failed as king, [[Samuel]] was sent to anoint David, and the Spirit of the LORD came upon him. David first entered Saul's court as a musician and armor-bearer, then became publicly known when he defeated [[Goliath]] in the name of the LORD.

David's rise was marked by suffering before rule. Though he served Saul faithfully, Saul became jealous and repeatedly tried to kill him. David fled, gathered a band of followers, formed a deep covenant friendship with [[Jonathan]], spared Saul more than once, and learned to wait for the kingdom rather than seize it by violence. His fugitive years show faith under pressure, restraint toward the LORD's anointed, and dependence on divine deliverance.

After Saul's death, David first ruled over Judah from Hebron and then became king over all Israel. He captured Jerusalem, made it the royal city, brought the ark toward the center of national worship, defeated enemies, and received the [[Davidic Covenant]] through [[Nathan]]. God promised that David's house, kingdom, and throne would endure, forming one of the central lines of messianic hope in Scripture.

David was also deeply flawed. His sin with [[Bathsheba]] and murder of [[Uriah the Hittite]] brought severe consequences. Nathan confronted him, and David repented, but his house was marked by sorrow: the death of the child, Amnon's sin, Absalom's rebellion, family violence, and political fracture. Later, David's census brought judgment on Israel, though it also led to the purchase of the threshing floor that became associated with the temple site.

In [[1 Chronicles]], David is especially remembered as the organizer of worship and preparer for the temple. Though he would not build the temple, he gathered materials, ordered Levites, appointed singers, and charged Solomon to build. The Psalms preserve David's voice as worshiper, sufferer, king, sinner, and man after God's heart.

The New Testament opens by identifying [[Jesus Christ]] as son of David. David is therefore not merely an ancient king; he is the pattern and promise that points toward the Messiah whose throne endures forever.

## Notes
- David is the model king, not because he is sinless, but because he is covenantally central and responsive to God's word.
- Later kings are repeatedly measured against David, making him the royal benchmark of Israel's history.
- David's life combines election, courage, worship, patience, sin, repentance, suffering, and hope.
- The Davidic covenant is one of the Bible's major messianic foundations.
- David's psalms give the vault a bridge between narrative history and prayerful theology.
