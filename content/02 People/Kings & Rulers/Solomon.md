# Solomon

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Third king of Israel; son of [[David]]; builder of the temple; wisdom king
- **Family:** Son of David and [[Bathsheba]]; father of [[Rehoboam]]
- **Key Books:** [[1 Kings]], [[2 Chronicles]], [[Proverbs]], [[Ecclesiastes]], [[Song of Solomon]]

## Biography

Solomon was the son of David and Bathsheba, born after the death of the child conceived through David's sin. The LORD loved Solomon and gave him the name Jedidiah through [[Nathan]]. Near the end of David's life, Solomon's succession was threatened by [[Adonijah]], but Bathsheba, Nathan, Zadok, and Benaiah secured Solomon's anointing as king.

Solomon began with promise. At Gibeon, the LORD appeared to him in a dream and invited him to ask for what he desired. Solomon asked for wisdom to govern God's people, and God gave him wisdom, riches, and honor. His early reign is marked by discernment, stability, international renown, and the famous judgment between the two women claiming one child.

Solomon's greatest achievement was the construction and dedication of the temple in Jerusalem. He built the house David had prepared for but was not permitted to build. At the dedication, Solomon prayed one of Scripture's great prayers, confessing that heaven and the heaven of heavens cannot contain God, yet asking the LORD to hear prayers made toward the temple.

Solomon's reign represents the high point of Israel's united monarchy in wealth, wisdom, territory, and international reputation. The visit of the [[Queen of Sheba]] displays the global reach of his wisdom and splendor. His writings or associated wisdom traditions shape Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and Song of Solomon.

Yet Solomon's story is also tragic. He loved many foreign women, and his wives turned his heart after other gods. He built high places and compromised the covenant faithfulness required of Israel's king. The LORD announced that the kingdom would be torn from his house, though not in his own days for David's sake. Solomon's downfall prepared the way for the divided kingdom under Rehoboam and [[Jeroboam]].

Solomon embodies both wisdom's glory and wisdom's warning: great gifts do not protect a heart that drifts from covenant loyalty.

## Notes
- Solomon's wisdom is real, but the narrative warns that wisdom must be joined to obedience.
- The temple is Solomon's greatest covenant achievement and the center of his legacy.
- Solomon's wealth and international fame fulfill royal grandeur but also expose temptations Deuteronomy warned kings against.
- His later idolatry is one of the main causes of the divided kingdom.
- Solomon is both a type of royal wisdom and a cautionary figure about divided love.
