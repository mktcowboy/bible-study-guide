# Felix

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Roman governor of Judea who heard Paul's case; kept Paul imprisoned for two years while hoping for a bribe
- **Family:** Formerly enslaved; freed by Emperor Claudius; husband of Drusilla (daughter of Herod Agrippa I)
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]]

## Biography
Marcus Antonius Felix served as the Roman procurator (governor) of Judea from approximately AD 52 to 59. He was a freedman — formerly enslaved — who rose to extraordinary power through imperial patronage, a fact noted with some sarcasm by the Roman historian Tacitus, who wrote that Felix "exercised the power of a king with the disposition of a slave, in all cruelty and lust." His brother Pallas was a powerful imperial freedman under Emperor Claudius, and this connection likely secured Felix his governorship. His wife Drusilla was a Jewish princess, daughter of Herod Agrippa I, which gave him some familiarity with Jewish customs and religious debates.

Paul arrived before Felix after being transferred from Jerusalem to Caesarea Maritima under a military escort of 470 soldiers, following a plot to assassinate him. The Jewish leaders, led by the high priest Ananias with the orator Tertullus, presented their case against Paul before Felix. Paul responded with a lucid defense, and Felix — who was "rather well acquainted with the Way" — adjourned the proceedings, saying he would decide when Lysias the commander came down (Acts 24:22).

What followed was a strange combination of genuine curiosity and corrupt calculation. Felix summoned Paul frequently and listened to him speak about faith in Christ Jesus — but when Paul "reasoned about righteousness and self-control and the coming judgment, Felix was alarmed and said, 'Go away for the present'" (Acts 24:25). The historical Felix's reputation for licentiousness and the irregularity of his marriage to Drusilla (she had left a previous husband to marry him) may explain why Paul's themes of righteousness and self-control were particularly unwelcome. At the same time, Felix hoped Paul would pay him a bribe for his release — a motive that kept Paul in custody for two years.

When Porcius [[Festus]] succeeded Felix as governor (approximately AD 59), Felix left Paul in prison as a favor to the Jewish leaders, with whom his relations had been troubled. Felix was eventually recalled to Rome and narrowly escaped punishment for his handling of a riot in Caesarea, again through his brother Pallas's influence. He disappears from history thereafter.

## Key Events
- Receives Paul transferred from Jerusalem under military escort (Acts 23:23–35)
- Hears the Jewish accusation presented by Tertullus (Acts 24:1–9)
- Hears Paul's defense and adjourns, claiming to wait for Lysias (Acts 24:10–22)
- Summons Paul repeatedly and converses about faith, but is alarmed by talk of judgment (Acts 24:24–25)
- Hopes for a bribe; keeps Paul imprisoned two years (Acts 24:26–27)
- Leaves Paul bound as a favor to Jewish leaders when replaced by [[Festus]] (Acts 24:27)

## Notes
- Felix is a rare example in Acts of a hearer who was moved by Paul's preaching enough to be "alarmed" but ultimately chose temporal advantage over spiritual response
- His hope for a bribe from Paul (Acts 24:26) provides an incidental confirmation that the early Christian community had some financial resources to draw on
- The combination of genuine curiosity and corrupt inaction makes Felix a model of the person who hears truth and delays indefinitely, the pattern condemned in Paul's own writings about the hardened heart
