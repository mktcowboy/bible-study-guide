# Ahasuerus

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Persian king (Xerxes I) who chose Esther as queen; central figure in the book of Esther
- **Family:** Son of Darius I; husband of [[Vashti]], then [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]]
- **Key Books:** [[Esther]]

## Biography
Ahasuerus is the Hebrew name for the Persian king known in classical sources as Xerxes I (reigned approximately 486–465 BC), son of Darius I the Great. His empire, described in Esther 1:1 as stretching "from India to Ethiopia over 127 provinces," was the largest in the ancient world at that time. The identification of Ahasuerus with Xerxes I is widely accepted by scholars and is supported by linguistic, chronological, and historical correlations.

The book of Esther opens with an extravagant banquet lasting 180 days, followed by a 7-day feast — a display of imperial power characteristic of the Persian court. When Queen [[Vashti]] refused to appear before the king and his drunken guests, Ahasuerus consulted his advisors and deposed her at their recommendation, partly to prevent other women in the empire from following her example of disobedience. This decision, born of wounded pride and guided by courtiers, sets the stage for the selection of a new queen.

The search for a new queen brings a Jewish girl, [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]], to the palace. Ahasuerus does not know she is Jewish — a detail that will become pivotal. He chooses her as queen above all others, drawn to her more than any other woman (Esther 2:17). Throughout the book, Ahasuerus is portrayed as a powerful but somewhat passive figure — easily influenced by his advisors, prone to impulsive decisions, and unaware of the dynamics playing out among his subjects. He grants [[Haman]] permission for the genocide of the Jews without apparently inquiring what people he is targeting (Esther 3:10–11).

The king's character contains both danger and opportunity. When [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]] approaches him uninvited — an act punishable by death unless the king extends his golden scepter — he receives her favorably and offers her up to half his kingdom. When [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]] finally reveals that [[Haman]] has plotted her people's destruction, Ahasuerus's anger turns on [[Haman]], and the courtier's attempt to beg [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]] for mercy is misread as an assault on the queen, sealing his fate. The irony is that the salvation of the Jewish people comes through navigating the moods and policies of a powerful but ultimately malleable king.

## Key Events
- Holds great banquets and deposes Queen [[Vashti]] for her refusal (Esther 1)
- Chooses [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]] as queen (Esther 2:17)
- Grants [[Haman]] his signet ring and permission for the Jewish genocide decree (Esther 3:10–11)
- Receives [[Esther (Queen)|Esther]]'s uninvited approach and extends his scepter (Esther 5:2)
- Has Mordecai publicly honored after a sleepless night reading the chronicles (Esther 6)
- Turns against [[Haman]] when his plot against the Jews is revealed (Esther 7:7–10)
- Issues a second decree allowing the Jews to defend themselves (Esther 8:8–14)
- Elevates [[Mordecai]] to Haman's former position (Esther 8:2)

## Relationships

- [[Mordecai]] - Esther's cousin; elevated to prime minister after Haman's fall
- [[Haman]] - chief minister; villain who plotted the Jewish genocide

## Notes
- Ahasuerus is identified with Xerxes I of Persia by most historians, whose invasion of Greece (480 BC) occurred between Esther 1 and Esther 2 on the chronology
- Despite being a pagan king who never explicitly acknowledges Israel's God, his decisions become the instruments of Jewish deliverance — a central theme of the book of Esther's theology of providence
- The feast in Esther 1 and the feasts of Purim in Esther 9 form a literary bracket around the entire narrative of threat and salvation
