# Caiaphas

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** High priest who presided at Jesus' trial before the Sanhedrin; declared it better that one man die for the people
- **Family:** Son-in-law of Annas the former high priest
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[John]], [[Acts]]

## Biography
Joseph Caiaphas served as the Jewish high priest from approximately AD 18 to 36, making him one of the longest-serving high priests of the Second Temple period. His tenure overlapped almost exactly with that of [[Pilate]] as Roman governor, and both men were removed from their positions by the Roman legate Vitellius in AD 36–37. His priestly family, the "house of Annas," dominated Jewish religious leadership during the early first century, with his father-in-law Annas having served as high priest (AD 6–15) and five of Annas's sons also serving in the office.

In the Gospel accounts, Caiaphas is portrayed as the primary instigator of the plot to arrest and execute Jesus. Following the raising of Lazarus, the Sanhedrin convened in emergency session, alarmed that Jesus' growing popularity might draw a Roman military response and destroy both the temple and the nation. It was Caiaphas who articulated what John presents as an ironic and unconsciously prophetic statement: "You know nothing at all. Nor do you understand that it is better for you that one man should die for the people, not that the whole nation should perish" (John 11:49–50). John explicitly identifies this as prophecy: Caiaphas, as high priest, unknowingly articulated the atonement theology of Jesus' death — one man dying for the nation, and "not for the nation only, but also to gather into one the children of God who are scattered abroad" (John 11:52).

At the trial before the Sanhedrin, Caiaphas presided over an irregular night proceeding that the Gospels suggest violated multiple provisions of Jewish legal standards. When the witnesses brought against Jesus failed to agree, the proceedings stalled until Caiaphas directly adjured Jesus to answer under oath: "I adjure you by the living God, tell us if you are the Christ, the Son of God" (Matthew 26:63). Jesus' affirmation — "You have said so. But I tell you, from now on you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven" (Matthew 26:64) — elicited Caiaphas's theatrical tearing of his robes and the verdict of blasphemy. The Sanhedrin condemned Jesus to death and sent him to [[Pilate]] for Roman execution.

Archaeological confirmation of Caiaphas's existence came in 1990, when an ossuary (bone box) inscribed "Joseph son of Caiaphas" was discovered in Jerusalem, possibly containing his actual skeletal remains.

## Key Events
- Articulates the politically expedient case for Jesus' death (John 11:49–50)
- Chairs the Sanhedrin meeting that decides to arrest and kill Jesus (John 11:53)
- Presides over the night trial before the Sanhedrin (Matthew 26:57–68)
- Adjures Jesus under oath; declares the answer blasphemy; tears his robes (Matthew 26:63–65)
- Sanhedrin binds Jesus and sends him to [[Pilate]] (Matthew 27:1–2)
- Interrogates Peter and John after the resurrection and healing of the lame man (Acts 4:6)

## Notes
- John's comment that Caiaphas's statement was unconscious prophecy (John 11:51) is one of Scripture's most striking examples of ironic divine sovereignty operating through human hostility
- His ossuary discovery in 1990 is one of the most significant archaeological finds related to New Testament figures
- The pattern of religious establishment crushing prophetic figures — priests killing prophets — reaches its apex with Caiaphas's role in the crucifixion
