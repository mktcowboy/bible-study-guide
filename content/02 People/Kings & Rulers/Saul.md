# Saul

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** First king of Israel; Benjaminite ruler rejected for disobedience
- **Family:** Son of Kish; father of [[Jonathan]], Michal, and others
- **Key Books:** [[1 Samuel]], [[2 Samuel]], [[1 Chronicles]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Saul was the son of Kish from the tribe of Benjamin. Israel demanded a king like the nations, and Saul's outward appearance seemed to fit the people's desire: he was tall, impressive, and from a notable family. [[Samuel]] anointed him, and the Spirit of God came upon him. His early reign included signs of promise, public confirmation, and deliverance from the Ammonite threat at Jabesh-gilead.

Saul's tragedy is that early promise gave way to fear, impatience, and disobedience. At Gilgal, he offered sacrifice unlawfully when Samuel delayed, revealing a king more controlled by pressure than trust. Later, he failed to obey the LORD's command concerning Amalek and [[Agag]], sparing what was devoted to destruction. Samuel's rebuke is one of the Bible's clearest statements about obedience: to obey is better than sacrifice.

After Saul's rejection, [[David]] was anointed, and the Spirit departed from Saul. David entered Saul's court as musician and warrior, but Saul's jealousy grew after David's victory over [[Goliath]] and the people's praise. Saul repeatedly sought to kill David, while David repeatedly refused to seize the throne by killing Saul.

Saul's family relationships sharpen the tragedy. [[Jonathan]], Saul's son, loved David and recognized God's hand on him. Michal, Saul's daughter, became David's wife and was used by Saul in his attempts to control David. Saul's fear of losing the kingdom drove him into violence, suspicion, and spiritual darkness.

Near the end, Saul consulted the medium at Endor after receiving no answer from the LORD. Samuel's appearance announced judgment. Saul and Jonathan died on Mount Gilboa in battle against the Philistines. David mourned their deaths, honoring Saul as the LORD's anointed despite Saul's long persecution.

Saul is a warning about leadership without obedient trust: public charisma, spiritual experiences, and royal position cannot substitute for a heart submitted to God's word.

## Notes
- Saul is not a cartoon villain; his tragedy is the collapse of real promise under fear and disobedience.
- His story contrasts outward kingship with the kind of heart God seeks in David.
- Saul often speaks religious language while resisting God's command, making him a cautionary example of piety without submission.
- David's refusal to kill Saul preserves the theme that the kingdom must be received from God, not seized.
