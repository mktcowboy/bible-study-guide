# Festus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Roman governor who succeeded Felix; brought Paul before King Agrippa and ultimately sent him to Rome
- **Family:** Roman
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]]

## Biography
Porcius Festus succeeded [[Felix]] as Roman governor of Judea around AD 59–60 and served until his death in office approximately two years later. He is described more favorably than his predecessor in both the biblical and historical record — Josephus portrays him as relatively just, though his brief tenure and the difficult political situation he inherited left little time to restore order. He inherited the problem of Paul's imprisonment from [[Felix]] and had to navigate the competing pressures of Roman legal obligations and Jewish political demands.

Upon arriving in his new province, Festus traveled to Jerusalem, where the chief priests and Jewish leaders immediately pressed him to transfer Paul to Jerusalem for trial — intending to ambush and kill him on the way. Festus declined the transfer and invited them to Caesarea to state their case there. After hearing the accusations, he asked Paul if he was willing to go to Jerusalem to be tried "before me" on these charges. Paul's response was legally savvy and theologically pointed: "I am standing before Caesar's tribunal, where I ought to be tried... I appeal to Caesar" (Acts 25:10–11). This appeal to Caesar — a right of Roman citizens — effectively removed the case from Festus's jurisdiction and set Paul on course for Rome.

Festus was left with a practical problem: he needed to write Emperor Nero an explanation of the charges against Paul, but could identify no specific Roman criminal offence. He consulted King [[Agrippa]] II (Herod Agrippa II) who happened to be visiting Caesarea with his sister Bernice for a courtesy call. Festus described the Jewish charges against Paul as being "about their own religion and about a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (Acts 25:19). This description — delivered entirely without comprehension — is one of the most succinct and ironic summaries of the resurrection in the New Testament.

At the subsequent hearing before Agrippa, when Paul delivered his lengthy and eloquent defense, Festus interrupted: "Paul, you are out of your mind; your great learning is driving you out of your mind" (Acts 26:24). His objection was sincere — the claims were extraordinary — but Paul's calm response redirected the argument back to verifiable historical events. After the hearing, Festus and Agrippa agreed privately that Paul had done nothing deserving death or imprisonment, and Agrippa remarked that Paul could have been set free had he not appealed to Caesar (Acts 26:32).

## Key Events
- Succeeds [[Felix]] and inherits Paul's case (Acts 24:27)
- Jewish leaders ask him to transfer Paul to Jerusalem; he declines (Acts 25:1–5)
- Hears the Jewish case against Paul in Caesarea (Acts 25:6–7)
- Asks Paul about going to Jerusalem; Paul appeals to Caesar (Acts 25:9–12)
- Consults King [[Agrippa]] II for help framing charges for the emperor (Acts 25:13–22)
- Presides over Paul's hearing before Agrippa (Acts 25:23–26:32)
- Declares Paul "out of his mind" during his defense (Acts 26:24)
- Agrees with Agrippa that Paul is innocent (Acts 26:31–32)

## Notes
- Festus's summary of Christianity as a dispute about "a certain Jesus, who was dead, but whom Paul asserted to be alive" (Acts 25:19) captures the core gospel claim from an outside perspective with unintentional precision
- His accusation that Paul was "out of his mind" is the typical response of purely rationalistic categories encountering the resurrection claim
- The appeal to Caesar, while separating Paul from Festus's jurisdiction, ultimately fulfilled Jesus' words that Paul would testify "before kings" and would go to Rome (Acts 9:15; 23:11)
