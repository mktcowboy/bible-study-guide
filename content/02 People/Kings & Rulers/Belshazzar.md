# Belshazzar

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Final Babylonian king; saw the handwriting on the wall the night Babylon fell to the Medes and Persians
- **Family:** Son of Nabonidus; historical co-regent with his father; called "son of Nebuchadnezzar" in Daniel (a dynastic designation)
- **Key Books:** [[Daniel]]

## Biography
Belshazzar appears exclusively in Daniel 5 as the final Babylonian king, who holds a great feast for a thousand of his lords on the very night Babylon falls to the Medes and Persians. For much of the 19th century, critics cited Belshazzar as a historical error in Daniel, since ancient records listed Nabonidus — not Belshazzar — as Babylon's last king. Archaeological discovery of the Nabonidus Chronicle and the Nabonidus Cylinder in the 19th and 20th centuries resolved this apparent discrepancy: Belshazzar was the son of Nabonidus and served as co-regent in Babylon while Nabonidus campaigned in Arabia. This explains why, in Daniel 5:16, Belshazzar offers [[Daniel]] the position of "third ruler in the kingdom" — not first or second, since Nabonidus and Belshazzar himself occupied those positions.

Daniel 5 describes the feast as a scene of willful sacrilege. Belshazzar commanded that the gold and silver vessels which [[Nebuchadnezzar]] had taken from the temple in Jerusalem be brought out so he and his guests could drink wine from them. While they drank and praised gods of gold, silver, bronze, iron, wood, and stone, a human hand appeared and began writing on the wall of the palace: "MENE, MENE, TEKEL, PARSIN." The king's color drained, his knees knocked together, and his court fell into confusion. None of the Babylonian wise men could interpret the inscription.

The queen (likely the queen mother, given the court context) remembered [[Daniel]]'s gifts and recommended him. [[Daniel]] appeared and delivered the interpretation with blunt directness, refusing the king's offered gifts until he had spoken. He recounted how [[Nebuchadnezzar]] had been humbled by God and warned Belshazzar that he had not humbled his heart in spite of knowing all this: "You have praised the gods of silver and gold, of bronze, iron, wood, and stone, which do not see or hear or know, but the God in whose hand is your breath, and whose are all your ways, you have not honored" (Daniel 5:23). The words meant: numbered, numbered, weighed, divided — Belshazzar's kingdom had been measured, found deficient, and was given to the Medes and Persians.

That very night, Belshazzar was killed, and [[Cyrus]] the Persian (through his general Gobryas) took the city of Babylon. Classical sources, including Xenophon and Herodotus, describe the Persians entering Babylon by diverting the Euphrates River and entering through the riverbed. The fall was sudden and total — exactly as the handwriting predicted. Belshazzar's defiant feast thus became his last act, and his story serves in Daniel as a paradigm of the judgment that falls on those who defy the God of Israel.

## Key Events
- Holds a great feast for 1,000 lords and desecrates the temple vessels (Daniel 5:1–4)
- The hand writes on the wall: MENE MENE TEKEL PARSIN (Daniel 5:5)
- Wise men fail to interpret the inscription (Daniel 5:7–8)
- Queen mother recommends [[Daniel]] (Daniel 5:10–12)
- [[Daniel]] refuses the king's gifts and interprets the writing as divine judgment (Daniel 5:17–28)
- Is killed that same night as Babylon falls to Darius the Mede (Daniel 5:30–31)

## Notes
- The archaeological confirmation of Belshazzar as co-regent is one of the most celebrated examples of historical accuracy in a passage once considered legendary
- Belshazzar's sin is described not as ignorance but as knowing better — he knew what happened to [[Nebuchadnezzar]] and still defied the God of Israel
- The phrase "handwriting on the wall" has entered world literature as a permanent idiom for imminent judgment
