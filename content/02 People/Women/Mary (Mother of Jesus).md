# Mary (Mother of Jesus)

## Overview

- **Testament:** New Testament — though her story fulfills deep OT patterns (Eve, Hannah, the daughter of Zion)
- **Role:** Mother of the Messiah; first and most faithful disciple; bearer of the Word made flesh; present at the beginning of the Gospel and at the beginning of the church
- **Books:** Luke (most detailed account), Matthew (genealogy and birth narrative), John (Cana, the cross), Acts (upper room)

Mary of Nazareth stands at the most significant moment in all of human history. She was the woman through whom God chose to enter his own creation in human flesh — carrying, nursing, and raising the eternal Son of God. She is not a peripheral figure; she is the hinge between the old covenant and the new, the first human being to hear and receive the word about Jesus and to say yes to it without condition. She is honored by all generations, as her own Magnificat predicted (Luke 1:48).

---

## Profile

Mary was a young Jewish woman living in Nazareth, a small and obscure town in Galilee, in the first century under Roman occupation. She was a virgin betrothed to a man named Joseph, a descendant of David (Luke 1:27; Matthew 1:16-20). Betrothal in first-century Jewish culture was legally binding — far more than a modern engagement. A betrothed woman was legally a wife; a breach of betrothal required a bill of divorcement, and unfaithfulness during betrothal was treated as adultery. Virginity before and during betrothal was expected and valued. The discovery that a betrothed woman was pregnant, in such a culture, carried profound social consequences — shame, the possibility of public disgrace, and the legal right of the husband to expose her formally. Mary's "yes" to the angel's announcement was therefore not a small thing. She accepted not only a miraculous calling but also its immediate social cost.

She was poor. When the time came for her purification after the birth of Jesus and for the [[Presentation of Jesus]] in the Temple, Mary and Joseph brought the offering prescribed in Leviticus 12:8 for those who could not afford a lamb: two turtledoves or two young pigeons (Luke 2:24). This was explicitly the provision for the poor. The one who would offer himself as the final sacrifice of the ages was born into a family that could only afford the poorest sacrifice the law required. God's condescension in the Incarnation extended to the economic circumstances of the family he chose.

Her family connections are briefly indicated. Elizabeth — who was pregnant with John the Baptist — was her "cousin" (KJV) or kinswoman (Luke 1:36). The Greek *syngenis* means a relative of unspecified degree. This connection meant that the mothers of the two central figures of the New Testament — the forerunner and the Messiah himself — were kin to one another. Whether Mary had other children has been debated throughout church history; the Gospels mention brothers of Jesus (Matthew 13:55; Mark 6:3), though the nature of that relationship is contested.

What the Gospels reveal of Mary's character is consistent and striking. She asked a direct and honest question when Gabriel appeared to her (Luke 1:34 — not unbelief, but a genuine inquiry about how). She submitted immediately and completely (1:38). She recognized the theological meaning of what was happening to her and gave voice to it in the Magnificat — one of the most theologically rich poems in Scripture. She "kept" and "pondered" things in her heart (Luke 2:19, 51) — she was a reflective, attentive witness who stored what she saw and heard. She knew when to act (at Cana, John 2:3) and when to step back (after Jesus's ministry began). She endured Simeon's prophecy about a sword piercing her soul and lived to see it fulfilled at the cross. She was at the foot of the cross (John 19:25) and in the upper room after the Ascension (Acts 1:14). She was a disciple in the fullest sense.

## The Annunciation and Fiat

[[Annunciation]] — Luke 1:26-38.

In the sixth month of Elizabeth's pregnancy, the angel Gabriel was sent from God to a city of Galilee named Nazareth, to a virgin betrothed to a man named Joseph, "and the virgin's name was Mary" (1:27). Gabriel's greeting was extraordinary: "Hail, thou that art highly favoured, the Lord is with thee: blessed art thou among women" (1:28). The Greek *kecharitomene* — "highly favoured" or "full of grace" — is a perfect passive participle, indicating a state of grace already received, a settled condition. She was troubled at the saying and "cast in her mind what manner of salutation this should be" (1:29) — she was not swept away by flattery but thought carefully about what this greeting meant.

Gabriel's announcement: she would conceive in her womb and bring forth a son and call his name Jesus. "He shall be great, and shall be called the Son of the Highest: and the Lord God shall give unto him the throne of his father David: And he shall reign over the house of Jacob for ever; and of his kingdom there shall be no end" (1:32-33). The announcement directly invokes the Davidic Covenant and the promise of an eternal kingdom — this was not merely a birth announcement but a declaration that the great promises of Israel were being fulfilled.

Mary's question was practical and honest: "How shall this be, seeing I know not a man?" (1:34). This is not the question of unbelief (contrast Zechariah's question in 1:18, which was rebuked; Mary's was answered). She understood that she was a virgin; she was asking about the mechanism. Gabriel's answer: "The Holy Ghost shall come upon thee, and the power of the Highest shall overshadow thee: therefore also that holy thing which shall be born of thee shall be called the Son of God" (1:35). The word *episkiazo* — "overshadow" — is the same word used of the cloud that covered the tabernacle and filled it with the glory of God at Exodus 40:35 (LXX). The tabernacle was the dwelling place of God in the wilderness; now Mary would become, in a unique and unrepeatable way, the vessel through whom God entered the world in flesh. No other typology in Scripture so perfectly captures the meaning of the moment.

Gabriel added that Elizabeth, her kinswoman, had also conceived in her old age — for "with God nothing shall be impossible" (1:37), echoing the word to Abraham and Sarah about Isaac (Genesis 18:14). Then Mary spoke her fiat — her "let it be done":

"Behold the handmaid of the Lord; be it unto me according to thy word." — Luke 1:38

She called herself the Lord's handmaid — *doule*, a female servant or slave, one with no rights of her own, entirely at the master's disposal. Her "yes" was not reluctant consent; it was glad surrender. She trusted God's word without conditions, without negotiations, without waiting for circumstances to be more convenient. This is the response of covenant faith. It is the most important "yes" any human being has ever spoken.

## The Magnificat

[[Visitation]] — Luke 1:39-56.

After the Annunciation, Mary rose and went with haste to a city of Judah in the hill country — likely the region of Hebron — to visit Elizabeth. When Mary's greeting reached Elizabeth's ears, the baby in her womb leaped for joy. Elizabeth was filled with the Holy Ghost and cried out:

"Blessed art thou among women, and blessed is the fruit of thy womb. And whence is this to me, that the mother of my Lord should come to me? For, lo, as soon as the voice of thy salutation sounded in mine ears, the babe leaped in my womb for joy. And blessed is she that believed: for there shall be a performance of those things which were told her from the Lord." — Luke 1:42-45

Two things stand out. Elizabeth — under prophetic inspiration — called the unborn child "my Lord." And she pronounced a beatitude on Mary specifically for her faith: "blessed is she that believed." Mary is commended not primarily for being chosen but for believing the word of God.

Mary's response — the Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55) — is the most theologically profound song in the New Testament. Its structure moves through three movements:

**Personal praise for what God has done to her (1:46-49):** "My soul doth magnify the Lord, and my spirit hath rejoiced in God my Saviour. For he hath regarded the low estate of his handmaiden: for, behold, from henceforth all generations shall call me blessed. For he that is mighty hath done to me great things; and holy is his name." She called God her Saviour — she knew she was not herself sinless but was a recipient of divine grace. She attributed everything to God's action, not her own merit. And she predicted with calm certainty that all generations would call her blessed — which is exactly what has happened.

**Declaration of God's universal character (1:50-53):** "And his mercy is on them that fear him from generation to generation. He hath shewed strength with his arm; he hath scattered the proud in the imagination of their hearts. He hath put down the mighty from their seats, and exalted them of low degree. He hath filled the hungry with good things; and the rich he hath sent empty away." This is theology compressed into poetry — God as the one who consistently reverses the expected order of the world, defending the lowly and confounding the powerful. The aorist tenses (past) describe things God has always been doing and is now doing definitively in this moment.

**Connection to Israel's covenant history (1:54-55):** "He hath holpen his servant Israel, in remembrance of his mercy; As he spake to our fathers, to Abraham, and to his seed for ever." The Incarnation is not a departure from God's purposes for Israel; it is their fulfillment. God is remembering his mercy, keeping his promise to Abraham. What Mary carries in her womb is the seed of Abraham in whom all nations will be blessed.

The Magnificat echoes [[Hannah's Prayer]] (1 Samuel 2:1-10) with unmistakable precision — the same themes of divine reversal, the lifting of the lowly, the mighty brought down, the hungry filled. Hannah was the mother of Samuel, who stood at the transition from the judges to the kings; Mary is the mother of the one who is the final and eternal King. Both songs celebrate the same God acting in the same pattern.

## The Birth and Early Years of Jesus

The journey to Bethlehem is narrated with deliberate simplicity in Luke 2:1-20. Caesar Augustus decreed a census; "all went to be taxed, every one into his own city" (2:3). Joseph took Mary, "being great with child," from Nazareth to Bethlehem, the city of David. In Bethlehem she brought forth her firstborn son, "and wrapped him in swaddling clothes, and laid him in a manger; because there was no room for them in the inn" (2:7). The Word by whom all things were made had nowhere to be born except a feeding trough.

Shepherds, announced by angels, came to see the child and told what they had been told. Mary's response is the key to understanding her inner life: "Mary kept all these things, and pondered them in her heart" (2:19). This phrase — in Greek *suntereo* (to preserve, hold together) and *symballein* (to ponder, throw together, weigh) — describes active, sustained reflection. She was not passively receiving events; she was storing them, cross-referencing them, turning them over. Luke uses the same phrase again at 2:51, after the episode of the Boy Jesus in the Temple. Mary was the primary witness from whom Luke almost certainly drew his account of the birth and childhood. She was the one who kept the memory.

The [[Presentation of Jesus]] brought the family to the Temple in Jerusalem (Luke 2:22-38). There Simeon took the infant Jesus in his arms and blessed God with the Nunc Dimittis — "Lord, now lettest thou thy servant depart in peace... for mine eyes have seen thy salvation." Then he said to Mary: "Behold, this child is set for the fall and rising again of many in Israel; and for a sign which shall be spoken against; (Yea, a sword shall pierce through thy own soul also,) that the thoughts of many hearts may be revealed" (2:34-35). From the very beginning of his life, the shadow of the cross fell across hers.

The flight to Egypt (Matthew 2:13-15) and the massacre of the innocents by [[Herod the Great]] meant that Mary's experience of motherhood was already bound up with danger, displacement, and grief. The holy family returned to Nazareth after Herod's death; and in Nazareth, the boy "increased in wisdom and stature, and in favour with God and man" (Luke 2:52) — in his mother's care.

The [[Boy Jesus in the Temple]] (Luke 2:41-52) is the last episode before his public ministry. The twelve-year-old Jesus remained in Jerusalem after the Passover festival without his parents' knowledge, found after three days of searching in the Temple courts among the teachers. Mary's response to finding him is entirely human and entirely maternal: "Son, why hast thou thus dealt with us? behold, thy father and I have sought thee sorrowing" (2:48). Jesus's reply — "How is it that ye sought me? wist ye not that I must be about my Father's business?" — is his first recorded statement, and it is a gentle but clear redefinition of his primary relationship. Joseph is "thy father"; God is "my Father." Mary "understood not the saying which he spake unto them" (2:50) — she continued to ponder.

## Wedding at Cana

[[Water to Wine]] — John 2:1-11.

Jesus and his disciples were invited to a wedding in Cana of Galilee, and Mary was there. When the wine ran out — a significant social failure in that culture — Mary went to Jesus: "They have no wine" (2:3). His response: "Woman, what have I to do with thee? mine hour is not yet come" (2:4). The word "Woman" (*gynai*) is not dismissive; it is a formal and respectful address (he uses it again from the cross in John 19:26). The phrase "what have I to do with thee?" is an idiomatic expression of mild distance — he was signaling that his actions would be governed not by family expectation but by his Father's timing. The "hour" in John always refers to the hour of the cross and glorification (see John 7:30; 8:20; 12:23; 17:1).

Mary's response is the last recorded saying of Mary in all of Scripture. She turned to the servants and said:

"Whatsoever he saith unto you, do it." — John 2:5

It is a perfect statement of discipleship. She did not argue with him, did not press her request, did not insist on her maternal authority. She pointed away from herself entirely and directed total obedience to him. After three decades of intimacy with the Son of God, after pondering all these things in her heart, her final words in Scripture are not about herself at all. They are a transfer of trust to him. She knew his authority; she knew his character; she trusted that whatever he decided would be right. The servants obeyed; the water became wine; "this beginning of miracles did Jesus in Cana of Galilee, and manifested forth his glory; and his disciples believed on him" (2:11). Mary's intercession — quiet, confident, and immediately self-effacing — was the occasion of the first sign.

## At the Cross

John 19:25-27.

"Now there stood by the cross of Jesus his mother, and his mother's sister, Mary the wife of Cleophas, and Mary Magdalene." She was there. While the male disciples, with the exception of the beloved disciple, had scattered, Mary stood at the foot of the cross. She watched her son die. She heard his voice, saw his suffering, witnessed his death. Simeon's word was fulfilled: the sword pierced through her soul.

From the cross, in the last acts of his life before "It is finished," Jesus looked down and saw his mother and the beloved disciple:

"When Jesus therefore saw his mother, and the disciple standing by, whom he loved, he saith unto his mother, Woman, behold thy son! Then saith he to the disciple, Behold thy mother! And from that hour that disciple took her unto his own home." — John 19:26-27

Even in the extremity of crucifixion — in the act of bearing the sin of the world — he provided for his mother. He entrusted her to the disciple he loved; he entrusted the disciple to her. "From that hour that disciple took her unto his own home" — she was not left alone. This act of filial care from the cross is one of the most humanly moving moments in the Gospels, and it underscores the genuine humanity of the one who was simultaneously making atonement for the sins of the world.

The tradition that the beloved disciple is John, the son of Zebedee, would mean that Mary lived the rest of her days in John's household. Early tradition places her remaining years in Ephesus, where John eventually settled.

## In the Upper Room

Acts 1:14.

After the Ascension, the disciples returned to Jerusalem and gathered in the upper room: "These all continued with one accord in prayer and supplication, with the women, and Mary the mother of Jesus, and with his brethren." She is named specifically — the only person named by name in this assembly besides the apostles. She was among the 120 gathered to pray and wait for the promise of the Father, the outpouring of the Holy Spirit.

This is a detail of extraordinary significance. Mary is named at the [[Annunciation]] — the very first moment of the Gospel story — and she is named here, at the very first moment of the church's story. She bridges both. She received the word about Jesus before he was born; she was praying with the church as it waited to receive the Spirit he had promised. She is the living thread connecting the mystery of the Incarnation to the reality of the community it had created.

She did not lead the assembly. She was not elevated above the apostles. She prayed with them, in one accord, in supplication. She models continuity between the old covenant people of God and the new covenant church — a faithful Jewish woman who believed God's word, followed God's son, and took her place in God's new community.

## Theological Significance

Mary's significance is not primarily doctrinal in the sense of generating a theological system; she is a person, a disciple, a mother, a witness. But her place in the story of salvation is unique and unrepeatable, and its implications are genuinely theological.

She is the one through whom God chose to enter human history in the most intimate way possible. She was not merely an instrument — a passive vessel — but a consenting, believing, reflecting participant. Her obedience mattered. God did not override her will; Gabriel waited for her answer. Her "yes" was the free response of faith, and through that faith the eternal Word was clothed in human flesh. In this sense she is rightly called the first believer — the first human being to receive the word about Jesus and to believe it.

The "new Eve" typology, present in the early church fathers, is grounded in Scripture's own pattern. Eve heard a word from a spiritual messenger, doubted, disobeyed, and brought ruin. Mary heard a word from a spiritual messenger, believed, obeyed, and became the vessel of redemption. Where Eve said no to God and yes to the serpent, Mary said yes to God without reserve. The obedience of the second Adam (Jesus) and the faith of the new Eve (Mary) together stand in contrast to the failure in the garden.

The Magnificat demonstrates that Mary did not merely receive a biological role; she understood the theological meaning of what was happening. She knew that God was reversing the order of the world, keeping his covenant, fulfilling his word to Abraham. She was a theologian of the Incarnation before the Incarnation had fully occurred.

She is presented in Acts 1 not as an object of prayer but as one who prays — a member of the worshipping community, a disciple among disciples. The honor due to her is the honor due to the most faithful of disciples: she heard the word of God and kept it. As Jesus himself said, "Blessed are they that hear the word of God, and keep it" (Luke 11:28).
