# Eunice

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Jewish mother of Timothy; commended by Paul for her genuine faith which she passed on to her son
- **Family:** Jewish; wife of a Greek man; daughter of [[Lois]]; mother of [[Timothy]]
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]], [[2 Timothy]]

## Biography
Eunice was a Jewish woman living in Lystra (in the region of Lycaonia, modern Turkey) who had married a Greek man whose name is not recorded. Their son [[Timothy]] would become Paul's closest and most trusted co-worker. The mixed marriage — a Jewish woman and a Gentile man — may explain why Timothy was not circumcised as an infant, since Jewish identity in that culture was typically transmitted through the mother but circumcision required the father's active participation.

When Paul visited Lystra on his second missionary journey, he met [[Timothy]] there and found him well-spoken of by the brothers in Lystra and Iconium. Paul circumcised Timothy (Acts 16:3) — not as a theological requirement but for pragmatic missionary purposes, so that Timothy could enter synagogues without controversy. Eunice is not mentioned by name in this Acts passage, but Acts 16:1 identifies her as a believing Jewish woman, confirming that her faith preceded or coincided with Paul's arrival.

Paul's most direct commendation of Eunice comes in 2 Timothy 1:5: "I am reminded of your sincere faith, a faith that dwelt first in your grandmother Lois and your mother Eunice and now, I am sure, dwells in you as well." This is one of the most touching passages in the pastoral letters — Paul tracing [[Timothy]]'s faith to its maternal and grandmaternal roots, acknowledging that the faith of an old woman and a middle-aged woman had produced the young man who was now Paul's right hand.

The continuity of faith across three generations (Lois, Eunice, Timothy) speaks to the power of household transmission of belief. Eunice's decision to raise her son in "the sacred writings" (2 Timothy 3:15) despite being in a religiously mixed household, and despite the challenges of Lystra's Gentile environment, is the kind of quiet, persistent faithfulness that the Scriptures consistently commend.

## Notes
- Eunice represents the irreplaceable role of mothers in faith transmission — Paul explicitly credits her as the immediate source of Timothy's sincere faith
- Her mixed marriage (Jewish woman, Greek husband) created the ambiguity about Timothy's status that Paul resolved through circumcision, illustrating how personal biography intersected with mission strategy in the early church
- The three-generation faith chain — Lois, Eunice, Timothy — is the New Testament's clearest affirmation that family discipleship is a legitimate and effective form of theological formation
