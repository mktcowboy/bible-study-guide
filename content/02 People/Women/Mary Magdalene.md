# Mary Magdalene

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Devoted follower of Jesus from whom he cast out seven demons; first witness of the resurrection
- **Family:** From Magdala (a town on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee); no family details given
- **Key Books:** [[Luke]], [[John]]

## Biography
Mary Magdalene is one of the most prominent women in the Gospels and one of the most misrepresented figures in Christian tradition. Her name identifies her as being from Magdala, a prosperous fishing town on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee. She is introduced in Luke 8:2 as one of several women who had been healed of evil spirits and infirmities, specifically that "seven demons had gone out" of her — a detail indicating her pre-discipleship life included serious spiritual oppression. She is listed first among the women in every Gospel listing that includes multiple women, suggesting she was recognized as the leader of the female disciples.

Despite centuries of popular identification, there is no biblical evidence connecting Mary Magdalene with the "sinful woman" who anointed Jesus' feet in Luke 7 (which immediately precedes her introduction) or with Mary of Bethany, the sister of Lazarus. These conflations appear in Western church tradition from at least Gregory the Great (AD 591) but are not supported by the text. The Gospels consistently present her simply as a devoted follower and witness, not as a former prostitute.

Mary Magdalene was among the women who followed Jesus from Galilee to Jerusalem and watched his crucifixion from a distance (Matthew 27:55–56; Mark 15:40–41; John 19:25). She watched as Joseph of Arimathea and Nicodemus buried Jesus (Matthew 27:61; Mark 15:47; Luke 23:55) and returned early Sunday morning with spices to anoint his body (Mark 16:1; Luke 24:10; John 20:1).

John 20 gives the most detailed and personal account of Mary Magdalene at the resurrection. She arrived at the tomb while it was still dark, found it empty, and ran to tell Peter and the Beloved Disciple. After they came and left, she remained outside the tomb weeping. She encountered two angels and then turned to see a man she assumed was the gardener — until Jesus spoke her name: "Mary." Her recognition — "Rabboni!" — and Jesus' commission — "Do not cling to me... but go to my brothers and say to them, I am ascending" — make her the first eyewitness of the resurrected Christ and the first commissioned announcer of the resurrection. The early church gave her the title "Apostle to the Apostles."

## Key Events
- Healed by Jesus of seven demons (Luke 8:2; Mark 16:9)
- Travels with Jesus and supports his ministry financially (Luke 8:2–3)
- Witnesses the crucifixion from a distance (Matthew 27:55–56)
- Watches the burial of Jesus (Matthew 27:61; Mark 15:47)
- Returns to the tomb on Sunday morning (John 20:1)
- Encounters the risen Jesus; her name is spoken by him (John 20:16)
- First to announce the resurrection to the disciples (John 20:18)

## Notes
- Mary Magdalene's title "Apostle to the Apostles" (apostola apostolorum) from patristic tradition captures the significance of her resurrection commission — she was sent (apostolos = sent one) to announce the risen Christ to those who would become the apostles
- The seven demons cast out of her do not imply moral failure — demon possession in the Gospels is consistently presented as affliction, not sin, and her post-deliverance life of devoted service reflects genuine transformation
- John 20's intimate scene — Jesus speaking her name, her recognizing him — is one of the most theologically resonant moments of recognition in Scripture, recalling Jesus' teaching that the shepherd calls his own sheep by name (John 10:3)
