# Martha

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Sister of [[Lazarus]] and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]]; one of Jesus' closest friends; known for active service and bold confession of faith
- **Family:** Sister of [[Lazarus]] and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]]; from [[Bethany]], near Jerusalem
- **Key Books:** [[Luke]], [[John]]

## Biography
Martha of Bethany stands as one of the most vivid characters in the Gospels — a woman of energetic hospitality, deep faith, and remarkable theological courage. Together with her sister [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] and her brother [[Lazarus]], she belonged to a household that Jesus loved in a particular and personal way: "Now Jesus loved Martha, and her sister, and Lazarus" (John 11:5). Their home in [[Bethany]], a village about two miles from Jerusalem on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives, served as Jesus' base and refuge when he came to the city in his final weeks.

Martha's first appearance in Luke 10:38–42 has defined how generations have read her character. While [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] "sat at Jesus' feet and heard his word," Martha was "cumbered about much serving" and appealed to Jesus to send Mary to help her. Jesus' reply was gentle but searching: "Martha, Martha, thou art careful and troubled about many things: But one thing is needful: and Mary hath chosen that good part, which shall not be taken away from her" (Luke 10:41–42). The text neither condemns Martha's service nor praises her distraction; it corrects her anxiety and commends her sister's chosen posture. Martha's impulse to serve was not wrong — it was the worry and the comparison that drew the rebuke.

Her greatest moment comes in [[Lazarus Raised|John 11]]. When Jesus arrived in [[Bethany]] four days after Lazarus had died and been entombed, it was Martha who went out to meet him on the road — a characteristic act of forward movement. She addressed him with grief-mixed faith: "Lord, if thou hadst been here, my brother had not died. But I know, that even now, whatsoever thou wilt ask of God, God will give it thee" (John 11:21–22). When Jesus told her "Thy brother shall rise again," she interpreted it as the general resurrection of the last day. Jesus then spoke to her one of the most profound of his "I am" declarations: "I am the resurrection, and the life: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live" (John 11:25). Martha's response is one of the highest confessions in all the Gospels: "Yea, Lord: I believe that thou art the Christ, the Son of God, which should come into the world" (John 11:27) — a confession nearly identical to Peter's at Caesarea Philippi.

At John 12:2, six days before the Passover, Martha is again at service — this time at a supper given in Jesus' honour in [[Bethany]], with Lazarus among those who sat at table and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] anointing Jesus' feet. Martha's presence serving at this final Bethany supper rounds out her portrait: faithful, active, devoted, unchanged in her gift of hospitality even after all she had witnessed.

## Key Events
- [[Lazarus Raised]] — Met Jesus on the road, received the "I am the resurrection" declaration, made her great confession (John 11:20–27)
- Rebuked gently by Jesus for anxiety over service while [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] sat at his feet (Luke 10:38–42)
- Present serving at the Bethany supper six days before the Passover (John 12:2)

## Notes
- Martha's confession in John 11:27 parallels Peter's confession in Matthew 16:16 almost word for word, yet it is often overlooked; she declared Jesus the Christ and Son of God to his face, in the midst of grief, before witnessing the miracle
- Her objection "Lord, by this time he stinketh: for he hath been dead four days" (John 11:39) is not a failure of faith but an honest statement — she had not yet understood the full scope of what Jesus was about to do
- The contrast with [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] in Luke 10 is frequently misread as a condemnation of active service; the text condemns anxiety and resentment, not hospitality itself
- Martha serves at every Bethany scene in which she appears — Luke 10, John 11, John 12 — making service her consistent, defining characteristic
