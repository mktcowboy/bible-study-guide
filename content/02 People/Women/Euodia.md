# Euodia

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Woman in the Philippian church whom Paul urges to be reconciled with Syntyche; a co-worker of Paul
- **Family:** Member of the Philippian church; no other details given
- **Key Books:** [[Philippians]]

## Biography
Euodia is mentioned only once in the New Testament, in Philippians 4:2–3, where Paul writes: "I entreat Euodia and I entreat Syntyche to agree in the Lord. Yes, I ask you also, true companion, help these women, who have labored side by side with me in the gospel together with Clement and the rest of my fellow workers, whose names are in the book of life."

The manner of Paul's appeal is notable. He does not address them together as a unit but separately — "I entreat Euodia" and "I entreat Syntyche" — addressing each woman individually as a person of dignity and moral agency. The call is to "agree in the Lord" (Greek: to auto phronein en kyrio), meaning not just to stop fighting but to share a common mind in Christ — the same language Paul has used throughout the letter for the entire congregation's unity (Philippians 1:27; 2:2; 2:5).

The dispute between Euodia and [[Syntyche]] is not described in any detail. It has given rise to much speculation, but Paul offers no indication of who was right or wrong, or what the conflict was about. What he does make clear is that both women were genuine, effective co-workers in the gospel. He describes them as having "labored side by side" with him — using the same athletic/combat metaphor (synathleō) he applies to the whole congregation's spiritual struggle. These were not peripheral members but women who had done frontline gospel work alongside Paul.

The appeal to a "true companion" to help mediate reflects Paul's pastoral approach: rather than resolving the dispute by apostolic decree, he involves the congregation's leadership in reconciliation. This models the community's responsibility to support the unity of its members, especially those who have served it well.

## Notes
- Paul's equal, separate appeal to Euodia and Syntyche (rather than addressing them together) signals that both bore equal responsibility for the conflict — neither is identified as the aggressor
- The affirmation that she "labored side by side" with Paul in the gospel and has her name in the book of life means that Euodia is remembered not primarily for her conflict but for her faithful ministry
- Her story is a reminder that even genuinely effective gospel workers can experience serious relational failures, and that Paul's vision of church unity was strong enough to address those failures directly
