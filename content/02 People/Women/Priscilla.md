# Priscilla

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Co-worker of Paul; wife of Aquila; expelled from Rome; explained the way of God more fully to Apollos
- **Family:** Wife of [[Aquila]]; no children mentioned; from Rome, later Corinth and Ephesus
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]], [[Romans]], [[1 Corinthians]], [[2 Timothy]]

## Biography
Priscilla (also called Prisca in Paul's letters) and her husband [[Aquila]] were among the most significant lay workers in the apostolic church. They were expelled from Rome around AD 49 when Emperor Claudius expelled all Jews from the city, and they settled in Corinth where they met Paul. They were tentmakers by trade, and Paul lived and worked with them during his eighteen months in Corinth (Acts 18:2–3), forming a deep partnership that would shape churches across the Roman world.

What distinguishes Priscilla in the New Testament records is the unusual pattern of her name appearing before her husband's in four of the six references to the couple (Acts 18:18, 18:26; Romans 16:3; 2 Timothy 4:19 — versus Acts 18:2 and 1 Corinthians 16:19 where Aquila is first). In the ancient world, the name appearing first in a couple's introduction typically indicated greater prominence, authority, or social standing. The consistency of this pattern across multiple authors and contexts (Luke in Acts, Paul in Romans and the pastorals) strongly suggests it reflects something real about Priscilla's spiritual stature within the partnership.

The episode with [[Apollos]] is the clearest window into Priscilla's ministry. When the eloquent and scripturally capable [[Apollos]] arrived in Ephesus, Priscilla and [[Aquila]] together heard him, recognized that his knowledge was incomplete (he knew only the baptism of John), and "took him aside and explained to him the way of God more accurately" (Acts 18:26). The private, gentle correction they offered — rather than public contradiction — reflects both wisdom and genuine concern for [[Apollos]]'s effectiveness. Her role in completing the theological education of one of the early church's most gifted preachers is impossible to overstate.

Romans 16:3–5 reveals that they had also risked their lives for Paul at some point: "greet Prisca and Aquila, my fellow workers in Christ Jesus, who risked their necks for my life, to whom not only I give thanks but all the churches of the Gentiles give thanks to them also." Their house in Rome was also a church meeting place. They had returned to Rome after Claudius's death and hosted a congregation there.

## Key Events
- Expelled from Rome with [[Aquila]] under Claudius's edict; settles in Corinth (Acts 18:2)
- Works as tentmaker alongside Paul; he stays with them (Acts 18:3)
- Accompanies Paul to Ephesus at close of second journey (Acts 18:18–19)
- Takes [[Apollos]] aside with [[Aquila]] and explains the way of God more accurately (Acts 18:26)
- Church meets in their house in Ephesus (1 Corinthians 16:19) and Rome (Romans 16:5)
- Risked her life for Paul (Romans 16:4)

## Notes
- Priscilla's name appearing before [[Aquila]]'s in the majority of references suggests the New Testament authors recognized her as the more spiritually prominent of the two, an early indicator of women's active teaching ministry in the apostolic church
- Her role in teaching [[Apollos]] — a man gifted enough to be compared favorably to Paul — demonstrates that in the apostolic era, theological instruction could flow from women to men when gifting and knowledge warranted it
- The pattern of house church hosting across multiple cities (Corinth, Ephesus, Rome) shows that Priscilla and [[Aquila]] made their entire life infrastructure available as a resource for the gospel
