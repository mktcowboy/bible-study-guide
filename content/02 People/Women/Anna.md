# Anna

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Prophetess, daughter of Phanuel; widowed after seven years of marriage; served in the temple with fasting and prayer; spoke of Jesus at his presentation
- **Family:** Daughter of Phanuel; from the tribe of Asher; widow
- **Key Books:** [[Luke]]

## Biography
Anna daughter of Phanuel, of the tribe of Asher, is introduced in Luke 2:36–38 as a prophetess who had served in the temple in Jerusalem her entire adult life. She had been married for seven years before her husband died, and then she had lived as a widow, "and then as a widow until she was eighty-four" (or possibly "for eighty-four years" — the Greek is ambiguous). Either reading places her as an elderly woman who had devoted the greater part of her long life to worship, fasting, and prayer in the temple courts, "day and night."

Her tribal identity — from Asher — is noteworthy. Asher was one of the ten northern tribes that had been largely dispersed or lost following the Assyrian conquest in 722 BC. The fact that Anna's Asherite lineage was preserved and known suggests either that her family had maintained tribal records carefully, or that her prophetic prominence made her background a matter of known public significance. Some scholars see this as Luke's deliberate signal that the coming of Jesus represented the restoration of all Israel, not just Judah — the representative of a "lost" tribe being among the first to recognize the Messiah.

The setting is the same as [[Simeon]]'s recognition of Jesus — the temple presentation, when Mary and Joseph brought the infant for the purification rites and the dedication of the firstborn. While [[Simeon]] took the child in his arms and delivered the Nunc Dimittis, Anna "came up at that very hour and began to give thanks to God and to speak of him to all who were waiting for the redemption of Jerusalem" (Luke 2:38). Her response was both vertical (thanksgiving to God) and horizontal (telling others) — the twin movements of authentic prophetic witness.

Anna and [[Simeon]] together form a carefully constructed literary pair in Luke's infancy narrative: an elderly man and an elderly woman, both devoted and Spirit-led, both waiting for Israel's redemption, both recognizing the infant. This pairing echoes the Old Testament pattern of paired witnesses and affirms the sufficiency of two witnesses (Deuteronomy 19:15) to establish a matter.

## Key Events
- Identified as a prophetess from the tribe of Asher, daughter of Phanuel (Luke 2:36)
- Married seven years, then a widow of great age who never left the temple (Luke 2:36–37)
- Served God day and night with fasting and prayer (Luke 2:37)
- Came at the very hour [[Simeon]] prophesied over Jesus (Luke 2:38)
- Gave thanks to God and spoke about Jesus to all waiting for Jerusalem's redemption (Luke 2:38)

## Notes
- Anna's tribal affiliation with Asher — one of the "lost" northern tribes — suggests Luke is signaling that Jesus' coming was the restoration of all Israel, not just Judah
- Her title "prophetess" places her in the line of [[Miriam]], [[Deborah]], and [[Huldah]] — the recognized female prophets of the Old Testament — suggesting the prophetic gift was understood as available to women throughout Israel's history
- The pairing of Anna and [[Simeon]] at the temple presentation reflects Luke's consistent literary pattern of presenting male-female pairs as witnesses (Zechariah/Elizabeth; Simeon/Anna; the two disciples on the road to Emmaus and the women at the tomb)
