# Elisheba

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Wife of Aaron; daughter of Amminadab; mother of the founding priestly family of Israel
- **Family:** Daughter of Amminadab; sister of Nahshon (prince of Judah); wife of [[Aaron]]; mother of Nadab, Abihu, Eleazar, and Ithamar
- **Key Books:** [[Exodus]]

## Biography
Elisheba daughter of Amminadab is one of the women whose brief mention carries enormous genealogical and theological weight. She appears only once by name in Scripture — Exodus 6:23 — yet her family connections link the two greatest tribal lines of early Israel.

Her father Amminadab and brother Nahshon are prominent figures from the tribe of Judah. Nahshon served as the prince of Judah (Numbers 1:7; 2:3) and was the first to bring his offering at the dedication of the tabernacle (Numbers 7:12). This same Nahshon appears in the genealogy of [[Ruth]] as the grandfather of [[Boaz]] (Ruth 4:20) and ultimately in the genealogy of [[David]] — and through David, in Matthew's genealogy of [[Jesus Christ]] (Matthew 1:4). Elisheba is thus the sister of a figure who stands in the direct Davidic and messianic line.

Her husband [[Aaron]] was the first high priest of Israel, and through him she became the mother of the entire Aaronic priestly line. Of her four sons: Nadab and Abihu served as priests until they were consumed by fire for offering unauthorized fire before the LORD (Leviticus 10:1–2). Eleazar succeeded [[Aaron]] as high priest and is the ancestor of all the Zadokite priests who served Israel through the monarchic period. Ithamar supervised the construction of the tabernacle furnishings (Exodus 38:21) and became the ancestor of the house of Eli.

The marriage of Elisheba — daughter of Judah — to [[Aaron]] — son of Levi — symbolically unites the two greatest tribes of Israel in the very families through whom Israel's religious and royal destinies would run. She herself is given no narrative beyond her genealogical identity, but the children and grandchildren of her union with [[Aaron]] shaped the entire priestly institution of ancient Israel.

## Key Events
- Identified as Aaron's wife and the mother of his four sons (Exodus 6:23)
- Her brother Nahshon served as prince of Judah (Numbers 1:7)
- Her sons Nadab and Abihu died offering unauthorized fire (Leviticus 10:1–2)
- Her son Eleazar became high priest after [[Aaron]] (Numbers 20:28)

## Notes
- The brief mention of Elisheba in Exodus 6:23 accomplishes enormous genealogical work — connecting the Levitical priesthood to the Davidic royal line through the same family
- Her sons Nadab, Abihu, Eleazar, and Ithamar together shaped the early history of Israelite priesthood through both their faithfulness (Eleazar, Ithamar) and their failure (Nadab, Abihu)
- She is a silent figure in Scripture whose significance is almost entirely expressed through her family connections, a pattern common to many women in the genealogical sections of the Pentateuch
