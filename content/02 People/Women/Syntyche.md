# Syntyche

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Woman in the Philippian church whom Paul urges to be reconciled with Euodia; a co-worker of Paul
- **Family:** Member of the Philippian church; no other details given
- **Key Books:** [[Philippians]]

## Biography
Syntyche is mentioned only once in the New Testament, in Philippians 4:2–3, alongside [[Euodia]] in Paul's appeal for their reconciliation: "I entreat Euodia and I entreat Syntyche to agree in the Lord. Yes, I ask you also, true companion, help these women, who have labored side by side with me in the gospel together with Clement and the rest of my fellow workers, whose names are in the book of life."

Her name is Greek and means "agreeable" or "fortunate" — an irony given that she is remembered precisely for a disagreement. Like [[Euodia]], she is addressed individually rather than as part of a pair, affirming her personal dignity and responsibility. Paul's appeal is not to change positions or submit to the other party, but to "agree in the Lord" — to find their common ground in Christ and in the gospel that both had faithfully served.

The conflict between Syntyche and [[Euodia]] is not described, and neither is identified as the party in the wrong. What the text does make abundantly clear is that both were genuine co-laborers in gospel work alongside Paul. Their partnership with Paul in the Philippian mission — which included being among the earliest converts in Europe when Paul came to Philippi in Acts 16 — was real and recognized. Their names appear in the same list as [[Clement]] and "the rest of my fellow workers, whose names are in the book of life."

The relational conflict between two effective gospel workers creates a pastoral problem in Philippi severe enough that Paul addresses it in writing from prison. This is the only point in his letter to the Philippians where he names individuals in a corrective context, suggesting that the conflict between Syntyche and [[Euodia]] had become a genuine threat to the church's unity, which Paul has been calling for throughout the letter (Philippians 1:27; 2:1–4).

## Notes
- Both Syntyche and [[Euodia]] are credited with being in the book of life and with co-laboring in the gospel — Paul's appeal assumes genuine faith on both sides, not a dispute between believer and unbeliever
- The Philippian church was founded partly through women (Acts 16 records [[Lydia]] as the first convert), and the prominence of women like Syntyche and [[Euodia]] among Paul's named co-workers reflects the particular character of that congregation
- Paul's request for a "true companion" to mediate shows that reconciliation in the church is a community responsibility, not left solely to the parties in conflict
