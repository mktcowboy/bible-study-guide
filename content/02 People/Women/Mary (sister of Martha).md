# Mary (sister of Martha)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Sister of Martha and Lazarus in Bethany; sat at Jesus' feet to hear his teaching; anointed Jesus' feet with costly ointment
- **Family:** Sister of [[Martha]] and [[Lazarus]]; from Bethany, near Jerusalem
- **Key Books:** [[Luke]], [[John]]

## Biography
Mary of Bethany is one of the most distinctive disciples in the Gospels, known for two acts that Jesus himself pronounced memorable and significant. She was the younger sister of [[Martha]] and the sister of [[Lazarus]] whom Jesus raised from the dead. The family appears to have been among Jesus' closest friends — John 11:5 explicitly states "Now Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus" — and Bethany, their village just two miles from Jerusalem, served as his regular base when he came to the city.

Mary's first appearance is in Luke 10:38–42, where she is contrasted with her sister [[Martha]]. While [[Martha]] busied herself with hospitality, "Mary sat at the Lord's feet and listened to his teaching" (Luke 10:39). When [[Martha]] complained that Mary had left her to serve alone, Jesus defended Mary: "Mary has chosen the good portion, which will not be taken away from her" (Luke 10:42). Sitting at a teacher's feet was the posture of a student or disciple — and this was an unusual posture for a woman in that culture. Jesus' defense of Mary's choice validated her right to learn as a disciple.

Her second defining moment comes at the raising of [[Lazarus]] (John 11). When Jesus arrived four days after her brother's death, Mary "fell at his feet" weeping — the same posture she took as a learner, now taken in grief. Jesus' response to her weeping is one of the shortest and most profound verses in Scripture: "Jesus wept" (John 11:35). Her grief moved him to tears even though he knew he was about to raise Lazarus.

Mary's third appearance is the anointing of Jesus in John 12:1–8. Six days before the Passover, she brought a pound of very expensive pure nard ointment and anointed Jesus' feet, wiping them with her hair — an act of extravagant devotion that filled the house with fragrance. When Judas Iscariot objected to the waste, Jesus defended her: "Leave her alone, so that she may keep it for the day of my burial... wherever the gospel is proclaimed in the whole world, what she has done will also be told in memory of her" (Mark 14:9). Mary's anointing was, Jesus said, a prophetic act of preparation for his burial — she alone among his disciples seems to have understood that he was going to die.

## Key Events
- Sits at Jesus' feet as a disciple while [[Martha]] serves (Luke 10:39–42)
- Defended by Jesus as having "chosen the good portion" (Luke 10:42)
- Falls at Jesus' feet weeping after Lazarus's death (John 11:32)
- Jesus weeps at her grief; then raises [[Lazarus]] (John 11:33–44)
- Anoints Jesus' feet with costly nard six days before the Passover (John 12:1–3)
- Her act praised by Jesus as preparation for burial; promised to be remembered forever (Mark 14:9)

## Notes
- Jesus' declaration that Mary "chose the good portion" (Luke 10:42) has been interpreted across the centuries as an affirmation of contemplative, receptive discipleship alongside active service
- Her anointing of Jesus is one of the few acts performed by a follower that Jesus declared would be eternally remembered — a remarkable commendation that the Gospel itself fulfills by recording it
- The three scenes together (sitting at feet to learn, falling at feet in grief, anointing his feet in devotion) form one of the most intimate portraits of discipleship in the Gospels
