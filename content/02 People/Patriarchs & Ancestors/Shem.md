# Shem

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Son of Noah; ancestor of the Semitic peoples and of Abraham's line
- **Family:** Son of [[Noah]]; brother of Ham and Japheth; ancestor of [[Abraham]]
- **Key Books:** [[Genesis]]

## Biography
Shem was the eldest son of [[Noah]], listed first among Noah's three sons (Shem, Ham, and Japheth) in the biblical genealogies. His name is widely believed to mean "name" or "renown." He and his brothers, along with their wives, survived the great flood in the ark with their father, and together they repopulated the earth afterward.

The most defining moment in Shem's early narrative comes after the flood, when Noah became drunk and lay uncovered in his tent. Ham saw his father's nakedness and told his brothers. Shem and Japheth responded with honor — they walked backward and covered their father without looking at him. When Noah awoke and learned what had happened, he blessed Shem specifically, saying "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem!" (Genesis 9:26). This blessing elevated Shem's line as the one through whom God's covenant purposes would flow.

Genesis 10 presents the "Table of Nations," in which Shem's descendants become the Semitic peoples — Elam, Asshur, Arphaxad, Lud, and Aram. The name "Semitic" itself is derived from Shem. More significantly, Genesis 11 traces the line from Shem's son Arphaxad through Shelah, Eber, Peleg, Reu, Serug, Nahor, Terah, and finally to [[Abraham]]. This Shemite genealogy is the backbone of the entire patriarchal narrative and ultimately the messianic line.

Shem is said to have lived 600 years, outliving many of his descendants including possibly living into the time of [[Abraham]] himself. Ancient Jewish tradition sometimes identifies Shem with the mysterious figure of Melchizedek, though this is speculative and not supported by the biblical text directly.

## Key Events
- Survives the flood in the ark with [[Noah]] (Genesis 6–8)
- Covers Noah's nakedness with honor alongside Japheth (Genesis 9:23)
- Receives Noah's blessing: "Blessed be the LORD, the God of Shem" (Genesis 9:26)
- Becomes progenitor of the Semitic peoples (Genesis 10:21–31)
- Genealogical line leads to [[Abraham]] (Genesis 11:10–26)

## Notes
- Shem's line becomes the vehicle of the Abrahamic covenant and ultimately the messianic promise
- The Semitic peoples — including the Hebrews, Arameans, Assyrians, and Arabs — all trace their lineage to Shem
- Noah's blessing of Shem over Ham foreshadows the narrative priority given to the Israelite people throughout the Old Testament
