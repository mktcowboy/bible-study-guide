# Noah

## Overview

- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Righteous man preserved through the flood; recipient of the Noahic covenant; ancestor of all post-flood humanity
- **Family:** Son of Lamech; husband of unnamed wife; father of [[Shem]], Ham, and Japheth
- **Key Books:** Genesis 5:28–10:32 (his theological shadow extends to Hebrews 11, 1 Peter 3, 2 Peter 2, Matthew 24)
- **Period:** c. 2900–1950 BC (traditional antediluvian chronology); lived 950 years (Genesis 9:29)

Noah stands at one of the most dramatic structural hinges in biblical history. He is the last man of the old world and the first man of the new. He alone is described as righteous in his generation, and through him God preserves his purposes for creation after a catastrophe of global scope. Yet Noah is no idealized hero — he fails significantly after the flood, and Scripture presents him as a flawed instrument of divine grace rather than a model of human perfection. His story raises the deepest questions about divine judgment, grace, the nature of righteousness, and the faithfulness of God to his creatures.

---

## His Context: The World Before the Flood (Genesis 6:1-7)

The Bible's account of Noah does not begin with Noah. It begins with the world he lived in — and the picture is catastrophic. Genesis 6:5 is one of the most complete indictments of human nature in all of Scripture: "And God saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, and that every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually." Every imagination. The thoughts of the heart — the inner life, not merely external actions. Only evil — without mixture. Continually — without pause or exception. The author of Genesis stacks qualifier upon qualifier to make the point: this is not a society with moral problems; this is a society that has become morally total in its corruption.

Genesis 6:1-4 describes the "sons of God" taking the "daughters of men" — a passage that has generated sustained interpretive debate across Jewish and Christian history. Whether the "sons of God" are fallen angels (the view of much early Jewish literature and early church fathers), Sethite patriarchs intermarrying with Cainite women, or powerful rulers taking women for harems, the narrative function is clear: the corruption of every line and boundary God has established, the failure of every social and moral structure. The Nephilim — the mighty ones, the men of renown — are the product. Greatness and power without righteousness is the description of the age.

The earth was filled with *hamas* — a Hebrew word meaning violence, injustice, wrongdoing. It is a dense word that encompasses physical violence, exploitation, judicial corruption, and oppression. The same word will be used by the prophets to describe the sins that bring judgment on Israel (Amos 3:10, Micah 6:12, Habakkuk 1:2). God saw it, and "it grieved him at his heart" (Genesis 6:6). The language of divine grief is not anthropomorphism to be explained away; it is the Scripture's insistence that God's relationship to creation is not cold sovereignty but engaged, personally invested care. The world he made and declared good has become a world of sorrow.

God said: "I will destroy man whom I have created from the face of the earth; both man, and beast, and the creeping thing, and the fowls of the air; for it repenteth me that I have made them" (6:7). The creation mandate of Genesis 1 — fill the earth, rule it, tend it — had been so corrupted that the creation itself would be undone. Or almost undone.

## Noah: Righteous in His Generation (Genesis 6:8-10)

"But Noah found grace in the eyes of the LORD" (Genesis 6:8). This is the first use of the word "grace" (*chen*) in the Bible. It appears without explanation or elaboration — simply that Noah found it, that God gave it. Before any record of Noah's righteousness, before his obedience to the ark commission, before his sacrifices: grace. The order matters. Noah's righteousness did not earn God's favor; God's grace preceded and enabled Noah's righteousness. The same pattern that will govern Abraham, Israel, and the new covenant is established here, at the beginning.

"These are the generations of Noah: Noah was a just man and perfect in his generations, and Noah walked with God" (6:9). Three statements — and they need careful reading. *Just* (*tsaddiq*) means righteous, upright in conduct, right in relation to God and neighbor. *Perfect* (*tamim*) means blameless, without defect — the same word used of sacrificial animals that were acceptable to God, and the same word used of Job (Job 1:1) and demanded of Abraham (Genesis 17:1). It does not mean sinless; it means wholehearted, integrated, without deliberate moral double-dealing. *Walked with God* is the phrase used of Enoch in Genesis 5:24 — the man who did not die but was taken by God. It describes an ongoing, intimate, obedient relationship. Noah lived in the same world as his neighbors. He breathed the same air. He saw the same violence. And he walked with God.

Genesis 6:9 says "in his generations" — an important qualifier. Noah was righteous relative to those around him, righteous as a man in that epoch of human history. The Bible does not claim he was without sin; his post-flood failure with wine will demonstrate otherwise. But he was genuinely different from his generation in the fundamental orientation of his life, and that is what God saw.

## The Commission to Build the Ark (Genesis 6:11-22)

God revealed his plan to Noah: he would bring a flood of waters upon the earth to destroy all flesh. And he gave Noah a commission: build an ark. The dimensions were specific — 300 cubits long, 50 cubits wide, 30 cubits high (approximately 450 feet by 75 feet by 45 feet). The material was gopher wood — traditionally identified as cypress or cedar. The entire structure was to be coated with pitch (*kopher*) inside and out. Three decks. Rooms throughout. One window at the top, one cubit square. One door in the side.

The theological significance of the ark's design is often overlooked: the plan was God's, not Noah's. Noah did not devise a boat, calculate capacity, and ask God to bless it. God gave the blueprint. Noah's role was obedient execution. This is consistent with every pattern in Scripture where God commissions a physical structure for his purposes — the tabernacle (Exodus 25–27), the temple (1 Chronicles 28:11-19), the new temple of Ezekiel (40–43). God provides the pattern; the human being builds according to the vision received. Noah's faithfulness was precisely faithfulness: following what had been revealed with exactness.

"Thus did Noah; according to all that God commanded him, so did he" (6:22). This statement, so simple on its surface, covers approximately 100 years of work. Noah was 500 years old when his sons were born (Genesis 5:32) and 600 when the flood came (7:6). A century of labor on a massive wooden structure, in a world that had never seen such a thing, for a catastrophe that had never occurred, with no visible evidence that it was coming. He did it "according to all that God commanded him." Not most of it. Not what he understood. All of it.

## The Faith of Noah: A Century of Obedience

Hebrews 11:7 is the New Testament's interpretive key to Noah: "By faith Noah, being warned of God of things not seen as yet, moved with fear, prepared an ark to the saving of his house; by the which he condemned the world, and became heir of the righteousness which is by faith." Every phrase carries weight.

"Things not seen as yet" — the flood had not happened; in many interpretations of Genesis 2:5-6, rain itself had not fallen on the pre-flood earth, which was watered by mist or underground springs. Noah was building a boat for water that did not exist yet, in response to a disaster that had never occurred. This is faith in its purest form: acting on revelation rather than visible evidence.

"Moved with fear" — not paralyzed by fear, but activated by it. The reverential awe that takes God's warnings seriously and responds. This is not the opposite of faith; it is faith's proper response to God's holiness and the gravity of coming judgment.

"Condemned the world" — his faithful obedience was, simply by existing, an implicit verdict on those around him. He preached for a century; 2 Peter 2:5 calls him "a preacher of righteousness." Not a single person outside his family believed and entered the ark. His life and work declared what could happen, what was coming. No one heeded. The ark itself was a sermon in wood that stood for a hundred years.

"Became heir of the righteousness which is by faith" — Noah's righteousness was not earned by his works but received through faith, the same pattern that Abraham would later embody (Genesis 15:6) and that Paul would systematize in Romans and Galatians.

## The Flood (Genesis 7–8)

God gave Noah the final instruction: enter the ark with his family and seven pairs of every clean animal and one pair of each unclean animal, for in seven days the rain would begin. Noah was 600 years, two months, and seventeen days old when the flood came. He and his wife, his sons Shem, Ham, and Japheth and their wives — eight people total — entered the ark. The animals came to Noah; he did not gather them. They came "two and two unto Noah into the ark" (7:9), an act that the narrative presents as divinely arranged.

"And the LORD shut him in" (7:16). Not Noah. Not his family. The LORD shut the door. This single detail is freighted with meaning: the safety of those inside the ark was not secured by their own action but by God's. When the door closed, it was closed by the hand of God. Outside: judgment. Inside: the ark, which means "box" or "chest" — the same Hebrew word (*tebah*) used for the basket in which Moses was placed in the Nile. Both Moses and Noah were placed in a *tebah* amid waters of death and preserved for a future purpose.

Rain fell for forty days and forty nights. The fountains of the great deep burst open. The waters prevailed and increased and covered the highest mountains by fifteen cubits. Every person, every land animal, every bird outside the ark died. "And every living substance was destroyed which was upon the face of the ground, both man, and cattle, and the creeping things, and the fowl of the heaven; and they were destroyed from the earth: and Noah only remained alive, and they that were with him in the ark" (7:23).

"And God remembered Noah" (8:1). This is not God forgetting and then recalling — it is the idiom of covenant attention, of God turning his purposes actively toward the one he has not abandoned. God made a wind blow over the earth; the waters began to recede. After 150 days the waters abated; on the 17th day of the 7th month the ark rested on the mountains of Ararat. By the first day of the tenth month, the tops of the mountains were visible. Noah waited 40 more days, then opened the window and sent out a raven — which flew back and forth until the waters were dried. Then three times he sent a dove: the first time it returned with no landing place; the second time it returned with a fresh olive leaf in its mouth — the waters were abating; the third time it did not return. The earth was dry. ([[The Flood]])

## The Sacrifice and God's Response (Genesis 8:20-22)

When Noah came out of the ark, his first act was not to survey the devastated world, plant crops, or secure shelter. He built an altar to the LORD and took of every clean animal and every clean bird and offered burnt offerings on the altar. This is the first altar in Genesis. It is also, notably, the first act of post-flood humanity: worship.

God smelled the "sweet savour" — the language of acceptable sacrifice. And God said in his heart: "I will not again curse the ground any more for man's sake; for the imagination of man's heart is evil from his youth; neither will I again smite any more every living thing, as I have done" (8:21). This divine resolution is remarkable for what it acknowledges: the imagination of man's heart is still evil. Not reformed. Not elevated by the experience of judgment. The flood cleansed the earth of a corrupt generation but did not change human nature. The problem of Genesis 6:5 remains in Genesis 8:21; the word is the same — *yetser*, imagination, inclination. The flood was not the solution to human sin. The flood was judgment; it was not redemption. Redemption requires something else entirely.

Yet God committed himself never again to destroy all living things with a flood. This is grace operating despite human nature, not because of it. The sacrifice of Noah was accepted; God's covenant purposes moved forward.

## The Noahic Covenant (Genesis 9:1-17)

God blessed Noah and his sons and renewed the creation mandate: "Be fruitful and multiply, and replenish the earth" (9:1). The vocabulary echoes Genesis 1:28 precisely. The new world after the flood is meant to be a fresh start on the same divine commission. But several things have changed.

The relationship between humanity and the animal world has shifted: "And the fear of you and the dread of you shall be upon every beast of the earth" (9:2). The harmonious dominion of Eden is no longer operative; the post-fall, post-flood world has a different character. Animals may now be eaten (9:3) — a new permission not found in Genesis 1-2, where both humans and animals were given plants for food. However, blood may not be eaten: "But flesh with the life thereof, which is the blood thereof, shall ye not eat" (9:4). The life is in the blood; the blood belongs to God.

Most significantly, capital punishment for murder is instituted: "Whoso sheddeth man's blood, by man shall his blood be shed: for in the image of God made he man" (9:6). The foundation for human government and justice is laid here, resting entirely on the *imago Dei* — because human beings bear God's image, their lives have inviolable worth, and the shedding of that blood demands an accounting. This verse is foundational to the entire biblical tradition of law and governance.

Then the formal covenant: "And I, behold, I establish my covenant with you, and with your seed after you; And with every living creature that is with you, of the fowl, of the cattle, and of every beast of the earth with you; from all that go out of the ark, to every beast of the earth" (9:9-10). The Noahic covenant is the widest of all biblical covenants in scope. It includes not only Noah and his descendants but every living creature — birds, cattle, beasts. It is not a covenant made with Israel or with any particular people; it is made with creation itself. And it is unilateral: God binds himself, without conditions on Noah's side.

The sign: the rainbow. In Hebrew, *qeshet* — the same word used for a warrior's bow, the weapon of a divine warrior. God hangs up his bow in the clouds. The warrior has laid down his weapon of judgment. "I do set my bow in the cloud, and it shall be for a token of a covenant between me and the earth" (9:13). The rainbow does not remind humanity of God's promise — it is said to be a sign for God himself: "And I will look upon it, that I may remember the everlasting covenant between God and every living creature" (9:16). Every rainbow is God keeping faith with Noah, with creation, with every human being who has ever lived. Every flood that falls short of total destruction is the Noahic covenant operating.

## The Vineyard and the Curse (Genesis 9:20-27)

After the flood, Noah planted a vineyard, made wine, drank, and became drunk. He lay uncovered in his tent. Ham, one of his three sons, saw his father's nakedness and went and told his brothers. The verb for "saw" is not incidental; it implies lingering, perhaps with contempt or mockery. Shem and Japheth took a garment, laid it on both their shoulders, walked backward into the tent, and covered their father's nakedness — their faces averted, refusing to see what their brother had publicized.

When Noah woke and understood what his youngest son had done, he spoke a curse: not on Ham, but on Canaan, Ham's son. "Cursed be Canaan; a servant of servants shall he be unto his brethren" (9:25). He then blessed Shem — "Blessed be the LORD God of Shem; and Canaan shall be his servant" — and Japheth — "God shall enlarge Japheth, and he shall dwell in the tents of Shem; and Canaan shall be his servant."

This episode is jarring. The man who walked with God through a century of ridicule, who alone remained righteous when the world was uniformly wicked, stumbles in domestic obscurity within a few verses of his greatest deliverance. Noah is not a perfect saint. He is a man of genuine faith who is also genuinely flawed. The biblical portrait is honest: the same Noah who "found grace in the eyes of the LORD" is also the Noah who lies naked and drunk in his tent. This is not inconsistency in the narrative; it is accuracy about the human condition. Righteousness in the biblical sense is not the absence of failure but a fundamental orientation toward God that holds when it is tested most severely — at the moment of the ark. After the crisis, Noah is an ordinary fallen man.

The incident also explains the ethnic and national tensions that will shape the rest of the Pentateuch. Ham's line produces Canaan — the people whose land Israel will eventually inherit. The curse of Canaan is not a blessing on any form of slavery but the narrative explanation for Israel's eventual displacement of the Canaanites. Shem's line — the Semitic peoples — leads directly to Abraham, Israel, and the Messiah. Japheth's descendants spread into the wider Gentile world.

## The Table of Nations (Genesis 10)

Genesis 10 — the Table of Nations — is a unique document in the ancient world. Seventy nations are traced from Noah's three sons. From Japheth: the peoples who settled Asia Minor, Greece, the Aegean, and northward. From Ham: Egypt, Canaan, the peoples of the ancient Near East's southern and western regions, including Nimrod — "a mighty hunter before the LORD," founder of Babylon and Nineveh (10:8-12). From Shem: the Semitic peoples — Elam, Asshur, and eventually the line that leads to Eber (the ancestor of the Hebrews) and on to Abram.

Every human being alive traces to one man who walked with God. The enormous diversity of post-flood humanity — its languages, lands, and peoples — is gathered under one narrative of divine preservation. Paul would invoke this when preaching in Athens: God "hath made of one blood all nations of men for to dwell on all the face of the earth" (Acts 17:26). The unity of the human race is a theological claim, and Noah is its demonstration.

## Noah in the New Testament

### The Days of Noah: Matthew 24 and Luke 17
Jesus used Noah as his primary illustration for the circumstances surrounding his second coming: "But as the days of Noe were, so shall also the coming of the Son of man be. For as in the days that were before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day that Noe entered into the ark, And knew not until the flood came, and took them all away; so shall also the coming of the Son of man be" (Matthew 24:37-39). The behavior Jesus describes is not particularly scandalous — eating, drinking, marrying. The point is normalcy: life proceeding without awareness of impending judgment, without urgency, without faith in the warning given. The flood came not upon people engaged in spectacular wickedness (though they were wicked) but upon people who were simply not paying attention to what God had said. Jesus's warning is directed at the same pattern of spiritual unawareness.

### The Ark as Type: 1 Peter 3
Peter draws a careful typological connection between Noah's ark and baptism: "Which sometime were disobedient, when once the longsuffering of God waited in the days of Noah, while the ark was a preparing, wherein few, that is, eight souls were saved by water. The like figure whereunto even baptism doth also now save us (not the putting away of the filth of the flesh, but the answer of a good conscience toward God,) by the resurrection of Jesus Christ" (1 Peter 3:20-21). The ark did not save by cleansing its passengers; the floodwaters were not bathing water but waters of judgment. The ark saved by passing through the waters of death and arriving on the other side. Baptism, Peter argues, works the same way — not as a physical washing but as a passage through death (Christ's death, identified with in baptism) into new life.

### Noah as Preacher: 2 Peter 2
"And spared not the old world, but saved Noah the eighth person, a preacher of righteousness, bringing in the flood upon the world of the ungodly" (2 Peter 2:5). Peter uses Noah as an example of God's willingness to deliver the righteous out of judgment while bringing that judgment on the wicked. The phrase "a preacher of righteousness" tells us something Genesis does not spell out — that Noah's testimony to his generation was verbal as well as visible. He not only built the ark; he spoke. The response was the same as the response to many prophets: no one listened.

### Hebrews 11: Faith That Condemns the World
"By faith Noah, being warned of God of things not seen as yet, moved with fear, prepared an ark to the saving of his house; by the which he condemned the world, and became heir of the righteousness which is by faith" (Hebrews 11:7). The Hall of Faith treats Noah as one of its foundational figures — the man who believed when there was no visible evidence, acted across a century when there was no encouragement, and whose obedience was simultaneously an act of salvation for his household and an implicit condemnation of everyone who refused to believe.

## Theological Significance

Noah occupies a unique structural position in the biblical narrative. He is the "remnant" — the believing minority preserved through judgment so that God's purposes for humanity can continue. This pattern of the remnant appears repeatedly in Scripture: Elijah alone among the prophets of Baal, the 7,000 who had not bowed to Baal (1 Kings 19:18), the faithful returning exiles, the Jewish-Gentile community of the church. Every use of "remnant" theology in the Old and New Testaments has its structural origin in Noah.

The Noahic covenant is foundational to all other biblical covenants. It establishes the category of God's self-binding commitment to his creation and to human beings. It is also the broadest covenant — encompassing all humanity, all living creatures, and the earth itself. Every subsequent covenant (Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, new) is narrower in scope but rests on the Noahic foundation: the world will continue; God will not destroy all life; his purposes for creation will not be abandoned.

Noah also illustrates that the problem of human sin is not solved by judgment. The pre-flood world was destroyed, but Genesis 8:21 acknowledges that human nature did not change. The flood was an act of justice, not redemption. It cleared the earth; it did not renew the heart. The solution to "every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (6:5) would require something more radical than a flood: the cross, the Spirit, the new creation. Noah points toward that need even as his story demonstrates God's patience with a world that has not yet received it.

Finally, Noah is the first recipient of what will become a sustained biblical emphasis: that God's grace moves toward the righteous — not because their righteousness is perfect, but because their fundamental orientation is toward God. Noah found grace before he built the ark. He was righteous before the commission. And after the flood, when he failed, he remained in the covenant. Grace is not conditioned on perfection; it is given to those who walk with God, as imperfect as that walk may be.
