# Abraham

## Overview

- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** First patriarch; father of Israel; the covenant originator through whom all nations are blessed; the Bible's defining example of justifying faith
- **Family:** Born Abram; son of Terah; husband of Sarah (Sarai) and later Keturah; father of Ishmael (through Hagar), Isaac (through Sarah), and 6 sons through Keturah; grandfather of Esau and Jacob
- **Key Books:** [[Genesis]] 11–25
- **Period:** c.2100–1925 BC (traditional)

---

## From Ur to Canaan: The Call (Genesis 11–12)

Abram was born in Ur of the Chaldeans — one of the most sophisticated urban centers of the ancient world, a city of literacy, commerce, and the worship of the moon god Nanna. His father Terah set out for Canaan with Abram, Sarai, and Lot, but they stopped in Haran, where Terah died at 205 years old. The call came in Haran, though Stephen's sermon in Acts 7:2-3 indicates the original vision appeared to Abraham while he was still in Mesopotamia, before his father's death.

The call is among the most important speeches in all of Scripture:

> "Get thee out of thy country, and from thy kindred, and from thy father's house, unto a land that I will shew thee: And I will make of thee a great nation, and I will bless thee, and make thy name great; and thou shalt be a blessing: And I will bless them that bless thee, and curse him that curseth thee: and in thee shall all families of the earth be blessed." (Genesis 12:1-3)

Three commands — leave your country, your kindred, your father's house. Three personal promises — a great nation, a great name, personal blessing. One ultimate purpose — "all families of the earth" blessed through him. The call is simultaneously addressed to one specific man and universal in its intended reach. Abram was 75 when he departed. Sarai was barren (11:30). God promised a great nation to a childless couple past the normal age of bearing children. The faith required was immediate and visible — everything Abram knew, he was leaving, and the destination was unnamed.

When they arrived in Canaan, God appeared again at Shechem by the oak of Moreh: "Unto thy seed will I give this land" (12:7). Abram built an altar there — a worshipping act that marked the land as belonging to the LORD even before it belonged to Abram. He continued to Bethel and built another altar, calling on the name of the LORD. These altars trace Abram's journey as a geography of worship.

Then famine struck. Abram went down to Egypt — a decision the text records without commentary, but whose consequences speak. He told Sarai to say she was his sister, because her beauty endangered him. Pharaoh took Sarai into his household. The LORD afflicted Pharaoh's house with great plagues on account of Sarai. When Pharaoh learned the truth, he rebuked Abram and sent him away with all his goods. Abram returned to Canaan wealthy in livestock, silver, and gold — but chastened. He had obtained his safety through deceit and had done so at the cost of Sarai's honor and Pharaoh's household's welfare. This failure is recorded honestly. The man of faith could also be the man of fear. He would repeat this same lie in Genesis 20, before Abimelech of Gerar.

## The Covenant (Genesis 15 and 17)

The most important theological event of Abraham's life spans two chapters and centers on God's unconditional commitment to his promises.

### Genesis 15 — The Covenant Ratified

After the return from Egypt and the separation from Lot, God spoke to Abram in a vision: "Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward" (15:1). Abram's response was direct — what good is a reward to a childless man? His heir would be his servant Eliezer. God took him outside and told him to count the stars. "So shall thy seed be" (15:5). Then comes the verse on which the entire New Testament doctrine of justification rests:

> "And he believed in the LORD; and he counted it to him for righteousness." (Genesis 15:6)

Not a law kept, not a sacrifice offered, not a work performed — Abram believed, and that act of trust was reckoned as righteousness. Paul builds Romans 4 and Galatians 3 entirely on this verse. It is the interpretive key to understanding the relationship between faith and justification throughout both Testaments.

God then made his covenant with Abram through one of the most arresting ceremonies in the ancient world. Animals were slaughtered and halved — a heifer, a she-goat, a ram, a turtledove, and a pigeon. The halved pieces were laid opposite one another. In covenant-ratification ceremonies of the ancient Near East, both parties would pass between the halved pieces, invoking upon themselves the fate of the animals if they broke the covenant. Abram fell into a deep sleep and a deep darkness came upon him. Then — God alone passed through the pieces as a smoking furnace and a burning lamp. Only God moved between the halves. The covenant was unilateral. God bound himself to the promise; Abram's fulfillment of conditions was not the basis of the covenant's validity.

The terms stated: descendants as numerous as the stars (15:5); possession of the land from the River of Egypt to the Euphrates (15:18-21, listing ten displaced nations); and a disclosure of the future — a 400-year sojourn of Abram's descendants in a foreign land, where they would be afflicted, before being brought out with great substance in the fourth generation (15:13-16). This is the announcement of Egypt and the Exodus, embedded in the covenant itself.

### Genesis 17 — The Covenant Renewed; Names Changed; Sign Given

Thirteen years after the birth of Ishmael, when Abram was 99, God appeared again: "I am the Almighty God; walk before me, and be thou perfect" (17:1). The covenant was restated with new dimensions. God changed Abram's name to Abraham — "father of a multitude" — though he had as yet no child through Sarai. God changed Sarai's name to Sarah. Both names anticipate a fulfillment not yet visible.

Circumcision was established as the sign of the covenant — a permanent mark in the flesh of every male in Abraham's household and of all his male descendants forever. "My covenant shall be in your flesh for an everlasting covenant: and the uncircumcised man child whose flesh of his foreskin is not circumcised, that soul shall be cut off from his people; he hath broken my covenant" (17:13-14). Every circumcised Israelite bore the mark of the covenant with Abraham. The sign was not the covenant itself; it was the seal applied to those who belonged to the covenant community.

"I will be their God" (17:8) — the covenant formula that echoes through Exodus, Leviticus, the Prophets, and Revelation. Its final iteration is Revelation 21:3: "Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his people, and God himself shall be with them, and be their God."

## The Three Visitors and the Intercession for Sodom (Genesis 18–19)

Three men appeared to Abraham by the oaks of Mamre in the heat of the day. Abraham, sitting at his tent entrance, ran to meet them, bowed to the ground, and urged them to stay. He hurried to Sarah and asked for cakes; he ran to the herd and selected a calf; he set curds and milk and the dressed calf before them. This hospitality — lavish, urgent, personally served — became the model of covenant hospitality throughout the ancient world and the explicit reference point of Hebrews 13:2: "Be not forgetful to entertain strangers: for thereby some have entertained angels unawares."

One of the three was the LORD (a theophany — a direct appearance of God in human form). The announcement came: "I will certainly return unto thee according to the time of life; and, lo, Sarah thy wife shall have a son" (18:10). Sarah, listening at the tent entrance, laughed within herself — she was past the age. The LORD heard her and asked why she laughed. "Is any thing too hard for the LORD?" (18:14). She denied laughing. He said, "Nay; but thou didst laugh" — a gentle, knowing rebuke. The child's name would carry the memory of her laughter: Isaac means "he laughs."

Then the visitors turned toward Sodom. The LORD disclosed his intention to Abraham — Sodom and Gomorrah's sin was very grievous, and he was going down to see it. Abraham drew near and began his intercession:

> "Wilt thou also destroy the righteous with the wicked? Peradventure there be fifty righteous within the city: wilt thou also destroy and not spare the place for the fifty righteous that are therein? That be far from thee to do after this manner, to slay the righteous with the wicked: and that the righteous should be as the wicked, that be far from thee: Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?" (18:23-25)

God agreed: for fifty righteous, the city would be spared. Abraham pressed: what if there were five fewer than fifty? God agreed to spare it for forty-five. Abraham pressed again: forty? Thirty? Twenty? Ten? At each step the LORD agreed. "I will not destroy it for ten's sake" (18:32). Then the LORD departed and Abraham returned to his place.

This is the Bible's first sustained intercessory prayer — bold, specific, repeated, grounded in theology ("Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?"), and humble ("I am but dust and ashes," 18:27). Abraham took God at his character and appealed to him on that basis. He was not challenging God; he was reasoning with God from God's own nature. The intercession did not save Sodom — ten righteous persons were not found there. But it did produce the rescue of Lot (19:29: "God remembered Abraham, and sent Lot out of the midst of the overthrow").

## Hagar and Ishmael (Genesis 16; 21)

Sarai had waited 10 years since the promise without conceiving. She gave her Egyptian servant Hagar to Abram as a wife — a practice well-attested in ancient Near Eastern legal customs, where a barren wife could provide an heir through her servant. Ishmael was born. Abram was 86.

But this was not the child of promise. The birth of Ishmael introduced rivalry into the household immediately — Hagar despised Sarai once she was pregnant, and Sarai treated her harshly, driving her out. The angel of the LORD met Hagar in the wilderness: "Return to thy mistress, and submit thyself under her hands" (16:9). He promised her son would be a great nation, though he would be a wild man, at odds with everyone. Hagar called God "Thou God seest me" — *El Roi* — the God who sees (16:13).

In Genesis 21, after Isaac's birth, the conflict erupted again. At Isaac's weaning feast, Sarah saw Ishmael mocking and demanded Abraham send Hagar and Ishmael away. "Cast out this bondwoman and her son: for the son of this bondwoman shall not be heir with my son, even with Isaac" (21:10). The thing was very grievous in Abraham's sight on account of his son. God told him to listen to Sarah: "In Isaac shall thy seed be called" (21:12). The promise of the great nation would be fulfilled through Isaac, not Ishmael — though God promised to make Ishmael a nation also, because he was Abraham's seed (21:13).

Hagar and Ishmael were sent away with bread and water and nearly died in the wilderness of Beersheba. God opened Hagar's eyes to a well of water. He was with Ishmael as he grew; Ishmael became a hunter and dwelt in the wilderness of Paran, and his mother took a wife for him from Egypt.

Paul reads the two sons in Galatians 4:21-31 as an allegory: Hagar represents the covenant of law given at Mount Sinai, producing bondage; Sarah represents the covenant of promise, the Jerusalem above, producing freedom. The two sons represent two fundamentally different ways of relating to God — seeking to earn the inheritance through flesh and works, versus receiving it through faith in the promise. "Cast out the bondwoman and her son: for the son of the bondwoman shall not be heir with the son of the freewoman" (Galatians 4:30). Paul is not condemning Ishmael the historical person; he is exposing a theological category.

## The Birth of Isaac and the Binding (Genesis 17–18; 21–22)

At the appointed time, Sarah conceived and bore a son to Abraham in his old age. Abraham was 100; Sarah was 90. Twenty-five years had passed since the call in Genesis 12. "And Abraham called the name of his son that was born unto him, whom Sarah bare to him, Isaac" (21:3). Sarah said: "God hath made me to laugh, so that all that hear will laugh with me" (21:6). The laughter of disbelief (18:12) became the laughter of joy.

Link to [[Birth of Isaac]].

Then came the supreme test of Abraham's life, and one of the most theologically dense passages in the entire Bible:

> "And he said, Take now thy son, thine only son Isaac, whom thou lovest, and get thee into the land of Moriah; and offer him there for a burnt offering upon one of the mountains which I will tell thee of." (Genesis 22:2)

"Thine only son" — not Ishmael, because the inheritance was through Isaac. "Whom thou lovest" — the language of personal bond. "Burnt offering" — total consumption, nothing held back. God was asking Abraham to offer the very gift through whom the promise was to be fulfilled. If Isaac died, the promise died. Every covenant God had made with Abraham, every star he had been shown, every nation that was to be blessed — all of it depended on Isaac surviving to have descendants.

Abraham rose early the next morning. He saddled his donkey, took two servants and Isaac, split the wood for the burnt offering, and set out for Moriah. The three-day journey. On the third day he saw the place from a distance. He told the servants to stay: "I and the lad will go yonder and worship, and come again to you" (22:5). "We will come again" — plural. Abraham expected them both to return.

Isaac carried the wood; Abraham carried the fire and the knife. They went together. Isaac asked: "Behold the fire and the wood: but where is the lamb for a burnt offering?" Abraham answered: "God will provide himself a lamb for a burnt offering: my son" (22:8). Whether Abraham knew exactly how God would provide or only trusted that he would, the answer was prophetic. They went together.

Abraham built the altar, bound Isaac, and laid him on it. He stretched out his hand and took the knife to slay his son. The angel of the LORD called from heaven: "Lay not thine hand upon the lad, neither do thou any thing unto him: for now I know that thou fearest God, seeing thou hast not withheld thy son, thine only son from me" (22:12). Abraham lifted his eyes and saw a ram caught in a thicket by its horns. He offered it as a burnt offering in the place of his son. He called that place *YHWH Jireh* — "the LORD will provide" — and added: "In the mount of the LORD it shall be seen" (22:14). The covenant oath followed, stronger than ever: "By myself have I sworn, saith the LORD... That in blessing I will bless thee, and in multiplying I will multiply thy seed as the stars of the heaven" (22:16-17).

Link to [[Binding of Isaac]].

Hebrews 11:17-19 provides the interpretive key: "By faith Abraham, when he was tried, offered up Isaac: and he that had received the promises offered up his only begotten son, Of whom it was said, That in Isaac shall thy seed be called: Accounting that God was able to raise him up, even from the dead; from whence also he received him in a figure." Abraham believed God could and would raise Isaac from the dead if necessary, because God's own promise about Isaac made that the only way the promise could survive. The Binding of Isaac is not merely a test of obedience; it is a demonstration of resurrection faith — the most forward-looking act of trust in the Old Testament.

## His Final Years and Death (Genesis 23–25)

Sarah died at age 127 in Hebron. Abraham mourned for her, then rose and negotiated with the Hittites for a burial place — the cave of Machpelah at the end of the field of Ephron at Machpelah near Mamre. The transaction is recorded in legal detail: 400 shekels of silver, weighed out at the current merchant's rate, a public transaction before all who sat at the gate of the city. The cave of Machpelah became Abraham's first legal possession in Canaan — obtained through a commercial transaction, not military conquest, not inheritance. He was a sojourner who bought a tomb before he owned a house. Hebrews 11 sees in this the posture of all the patriarchs: "These all died in faith, not having received the promises, but having seen them afar off, and were persuaded of them, and embraced them, and confessed that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth" (11:13).

Abraham was not content to leave the promise in jeopardy. He called his chief servant (traditionally identified as Eliezer) and made him swear: do not take a wife for Isaac from the daughters of the Canaanites; go to my country and my kindred and take a wife from there. Under no circumstances was Isaac to return to Mesopotamia — the land of promise must not be abandoned. The servant went, prayed for guidance at the well outside Nahor's city, and Rebekah appeared. She gave him and his camels water; she was willing to return with him; she was from Abraham's family. She became Isaac's wife, and Isaac was comforted after his mother's death (Genesis 24).

Abraham took another wife, Keturah, and had six more sons through her — Zimran, Jokshan, Medan, Midian, Ishbak, and Shuah — ancestors of various Arabian and Midianite peoples. He gave gifts to these sons and sent them eastward, away from Isaac.

Abraham died at 175 years old: "Then Abraham gave up the ghost, and died in a good old age, an old man, and full of years; and was gathered to his people" (25:8). Isaac and Ishmael buried him together in the cave of Machpelah beside Sarah. The two estranged sons stood together at their father's grave — a quiet, unremarked reconciliation embedded in a burial notice. Abraham is buried in Canaan, in the only piece of land he ever legally owned, waiting for what he had seen afar off.

## Abraham in the New Testament

Abraham is not merely a historical figure in the New Testament — he is a living theological argument. Four major NT treatments:

**Romans 4** — Paul's central and most extended treatment. The argument turns on chronology: Genesis 15:6 (faith counted as righteousness) precedes Genesis 17 (circumcision) by more than 13 years. Therefore Abraham was justified while uncircumcised — making him the father of all who believe without being circumcised (Gentiles), as well as the father of those who are circumcised and who also follow the faith of uncircumcised Abraham (Jews). The promise did not come through law (which had not yet been given) but through the righteousness of faith (4:13). Abraham "staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God; And being fully persuaded that, what he had promised, he was able also to perform. And therefore it was imputed to him for righteousness" (4:20-22). The reckoning of righteousness was not for Abraham's sake alone — "it was written also for our sakes, to whom it shall be imputed, if we believe on him that raised up Jesus our Lord from the dead" (4:23-24).

**Galatians 3:6-9** — The gospel was preached beforehand to Abraham: "The scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the heathen through faith, preached before the gospel unto Abraham, saying, In thee shall all nations be blessed" (3:8). The Gentile mission was not a new development or a plan B — it was announced to Abraham in Genesis 12:3 and is the culmination of what God said he would do from the beginning. "So then they which be of faith are blessed with faithful Abraham" (3:9).

**Hebrews 11:8-19** — Faith of Abraham in four movements: (1) He went out "not knowing whither he went" — obedience without a map. (2) He sojourned in the land of promise as in a foreign country — "he looked for a city which hath foundations, whose builder and maker is God" (11:10). His citizenship was elsewhere. (3) He received power to conceive seed "when he was past age, because he judged him faithful who had promised" (11:11) — faith in the character of the promiser. (4) He offered up Isaac, accounting that God could raise him from the dead (11:17-19). Four acts of faith, each more demanding than the last.

**James 2:21-23** — "Was not Abraham our father justified by works, when he had offered Isaac his son upon the altar? Seest thou how faith wrought with his works, and by works was faith made perfect? And the scripture was fulfilled which saith, Abraham believed God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness: and he was called the Friend of God." James and Paul are not in contradiction. Paul addresses the ground of justification (faith, not works of law). James addresses the evidence of justifying faith — the faith that justifies is a living, active, obedient faith, not a bare intellectual assent. Abraham's offering of Isaac (Genesis 22) demonstrated that his believing of Genesis 15:6 was genuine.

**John 8:56** — "Your father Abraham rejoiced to see my day: and he saw it, and was glad." Jesus locates the content of Abraham's faith as ultimately Christological — Abraham looked ahead and saw the day of Christ.

## Theological Significance

Abraham stands at the Bible's pivotal narrowing. The movement of Genesis is from creation (all humanity), to the fall (all humanity under judgment), to the flood and the nations (Genesis 1–11), to the one man through whom the reversal begins (Genesis 12). From Abraham all nations will be blessed — meaning Genesis 12 is the beginning of God's answer to Genesis 3. The promise to Abraham is not a tribal arrangement; it is the engine of redemptive history.

Paul identifies the promise to Abraham as the gospel in anticipatory form (Galatians 3:8). The method was the same — faith. The scope was the same — all nations. The basis was the same — God's faithfulness, not human merit. Abraham received the promise when he was not yet circumcised, before the law existed, with no track record of obedience to evaluate. God simply called, and Abraham simply believed, and God counted that as righteousness.

Abraham's life also teaches that faith is not the absence of doubt or failure. He lied twice about Sarah (Genesis 12; Genesis 20). He fathered Ishmael in impatience rather than waiting for the promised son. He laughed when told that a 100-year-old man and a 90-year-old woman would conceive (17:17 — Abraham's own laughter, rarely noted alongside Sarah's). He wavered and doubted and took matters into his own hands. Yet the verdict of Scripture is that he believed God, did not stagger at the promise, and was strong in faith giving glory to God. This is not a contradiction; it is a portrait of authentic faith — trust that persists through failure, that keeps returning to the promise even after it has been doubted or circumvented.

He received a pledge of the land but never owned more than a burial cave. He received the promise of an innumerable seed but saw only one son of promise before he died. He was called the friend of God (Isaiah 41:8; James 2:23; 2 Chronicles 20:7) — not a servant keeping a contract, but a friend admitted to God's counsels, who walked with God over decades of pilgrimage and waiting. He is the father of all who believe — not because of ethnic descent, but because of the faith he exemplified: trusting God's word when nothing visible confirmed it, and walking toward a promise that lay entirely beyond his power to produce.
