# Lazarus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Friend of [[Jesus Christ]] and brother of [[Martha]] and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]]; died and was raised from the dead after four days in the tomb
- **Family:** Brother of [[Martha]] and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]]; from [[Bethany]], near Jerusalem
- **Key Books:** [[John]]

## Biography
Lazarus of [[Bethany]] is one of the most remarkable figures in the Gospels — not for what he said, for he speaks no recorded word, but for what was done to and through him. He was the brother of [[Martha]] and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]], and his household in [[Bethany]] was among the closest to Jesus of any in the Gospels. The Gospel of John states plainly: "Now Jesus loved Martha, and her sister, and Lazarus" (John 11:5). This is one of the rare direct statements of personal affection in the entire Gospel record.

Lazarus fell sick while Jesus was beyond the Jordan, and his sisters sent word: "Lord, behold, he whom thou lovest is sick" (John 11:3). Jesus' response — to delay two more days before departing — was not indifference but divine intention: "This sickness is not unto death, but for the glory of God, that the Son of God might be glorified thereby" (John 11:4). By the time Jesus arrived in [[Bethany]], Lazarus had been dead four days and lay entombed. The four-day detail is theologically precise: Jewish belief of the period held that the spirit lingered near the body for three days; by the fourth day, decomposition was certain and all hope extinguished.

The raising of Lazarus (John 11:1–44) is the seventh and climactic sign in John's Gospel. Jesus wept at the tomb — "Jesus wept" (John 11:35), the shortest verse in Scripture — before commanding the stone removed and calling in a loud voice: "Lazarus, come forth" (John 11:43). Lazarus emerged still bound in graveclothes, and Jesus gave the command: "Loose him, and let him go" (John 11:44). The miracle was witnessed by a large crowd and immediately reported to the Pharisees. It precipitated the final decision of the chief priests and Pharisees to put Jesus to death: "Then from that day forth they took counsel together for to put him to death" (John 11:53).

Lazarus reappears in John 12:1–11 at a supper given in Jesus' honour in [[Bethany]] six days before the Passover. He sat at table with Jesus while [[Martha]] served and [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] anointed Jesus' feet with costly ointment. His very presence was powerful testimony: "much people of the Jews therefore knew that he was there: and they came not for Jesus' sake only, but that they might see Lazarus also, whom he had raised from the dead" (John 12:9). This public witness was so threatening that "the chief priests consulted that they might put Lazarus also to death; Because that by reason of him many of the Jews went away, and believed on Jesus" (John 12:10–11). Lazarus became, in death and in resurrection, both the occasion for the greatest sign and a target of the same hatred directed at Jesus himself.

## Notes
- Lazarus speaks no recorded word in any Gospel; his significance is entirely in what is done to him and the effects that follow
- The raising of Lazarus is John's seventh and greatest sign; it functions as a public rehearsal of the resurrection of Jesus — sealed tomb, word of command, emergence from graveclothes
- The four-day interval was not incidental: it refuted any claim that Lazarus was merely in a coma or trance, ensuring the miracle could not be explained away
- Lazarus is the only named individual in the Gospels whom Jesus raised after a burial of multiple days (as opposed to the daughter of Jairus, raised immediately, and the son of the widow of Nain, raised on the way to burial)
- His continued existence after being raised made him a living threat to the religious establishment — the chief priests wanted to suppress the evidence by killing him again (John 12:10–11)
- No tradition records what became of Lazarus after John 12; later church tradition places him variously as bishop of Kition in Cyprus, but this is extra-biblical
