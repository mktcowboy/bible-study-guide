# Zerubbabel

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Governor of Judah, Davidic heir, and leader of the first return from Babylonian exile; oversaw the laying of the temple foundation
- **Family:** Son of Shealtiel; grandson of King Jehoiachin; descendant of David
- **Key Books:** [[Ezra]], [[Haggai]], [[Zechariah]]

## Biography
Zerubbabel was a Davidic prince who served as the civil governor of Judah during the first return from Babylonian exile, authorized by the decree of [[Cyrus]] the Persian king in 538 BC. His name means "seed of Babylon" or "shoot of Babylon," reflecting his birth in exile. Despite this name, he became the primary figure in the physical and symbolic restoration of Israel's national and religious life.

He returned from Babylon to Judah at the head of approximately 49,697 exiles, accompanied by the high priest [[Jeshua]] (Joshua son of Jehozadak). Together, they formed the dual leadership of the restored community — civil and priestly authority working in tandem, a pattern that would persist throughout the post-exilic period. Their first act upon arriving was to rebuild the altar of burnt offering and restore the Feast of Tabernacles before even beginning work on the temple itself (Ezra 3:1–6).

The laying of the temple foundation in approximately 536 BC was a moment of both celebration and mourning. The elders who had seen Solomon's temple wept at the comparison, while the younger generation shouted for joy — and the two sounds mingled so loudly that they could not be distinguished from afar (Ezra 3:12–13). Opposition from the peoples of the land quickly halted the work, and construction lay dormant for approximately fifteen years until the prophets [[Haggai]] and [[Zechariah]] stirred the community back to action in 520 BC.

The prophetic support Zerubbabel received from [[Haggai]] and [[Zechariah]] is extraordinary. Haggai 2:23 presents him as a "signet ring" — the reversal of the curse placed on his grandfather Jehoiachin (Jeremiah 22:24), signaling Davidic restoration. Zechariah 4 presents the famous vision of the lampstand flanked by two olive trees, interpreted as Zerubbabel and [[Jeshua]], with the divine word: "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit, says the LORD of hosts" (Zechariah 4:6). Zerubbabel completed the temple in 516 BC, the sixth year of King Darius (Ezra 6:15).

## Key Events
- Returns from Babylon leading the first wave of exiles under Cyrus's decree (Ezra 1–2)
- Rebuilds the altar and restores sacrificial worship before temple work begins (Ezra 3:1–6)
- Lays the foundation of the second temple (Ezra 3:8–13)
- Construction halted by local opposition for approximately 15 years (Ezra 4)
- Prophets [[Haggai]] and [[Zechariah]] renew the work (Ezra 5:1–2; Haggai 1)
- Designated as God's "signet ring," reversing Jehoiachin's curse (Haggai 2:23)
- Completes the temple in 516 BC (Ezra 6:15)

## Notes
- Zerubbabel appears in both Matthew's and Luke's genealogies of Jesus, placing him in the direct line of the Messiah (Matthew 1:12; Luke 3:27)
- The designation "signet ring" in Haggai 2:23 reverses the curse on Jehoiachin (Jeremiah 22:24), signaling renewed Davidic hope after exile
- Zerubbabel and [[Jeshua]] together pattern the dual anointed ones of Zechariah 4 — one royal, one priestly — which has rich messianic typology
