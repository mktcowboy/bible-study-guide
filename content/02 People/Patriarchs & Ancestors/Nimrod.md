# Nimrod

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Mighty hunter and founder of the first empire, including Babel and Nineveh
- **Family:** Son of Cush; grandson of Ham; great-grandson of [[Noah]]
- **Key Books:** [[Genesis]]

## Biography
Nimrod is introduced in the Table of Nations (Genesis 10) as the son of Cush and great-grandson of [[Noah]] through Ham. He is described as "the first on earth to be a mighty man" and "a mighty hunter before the LORD," a phrase that became a proverb — "like Nimrod, a mighty hunter before the LORD" (Genesis 10:9). The name Nimrod may derive from a root meaning "to rebel," though this etymology is disputed among scholars.

What makes Nimrod distinctive is that he is the first figure in Scripture explicitly credited with founding a kingdom. His dominion began in the land of Shinar — the fertile plain of Mesopotamia — where he built Babel, Erech, Accad, and Calneh. This is the same Babel associated with the Tower of Babel incident in Genesis 11, suggesting that Nimrod may have been the cultural force behind that infamous human project, though the text does not make the connection explicit.

Nimrod then extended his kingdom northward into Assyria, where he built Nineveh, Rehoboth-Ir, Calah, and Resen. Nineveh would later become the great Assyrian capital and the city [[Jonah]] was sent to preach in. The founding of Nineveh by Nimrod thus links the very origins of Israel's most feared enemy to the post-flood generation's earliest act of empire-building.

Nimrod represents a theological archetype in Scripture — the powerful human king who builds an empire and seeks renown, in contrast to those who walk humbly with God. The phrase "mighty hunter before the LORD" may carry an ambiguous tone, possibly suggesting that his hunting was conducted in defiance of, rather than in service to, God. Later Jewish and Christian tradition expanded his role considerably, often casting him as the instigator of the Tower of Babel and a protoantagonist of [[Abraham]].

## Key Events
- Named "mighty man" and "mighty hunter before the LORD" (Genesis 10:8–9)
- Founds his kingdom in Babel, Erech, Accad, and Calneh in Shinar (Genesis 10:10)
- Extends empire into Assyria; builds Nineveh, Rehoboth-Ir, Calah, and Resen (Genesis 10:11–12)

## Notes
- Nimrod is the first person in the Bible described as founding a political empire, making him the prototype of human imperial ambition
- His association with both Babel and Nineveh links him to two of the most significant antagonists of Israel's history: the Babylonians and the Assyrians
- Micah 5:6 refers to Assyria as "the land of Nimrod," showing his name remained associated with that empire for centuries
