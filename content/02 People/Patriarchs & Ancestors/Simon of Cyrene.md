# Simon of Cyrene

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** A man from Cyrene seized by Roman soldiers and compelled to carry the cross of [[Jesus Christ]] on the road to Golgotha
- **Family:** Father of Alexander and Rufus (Mark 15:21); nothing further is recorded of his wife or broader household
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]]

## Biography

Simon of Cyrene appears at a single moment in the passion narrative, yet that moment is recorded by three of the four evangelists and preserved with unusual specificity. As the condemned [[Jesus Christ]] was led out from the city toward the place of execution, the Roman soldiers requisitioned Simon for the cross. Matthew records simply: "And as they came out, they found a man of Cyrene, Simon by name: him they compelled to bear his cross" (Matthew 27:32). Luke adds that the soldiers "laid hold upon one Simon, a Cyrenian, coming out of the country, and on him they laid the cross, that he might bear it after Jesus" (Luke 23:26). The Greek verb used in Matthew — *aggareuō* — is the same verb Jesus had used in the Sermon on the Mount when he spoke of being compelled to walk a mile with a soldier (Matthew 5:41). It denotes the Roman legal right to conscript a civilian for temporary service; Simon had no choice in the matter.

Cyrene was a major city on the North African coast, in the region known today as eastern Libya. It had a substantial Jewish diaspora community — large enough that Cyrenian Jews had their own synagogue in Jerusalem (Acts 6:9), and among those scattered by the persecution of Stephen were "men of Cyprus and Cyrene" who took the gospel to Antioch (Acts 11:20). Simon may have been a diaspora Jew visiting Jerusalem for the [[Passover]], as the pilgrim feasts drew Jews from across the Roman world. That he was "coming out of the country" (Luke 23:26) — that is, coming in from the fields or the countryside — suggests he was encountered by chance on the road.

The detail that distinguishes Mark's account from the other two is its identification of Simon's sons: "And they compel one Simon a Cyrenian, who passed by, coming out of the country, the father of Alexander and Rufus, to bear his cross" (Mark 15:21). Mark alone includes this parenthetical identification, and he offers no introduction to these men — he writes as though his readers would know who Alexander and Rufus are. This strongly suggests that by the time Mark's Gospel was written, Alexander and Rufus were known figures in the early church, recognizable to Mark's audience. Paul's letter to the Romans, likely addressed to the church in Rome (where Mark's Gospel is traditionally associated), greets "Rufus chosen in the Lord, and his mother and mine" (Romans 16:13) — a Rufus whose mother had shown Paul such maternal care that he calls her his own. Whether this is the same Rufus cannot be proven, but the convergence of detail — Cyrene, Rome, a family known to the church — has long led scholars to regard the identification as plausible.

Simon himself leaves no further mark in the canonical record. He is a man swept into the greatest event in history by Roman coercion, shouldering the instrument of the world's redemption for a few hundred yards up a hill. Whether the experience transformed him — whether he became a follower of [[Jesus Christ]], whether his sons' apparent prominence in the church traces back to that road — the text does not say. He is a figure of history's margins who, for one moment, bore the cross of the Son of God.

## Notes
- The Greek *aggareuō* (Matthew 27:32) — compelled, conscripted — reflects the Roman *angaria*, the legal right to press civilians into temporary service; it is the same word Jesus used in Matthew 5:41 ("compel thee to go a mile"), giving his hearers a concrete frame of reference
- Cyrene (modern Shahhat, Libya) was the capital of the Roman province of Cyrenaica; its Jewish population was large enough to support its own Jerusalem synagogue (Acts 6:9) and to produce early evangelists (Acts 11:20)
- Mark's identification of Simon as "the father of Alexander and Rufus" — with no further explanation — is the clearest internal evidence in the Gospels that a named minor figure's family had become known and significant to the early church community
- The image of Simon bearing the cross "after Jesus" (Luke 23:26, *opisthen*) echoes Jesus's own words about discipleship: "If any man will come after me, let him deny himself, and take up his cross daily, and follow me" (Luke 9:23); whether Luke intends this echo is debated, but the verbal and visual resonance is striking
- John's Gospel (John 19:17) states that Jesus "bearing his cross went forth" — John omits Simon entirely, perhaps emphasizing Jesus's sovereign self-offering rather than his physical exhaustion
