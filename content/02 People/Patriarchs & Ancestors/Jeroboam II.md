# Jeroboam II

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Long-reigning king of northern Israel; contemporary of the prophet Amos; Israel prospered materially but spiritual corruption deepened
- **Family:** Son of Joash (Jehoash); great-grandson of [[Jehu]]; from the dynasty of Jehu
- **Key Books:** [[2 Kings]], [[Amos]]

## Biography
Jeroboam II was the fourth king of the dynasty of Jehu and the most successful monarch of the northern kingdom of Israel by any political or economic measure. His 41-year reign (approximately 793–753 BC) was the longest of any king in the northern kingdom and coincided with a period of remarkable territorial expansion and material prosperity. He is not to be confused with [[Jeroboam]] I, the first king of the divided northern kingdom who "made Israel to sin" with the golden calves at Bethel and Dan.

Despite his political accomplishments, 2 Kings 14 frames his reign with the standard negative evaluation given to northern kings: "He did what was evil in the sight of the LORD. He did not depart from all the sins of Jeroboam the son of Nebat, which he made Israel to sin" (2 Kings 14:24). Yet immediately after this condemnation, the text reports that God used Jeroboam II to restore Israel's borders "from Lebo-hamath as far as the Sea of the Arabah" — fulfilling a prophecy given through [[Jonah (Prophet)|Jonah]] the son of Amittai. This paradox — an evil king used by God for Israel's temporal benefit — illustrates the complexity of divine providence.

The fuller picture of Jeroboam II's era comes from the prophet [[Amos (Prophet)|Amos]], who ministered during his reign. Amos paints a portrait of a society drunk on affluence: the wealthy lounged on beds of ivory, ate choice lambs, drank wine by the bowlful, and anointed themselves with the finest oils while being "not grieved over the ruin of Joseph" (Amos 6:4–6). The religious institutions were active but corrupted — worship at Bethel was elaborate but syncretistic and self-serving. Social injustice was rampant, with the poor being sold for a pair of sandals and the needy being trampled (Amos 2:6–7).

The death of Jeroboam II in 753 BC inaugurated a period of rapid collapse for the northern kingdom. In the 25 years following his death, Israel had six different kings, four of whom were assassinated. The Assyrian empire under Tiglath-pileser III began pressing into Israelite territory. Samaria fell to Assyria in 722 BC, just three decades after Jeroboam II's death. The prophet [[Hosea (Prophet)|Hosea]] also ministered during the end of this era, sounding warnings that the prosperity was built on spiritual sand.

## Key Events
- Becomes king of Israel as son of Joash (2 Kings 14:23)
- Restores Israel's borders from Lebo-hamath to the Sea of the Arabah, fulfilling prophecy (2 Kings 14:25)
- Reign characterized by material prosperity but deep social and spiritual corruption (Amos 6)
- [[Amos]] preaches against Israel during his reign, warned to leave Bethel by Amaziah the priest (Amos 7:10–17)
- [[Jonah]] the son of Amittai prophesies the territorial restoration (2 Kings 14:25)
- Dies and is buried in Samaria (2 Kings 14:28–29)

## Notes
- Jeroboam II illustrates that material prosperity is no indicator of divine favor — his era of maximum economic strength coincided with minimum spiritual faithfulness
- God's use of an evil king to fulfill prophecy and restore Israel's borders demonstrates that divine purposes operate through human history regardless of the moral character of instruments
- [[Amos (Prophet)|Amos]]'s ministry during this reign provides the most detailed picture of northern Israelite society at its peak, making Jeroboam II's era crucial for understanding the prophetic movement
