# Michael

## Overview
- **Testament:** Both
- **Role:** Archangel who contends for Israel in Daniel's visions; battles the dragon in Revelation
- **Family:** Not applicable; an angel
- **Key Books:** [[Daniel]], [[Jude]], [[Revelation]]

## Biography
Michael is one of only two angels named in the Protestant biblical canon (the other being [[Gabriel]]), and he is the only figure explicitly called an "archangel" (archangelos) in the New Testament (Jude 9). His name means "Who is like God?" — a rhetorical question affirming divine incomparability. He appears in four distinct contexts across the Old and New Testaments, and in each he is associated with conflict, contention, and the defense of God's people against spiritual opposition.

In Daniel 10, a heavenly messenger tells [[Daniel]] that "the prince of the kingdom of Persia" withstood him for 21 days until "Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me" (Daniel 10:13). This passage provides one of the most detailed glimpses in the Old Testament of an unseen spiritual warfare running parallel to earthly political events. Daniel 10:21 identifies Michael as "your prince" — the heavenly guardian specifically assigned to Israel. Daniel 12:1 elaborates: "At that time shall arise Michael, the great prince who has charge of your people. And there shall be a time of trouble, such as never has been since there was a nation till that time. But at that time your people shall be delivered."

In the New Testament, Jude 9 references a tradition about Michael contending with the devil over the body of Moses: "when contending with the devil he disputed about the body of Moses, he did not presume to pronounce a blasphemous judgment, but said, 'The Lord rebuke you.'" This detail, not recorded elsewhere in the Old Testament, comes from an extra-canonical tradition (possibly the Assumption of Moses), and its inclusion in Jude lends it apostolic authority. The restraint Michael shows — appealing to God's authority rather than asserting his own against the devil — is cited as a model of proper spiritual warfare.

Revelation 12:7–9 portrays Michael as the commander of the heavenly army that wars against the dragon: "Now war arose in heaven, Michael and his angels fighting against the dragon. And the dragon and his angels fought back, but he was defeated, and there was no longer any place for them in heaven." This apocalyptic vision of the expulsion of Satan connects Michael's cosmic warfare to the salvation accomplished through Christ's death and resurrection (Revelation 12:11).

## Key Events
- Comes to the aid of the heavenly messenger fighting the "prince of Persia" (Daniel 10:13–14)
- Identified as Israel's "great prince" and guardian (Daniel 10:21; 12:1)
- Predicted to arise in the great tribulation to deliver Israel (Daniel 12:1)
- Disputes with the devil over the body of Moses (Jude 9)
- Commands heavenly armies that defeat the dragon (Revelation 12:7–9)

## Notes
- Michael's restraint before the devil in Jude 9 — refusing to pronounce judgment and instead appealing to God — is interpreted in the context of false teachers who show no such restraint, serving as a sharp contrast
- The association of Michael with Israel's national protection (Daniel 10–12) and with the great end-time tribulation (Daniel 12:1) places him at the intersection of angelic and historical theology
- His title "archangel" (Jude 9) and his role as commander of heavenly armies (Revelation 12:7) suggest a position of supreme authority among created angelic beings
