# Philip (Apostle)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles; early Galilean disciple; witness who brought [[Nathanael]] to Jesus
- **Family:** Not specified
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Philip the apostle was from Bethsaida, the same city associated with [[Andrew]] and [[Peter]]. In John 1, [[Jesus Christ]] directly called Philip with the words, "Follow me." Philip then found [[Nathanael]] and announced that they had found the one Moses and the prophets wrote about: Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph. When Nathanael questioned whether anything good could come from Nazareth, Philip replied with the simple invitation, "Come and see."

Philip appears several times in John as a practical, questioning disciple. Before the [[Feeding of the Five Thousand]], Jesus tested Philip by asking where bread could be bought for the multitude. Philip calculated the impossibility: two hundred pennyworth of bread would not be enough. His answer shows realistic concern, but also limited imagination before Jesus' abundance.

Later, when Greeks came seeking Jesus, they approached Philip, perhaps because of his Greek name or because he was from a region with Gentile contact. Philip told [[Andrew]], and together they brought the request to Jesus. The scene hints at the widening horizon of Jesus' mission: outsiders seeking access to him as the hour of the cross approaches.

At the [[Last Supper]], Philip asked Jesus, "Lord, shew us the Father, and it sufficeth us." Jesus answered that whoever had seen him had seen the Father. Philip's question therefore became the occasion for one of the clearest statements of Jesus' unity with the Father.

Philip the apostle must be distinguished from [[Philip (Evangelist)]], who appears in Acts 6 and 8 as one of the seven and as the evangelist to Samaria and the Ethiopian eunuch.

## Notes
- Philip often appears at the boundary between seeking and seeing: Nathanael is invited to see Jesus, the hungry crowd reveals need, Greeks seek Jesus, and Philip asks to see the Father.
- His questions and calculations are not mocked by the text; they become teaching moments through which Jesus reveals more of himself.
- Keep this note distinct from [[Philip (Evangelist)]], whose ministry belongs mainly to Acts.
