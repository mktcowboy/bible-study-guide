# James (son of Zebedee)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles; son of Zebedee; brother of [[John (Apostle)]]; member of Jesus's inner circle of three; first of the Twelve to be martyred
- **Family:** Son of Zebedee, a fisherman prosperous enough to have hired servants (Mark 1:20); brother of [[John (Apostle)]]; his mother is widely identified with Salome, possibly a sister of [[Mary (Mother of Jesus)]] (cf. Matthew 27:56; Mark 15:40; John 19:25)
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

James was a fisherman working with his father Zebedee and his brother [[John (Apostle)]] on the Sea of Galilee when [[Jesus Christ]] called him. The account in Mark is terse and electrifying: Jesus "called them: and they left their father Zebedee in the ship with the hired servants, and went after him" (Mark 1:20). The presence of hired servants indicates Zebedee's household was not poor — the brothers left material security for an unknown future. Together with [[Peter]] and [[Andrew]], James and [[John (Apostle)]] form the first four called, and James is consistently listed second or third in every apostolic list in the Gospels and Acts (Matthew 10:2; Mark 3:17; Luke 6:14; Acts 1:13).

[[Jesus Christ]] gave James and his brother [[John (Apostle)]] the surname Boanerges, meaning "the sons of thunder" (Mark 3:17). The nickname is fitting: both brothers exhibited a fiery temperament. When a Samaritan village refused to receive Jesus, James and John asked, "Lord, wilt thou that we command fire to come down from heaven, and consume them, even as Elias did?" (Luke 9:54). Jesus rebuked them. Their mother later approached Jesus requesting that her sons sit at his right and left hand in his kingdom (Matthew 20:20-21), a request Jesus met with the sobering question: "Are ye able to drink of the cup that I shall drink of, and to be baptized with the baptism that I am baptized with?" Both James and John answered "We are able" (Matthew 20:22) — a confidence whose meaning James would fully understand at his death.

James was one of the three disciples — with [[Peter]] and [[John (Apostle)]] — who formed Jesus's closest inner circle. These three alone were present at the [[Transfiguration]], where Jesus was transformed before them and Moses and Elijah appeared (Mark 9:2-8), and where a voice from the cloud declared, "This is my beloved Son: hear him" (Mark 9:7). The same three were taken to the inner garden at [[Gethsemane]] on the night of the betrayal and asked to watch and pray while Jesus agonized before the Father (Mark 14:33-34). They were also among the small group present at the raising of Jairus's daughter (Mark 5:37), the only resurrection miracle performed before a restricted audience during Jesus's ministry.

In Acts, James appears by name only once — at his death. Herod Agrippa I, seeking to please the Jewish leadership, "killed James the brother of John with the sword" (Acts 12:2). This is the first martyrdom of one of the Twelve recorded in the New Testament and likely occurred around A.D. 44. The Greek verb used (*anaireo*) denotes an official execution. That Herod saw this death as politically profitable — immediately arresting [[Peter]] next — indicates the early church's prominence in Jerusalem at that time. James had drunk the cup his Lord had drunk, as Jesus had foretold (Mark 10:39).

## Key Events
- [[Call of the First Disciples]] — called with [[John (Apostle)]] while mending nets; left Zebedee and followed Jesus (Mark 1:19-20)
- Named Boanerges, "sons of thunder," by [[Jesus Christ]] (Mark 3:17)
- [[Transfiguration]] — present with [[Peter]] and [[John (Apostle)]] on the mountain (Mark 9:2-8)
- Raising of Jairus's daughter — one of three witnesses (Mark 5:37)
- [[Gethsemane]] — asked by Jesus to watch and pray; fell asleep (Mark 14:33-37)
- Martyrdom by Herod Agrippa I — first of the Twelve to die for the faith; executed with the sword (Acts 12:1-2)

## Notes
- The epithet "the Greater" (James the Greater) distinguishes this James from [[James (Brother of Lord)]] — "Greater" likely refers to seniority or stature, not spiritual rank
- Jesus's prediction that James would drink his cup (Mark 10:39) was fulfilled literally; James was the first apostle to be martyred, John the last to die (of natural causes)
- The brevity of the martyrdom account in Acts 12:2 — a single clause — may reflect the speed with which the early church moved past grief into concern for [[Peter]]'s imprisonment, or the author Luke's theological restraint
- Zebedee's household prosperity (hired servants, Mark 1:20) and the possible kinship between Salome/James's mother and [[Mary (Mother of Jesus)]] may explain the bold request for places of honor in the kingdom (Matthew 20:20-21)
- No epistle or Gospel is attributed to James; his legacy is entirely in his witness and death — the first apostolic blood shed for the name of Christ
