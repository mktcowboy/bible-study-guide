# Thomas

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles; called Didymus, meaning "the Twin"; remembered for his honest questioning and ultimate confession of Christ's deity
- **Family:** Unknown; his twin is not named in Scripture
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Thomas appears in all four apostle lists in the Gospels and Acts, always among the Twelve. His Hebrew name Thomas corresponds to the Aramaic word for "twin," and [[John]] alone identifies him by the Greek equivalent: "Thomas, which is called Didymus" (John 11:16; 20:24; 21:2). Nothing is recorded of his background before his call, but he is consistently portrayed as a man of sincere, if sometimes uncertain, devotion to [[Jesus Christ]].

His most famous moment of courage comes in John 11, when [[Jesus Christ]] resolves to return to [[Judea]] despite mortal danger. While the other disciples hesitated, Thomas spoke with a kind of dark bravery: "Let us also go, that we may die with him" (John 11:16). This is not the voice of a doubter but of a man who loved Jesus enough to walk into what he believed was certain death. In the same way, at the [[Last Supper]], when [[Jesus Christ]] spoke of going to prepare a place and said "whither I go ye know, and the way ye know," Thomas honestly admitted what others perhaps felt but did not say: "Lord, we know not whither thou goest; and how can we know the way?" (John 14:5). This question drew from Jesus the great declaration, "I am the way, the truth, and the life: no man cometh unto the Father, but by me" (John 14:6). Thomas's honest uncertainty became the occasion for one of the clearest self-revelations Christ ever gave.

The episode for which Thomas is most remembered occurred after the [[Resurrection]]. When the risen [[Jesus Christ]] appeared to the gathered disciples, Thomas was absent. Told by the others that they had seen the Lord, Thomas replied with characteristic directness: "Except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails, and put my finger into the print of the nails, and thrust my hand into his side, I will not believe" (John 20:25). Eight days later [[Jesus Christ]] appeared again, this time with Thomas present. He invited Thomas to examine his wounds. Thomas's response was immediate and total — not the measured conclusion of a skeptic at last persuaded, but the prostrate confession of a worshipper: "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28). It is the most explicit declaration of Christ's deity spoken by any disciple in the Gospels. Jesus received it without correction: "Thomas, because thou hast seen me, thou hast believed: blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed" (John 20:29).

Thomas appears once more before the ascension, present among the seven disciples by the [[Sea of Tiberias]] (John 21:2) when the risen Lord appeared at the shore. He is listed among the eleven who gathered in Jerusalem after the ascension (Acts 1:13), awaiting the promised Spirit. Church tradition associates Thomas with missionary work as far as Persia and India, where ancient Christian communities trace their origin to him, though this lies outside the canonical record.

## Notes
- The label "doubting Thomas" is historically reductive; his confession in John 20:28 is the highest explicit declaration of Christ's deity in all four Gospels
- Thomas's absence from the first resurrection appearance is not explained; the text simply notes he "was not with them when Jesus came" (John 20:24)
- Jesus's words in John 20:29 implicitly bless subsequent generations of believers who believe without physical sight — a pastoral word that extends beyond Thomas to all readers
- The [[Upper Room]] appearances (John 20:19-26) are the setting for Thomas's encounter with the risen Christ
