# Peter

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles; spokesman and leader among the disciples; preacher at Pentecost; major witness in Acts
- **Family:** Brother of [[Andrew]]; son of Jonas/John; married
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]], [[Galatians]], [[1 Peter]], [[2 Peter]]

## Biography

Peter was a Galilean fisherman, originally named Simon. His brother [[Andrew]] brought him to [[Jesus Christ]], who gave him the name Cephas/Peter, meaning rock. The Synoptic Gospels later record Jesus calling Peter and Andrew by the Sea of Galilee to become fishers of men. Peter's home in Capernaum became a setting for Jesus' ministry, including the healing of Peter's mother-in-law.

Peter often acts as the spokesman for the Twelve. He confesses Jesus as the Christ at Caesarea Philippi, receives Jesus' teaching about the keys of the kingdom, and is also sharply rebuked when he resists the way of the cross. His discipleship is marked by boldness, insight, impulsiveness, fear, failure, and restoration.

With [[James (son of Zebedee)]] and [[John (Apostle)]], Peter belongs to Jesus' inner three. He witnesses the raising of Jairus's daughter, the [[Transfiguration]], and Jesus' agony in [[Gethsemane]]. He also walks on the water briefly before sinking, asks questions on behalf of the disciples, and makes confident promises of loyalty before the passion.

Peter's lowest moment comes during Jesus' trial, when he denies knowing Jesus three times. The rooster crows, and Peter weeps bitterly. After the [[Resurrection]], Jesus restores Peter by the Sea of Tiberias with the repeated command to feed his sheep. Peter's story therefore becomes one of the clearest biblical portraits of failure met by grace and recommissioning.

In Acts, Peter becomes the leading public witness in Jerusalem. He preaches at [[Pentecost and the Birth of the Church]], heals the lame man at the temple with John, confronts Ananias and Sapphira, suffers imprisonment, and announces the gospel to [[Cornelius]], opening the door of Gentile fellowship. Later, Paul confronts Peter at Antioch when Peter's conduct compromises table fellowship with Gentile believers. Peter's letters emphasize holiness, suffering, humility, shepherding, false teaching, and hope in Christ's return.

## Notes
- Peter's life is not a simple arc from weakness to strength; he continues to need correction even after Pentecost.
- Jesus does not erase Peter's failure but transforms it into shepherding responsibility.
- Peter is central for seeing continuity between Jesus' earthly ministry and the church's apostolic witness.
- The name Cephas is the Aramaic form used often by Paul; Peter is the Greek equivalent.
