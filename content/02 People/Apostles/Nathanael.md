# Nathanael

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve, commonly identified with Bartholomew; disciple from Cana in Galilee
- **Family:** Not specified
- **Key Books:** [[John]], [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Nathanael appears by name only in the Gospel of John, while Bartholomew appears in the Synoptic apostle lists and Acts. Because Bartholomew is paired with [[Philip (Apostle)]] in the Synoptic lists, and Nathanael is brought to Jesus by Philip in John, many interpreters identify Nathanael with Bartholomew. This note follows that traditional identification while recognizing that John never explicitly says "Bartholomew."

Nathanael was from Cana in Galilee. When [[Philip (Apostle)]] told him, "We have found him, of whom Moses in the law, and the prophets, did write," Nathanael responded skeptically: "Can there any good thing come out of Nazareth?" Philip did not argue; he simply said, "Come and see." Nathanael's skepticism was not hardened unbelief but honest doubt that could still be invited toward Christ.

When Jesus saw Nathanael, he called him "an Israelite indeed, in whom is no guile." Nathanael was astonished that Jesus knew him, and Jesus revealed that he had seen him under the fig tree before Philip called him. Nathanael responded with one of the earliest confessions in John's Gospel: "Rabbi, thou art the Son of God; thou art the King of Israel." Jesus then promised that Nathanael would see greater things, echoing Jacob's ladder with angels ascending and descending upon the Son of Man.

Nathanael appears again after the [[Resurrection]], among the seven disciples by the Sea of Tiberias when the risen Jesus appeared and restored [[Peter]]. If Nathanael is Bartholomew, he is also present in the apostle lists and in the upper room after the ascension.

## Notes
- "Bartholomew" may be a patronymic or family name, while "Nathanael" may be the personal name.
- Nathanael moves from local prejudice about Nazareth to a sweeping confession of Jesus' identity.
- Jesus' words about angels ascending and descending connect Nathanael's confession to the Jacob/Israel story and to Jesus as the true meeting place between heaven and earth.
