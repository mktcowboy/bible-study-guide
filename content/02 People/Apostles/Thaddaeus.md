# Thaddaeus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles
- **Family:** Identified in Luke and Acts as Judas son of James
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Thaddaeus is one of the Twelve. Matthew and Mark list him as Thaddaeus, while some textual traditions include the name Lebbaeus. Luke and Acts name the corresponding apostle as Judas son of James. John refers to "Judas, not Iscariot," who speaks during the [[Farewell Discourse]]. This note treats these names as referring to the same apostolic figure, the common harmonization in Christian interpretation.

Thaddaeus's main individual scene occurs in John 14. During the upper room discourse, Jesus says that the one who loves him will be loved by the Father and that Jesus will manifest himself to that person. Judas, not Iscariot, asks why Jesus will manifest himself to the disciples and not to the world. The question reveals an expectation that Messiah's revelation should be public and visible. Jesus answers by focusing on love, obedience, the Father's dwelling with believers, and the coming work of the Holy Spirit.

Thaddaeus therefore functions as a useful voice in the Farewell Discourse. His question draws out Jesus' teaching that the kingdom's revelation will not first come as worldly spectacle, but through love, obedience, indwelling presence, and Spirit-taught remembrance.

Like several of the Twelve, Thaddaeus receives little narrative attention outside the lists. Yet he is named among those appointed by Jesus, sent to proclaim the kingdom, gathered after the resurrection, and included in the apostolic witness before Pentecost.

## Notes
- The name "Judas" was common; John carefully distinguishes this apostle from [[Judas Iscariot]].
- His question in John 14 helps clarify the hidden, relational, Spirit-mediated nature of Jesus' post-resurrection presence with his people.
- Thaddaeus is one of the best examples of why aliases matter in this vault: the same apostle appears under different names across the Gospel lists.
