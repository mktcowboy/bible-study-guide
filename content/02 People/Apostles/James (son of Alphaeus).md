# James (son of Alphaeus)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles
- **Family:** Son of Alphaeus
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

James son of Alphaeus is named in every list of the Twelve, but the New Testament gives no separate narrative episode focused on him. His importance therefore rests in his appointment by [[Jesus Christ]], his inclusion in the apostolic circle, and his witness alongside the other apostles after the resurrection.

The name James was common in the first century, and the New Testament contains several men by that name. This James must be distinguished from [[James (son of Zebedee)]], the brother of John and member of the inner three, and from [[James (Brother of Lord)]], the leader associated with the Jerusalem church and the epistle of James. Mark 15:40 mentions "James the less," who may be James son of Alphaeus, though the identification is not certain.

James son of Alphaeus appears in the apostle lists in the second half of the Twelve. That placement does not imply lesser faithfulness. The Gospels often give more narrative attention to Peter, James son of Zebedee, John, Thomas, Philip, and Judas Iscariot, but the quieter apostles still form part of the foundational witness of the church.

After the ascension, James son of Alphaeus is listed among those gathered in the upper room in Jerusalem. He therefore belongs to the apostolic company that waited in prayer for the promised Spirit before [[Pentecost and the Birth of the Church]].

## Notes
- The lack of individual narrative does not make James unimportant; the apostolic office itself is his main biblical significance.
- "James the Less" is a traditional identification, but the biblical text does not explicitly equate that title with James son of Alphaeus.
- His note is useful for distinguishing the several New Testament men named James.
