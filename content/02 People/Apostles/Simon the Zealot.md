# Simon the Zealot

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles
- **Family:** Not specified
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Simon the Zealot is named in all four New Testament lists of the apostles but receives no separate narrative scene. Matthew and Mark call him "the Cananaean" or "Cananite," while Luke and Acts call him "Zelotes," meaning the Zealot. These labels likely point to zeal, possibly connected to nationalist or religious fervor, though the text does not give his backstory.

Simon's title distinguishes him from [[Peter]], who is also named Simon. In a group that also included [[Matthew (Apostle)]], a former tax collector associated with Roman taxation, Simon's presence is striking. If Simon's title suggests anti-Roman zeal, then Jesus gathered into the same apostolic circle men whose former social and political worlds would naturally have clashed.

The Gospels do not record Simon speaking, questioning, failing, or leading in any individual scene. Yet his inclusion in the Twelve matters. He was appointed by [[Jesus Christ]], sent to proclaim the kingdom, present among the apostles after the resurrection, and part of the foundational witness that waited for the Spirit in Jerusalem.

Simon is therefore a useful reminder that not every faithful witness receives a long narrative. Scripture can honor a person by naming him among those chosen and sent, even when it does not preserve a personal episode.

## Notes
- "Zealot" may refer to personal zeal rather than formal membership in the later Zealot party; the text does not settle the question.
- Simon's pairing with Matthew in the Twelve illustrates how Jesus forms a new community that cuts across former loyalties.
- Sparse biblical data should be respected: Simon's main significance is his apostolic calling and witness.
