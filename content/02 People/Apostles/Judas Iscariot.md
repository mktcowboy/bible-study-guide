# Judas Iscariot

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** One of the Twelve Apostles; keeper of the moneybag; betrayer of [[Jesus Christ]]
- **Family:** Son of Simon Iscariot
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]]

## Biography

Judas Iscariot was chosen by [[Jesus Christ]] as one of the Twelve, which makes his betrayal one of the most sobering stories in Scripture. He heard Jesus' teaching, saw his miracles, traveled with the apostolic company, and shared in the outward privileges of discipleship. Yet the Gospels present him as a man whose proximity to holy things did not produce faithfulness.

John gives the clearest window into Judas's character before the betrayal. Judas kept the disciples' moneybag, but John says he was a thief and took what was put into it. At the anointing at Bethany, Judas objected that the ointment should have been sold and given to the poor, but John interprets the objection as a cover for greed. His betrayal was not a sudden isolated failure; it was the final exposure of a heart already bent away from Christ.

The Synoptic Gospels record that Judas went to the chief priests and agreed to betray Jesus for thirty pieces of silver. At the [[Last Supper]], Jesus identified the betrayer while still sharing table fellowship with him. Judas then went out into the night. In [[Gethsemane]], he led the arresting party and identified Jesus with a kiss, turning a sign of affection into an act of treachery.

After Jesus was condemned, Judas felt remorse and returned the silver, confessing that he had betrayed innocent blood. The chief priests refused responsibility. Judas went away and hanged himself, and the money was used to buy the potter's field, remembered as a field of blood. Acts 1 presents his place among the Twelve as vacated and replaced by Matthias.

Judas's story is a warning about hidden sin, religious privilege without repentance, greed disguised as concern, and the terrible possibility of being near Jesus outwardly while rejecting him inwardly.

## Relationships
- [[Jesus Christ]] - Teacher and Lord whom Judas betrayed
- [[The Twelve]] - Apostolic circle from which Judas fell
- [[Peter]] - Fellow apostle whose failure ended in restoration, unlike Judas's despair
- Chief priests - Religious leaders who used Judas and then rejected his remorse
- [[Thaddaeus]] - Distinguished from Judas Iscariot in John 14

## Notes
- Judas remains one of the Twelve historically, but his note also carries the `villains` tag because his defining narrative role is betrayal.
- Judas's remorse is not the same as repentance; the Gospels contrast his despair with Peter's bitter weeping and later restoration.
- The thirty pieces of silver connect Judas's betrayal with prophetic patterns of rejected shepherding and the price of contempt.
- Judas is a warning that access to spiritual community and ministry does not replace a faithful heart.
