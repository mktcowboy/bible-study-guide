# Sosthenes

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Leader of the Corinthian synagogue who was beaten before Gallio's tribunal; later Paul's co-author of 1 Corinthians
- **Family:** No family details given; likely a Hellenistic Jew of Corinth
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]], [[1 Corinthians]]

## Biography
Sosthenes appears in two distinct New Testament contexts separated by enough time that a remarkable personal transformation likely occurred between them. In Acts 18:17, he is "the ruler of the synagogue" — the same position previously held by Crispus (Acts 18:8), who had converted to faith in Christ. When the Jewish community in Corinth brought Paul before the proconsul Gallio on charges of unlawful religious teaching, Gallio dismissed the case as an internal Jewish matter. In the resulting confusion, the Corinthian crowd turned on Sosthenes and beat him before the judgment seat, while Gallio paid no attention. The text does not specify whether it was Greeks or the Jewish crowd who attacked him, nor does it indicate whether he was seen as having failed to prosecute Paul effectively.

Then, in 1 Corinthians 1:1, Paul identifies his co-author: "Paul, called by the will of God to be an apostle of Christ Jesus, and our brother Sosthenes." If this is the same Sosthenes — and there is no strong reason to assume otherwise — then a synagogue leader who had presided over Jewish opposition to Paul became a fellow worker closely enough associated with Paul to co-author a letter. His beating before Gallio may have been a turning point that opened him to the gospel, though the text does not make this explicit.

Whether or not his conversion was triggered by his suffering at Corinth, his inclusion as co-author of 1 Corinthians is significant. Paul rarely names co-authors (aside from consistent mentions of [[Timothy]] and [[Silas]]). That Sosthenes is named suggests he was well-known to the Corinthian church — they would have recognized the name of their former synagogue ruler now identified as a "brother" — which gave his co-authorship a pointed resonance.

## Key Events
- Serves as ruler of the Corinthian synagogue (Acts 18:17)
- Beaten before Gallio's judgment seat after Paul's case is dismissed (Acts 18:17)
- Named as Paul's "brother" and co-author of 1 Corinthians (1 Corinthians 1:1)

## Relationships
- Paul - co-author of 1 Corinthians; apparent conversion and ministry connection
- Crispus - predecessor as synagogue ruler, who had converted (Acts 18:8)
- Gallio - proconsul before whom the beating occurred
- The Corinthian church - his former congregation who would recognize his name

## Notes
- The identification of the beaten synagogue ruler with Paul's co-author, if correct, represents one of the most striking personal transformations in Acts — an opponent becoming a partner
- Gallio's indifference to the beating of a synagogue leader in his own court reflects the casual Roman attitude toward internal Jewish conflicts, which the early church learned to exploit as a legal buffer
- Sosthenes's presence in 1 Corinthians 1:1 would have carried immediate recognition value for the Corinthian recipients — they knew exactly who this man was and what his conversion meant
