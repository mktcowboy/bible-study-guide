# Paul (Apostle)

## Summary
Paul, formerly Saul of Tarsus, was a Pharisee, persecutor of the early church, and then the foremost missionary theologian of the New Testament. After encountering the risen Christ on the road to [[Damascus]], he was converted, baptized, and commissioned as an apostle to the Gentiles. His ministry carried the gospel across major centers of the Roman world through preaching, church planting, discipleship, suffering, and letter writing.

Paul's epistles explain justification by faith, union with Christ, the place of the law, the nature of the church, the hope of resurrection, and the mission of God among the nations. He repeatedly presents himself as weak in himself but sustained by grace. His life displays both bold doctrinal clarity and deep pastoral affection.

## Notes
- Paul is crucial for tracing how the gospel moved from Jerusalem into the Gentile world without becoming detached from Israel's Scriptures.
- His letters are occasional documents written to real churches, yet together they provide the church's clearest sustained explanation of the meaning of Christ's death and resurrection.
- He combines missionary urgency with local church concern: doctrine, worship, holiness, leadership, generosity, and perseverance all appear together in his ministry.
- Paul often interprets suffering as part of apostolic calling rather than as evidence of failure. That perspective shapes his tone in letters like [[2 Corinthians]] and [[Philippians]].
