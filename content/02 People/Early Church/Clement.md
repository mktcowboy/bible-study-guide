# Clement

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Co-worker of Paul at Philippi whose name is in the book of life
- **Family:** No family details given; a member of the Philippian church
- **Key Books:** [[Philippians]]

## Biography
Clement is mentioned only once in the New Testament, in Philippians 4:3, where Paul writes: "Yes, I ask you also, true companion, help these women, who have labored side by side with me in the gospel together with Clement and the rest of my fellow workers, whose names are in the book of life."

This brief mention is remarkable for what it says. Paul groups Clement with [[Euodia]] and [[Syntyche]] — two women involved in a personal conflict — as people who had genuinely "labored side by side" with him in the gospel. The Greek word (synathleō) used here means to contend alongside, to struggle together — the same athletic metaphor Paul uses elsewhere for strenuous missionary work. Clement was not a peripheral figure in the Philippian church; he was among the core laborers of Paul's Philippian mission.

Paul's affirmation that Clement's name is "in the book of life" echoes apocalyptic language (Exodus 32:32; Daniel 12:1; Revelation 3:5; 20:12–15) affirming the eternal security and recognition of faithful servants. This is not merely a biographical note — it is a theological statement of belonging and divine recognition.

Early church tradition identified this Clement with Clement of Rome, the author of 1 Clement (c. AD 96) and the third or fourth bishop of Rome. This identification was known to Origen and others in the early church, but it is uncertain. The chronology is possible but not proven, and "Clement" was a common Latin name in the Roman world. If the identification is correct, this brief mention in Philippians preserves the only New Testament reference to one of the most important figures of the sub-apostolic generation.

## Notes
- The affirmation that Clement's name is "in the book of life" is one of only a few places in the New Testament where an individual's eternal standing is specifically commended by an apostle
- His Latin name was extremely common, making the identification with Clement of Rome plausible but not certain; if correct, it provides a fascinating biographical thread from Paul's inner circle to the post-apostolic church leadership
- Being grouped with women co-laborers (Euodia and Syntyche) in Paul's list reinforces that Paul's missionary team at Philippi was genuinely mixed in gender, status, and background
