# Fortunatus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Member of Corinthian delegation who visited Paul in Ephesus with Stephanas and Achaicus
- **Family:** Member of the Corinthian church; no other details given
- **Key Books:** [[1 Corinthians]]

## Biography
Fortunatus is mentioned only once in the New Testament, in 1 Corinthians 16:17, where Paul writes: "I rejoice at the coming of Stephanas and Fortunatus and Achaicus, because they have made up for your absence, for they refreshed my spirit as well as yours. Give recognition to such men."

His Latin name — Fortunatus means "fortunate" or "blessed" — was common in the Roman world, suggesting he was either a Roman Gentile, a freed slave, or a Hellenistic Jew who had taken a Latin name for social purposes. The name's common use among freedmen has led some scholars to suggest he was a former slave, perhaps part of a wealthier household in Corinth. The social composition of the Corinthian church included both wealthy householders and those from lower social orders (1 Corinthians 1:26–29), and Fortunatus's name fits within that broad social range.

He traveled with [[Stephanas]] and [[Achaicus]] from Corinth to Ephesus to visit Paul — a journey of several days. Paul describes their visit as refreshing both his own spirit and that of the Corinthians, implying that their coming brought news, encouragement, and personal communion that bridged the distance between Paul and the troubled congregation. The group may have been the bearers of the letter from the Corinthian church to which much of 1 Corinthians is a reply (1 Corinthians 7:1).

Though Fortunatus is given no individual narrative, Paul's instruction to "give recognition to such men" places him alongside [[Stephanas]] and [[Achaicus]] as worthy of the church's honor — people who served quietly but whose contribution mattered to the apostle.

## Notes
- Fortunatus represents the unnamed majority of early believers whose service was real and valued but whose individual stories Scripture records only briefly
- The Latin name suggests possible Gentile or freedman background, reflecting the social diversity of the Corinthian church
- His inclusion in Paul's warmly worded greeting — alongside the instruction to honor him — ensures that his small act of visiting Paul is permanently recorded as worthy of recognition
