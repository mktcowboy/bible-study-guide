# Priscilla and Aquila

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Tentmakers; co-workers of Paul; teachers of Apollos; house church hosts
- **Book(s):** [[Acts]], [[Romans]], [[1 Corinthians]], [[2 Timothy]]

## Profile
Priscilla and Aquila are the premier married couple in the apostolic mission, mentioned six times in the New Testament and always together — Paul never names one without the other. They are a single ministry unit. Aquila was a Jew from Pontus (on the Black Sea coast of modern Turkey); Priscilla is the more frequently named of the two, and in four of the six references her name comes first — an unusual ordering in the ancient world that many interpreters take as a marker of her greater prominence in ministry. They were tentmakers by trade, and their craft gave them a cross-cultural, economically independent platform for ministry wherever they settled.

Paul first met them at [[Corinth]] during the [[Paul's Second Missionary Journey]]. They had recently arrived from Rome, having been expelled along with the broader Jewish community by the Emperor Claudius's edict (around AD 49), an expulsion known from the Roman historian Suetonius as connected to disputes in the Jewish community over "Chrestus" — very likely conflicts about Jesus. Paul "because he was of the same craft, he abode with them, and wrought: for by their occupation they were tentmakers" (Acts 18:3). For eighteen months in Corinth they shared home, workshop, and mission. When Paul sailed for Syria, Priscilla and Aquila went with him as far as [[Ephesus]] — where they remained when he pressed on to Antioch.

At Ephesus, Priscilla and Aquila demonstrated a ministry gift that ranks among the most consequential acts of private instruction in the New Testament. Apollos — an eloquent Alexandrian Jew, "mighty in the scriptures," "fervent in the spirit," teaching and preaching "diligently the things of the Lord" but knowing "only the baptism of John" (Acts 18:24-25) — came to Ephesus. Priscilla and Aquila heard him in the synagogue, "took him unto them, and expounded unto him the way of God more perfectly" (Acts 18:26). Apollos then crossed to [[Achaia]] and "mightily convinced the Jews, and that publickly, shewing by the scriptures that Jesus was Christ" (Acts 18:28). The intellectual giant of Alexandrian Judaism who would become one of the most effective preachers of the early church received his theological completion through the private instruction of two tentmakers. First Corinthians reveals that Apollos became a major figure in the Corinthian church — an "Apollo" party competed with Paul's and Peter's — and the theological formation Priscilla and Aquila gave him shaped that entire ministry.

By the time Paul wrote Romans (approximately AD 57, from Corinth), Priscilla and Aquila had returned to Rome — perhaps after Claudius's death lifted the expulsion — and were again hosting a house church: "Greet Priscilla and Aquila my helpers in Christ Jesus: Who have for my life laid down their own necks: unto whom not only I give thanks, but also all the churches of the Gentiles" (Romans 16:3-4). Paul states plainly that they had risked their lives for him — probably a reference to the Ephesian crisis, though the specific occasion is not narrated. His gratitude extends beyond himself to "all the churches of the Gentiles" — a remarkable acknowledgment of the breadth of their influence. Second Timothy 4:19, Paul's last letter, closes with "Salute Prisca and Aquila" — they appear at the beginning and the end of his preserved correspondence.
