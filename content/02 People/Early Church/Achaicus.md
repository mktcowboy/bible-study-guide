# Achaicus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Member of Corinthian delegation who visited Paul with Stephanas and Fortunatus; their visit refreshed Paul's spirit
- **Family:** Member of the Corinthian church; no other details given
- **Key Books:** [[1 Corinthians]]

## Biography
Achaicus is mentioned only once in the New Testament, in 1 Corinthians 16:17, as one of three members of the Corinthian church who made the journey to Ephesus to visit Paul. His name is simply the Greek adjective for "Achaian" — meaning "from Achaia" — which suggests he may have been a slave or freed slave whose name was derived from his place of origin. This was a common practice in the Roman world, where enslaved people were often named for their geographic origin. The name thus carries a poignant irony: the man is remembered in Scripture as "the Achaian," his identity crystallized in the very region Paul was hoping to reach.

Along with [[Stephanas]] and [[Fortunatus]], Achaicus traveled from Corinth to Ephesus to visit Paul. Paul writes that "they have made up for your absence, for they refreshed my spirit as well as yours" (1 Corinthians 16:18). The word "refreshed" (anapauō) means to give rest, to relieve, to renew — it implies that Paul's spirits were genuinely low before their arrival and genuinely lifted afterward. Personal presence, news from beloved friends, and the encouragement of shared faith had a real effect on the apostle.

Paul's instruction — "Give recognition to such men" (1 Corinthians 16:18) — applies to the whole delegation including Achaicus. In a letter dealing with status, divisions, and spiritual pride in Corinth, this simple instruction to honor quiet, faithful servants is itself a counter-cultural statement.

## Notes
- The name "Achaicus" may indicate slave or freedman background, making his honor in Paul's letter particularly meaningful — those of low status in the world are given recognition in the church
- Together with [[Stephanas]] and [[Fortunatus]], Achaicus represents the kind of ordinary, faithful service that Paul explicitly commands the church to honor above status or spiritual gifts
- His visit was one of the personal connections that sustained Paul emotionally during a prolonged and difficult period of ministry
