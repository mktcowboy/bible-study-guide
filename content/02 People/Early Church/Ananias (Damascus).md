# Ananias (Damascus)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Disciple at Damascus; instrument of Saul's restoration and commissioning
- **Book(s):** [[Acts]]

## Profile
Ananias of Damascus is one of the most humanly compelling figures in the book of Acts precisely because of what he nearly did not do. He was a disciple living in Damascus — the Syrian capital city to which Saul of Tarsus was traveling with arrest warrants for the followers of "the Way." When the Lord appeared to Ananias in a vision and commanded him to go to Saul, Ananias's response was not cowardice but honest objection grounded in hard information: "Lord, I have heard by many of this man, how much evil he hath done to thy saints at Jerusalem: And here he hath authority from the chief priests to bind all that call on thy name" (Acts 9:13-14). Every syllable of the objection was factually correct. Saul had been present at and consenting to [[Stephen's Martyrdom]]. He had "made havock of the church, entering into every house, and haling men and women committed them to prison" (Acts 8:3). Ananias was not being faithless — he was being prudent by every natural measure.

The Lord's response overrode the objection not by dismissing the danger but by revealing a purpose that reframed everything: "He is a chosen vessel unto me, to bear my name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel: For I will shew him how great things he must suffer for my name's sake" (Acts 9:15-16). The persecutor is now the chosen instrument; the suffering he inflicted will be suffered by him. Ananias obeyed. He found Saul in a house on a street called Straight — fasting, blind, and praying for three days — laid his hands on him, and spoke the words of restoration: "Brother Saul, the Lord, even Jesus, that appeared unto thee in the way as thou camest, hath sent me, that thou mightest receive thy sight, and be filled with the Holy Ghost" (Acts 9:17). The word "brother" — applied by a man who had every reason to treat Saul as an enemy — is one of the most grace-saturated words in Acts. Immediately scales fell from Saul's eyes, he received his sight, was baptized, and ate. He began preaching Christ in the Damascus synagogues within days.

Paul's own retelling of the Damascus road event in Acts 22 adds a detail about Ananias's character: he was "a devout man according to the law, having a good report of all the Jews which dwelt there" (Acts 22:12) — a man of impeccable Jewish credentials, which made him an effective and credible vehicle for the commission Paul then received. In the narrative of the [[Conversion of Saul]], Ananias is the indispensable human link between the risen Christ's call on the Damascus road and Paul's full entry into apostolic ministry. He asked for no recognition, sought no ongoing role, and disappears from the text after his task was complete — the model of an obedient servant who plays a critical part in a story not his own.
