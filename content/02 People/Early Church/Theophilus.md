# Theophilus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Named recipient of the Gospel of Luke and the Acts of the Apostles; possibly a Roman official or patron
- **Family:** No personal details given beyond the honorific title "most excellent"
- **Key Books:** [[Luke]], [[Acts]]

## Biography
Theophilus is the named dedicatee of both the Gospel of Luke and the Acts of the Apostles — together comprising approximately 27% of the New Testament. Luke addresses him directly in both prologues: "It seemed good to me also, having followed all things closely for some time past, to write an orderly account for you, most excellent Theophilus" (Luke 1:3) and "In the first book, O Theophilus, I have dealt with all that Jesus began to do and teach" (Acts 1:1). This makes Theophilus one of the most consequential unnamed figures in Christian history, in that the two-volume account of Jesus' ministry and the early church was written specifically for him.

The identity of Theophilus has generated sustained scholarly debate. The honorific "most excellent" (kratistos) used in Luke's Gospel but not in Acts suggests official Roman rank — the same title is used for the governors Felix (Acts 23:26; 24:3) and Festus (Acts 26:25). This would place Theophilus in the category of Roman officials or persons of senatorial or equestrian rank. Some scholars propose he was a Roman patron who funded Luke's literary project; others suggest he was a government official who would need a credible account of Christianity for legal purposes.

A significant alternative interpretation treats "Theophilus" not as a personal name but as a symbolic designation: the name means "lover of God" or "friend of God" in Greek, leading some interpreters to see Luke's work as addressed to any "lover of God" — a literary dedication to the reader of faith rather than a historical patron. However, the specific address "most excellent" and the reference to "what you have been taught" (Luke 1:4) in a context that assumes a specific prior catechesis suggests a real individual rather than a symbolic address.

The mention in Luke 1:4 — that Luke's purpose was so Theophilus might "know the certainty of the things about which you have been instructed" — places Theophilus within the sphere of early Christian catechesis. He had already received some instruction about Jesus; Luke wrote to confirm and ground that faith in orderly, historically researched testimony. Whether Theophilus was an inquirer, a new believer, or an established Christian seeking deeper assurance is not specified.

## Key Events
- Receives Luke's two-volume work — the Gospel and Acts — as the named dedicatee (Luke 1:1–4; Acts 1:1)
- Previously received some form of Christian instruction (Luke 1:4)

## Notes
- The dedicatory format Luke uses (addressing a named individual of high social status) was a standard convention of Hellenistic literary patronage, lending Luke-Acts the character of serious historical literature rather than religious tracts
- Whether Theophilus was a Roman official, a wealthy patron, or a symbolic address, his existence provided Luke with the literary occasion to produce the two most historically comprehensive accounts of Christian origins in the New Testament
- Luke's explicit goal of producing "certainty" (asphaleia) for Theophilus establishes that the Gospel was written with an apologetic intent — to provide a defensible, orderly account for a person who needed historical grounding for his faith
