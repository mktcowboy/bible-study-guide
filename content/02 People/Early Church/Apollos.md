# Apollos

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** Eloquent Alexandrian Jew, mighty in scripture; arrived in Ephesus knowing only John's baptism; Priscilla and Aquila explained the faith more fully
- **Family:** Jewish; from Alexandria, Egypt
- **Key Books:** [[Acts]], [[1 Corinthians]], [[Titus]]

## Biography
Apollos was a Jewish man from Alexandria in Egypt, described in Acts 18:24–25 with an impressive cluster of attributes: "an eloquent man, competent in the Scriptures," who "had been instructed in the way of the Lord," was "fervent in spirit," and "spoke and taught accurately the things concerning Jesus." Alexandria was the intellectual capital of the Jewish diaspora, home to the great library and the flourishing tradition of Hellenistic Jewish scholarship typified by Philo of Alexandria. Apollos's Alexandrian origin almost certainly explains his rhetorical sophistication and scriptural depth.

When Apollos arrived in Ephesus and began speaking boldly in the synagogue, [[Priscilla]] and [[Aquila]] heard him and recognized that his knowledge, while accurate as far as it went, was incomplete: "he knew only the baptism of John." They took him aside privately and "explained to him the way of God more accurately" (Acts 18:26). This episode is remarkable for several reasons: a woman (and her husband) instructing an already eloquent and capable teacher; the gentle, private manner of correction rather than public rebuke; and the outcome — Apollos went on to become an even more effective witness with his completed understanding.

From Ephesus, Apollos moved to Achaia (Greece), where his impact at Corinth was substantial. Acts 18:28 notes that "he powerfully refuted the Jews in public, showing by the Scriptures that the Christ was Jesus." His effectiveness led to a cult of personality problem at Corinth: some said "I follow Apollos" while others said "I follow Paul" (1 Corinthians 1:12). Paul addresses this directly in 1 Corinthians 3–4, emphasizing that neither he nor Apollos was the foundation — Christ alone was — and that he and Apollos were simply servants with complementary roles: "I planted, Apollos watered, but God gave the growth" (1 Corinthians 3:6). Notably, Paul expresses no personal rivalry with Apollos; he calls him "our brother" and urges him to visit Corinth (1 Corinthians 16:12).

Apollos appears once more in Titus 3:13, where Paul asks Titus to help him and the lawyer Zenas on their journey. Some scholars have proposed Apollos as the author of Hebrews, based on his Alexandrian background, his eloquence, and the letter's sophisticated rhetorical structure — but this is speculative.

## Key Events
- Arrives in Ephesus, teaches accurately but incompletely about Jesus (Acts 18:24–25)
- [[Priscilla]] and [[Aquila]] take him aside and complete his understanding (Acts 18:26)
- Moves to Achaia with letters of recommendation from Ephesian believers (Acts 18:27)
- Powerfully demonstrates from Scripture that Jesus is the Messiah in Corinth (Acts 18:28)
- Becomes the focus of a Corinthian faction alongside Paul and Cephas factions (1 Corinthians 1:12)
- Paul treats him as a co-laborer, not a rival (1 Corinthians 3:6)
- Mentioned as traveling with Zenas the lawyer (Titus 3:13)

## Notes
- Apollos is the most prominent example in the New Testament of a gifted teacher who needed correction rather than dismissal — his response to private instruction was receptive, not defensive
- The "I follow Apollos" faction at Corinth likely arose from the contrast between his rhetorical polish and Paul's deliberately plain style (1 Corinthians 2:1–5)
- The proposal that Apollos authored Hebrews — first floated by Martin Luther — remains one of the most interesting unsolved questions in New Testament authorship
