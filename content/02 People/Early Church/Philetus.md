# Philetus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Role:** False teacher associated with Hymenaeus who taught that the resurrection was past
- **Family:** No background details given; associated with the Ephesian church context
- **Key Books:** [[2 Timothy]]

## Biography
Philetus is mentioned only once in the New Testament, in 2 Timothy 2:17–18, where Paul links him directly with [[Hymenaeus]] as co-teachers of a specific false doctrine: "their talk will spread like gangrene. Among them are Hymenaeus and Philetus, who have swerved from the truth, saying that the resurrection has already happened. They are upsetting the faith of some."

His name derives from the Greek philētos, meaning "beloved" or "acceptable" — an ironic contrast to the nature of his teaching. He appears only in association with [[Hymenaeus]]; there is no mention of him being disciplined separately or of a prior history with Paul. This suggests he may have been a follower or associate of [[Hymenaeus]] who adopted or spread the same erroneous position, rather than an independent source of false teaching.

The specific error attributed to both Philetus and [[Hymenaeus]] — claiming that the resurrection had already taken place — represented a fundamental distortion of Christian hope. Paul's teaching in 1 Corinthians 15 makes clear that the bodily resurrection of the dead is not a past event but a future one awaiting the return of Christ. To teach that it had already occurred was to undercut the eschatological dimension of Christian faith, remove the hope of bodily resurrection, and potentially justify a spiritualized or gnostic interpretation of the gospel that denied material redemption.

Paul describes this teaching as "spreading like gangrene" and as having the effect of "upsetting the faith of some" — both suggesting active infection of the congregation's spiritual health and real pastoral damage to believers whose faith was destabilized by it.

## Notes
- Philetus is one of Scripture's briefest but most clearly defined negative examples — named once, in association with a specific false doctrine, with no redemptive arc provided
- Unlike [[Hymenaeus]], who was subject to the drastic discipline of being "handed over to Satan" (1 Timothy 1:20), Philetus receives only public naming and warning — his relative absence from the record suggests he may have been a lesser figure in the movement
- The resurrection error both men taught anticipates the proto-gnostic tendencies that would become a major challenge to the church in the 2nd century
