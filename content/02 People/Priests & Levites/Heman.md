# Heman

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Chief musician and seer under David; attributed author of Psalm 88; son of Joel, grandson of Samuel
- **Family:** Son of Joel; grandson of the prophet [[Samuel]]; from the tribe of Levi (Kohathite)
- **Key Books:** [[1 Chronicles]], [[Psalms]]

## Biography
Heman son of Joel was one of the three chief Levitical musicians appointed by [[David]] to lead temple worship, alongside [[Asaph]] and Ethan ([[Jeduthun]]). As a Kohathite Levite and grandson of the prophet [[Samuel]], he stood at the intersection of Israel's prophetic and priestly streams. The genealogy in 1 Chronicles 6:33–38 traces his lineage back through Joel, Samuel, Elkanah, and ultimately to Levi, confirming his Levitical legitimacy for this temple role.

1 Chronicles 15:17–19 names Heman as one of the three principal singers appointed to minister before the ark when [[David]] brought it to Jerusalem — the defining moment of Israel's new worship order. These three musicians played bronze cymbals, and they led Israel's largest and most elaborate worship procession. Heman's placement at the center of this list (with [[Asaph]] on his right and Ethan on his left) may indicate a position of particular honor.

1 Chronicles 25 provides the most detailed picture of Heman's role. He is described as "the king's seer" (1 Chronicles 25:5) — a title combining prophetic function with musical ministry, indicating that his work was not merely artistic but charismatic and revelatory. He had fourteen sons and three daughters, all of whom served in the temple music ministry, making his family one of the most musically prolific in Israel's worship life. 1 Chronicles 25:5 adds that "God had given Heman fourteen sons and three daughters, to exalt his horn," suggesting that divine blessing on his family was recognized as a sign of God's favor.

Psalm 88 bears the superscription "A Song. A Psalm of the Sons of Korah. To the choirmaster: according to Mahalath Leannoth. A Maskil of Heman the Ezrahite." This psalm is arguably the darkest in the entire Psalter — it contains no resolution, no turn toward praise, ending with the desolate words "darkness is my closest friend" (Psalm 88:18 NIV). Attributed to Heman the Ezrahite (possibly the same Heman or a namesake), it represents the extreme edge of lament theology: prayer from the deepest pit with no visible answer.

## Notes
- Heman's title "the king's seer" (1 Chronicles 25:5) is unique and indicates that prophetic speech could operate through music and song in Israel's worship
- Psalm 88's unresolved ending — no turn to praise, ending in darkness — is preserved in Scripture as evidence that honest, raw lament belongs in Israel's worship, even when no resolution is visible
- The grandson of [[Samuel]] serving as chief musician under [[David]] creates a symbolic continuity: Samuel anointed David, and Samuel's grandson led music at the height of David's reign
