# Jeshua

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** High priest who returned from exile with Zerubbabel; oversaw rebuilding of the altar and temple foundation
- **Family:** Son of Jehozadak; grandson of Seraiah (the last high priest before the exile)
- **Key Books:** [[Ezra]], [[Nehemiah]], [[Zechariah]]

## Biography
Jeshua son of Jehozadak (also spelled Joshua in Haggai and Zechariah) was the first high priest of the restored community following the Babylonian exile. His grandfather Seraiah had been the last chief priest before [[Nebuchadnezzar]] destroyed Jerusalem and executed the temple leadership (2 Kings 25:18–21). Jeshua thus represented the restoration of a broken priestly line — the bridge between pre-exilic and post-exilic Israel's religious leadership.

He returned from Babylon alongside [[Zerubbabel]] in approximately 538 BC under the authorization of [[Cyrus]]'s decree. Together, this pair led the first wave of returning exiles — approximately 49,697 people — back to the land of their ancestors. Their dual leadership (civil/royal and priestly) formed the governing structure of the restored community and established a model of collaborative governance that would characterize the post-exilic period.

Jeshua's first recorded act with [[Zerubbabel]] was the rebuilding of the altar of burnt offering in Jerusalem, which they restored before even laying the temple foundation. The text emphasizes that they did this "despite their fear of the peoples of the lands" (Ezra 3:3), suggesting real threat from surrounding populations who opposed the restoration. The re-establishment of the daily burnt offerings and the observance of the Feast of Tabernacles were Jeshua's priestly contributions to this foundational moment.

The most extensive theological treatment of Jeshua comes in Zechariah 3, where he appears in a heavenly courtroom scene. The Accuser stands at his right hand to oppose him, but the Angel of the LORD silences the Accuser: "Is not this a brand plucked from the fire?" (Zechariah 3:2). Jeshua stands in filthy garments — representing the priesthood's contamination through exile and sin — and the Angel commands that clean garments be put on him. This vision represents the cleansing and restoration of the priestly office after the defilement of exile. Zechariah then addresses Jeshua prophetically, pointing to a figure called "the Branch" who will come in the future, and declares that if Jeshua walks in God's ways he will have authority in God's courts (Zechariah 3:7–8).

## Key Events
- Returns from Babylon with [[Zerubbabel]] at the head of the first wave of exiles (Ezra 2:2)
- Rebuilds the altar and restores the daily burnt offerings and feasts (Ezra 3:2–6)
- Helps oversee the laying of the temple foundation (Ezra 3:8–10)
- Withstands opposition from peoples of the land (Ezra 4:3)
- Resumes temple construction under prophetic encouragement of [[Haggai]] and [[Zechariah]] (Ezra 5:1–2)
- Appears in Zechariah's vision cleansed and re-commissioned as high priest (Zechariah 3)

## Relationships

- [[Zerubbabel]] - civil governor and partner in leadership
- [[Haggai]] - prophet who addressed him alongside Zerubbabel
- [[Zechariah]] - prophet who gave him the cleansing vision

## Notes
- Zechariah 3's vision of Jeshua in filthy garments represents the priestly institution's contamination through exile, and the vision of cleansing anticipates the ultimate purification of the priesthood in the Messiah
- Jeshua and [[Zerubbabel]] together are interpreted by Zechariah 4 as the "two olive trees" — the anointed priestly and royal leaders who supply oil to the lampstand (Israel's worship life)
- The name "Jeshua" is the Hebrew form of "Jesus" (Yeshua), and the priestly Joshua/Jeshua of Zechariah 3–6 prefigures Christ as the great high priest who bears humanity's defilement and is clothed in righteousness
