# Pashhur

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Priest and temple official who had Jeremiah beaten and put in stocks; Jeremiah renamed him "Magor-missabib" (terror on every side)
- **Family:** Son of Immer; a priest
- **Key Books:** [[Jeremiah]]

## Biography
Pashhur son of Immer was the chief officer of the temple in Jerusalem during the early ministry of [[Jeremiah]], responsible for maintaining order in the sacred precincts. He represents the institutional religious establishment's hostility toward prophetic critique — a pattern that runs throughout the book of [[Jeremiah]] and reaches its most violent expression in the prophet's arrest and eventual imprisonment.

The incident that defines Pashhur's place in the biblical narrative comes in Jeremiah 20. [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] had just delivered his shattering sermon at the Potsherd Gate (Jeremiah 19), smashing a clay jar as a prophetic symbol of the destruction coming on Jerusalem and Judah. When Pashhur heard [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] prophesying these things in the temple courts, he struck the prophet and had him put in stocks at the upper Benjamin Gate of the temple — a publicly humiliating and painful form of restraint. Pashhur's action was the first recorded physical violence against [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] by a named individual.

When Pashhur released [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] the following day, [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]]'s response was devastating. Rather than backing down, [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] immediately renamed him: "The LORD does not call your name Pashhur, but Terror on Every Side" (Jeremiah 20:3). This renaming was both prophecy and verdict. [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] declared that Pashhur would witness the fall of all his companions, that he himself would be taken captive to Babylon along with his household, his friends, and all Judah, and that he would die there and be buried there — because he had "prophesied falsely" to the people of Judah.

The irony is theological: Pashhur's name may have meant something like "freedom on every side" or "Horus rules," but [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]]'s renaming makes him a walking symbol of the very terror he refused to proclaim. By silencing and punishing the true prophet, Pashhur sealed his own fate in the judgment that was coming. His story is one of the starkest examples in the Old Testament of the consequences of suppressing prophetic warning.

It should be noted that a second "Pashhur" appears in Jeremiah 38:1, son of Malchiah, who was one of the officials who had [[Jeremiah]] thrown into a cistern. This appears to be a different individual, though the repetition of the name reinforces the portrait of priestly and official resistance to [[Jeremiah]]'s ministry.

## Key Events
- Hears [[Jeremiah (Prophet)|Jeremiah]] prophesying judgment at the temple (Jeremiah 20:1)
- Strikes [[Jeremiah]] and puts him in stocks at the Benjamin Gate (Jeremiah 20:2)
- Releases [[Jeremiah]] the next day (Jeremiah 20:3)
- Receives the new name "Magor-missabib" (Terror on Every Side) from [[Jeremiah]] (Jeremiah 20:3)
- Prophesied to fall into the hand of [[Nebuchadnezzar]] and die in Babylon (Jeremiah 20:4–6)

## Notes
- Pashhur embodies the danger of institutional religion protecting its own authority against prophetic critique — a pattern Scripture condemns repeatedly
- The renaming "Magor-missabib" (Terror on Every Side) is particularly ironic because [[Jeremiah]] had just used the same phrase to describe the coming judgment (Jeremiah 20:10), making Pashhur a living embodiment of what he refused to warn others about
- The attack on [[Jeremiah]] in Jeremiah 20 triggers the most psychologically intimate passage in the entire book — Jeremiah's "confessions" in 20:7–18, in which he wrestles with God about his call, his suffering, and his inability to stop speaking
