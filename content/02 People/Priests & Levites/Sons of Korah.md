# Sons of Korah

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Role:** Levitical guild of musicians and gatekeepers; attributed authors of eleven psalms
- **Family:** Descended from [[Korah]] (Numbers 16) through the line of Kohath
- **Key Books:** [[Psalms]], [[1 Chronicles]]

## Biography
The Sons of Korah are the attributed authors of eleven psalms (42-49, 84-85, 87-88), ranging from the yearning of Psalm 42 ("As the hart panteth after the water brooks") to the royal celebrations of Psalms 44-48 and the rich temple longing of Psalm 84. Their authorship superscriptions appear in the Hebrew text as "for the sons of Korah."

The guild survived the rebellion of their ancestor [[Korah]] (Numbers 16:1-35), who was swallowed up by the earth for challenging Moses and Aaron's authority. Numbers 26:11 explicitly notes that "the children of Korah died not." This survival from judgment becomes the background of their most personal psalms — a family that stood at the edge of destruction and lived.

In 1 Chronicles 9 and 26, the Korahites served as gatekeepers and guards of the temple thresholds, and some served as bakers of the offering bread. Their role was sacred but not at the highest level — they stood at the doorway, not before the altar. This liminal position — standing at the threshold between the holy and the common — shapes the yearning quality of their psalms: they were near the house of God but not at the center.

## Notes
- Psalm 84:10 — "a doorkeeper in the house of my God" — is the most famous Korahite verse and directly reflects their actual liturgical role as gatekeepers.
- The survival of the Sons of Korah from their ancestor's judgment is itself a kind of grace-in-judgment theme that underlies their most poignant psalms.
- Their psalms are among the most emotionally vivid in the Psalter, combining deep longing, royal confidence, and theological reflection.
