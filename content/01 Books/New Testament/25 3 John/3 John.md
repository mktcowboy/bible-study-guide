# 3 John

> "Beloved, follow not that which is evil, but that which is good. He that doeth good is of God: but he that doeth evil hath not seen God." — 3 John 1:11 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[John]] the Apostle ("the elder")
- **Date Written:** ~AD 85–95, from Ephesus
- **Key Themes:** Hospitality, walking in truth, pride vs. humility, Christlike character
- **Chapters:** 1 (15 verses — the shortest book in the New Testament by verse count)

## Summary

3 John is the shortest book in the New Testament (15 verses) and the most personal of the three Johannine epistles. Written by John, now an elderly apostle identifying himself only as "the elder," the letter is addressed to a specific individual, [[Gaius]], a beloved friend and fellow worker whom John commends for his faithful hospitality to traveling missionaries.

The letter has three focal points, each centered on a named individual. [[Gaius]] is warmly praised for receiving and supporting itinerant brethren — men who were strangers to him — and is encouraged to continue doing so, for such hospitality is cooperation with the work of the gospel itself. [[Diotrephes]], by contrast, is rebuked for loving the preeminence, refusing to receive the brethren, speaking maliciously against John, and expelling those who showed hospitality. He represents pride, self-promotion, and the abuse of church authority. [[Demetrius]] is briefly commended, bearing a good report from all and from the truth itself.

The letter is a window into early church life: the role of itinerant teachers and missionaries, the centrality of hospitality as a ministry, and the tensions that arise when ambition corrupts leadership. Theologically, it grounds Christian ethics in identity — doing good is evidence of knowing God (v. 11).

## Notes & Reflections

- Shortest NT book by verse count (15 verses); shorter than some individual psalms
- John calls himself "the elder" — not asserting apostolic title, but relational authority
- "I have no greater joy" (v. 4) — among the most tender expressions in all of Paul's and John's writings
- Diotrephes is the only NT figure explicitly condemned by name for church pride and power-abuse — a cautionary portrait of what happens when a leader loves prominence over people
- The phrase "the truth itself bears witness" (v. 12, regarding Demetrius) is striking — truth as an active moral witness
- Hospitality in the 1st century church was not merely social — traveling missionaries depended entirely on the network of believers for lodging and provision
- "Greet the friends by name" (v. 14) — deeply personal; John knows the community intimately
