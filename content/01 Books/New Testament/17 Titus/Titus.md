# Titus

> "For the grace of God that bringeth salvation hath appeared to all men, Teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world." — Titus 2:11–12 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]] the Apostle
- **Date Written:** ~AD 62–65, written from Macedonia or Corinth after Paul's release from first Roman imprisonment
- **Key Themes:** Sound doctrine producing good works, church order, elder qualifications, rebuking false teachers, grace-motivated holiness
- **Chapters:** 3

## Summary

Titus is the third of Paul's Pastoral Epistles, written to [[Titus]], a trusted Greek co-worker whom Paul had left on the island of [[Crete]] with a specific mandate: "set in order the things that are wanting, and ordain elders in every city" (1:5). The Cretan churches were young, vulnerable to false teaching, and operating without proper leadership structure. Paul writes to give Titus both the qualifications and the authority to act.

The letter is compact and practical — less personal than 1–2 Timothy, more structured in its ethics. Three chapters follow a clear arc: **establish right leadership** (ch. 1), **teach right living** (ch. 2), and **remember right foundations** (ch. 3).

**Chapter 1** opens with a theologically rich salutation — eternal life promised before the world began (1:2) — before quickly pivoting to the practical crisis. Elders must be appointed to counter the "many unruly and vain talkers and deceivers, especially they of the circumcision" (1:10) who are overturning whole households by teaching for dishonest gain. The Cretans' own poet had called them liars, evil beasts, and slow bellies — Paul confirms the diagnosis (1:12–13) and charges Titus to rebuke them sharply.

**Chapter 2** is the doctrinal center of the letter and one of Paul's most elegant summaries of grace-to-ethics theology. Different groups — older men, older women, younger women, younger men, servants — are each given specific behavioral instructions, but the foundation is the same: God's grace has appeared, teaching us to renounce ungodliness and live self-controlled, righteous, godly lives. We are waiting for the blessed hope — the glorious appearing of our great God and Saviour Jesus Christ (2:13), who gave himself to purify for himself a people of his own who are zealous for good works.

**Chapter 3** grounds Christian social behavior (submission to rulers, peaceableness, humility toward all) in the radical grace of salvation: "not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to his mercy he saved us, through the washing of regeneration, and renewing of the Holy Ghost" (3:5). Having remembered what we were and who saved us, we have both motivation and foundation for good works. The letter ends with practical instructions about Artemas and Tychicus, warnings about divisive persons, and a final command to send Zenas and Apollos on their way.

## Notes & Reflections

- Titus himself never appears in Acts — he is only known through Paul's letters; yet Paul trusted him with some of the most difficult missionary assignments (Corinth, Crete, Dalmatia)
- The description of the Cretan character in 1:12 is Paul's only explicit quotation of a pagan poet as true — he uses it not to condemn the people but to explain why strong leadership is needed
- The "blessed hope" (2:13) — *hē makaria elpis* — is a unique phrase in the NT; *epiphaneia* (glorious appearing) is the same word used for both Christ's first and second advents
- "Washing of regeneration" (3:5) — *palingenesias* — a rare word in the NT (also Matthew 19:28 of cosmic renewal); the closest Paul comes to baptismal language
- Titus 2:11–14 is one of the most complete summaries of the Christian life in Paul's writings: what grace did (appeared), what grace teaches (soberness, righteousness, godliness), what we await (second coming), what Christ accomplished (redemption + purification), and what kind of people we should be (zealous for good works)
