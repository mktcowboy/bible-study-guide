# Colossians

> "Who is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature: For by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether they be thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him: And he is before all things, and by him all things consist." — Colossians 1:15–17 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]], with [[Timothy]]
- **Date Written:** ~AD 60–62, written during Paul's Roman imprisonment
- **Key Themes:** The supremacy and sufficiency of Christ, the Colossian heresy, fullness in Christ, the new life, household ethics
- **Chapters:** 4
- **Source Text:** [[Colossians (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Colossians was written to a church Paul had never personally visited — founded by [[Epaphras]], who had brought the gospel to [[Colossae]] during Paul's three-year Ephesian ministry. Epaphras had reported to Paul (now imprisoned in Rome) that a troubling philosophical-religious system had emerged in the church, threatening to displace Christ from His proper place.

The "Colossian heresy" (never named but reconstructed from Paul's responses) appears to have been a syncretistic blend of Jewish ritual requirements (circumcision, food laws, sabbath observance — 2:16), angelic intermediaries and the "worship of angels" (2:18), ascetic practices (2:21–23: "Do not handle, do not taste, do not touch"), and speculative "philosophy" and "tradition of men" (2:8) — likely a Jewish-influenced Hellenistic mysticism that presented a hierarchy of spiritual powers between God and humanity, reducing Christ to one among many mediators.

Paul's response is one of the highest Christological arguments in the NT. In the magnificent poem of 1:15–20 (likely an early hymn), Christ is presented as the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of all creation, the creator and sustainer of all things — including every supposed angelic hierarchy — and the head of the church, the firstborn from the dead. "For it pleased the Father that in him should all fullness dwell" (1:19). The answer to every mediator-system is complete sufficiency in Christ: "ye are complete in him" (2:10).

**Chapter 1** opens with thanksgiving, prayer, and the Christ hymn. **Chapter 2** directly confronts the false teaching: don't be taken captive by hollow philosophy; the fullness of deity dwells in Christ bodily (2:9); baptism unites believers to His death and resurrection; the "handwriting of ordinances" against us has been nailed to the cross. **Chapter 3** unfolds the implications: if you have been raised with Christ, seek things above. Put off the old nature (with its catalog of sins), put on the new (compassion, kindness, humility, patience, love). **Chapter 4** addresses household relationships and closes with extensive greetings — the richest window into Paul's co-worker network anywhere in his letters.

## Notes & Reflections

- The Christ hymn (1:15–20) is one of the highest and most concentrated Christological poems in the NT; its two strophes cover Christ's relation to creation (1:15–17) and to the church/new creation (1:18–20)
- "Firstborn of all creation" (1:15) — not "first created" (which would make Christ a creature) but "firstborn" as a title of preeminence and sovereignty (cf. Psalm 89:27)
- "All things consist" (1:17) — *synestēken* — "hold together"; Christ is the cohesive principle of the cosmos; the universe would dissolve without Him
- The Colossian heresy anticipates later Gnosticism in its emphasis on secret wisdom, angelic hierarchies, and body-denying asceticism
- The "handwriting of ordinances" (2:14) nailed to the cross — a vivid image of a certificate of debt canceled and publicly nullified
- The letter to the Laodiceans (4:16) is mentioned — it has not survived; some identify it with the letter to the Ephesians (several early MSS of Ephesians lack "in Ephesus")
- Colossians has the richest co-worker greeting list of any prison epistle — a window into the Pauline mission network
