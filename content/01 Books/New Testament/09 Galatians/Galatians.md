# Galatians

> "Stand fast therefore in the liberty wherewith Christ hath made us free, and be not entangled again with the yoke of bondage." — Galatians 5:1 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]]
- **Date Written:** ~AD 48–55 (debated — possibly the earliest of Paul's letters if written to South Galatia after the first missionary journey; or mid-50s if written to North Galatia)
- **Key Themes:** Justification by faith alone, freedom in Christ, the curse of the law, the promise to Abraham, the fruit of the Spirit, the new creation
- **Chapters:** 6

## Summary

Galatians is Paul's most polemical letter — written in a white heat of righteous anger. Unlike Romans, which is a measured systematic exposition of justification, Galatians is a direct, urgent, and intensely personal defense of the gospel. Paul is "astonished" (1:6) — a word he uses nowhere else — that the Galatian believers are deserting the gospel so quickly. The Judaizers, Jewish-Christian teachers insisting that Gentile believers must be circumcised and keep the Mosaic law, have infiltrated the Galatian churches and are perverting the gospel of grace.

The stakes are as high as they get: Paul says anyone — even an angel from heaven — who preaches another gospel should be "anathema" (accursed — 1:8–9). This is not hyperbole. The gospel of justification by faith alone is the hinge on which everything turns.

**Chapter 1** opens without the customary thanksgiving — Paul goes straight to the alarm. He defends the divine origin of his gospel: he received it not from men but from Jesus Christ by revelation. His autobiography through ch. 2 establishes that his gospel was independent of Jerusalem and was confirmed by the pillar apostles.

**Chapter 2** reaches its climax in Paul's confrontation of Peter at Antioch — Peter, fearing the Judaizers, had withdrawn from table fellowship with Gentiles. Paul "withstood him to the face" — the very principle of gospel unity was at stake. Then comes the great declaration: "a man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ" (2:16). "I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me" (2:20).

**Chapters 3–4** are the theological argument. The Spirit came through hearing with faith, not through law-keeping (3:2). Abraham was justified by faith before the law existed (3:6). The law was a *paidagōgos* (tutor, guardian) to bring us to Christ — not the end itself. Now that faith has come, we are sons, not servants; heirs, not slaves. The allegory of Hagar and Sarah illustrates the two covenants: slavery vs. freedom.

**Chapters 5–6** are practical: stand firm in freedom; don't use freedom as an opportunity for the flesh; walk by the Spirit; the fruit of the Spirit vs. works of the flesh; bear one another's burdens; sow to the Spirit, reap eternal life. The letter ends with Paul's personal signature — "with large letters I have written with my own hand" — and the cross as the only ground of boasting.

## Notes & Reflections

- Galatians is the "Magna Carta of Christian Liberty" — Luther called it his wife; his Reformation theology was essentially an explication of Galatians
- Paul's confrontation of Peter (2:11–14) is one of the most remarkable scenes in the NT — a junior apostle publicly correcting the most prominent apostle for behavior that undermined the gospel
- The circumcision party was not demanding circumcision as an add-on to faith — in Paul's analysis, circumcision as a requirement of salvation *displaces* Christ entirely: "Christ is become of no effect unto you, whosoever of you are justified by the law" (5:4)
- "New creation" (6:15) — the ultimate reality; neither circumcision nor uncircumcision matters — only new creation in Christ
- The "large letters" of 6:11 suggest Paul took the pen from Tertius and wrote the closing himself — both an authentication mark and perhaps evidence of poor eyesight

## Sequential Connections

- **May be the earliest Pauline letter:** If addressed to South Galatian churches (Pisidian Antioch, Iconium, Lystra, Derbe) after Acts 13–14, it predates 1 Thessalonians
- **Companion to:** [[Romans]] — Both treat justification by faith; Romans is more measured, Galatians more urgent
- **Acts background:** [[Acts]] 13–15 — First missionary journey and Jerusalem Council
- **Followed by:** [[Ephesians]] in canonical order
