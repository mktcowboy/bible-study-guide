# 1 Corinthians

> "For the preaching of the cross is to them that perish foolishness; but unto us which are saved it is the power of God." — 1 Corinthians 1:18 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]], with [[Sosthenes]]
- **Date Written:** ~AD 53–55, from Ephesus during Paul's third missionary journey
- **Key Themes:** Unity in the body, the wisdom of the cross, sexual ethics, the Lord's Supper, spiritual gifts, the love chapter, the resurrection
- **Chapters:** 16
- **Source Text:** [[1 Corinthians (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

1 Corinthians is the most comprehensive pastoral letter in the NT — a manual for church life written to one of the most troubled congregations in Paul's orbit. Corinth was a port city of enormous commercial wealth, cultural diversity, and moral permissiveness. Its church reflected its context: gifted, proud, divided, and immature. Paul had planted the church (Acts 18), left it in the care of others, and is now writing in response to both a report from "Chloe's people" (1:11) about divisions, and a letter the Corinthians had sent him with theological and practical questions (7:1).

The result is a letter addressing an extraordinary breadth of issues — from ego-driven factionalism to incest to lawsuits to marriage to food sacrificed to idols to disorderly worship to the nature of the resurrection. Each answer flows from a common theological well: the cross. The "word of the cross" (1:18) is Paul's master key — it inverts the world's wisdom, redefines power as weakness, and sets the standard for all Christian community.

**Chapters 1–4** address divisions in the church: parties forming around Paul, Apollos, and Peter. Paul's answer: the cross levels all human wisdom and status. He and Apollos are merely servants; God is the builder. **Chapters 5–6** tackle sexual immorality (a man living with his father's wife — 5:1) and lawsuits between believers. **Chapter 7** answers questions on marriage: the goodness of celibacy; the sanctity of existing marriages; divorce; widows. **Chapters 8–10** wrestle with food offered to idols — the principle of "knowledge puffs up, but love builds up" (8:1). **Chapter 11** addresses the Lord's Supper — the most detailed description of communion in the NT, including the institution of the Supper (11:23–26). **Chapters 12–14** address spiritual gifts — the body metaphor (12), the supremacy of love (13), and the ordering of tongues and prophecy in worship (14). **Chapter 15** is one of the greatest theological chapters in the NT — the resurrection: if Christ is not raised, our faith is futile; the sequence of resurrection; the resurrection body; "O death, where is thy sting?" **Chapter 16** closes with collection instructions and personal greetings.

## Notes & Reflections

- 1 Corinthians 15:3–8 contains what most scholars consider the earliest creedal formula in the NT — the resurrection appearances, probably formulated within 3–5 years of the resurrection itself
- The Love Chapter (13) is not a parenthetical — it is the structural center of the gifts section (12 and 14 bracket it), making love the interpretive framework for all spiritual gifts
- Paul's statement that he received the Lord's Supper tradition "from the Lord" (11:23) is remarkable — either direct revelation or the formalized tradition tracing back to Jesus himself
- The resurrection body (15:35–57) — Paul uses three analogies (seeds, flesh types, celestial vs. terrestrial bodies) to explain continuity + transformation; "spiritual body" (*sōma pneumatikon*) means Spirit-animated, not immaterial
- Corinth is notable as having the most women mentioned by name in any NT church — Chloe (1:11), and in Romans 16 (written from Corinth), at least 10 women co-workers
