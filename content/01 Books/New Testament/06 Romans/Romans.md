# Romans

> "For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ: for it is the power of God unto salvation to every one that believeth; to the Jew first, and also to the Greek. For therein is the righteousness of God revealed from faith to faith: as it is written, The just shall live by faith." — Romans 1:16–17 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]], with Tertius as scribe (16:22)
- **Date Written:** ~AD 57, from Corinth, during Paul's third missionary journey
- **Key Themes:** The righteousness of God, justification by faith, sin and grace, life in the Spirit, Israel's future, Christian ethics
- **Chapters:** 16
- **Source Text:** [[Romans (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Romans is the most systematic and theologically dense of Paul's letters — the nearest thing to a comprehensive statement of the Christian faith in the NT. Unlike most of his letters, Paul writes to a church he has not yet visited, introducing himself and his gospel in advance of a planned visit. He is writing from Corinth near the end of his third missionary journey, with his eyes on Rome and ultimately Spain (15:24, 28).

The letter has a logical architecture that can be traced in three movements: **the problem** (1:18–3:20), **the solution** (3:21–8:39), and **the implications** (9:1–16:27).

**The Problem (1:18–3:20):** All humanity is under the wrath of God. The Gentiles have suppressed the knowledge of God available in creation (1:18–32). The moralist has no standing to judge (2:1–16). The Jew, despite covenant privilege, has also failed — for God is not impressed by external religion when the heart is uncircumcised (2:17–3:8). The conclusion is devastating: "There is none righteous, no, not one" (3:10). "All have sinned and come short of the glory of God" (3:23).

**The Solution (3:21–8:39):** God has provided a righteousness apart from law — through faith in Jesus Christ, whose death was a *propitiation* (3:25), a satisfaction of divine justice. This is justification: God declares the ungodly righteous through faith (4:5). Abraham is the model — he believed God before circumcision, before the law, and God credited it as righteousness. The result: peace with God (5:1), access to grace, joy in tribulation, hope that does not disappoint.

Chapters 6–8 address the implications for the Christian life. The old self has died with Christ; we are no longer enslaved to sin. The law, though holy, only intensifies the recognition of sin (7). Chapter 8 is the apex of the letter — life in the Spirit: no condemnation, adoption as children, the Spirit intercedes, all things work together for good, nothing separates from the love of God.

**Israel's Future (9–11):** God's word has not failed. Israel's rejection is not total or final. God has always worked through a remnant chosen by grace. Israel has stumbled, but their transgression has opened the door to the Gentiles — and when the fullness of the Gentiles comes in, "all Israel shall be saved" (11:26). The section closes with the famous doxology: "O the depth of the riches both of the wisdom and knowledge of God!" (11:33).

**Practical Living (12–16):** Present your bodies as living sacrifices. Transformation by renewing the mind. Humble self-assessment. Love that is genuine. Honor, zeal, generosity. Submit to governing authorities. Love your neighbor. Don't judge one another over debatable matters. The strong bear with the weak. The letter ends with the richest greeting list in Paul's letters and the final doxology.

## Notes & Reflections

- Romans is the closest thing the NT has to a systematic theology — Luther said "every Christian should memorize it"
- Chapter 8 is widely considered the greatest chapter in the Bible — it moves from "no condemnation" to "inseparable love" in a single sustained argument
- The "wretched man" passage (7:14–25) is one of the most debated in Romans: is Paul describing his pre-conversion struggle, his Christian experience, or the experience of the morally sensitive unregenerate? Most Reformed interpreters see it as describing the ongoing conflict within the regenerate person
- Israel's future (ch. 9–11) is among the most contested sections theologically — whether "all Israel shall be saved" (11:26) means national/ethnic Israel or the full number of the elect depends on one's eschatology
- The greeting list in ch. 16 includes 29 named individuals — among them 10 women, two of whom (Priscilla and Junia) are described in honored terms
- Phoebe (16:1–2) is likely the one who physically delivered the letter and may have read and explained it to the Roman congregations — a remarkable role for a woman in first-century context
