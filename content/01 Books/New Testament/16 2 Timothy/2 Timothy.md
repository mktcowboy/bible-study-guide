# 2 Timothy

> "I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith: Henceforth there is laid up for me a crown of righteousness, which the Lord, the righteous judge, shall give me at that day." — 2 Timothy 4:7–8 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]] the Apostle
- **Date Written:** ~AD 67–68 — Paul's final letter, written from a Roman prison cell during his second imprisonment, shortly before his execution under Nero
- **Key Themes:** Endurance in suffering, the sufficiency and inspiration of Scripture, preaching the Word, guarding the deposit, the loneliness of faithful ministry, Paul's farewell
- **Chapters:** 4

## Summary

2 Timothy is Paul's last letter — his farewell. Written from the Mamertine Prison in Rome, cold and alone (only Luke remained with him), facing imminent execution, Paul writes to his beloved son in the faith [[Timothy]] with a final, urgent charge. This is not a letter of despair but of triumphant faith — the words of a man who has run his race and knows the finish line is near.

The letter is permeated by two competing realities: **the suffering of faithful ministry** and **the certainty of God's faithfulness**. Paul is suffering, abandoned by some (Demas, having loved the world), and facing death. Yet the note running through every chapter is confidence: "Nevertheless I am not ashamed: for I know whom I have believed" (1:12). God's foundation stands. The Lord knows His own. The Word endures.

**Chapter 1** opens with warm personal affection — Paul's longing to see Timothy, his confidence in Timothy's sincere faith (passed through grandmother Lois and mother Eunice), and the charge not to be ashamed of the testimony of Christ. Paul gives his personal creed in 1:12 — he knows in whom he has believed, and he is persuaded that God is able to keep his deposit.

**Chapter 2** is the central pastoral charge: be strong in grace, endure hardship like a soldier, an athlete, and a farmer. Don't get entangled in civilian affairs; compete lawfully; work hard. Handle the Word correctly (rightly divide the word of truth, 2:15). Avoid profane and vain babblings that eat like cancer.

**Chapter 3** delivers the darkest description of the last days in the NT — a catalog of 18 character failures marking perilous times (3:1–5), including the self-lovers, money-lovers, proud, unthankful, unholy, without natural affection. Against this, Paul points to two anchors: Timothy's own knowledge of Paul's life and sufferings, and the Scriptures themselves — "all scripture is God-breathed" (3:16–17), profitable for doctrine, reproof, correction, and instruction in righteousness.

**Chapter 4** opens with Paul's most solemn charge: preach the word, in season and out of season — for a time is coming when people will not endure sound doctrine. Then Paul shares his personal situation with remarkable transparency: Demas has forsaken him; others have departed; he is alone except for Luke. He asks Timothy to come quickly, to bring Mark, and to bring his cloak and parchments. Then the great passage: "I have fought a good fight." And the sober warning: "Alexander the coppersmith did me much evil."

## Notes & Reflections

- The most personal of all Paul's letters; written as a man facing death — the only purely personal farewell document in the NT
- "God-breathed" (*theopneustos*, 3:16) — the NT's primary statement of biblical inspiration; the word appears only here in the Greek NT
- The 18-trait catalog in 3:1–5 mirrors in some ways the "works of the flesh" in Galatians 5 — both point to a society (and church) that has abandoned the Spirit
- Demas is a tragic figure — mentioned earlier in Colossians 4:14 and Philemon 24 as a co-worker, now described simply as one who "loved this present world" (4:10)
- Mark's rehabilitation is complete here — once rejected by Paul (Acts 15:36–39) because he had deserted during the first journey, now Paul explicitly says "he is profitable to me for the ministry" (4:11)
- "Rightly dividing" (2:15, *orthotomeo*) — literally "to cut straight" — an image from tent-making (cutting leather accurately) or road-building; one of Paul's great metaphors for careful biblical interpretation
