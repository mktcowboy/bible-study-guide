# 2 John

> "And this is love, that we walk after his commandments. This is the commandment, That, as ye have heard from the beginning, ye should walk in it." — 2 John 1:6 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[John]] the Apostle ("the elder")
- **Date Written:** ~AD 85–95, from Ephesus
- **Key Themes:** Truth and love, discernment in hospitality, guarding against false teachers, abiding in Christ's doctrine
- **Chapters:** 1 (13 verses)
- **Source Text:** [[2 John (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

2 John is the second shortest book in the New Testament (13 verses) and one of the most practically focused of the Johannine epistles. Written by John, who again identifies himself simply as "the elder," the letter is addressed to "the elect lady and her children" — a phrase interpreted either as a specific Christian woman and her household, or more likely as a metaphor for a local church and its members, consistent with John's use of feminine imagery for the community of believers.

The letter balances two inseparable realities: **truth** and **love**. John's joy in learning that some of the recipients are walking in truth (v. 4) mirrors his language in 3 John. He reaffirms the ancient commandment to love one another (v. 5–6), grounding love not in sentiment but in obedience to the Father's word.

The letter then pivots sharply to **warning**. Many deceivers have gone out into the world who do not confess Jesus Christ as coming in the flesh — this is the spirit of antichrist (v. 7). John's concern is not merely doctrinal but pastoral: false teachers who deny the incarnation are not simply wrong — they are spiritually dangerous. His instruction is decisive: do not receive them into your house, do not bid them "godspeed," for to do so is to share in their evil deeds (v. 10–11). This is not rudeness but discernment — hospitality extended to error is complicity in its spread.

The letter ends warmly, with John expressing a desire to visit and speak face-to-face rather than continue in writing (v. 12) — "full joy" comes from personal communion, not correspondence.

## Notes & Reflections

- "The elect lady" — debate continues: individual woman (Kyria = "Lady" as a proper name?) or personification of a church. The parallel with "thy elect sister" (v. 13) suggests two churches corresponding to each other
- The incarnation is the doctrinal line John draws — denying Christ "come in the flesh" is the defining mark of antichrist in Johannine thought (cf. 1 John 4:2)
- John's instruction not to "bid godspeed" (v. 11) is among the strongest separation commands in the NT — not social rudeness, but spiritual self-protection
- "Abide in the doctrine of Christ" (v. 9) — the danger isn't falling short of Christ's teaching but going *beyond* it into speculative theology that erodes the core
- Love and truth are inseparable in Johannine theology — love without truth becomes sentimentality; truth without love becomes harshness. This letter holds both in tension
