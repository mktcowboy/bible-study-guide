# James

> "My brethren, count it all joy when ye fall into divers temptations; Knowing this, that the trying of your faith worketh patience. But let patience have her perfect work, that ye may be perfect and entire, wanting nothing." — James 1:2–4 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[James (Brother of Lord)|James]], brother of Jesus, leader of the Jerusalem church
- **Date Written:** ~AD 45–50 — possibly the earliest NT letter written
- **Key Themes:** Practical wisdom, faith and works, trials producing patience, the tongue, the wisdom from above, prayer, care for the poor
- **Chapters:** 5

## Summary

James is the Proverbs of the New Testament — a collection of practical wisdom for the Christian life, strikingly similar in form to Jewish wisdom literature. Unlike Paul's letters, which are heavily doctrinal before becoming ethical, James begins with ethics and stays there. It is not that James ignores theology — his theology is everywhere implied — but his concern is the *embodiment* of faith in daily life.

The author is [[James (Brother of Lord)|James]], the brother of Jesus, who was a skeptic during Jesus' earthly ministry (John 7:5) but became a believer after the resurrection (1 Corinthians 15:7) and rose to become the leader of the Jerusalem church. He was killed by stoning at the instigation of the high priest Ananus around AD 62, according to Josephus.

The letter is addressed to "the twelve tribes which are scattered abroad" — Jewish Christians dispersed throughout the Roman world. The community James addresses is struggling with poverty/wealth tensions, favoritism in worship, boasting, conflict, and spiritual complacency.

**Chapter 1** introduces the letter's controlling themes: trials as the crucible of patience; ask God for wisdom; the poor are exalted, the rich humbled; God doesn't tempt; every good gift is from God; be doers of the Word, not hearers only; pure religion = care for orphans and widows.

**Chapter 2** confronts favoritism — showing partiality toward the rich in the assembly is a violation of the royal law of love. Then comes the famous "faith without works" passage: Abraham's faith was made complete by his works; Rahab's works demonstrated faith; a dead faith is no faith.

**Chapter 3** is the anatomy of the tongue — the most dangerous small thing in the world. The same mouth that blesses God curses men. True wisdom is peaceable, gentle, open to reason, full of mercy — contrasted with the earthly, unspiritual, demonic wisdom that produces envy and disorder.

**Chapter 4** addresses conflict in the community: wars and fightings come from lusts within; friendship with the world is enmity with God; humble yourself; don't speak evil of one another; don't presume on the future.

**Chapter 5** turns to the rich who have oppressed laborers; then to patience in suffering (Job as example); to the prohibiting of oaths; to prayer for the sick; to the power of Elijah's prayer; and finally to the restoration of those who wander from truth.

## Notes & Reflections

- Luther famously called James an "epistle of straw" — objecting to its apparent works-emphasis as a counterweight to Paul's "faith alone." The perceived tension is resolved when James and Paul are seen as addressing different problems: Paul refutes *works as the basis of justification*; James refutes *faith that produces no obedience*
- "Abraham believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness" (2:23) — James and Paul both quote Genesis 15:6, but with different points: Paul uses it to show faith precedes works; James shows that Abraham's later obedience (Genesis 22) demonstrated the reality of that faith
- The word *psychikos* (4:15, "sensual/natural") literally means "soulish" — governed by the appetites rather than the Spirit
- James's description of prayer (5:13–18) is the NT's primary text for the practice of anointing with oil for healing — the one context where James explicitly mandates elders' participation in a pastoral act
- Elijah prayed "earnestly" (5:17) — the Greek is *proseuchē prosēuxato*, "he prayed with prayer" — an intensifying Hebrew idiom embedded in Greek
