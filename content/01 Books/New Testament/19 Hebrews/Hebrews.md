# Hebrews

> "Now faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen." — Hebrews 11:1 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** Unknown — traditionally attributed to Paul, but internal evidence suggests otherwise; Apollos, Barnabas, Priscilla, and Luke have all been proposed
- **Date Written:** ~AD 60–70 — before the destruction of the Temple (AD 70), as the Temple system is still spoken of as present
- **Key Themes:** The superiority of Christ over everything in the Old Covenant, the priesthood of Melchizedek, the new covenant, the danger of apostasy, faith as endurance, the "hall of faith"
- **Chapters:** 13

## Summary

Hebrews is the great theological bridge between the Old and New Testaments — a sustained argument that everything the OT pointed toward has been perfectly and finally fulfilled in Jesus Christ. It is addressed to Jewish believers (hence "Hebrews") who are tempted to drift back toward the Mosaic system, whether from persecution, intellectual uncertainty, or a failure to grasp the finality of Christ's work.

The argument is structured as a series of comparisons, each establishing Christ's superiority:

- Christ superior to **angels** (1–2): The Son is the exact imprint of God's nature; angels worship him; he became lower than angels temporarily to taste death for everyone
- Christ superior to **Moses** (3–4): Moses was a faithful servant in God's house; Christ is the Son over God's house. Therefore — don't harden your hearts as Israel did; enter the rest
- Christ as a greater **High Priest** (5–10): Aaron was the OT type; Christ is the fulfillment — not of the Levitical order but of the eternal order of **Melchizedek** (Psalm 110:4), without beginning or end of days. He entered not an earthly sanctuary but the heavenly one, with not animal blood but his own blood, offering not annual sacrifices but **one sacrifice, once for all, for all time** (10:12). The new covenant (Jeremiah 31) replaces the old.
- Faith and **endurance** (11–13): The "hall of faith" — Abel, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Sarah, Moses, Rahab, and the unnamed heroes who suffered and were not delivered in this age — all died in faith "not having received the promises" (11:13). Therefore: throw off hindrances; run with endurance; keep your eyes on Jesus, "the author and finisher of our faith."

Throughout, Hebrews intersperses **five warning passages** — solemn cautions against drifting, hardening, falling short of grace, apostasy, and refusing the one who speaks. The warnings are debated: do they refer to genuine believers who can lose salvation, or to professing believers who never had saving faith? The debate is long-standing. What is clear: Hebrews takes the danger of unbelief with deadly seriousness.

## Notes & Reflections

- The authorship debate is ancient — Origen (3rd century) said "only God knows" who wrote it; the polished Greek, the Alexandrian allegorical method (especially in the Melchizedek section), and the absence of Paul's usual signature all point away from Paul
- Melchizedek (ch. 7) is one of the most theologically rich typological interpretations in the NT — his "without beginning of days or end of life" (7:3) doesn't mean he was eternal, but that his genealogy is unrecorded, making him a perfect type of Christ's eternal priesthood
- The "once for all" (*ephapax*) sacrifice is the great declaration of Hebrews: the repetition of OT sacrifices pointed to their inadequacy; Christ's sacrifice needs no repetition
- The interpretation of the apostasy warnings (especially 6:4–6 and 10:26–31) has generated enormous debate in Reformed, Arminian, and other traditions; the warnings function pastorally regardless of their ultimate theological resolution
- Chapter 11 is structured as a catalog of faith-acts: "By faith [person] [did something]" — the repetition is deliberate, almost liturgical, building momentum toward the climax
