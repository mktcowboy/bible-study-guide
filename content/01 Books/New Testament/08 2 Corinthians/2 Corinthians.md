# 2 Corinthians

> "We are troubled on every side, yet not distressed; we are perplexed, but not in despair; Persecuted, but not forsaken; cast down, but not destroyed; Always bearing about in the body the dying of the Lord Jesus, that the life also of Jesus might be made manifest in our body." — 2 Corinthians 4:8–10 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]], with [[Timothy]]
- **Date Written:** ~AD 55–56, from Macedonia, after a painful visit to Corinth and the subsequent reconciliation
- **Key Themes:** The nature of true apostolic ministry, weakness as the vehicle for God's power, the treasure in earthen vessels, reconciliation, generous giving, the defense of Paul's apostleship
- **Chapters:** 13

## Summary

2 Corinthians is the most emotionally transparent of Paul's letters — and among the most personal documents in the NT. It is written in the aftermath of a crisis: a "painful visit" to Corinth (2:1), a "severe letter" written in tears (2:3–4, now lost), and a period of deep personal anguish before [[Titus]] arrived with news of reconciliation (7:5–7). The letter reflects the full range of Paul's pastoral heart — relief, joy, vulnerability, indignation, and fierce defense of the gospel.

The Corinthians had come under the influence of "super-apostles" (*hoi hyperlian apostoloi*, 11:5) — traveling teachers who prided themselves on impressive rhetoric, letters of commendation, and spiritual credentials. By their standards, Paul was contemptible: unimpressive in person, weak in bodily presence, afflicted, and without a salary. Paul's defense inverts their criteria entirely: true apostolic ministry is validated by *suffering*, not success; by *weakness* through which God's power is made perfect.

**Chapters 1–7** are largely personal and pastoral. Paul explains the change in his travel plans (not fickleness but pastoral sensitivity — 1:15–22). He describes the "treasure in earthen vessels" — the overwhelming power is God's, not ours (4:7). He expounds the ministry of reconciliation (5:11–21) — the most compact summary of substitutionary atonement in Paul: "He made him who knew no sin to be sin for us, that we might become the righteousness of God in him" (5:21). He appeals for the Corinthians' full reconciliation.

**Chapters 8–9** address the Jerusalem collection — a generous offering Paul has been gathering from Gentile churches for the poor saints in Jerusalem. These chapters contain the NT's most developed theology of Christian giving: proportionality, cheerfulness, God's prior grace as the model and motivation (8:9: "though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor").

**Chapters 10–13** represent a sharp tonal shift — Paul turns polemical. He confronts the false apostles directly, "boasting" ironically in his weaknesses. He gives the most extensive account of his sufferings anywhere in his letters (11:23–29). He shares the experience of being caught up to the third heaven (12:1–4). He reveals the "thorn in the flesh" (12:7–9) — God's answer: "My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness." The letter ends with a severe warning: Christ is not weak toward them — he will not spare when he comes.

## Notes & Reflections

- The "thorn in the flesh" (12:7) — one of the most discussed personal details in Paul's biography; candidates include: eye disease (Galatians 4:15), epilepsy, a speech impediment, persistent opposition, sexual temptation. Paul never says. The ambiguity may be intentional
- 2 Corinthians 5:21 — "He made him to be sin" — the most compact statement of penal substitution in the NT; the theological weight is extraordinary: the sinless one became the sin-bearer so that sinners might become the righteousness of God
- The "third heaven" (12:2–4) — Paul uses distancing language ("I know a man") about himself; the content of what he heard is "unspeakable" — literally *arrētos*, "not able to be spoken"
- The collection for Jerusalem (ch. 8–9) was arguably the most politically significant act of Paul's ministry — a concrete demonstration of Gentile-Jewish unity in the church body
- Godly sorrow vs. worldly sorrow (7:10) — one of Paul's most useful pastoral distinctions: worldly sorrow produces death (mere regret about consequences); godly sorrow produces repentance leading to salvation
