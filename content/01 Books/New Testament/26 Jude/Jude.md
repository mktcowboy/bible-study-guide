# Jude

> "Beloved, when I gave all diligence to write unto you of the common salvation, it was needful for me to write unto you, and exhort you that ye should earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints." — Jude 1:3 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Jude]] (Judas), brother of [[James]] and half-brother of Jesus
- **Date Written:** ~AD 65–80 (likely before the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70)
- **Key Themes:** Contending for the faith, false teachers, judgment on the ungodly, the mercy of God, doxology
- **Chapters:** 1 (25 verses)
- **Source Text:** [[Jude (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Jude is a brief but fierce letter — a trumpet blast of warning against apostasy and false teaching that had infiltrated the church. Jude identifies himself as "a servant of Jesus Christ, and brother of James," deliberately not claiming blood relation to Christ (a mark of genuine humility). His original intention was to write a joyful letter about the common salvation; instead, urgent circumstances compelled him to fight.

False teachers had "crept in unawares" (v. 4) — not attacking from outside but infiltrating from within. Jude's description of them is withering: they deny the Lord, turn grace into licentiousness, defile the flesh, despise dominion, and speak evil of things they do not understand. They are like waterless clouds, fruitless trees, raging sea waves, and wandering stars — all images of things promising life and direction that deliver neither.

Jude reaches back into Israel's history and into extrabiblical Jewish tradition to illustrate the certainty of judgment on apostasy. He draws on three OT examples — the generation of Israelites who died in the wilderness despite being delivered from Egypt (v. 5), the fallen angels who left their proper domain (v. 6), and Sodom and Gomorrah (v. 7) — as anchors for the argument that God does not ignore rebellion, regardless of privilege. He also references the apocryphal *Book of Enoch* (v. 14–15) and the *Assumption of Moses* (v. 9), using these culturally familiar sources without canonizing them.

The letter pivots in verse 20 to pastoral exhortation. Believers are to build themselves up in faith, pray in the Holy Spirit, keep themselves in the love of God, and await the mercy of Christ. They are also to have mercy on those who doubt — pulling some from fire, extending compassion to others while being careful not to be contaminated by their error.

Jude closes with one of the most magnificent doxologies in all of Scripture (v. 24–25), grounding the entire letter's urgency in the assurance that God is able to keep His people from falling.

## Notes & Reflections

- Jude parallels 2 Peter 2 extensively — the exact relationship (common source, one borrowing from the other, or independent warnings about the same problem) is debated; most scholars think Jude was used by Peter, or both drew from common teaching
- Jude's use of the *Book of Enoch* and *Assumption of Moses* is remarkable — not endorsing them as scripture, but drawing from culturally known texts to make his argument accessible; a form of apologetic engagement
- The doxology (v. 24–25) is arguably the most complete and theologically dense benediction in the NT — combining God's ability, the believer's security, and the eternal praise of the Godhead
- "Contend earnestly" (v. 3, *epagōnizomai*) is a vivid athletic/combat term — not passive defense but active, strenuous engagement for the faith
- The three OT examples (v. 5–7) are carefully chosen: a nation, a class of spiritual beings, and a city — showing that no category of creature is exempt from judgment
