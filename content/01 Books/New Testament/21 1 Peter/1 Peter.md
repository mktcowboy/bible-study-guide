# 1 Peter

> "Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, which according to his abundant mercy hath begotten us again unto a lively hope by the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, To an inheritance incorruptible, and undefiled, and that fadeth not away, reserved in heaven for you." — 1 Peter 1:3–4 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Peter]] (Simon Peter, the Apostle), with [[Silas|Silvanus]] (Silas) as scribe
- **Date Written:** ~AD 62–64, written from Rome ("Babylon" — 5:13) before the Neronian persecution intensified
- **Key Themes:** Suffering for righteousness, living hope through resurrection, holiness as strangers and aliens, submission to authority, Christ as the suffering example
- **Chapters:** 5

## Summary

1 Peter is written to scattered believers throughout five provinces of Asia Minor (modern Turkey) — strangers and pilgrims who are experiencing the reality of social marginalization and the beginning of active persecution. Peter's pastoral purpose is to reframe suffering: not as evidence that God has abandoned them, but as participation in the pattern of Christ himself, and as the refining fire that produces genuine faith.

The letter is framed by two great theological anchors: the **resurrection** (which grounds hope, 1:3) and the **return of Christ** (which sustains endurance, 1:7, 13; 4:13; 5:4). Between these anchors, Peter builds a theology of the Christian life as lived between two worlds — called out of the old life, not yet arrived in the new.

**Chapter 1** opens with one of the most magnificent doxologies in the NT — the "lively hope" begotten through Christ's resurrection. The recipients are elect, known by God before the foundation of the world. The prophets searched to understand what the Spirit of Christ was pointing to in the suffering and glory of the Messiah. Therefore: be holy, for God is holy; fear God, remember the precious blood, love one another fervently.

**Chapter 2** develops the "living stones" image — believers are a spiritual house, a holy priesthood, a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, a holy nation (2:9). Then comes the submission section: submit to governing authorities, to masters — even unjust ones — because Christ suffered leaving an example, "that ye should follow his steps" (2:21). The Isaiah 53 allusions are dense and deliberate.

**Chapter 3** extends submission to the household: wives and husbands (3:1–7), then all believers to each other (3:8–9). The section ends with the mysterious passage about Christ preaching to spirits in prison (3:18–22) — the most debated passage in 1 Peter.

**Chapter 4** addresses suffering directly: arm yourselves with Christ's mindset toward suffering; don't be surprised by the fiery trial; rejoice in sharing Christ's sufferings; judgment begins at the house of God.

**Chapter 5** gives instructions to elders (shepherd the flock, not for gain), to young men (humble yourselves), and to all (cast every anxiety on God, because he cares; resist the devil; the God of all grace will restore you).

## Notes & Reflections

- 1 Peter 2:9 ("royal priesthood, holy nation, peculiar people") draws directly from Exodus 19:5–6 — Peter applies Israel's covenant identity to the church
- "Spirits in prison" (3:19) — one of the most debated passages in the NT; main interpretations: (1) Christ proclaimed victory to fallen angels (Genesis 6 "sons of God"), (2) Noah preached to the now-imprisoned generation of the flood, (3) Christ descended to Hades; most Reformed interpreters favor interpretation 1 or 2
- The letter anticipates imminent persecution — likely written just before (or at the beginning of) the Neronian pogrom following the Great Fire of Rome (AD 64)
- Silvanus as scribe explains the polished Greek — Peter was a Galilean fisherman; the letter's rhetorical quality reflects a highly educated secretary writing in Peter's name
- "Babylon" (5:13) as a code name for Rome is consistent with usage in Revelation 17–18 and Jewish apocalyptic literature of the period
