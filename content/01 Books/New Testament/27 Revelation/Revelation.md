# Revelation

> "I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty." — Revelation 1:8 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[John]] the Apostle — exiled on the island of Patmos during the reign of Domitian; same John who wrote the Gospel of John and the three epistles; the book explicitly names him (1:1, 1:4, 1:9, 22:8)
- **Date Written:** ~AD 95–96, during the reign of Domitian (AD 81–96), who demanded emperor worship and persecuted Christians for refusing; some scholars argue an earlier Neronian date (~AD 68)
- **Key Themes:** The sovereignty of God over history, the lordship of Christ over all earthly powers, the defeat of Satan and evil, the vindication of martyred saints, the new creation as the consummation of all things
- **Chapters:** 22

## Summary

Revelation is the most complex, contested, and misread book in the Bible — and one of the most magnificent. It is a letter (1:4), a prophecy (1:3), and an apocalypse (1:1) simultaneously. Its genre is *apocalyptic* — a literary form common in Second Temple Judaism (Daniel, Enoch, 4 Ezra) that uses symbolic visions, cosmic imagery, and numerical codes to address present suffering in light of divine sovereignty over history. Revelation is not a newspaper-in-advance; it is pastoral literature written to suffering churches under Roman imperial pressure.

The book's address is immediate: "the seven churches which are in Asia" (1:4) — real congregations facing real persecution and compromise in ~AD 95. The symbolic world of the book is drawn almost entirely from the OT (Ezekiel, Daniel, Isaiah, Zechariah, Exodus) — John does not quote but saturates every verse with OT imagery, demanding a biblically literate reader. The "beast" (ch. 13) is drawn from Daniel 7; "Babylon" (ch. 17–18) echoes Babylon in Isaiah and Jeremiah; "666" functions within the numerical code of gematria.

**The book's structure** follows a rough sequence of John's visions, though with extensive recapitulation (the seals, trumpets, and bowls likely depict the same period from different angles rather than strictly sequential events):

- **Prologue** (1:1–20): John on Patmos; vision of the glorified Christ among seven lampstands
- **Seven Letters** (2–3): Individual letters to Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, Laodicea — each with diagnosis, warning, and promise
- **Heavenly Throne Room** (4–5): God on the throne surrounded by four living creatures and 24 elders; the Lamb alone worthy to open the sealed scroll
- **Seven Seals** (6–8:1): White, red, black, pale horses (the four horsemen); martyrs under the altar; cosmic disruption; interlude: 144,000 sealed, great multitude
- **Seven Trumpets** (8–11): Ecological and cosmic disasters; two witnesses; seventh trumpet: "the kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord"
- **The Dragon and the Beasts** (12–14): Woman clothed with the sun; dragon cast out; beast from the sea (666); beast from the earth; the Lamb on Mount Zion
- **Seven Bowls** (15–16): Wrath poured out; Armageddon
- **Babylon the Harlot** (17–18): The great city; merchants lament; her fall
- **Christ's Victory and the Millennium** (19–20): Marriage Supper of the Lamb; the rider on the white horse; beast defeated; Satan bound 1,000 years; great white throne judgment
- **New Creation** (21–22): New heaven and new earth; New Jerusalem descends; the river of life; "behold I make all things new"; "come, Lord Jesus"

**Interpretive schools:** Preterists see fulfillment primarily in AD 70 (Rome = Babylon; Nero = beast). Historicists map the visions onto church history. Futurists see the bulk as still future. Idealists read it as timeless symbolic truth about God's triumph over evil. Most careful interpreters blend elements — the visions had immediate relevance to first-century readers *and* speak to the universal pattern of God's kingdom overcoming evil empires throughout history, *and* point toward an ultimate consummation.

## Notes & Reflections

- The name "Revelation" comes from the Greek *apokalypsis* (1:1) — meaning "unveiling" or "disclosure"; it does not mean "hidden" but "revealed" — the book claims to be a revelation *of* Jesus Christ, not a puzzle to be solved
- "666" (13:18) — the "number of a man" most likely refers to Nero Caesar in Hebrew/Greek gematria (*nrwn qsr* = 666), which explains why some manuscripts have "616" (the Latin form of Nero's name). This doesn't limit its application — the beast pattern repeats throughout history
- The millennium (20:1–6) generates the primary eschatological divisions: Premillennialists see a literal future 1,000-year reign; Amillennialists see it as the current church age (Satan bound, saints reigning with Christ); Postmillennialists see a future golden age of gospel dominance before Christ returns. The debate is longstanding and all three positions have serious defenders
- The New Jerusalem's dimensions (21:16) — 12,000 stadia on each side (~1,380 miles) — form a perfect cube, echoing the Holy of Holies (which was also a cube, 1 Kings 6:20); the entire city becomes the Most Holy Place — God dwelling with his people without mediation
- Revelation uses the OT more densely than any other NT book — Nestle-Aland critical apparatus identifies 404 allusions to the OT in 404 verses; John never formally quotes but saturates every verse with OT imagery, making it unintelligible apart from the OT
- The book ends as it begins — Eden imagery (tree of life, river, no curse), but expanded: what was a garden becomes a city; what was one couple becomes a redeemed multitude from every nation; the serpent is gone; God and the Lamb are the temple and the light

## Sequential Connections

- **Preceded by:** [[Jude]] in canonical order
- **Closes the canon:** Revelation is the capstone of all 66 books — the fulfillment of Genesis (tree of life, no curse), the Psalms (Lamb on the throne), the Prophets (new heavens and earth), and all NT promise
- **Fulfillment of:** Genesis 1–3 (creation, Eden, tree of life); Daniel 7–12 (the final kingdom); Isaiah 65–66 (new creation); Ezekiel 40–48 (new Jerusalem/temple); all NT promises about Christ's return
- **Final word of Scripture:** "Even so, come, Lord Jesus. The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you all. Amen." (22:20–21)
