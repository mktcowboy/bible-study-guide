# Acts

> "But ye shall receive power, after that the Holy Ghost is come upon you: and ye shall be witnesses unto me both in Jerusalem, and in all Judaea, and in Samaria, and unto the uttermost part of the earth." — Acts 1:8 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Luke]] the physician — companion of Paul; same author as the Gospel of Luke; both addressed to [[Theophilus]]
- **Date Written:** ~AD 62–65 — the abrupt ending (Paul under house arrest in Rome) suggests composition before his death; possibly completed before Paul's trial outcome was known
- **Key Themes:** The Holy Spirit as the driving force of mission, the gospel's expansion from Jerusalem to Rome, the transition from Jewish to Gentile-majority church, persecution as a catalyst for growth, the continuity of Israel's story fulfilled in Jesus
- **Chapters:** 28
- **Source Text:** [[Acts (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Acts is the only narrative history of the early church in the NT canon — a sequel to the Gospel of Luke, picking up where the resurrection appearances leave off and ending with Paul preaching in Rome. Its full title in many manuscripts is "The Acts of the Apostles," though it might more accurately be called "The Acts of the Holy Spirit": the Spirit is mentioned over 70 times and is the ultimate protagonist, directing, empowering, and expanding the mission at every critical juncture.

The book's structure is provided by Acts 1:8 itself, which functions as the table of contents: the gospel moves outward in concentric rings — **Jerusalem** (chs. 1–7), **Judaea and Samaria** (chs. 8–12), and **the uttermost parts of the earth** (chs. 13–28). This geographical expansion mirrors a social and ethnic expansion: from Jews only, to Samaritans (Acts 8), to God-fearing Gentiles (Cornelius, Acts 10), to full Gentile inclusion (Acts 13ff.), validated by the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15).

**Acts has two great human protagonists:** Peter dominates chapters 1–12; Paul dominates chapters 13–28. This is not accidental — Luke presents them as parallel figures, each healing the lame, each raising the dead, each confronting sorcerers, each arrested and miraculously delivered. The parallelism validates Paul's apostolic authority alongside the original twelve.

**Chapters 1–7 (Jerusalem):** The Spirit descends at Pentecost on 120 gathered believers; Peter's sermon draws 3,000 converts (2:41). The early Jerusalem church is characterized by communal sharing, temple attendance, and miraculous signs. Opposition from the Sanhedrin grows. Stephen, one of the seven deacons, delivers the most panoramic OT-to-Christ sermon in the NT and becomes the first martyr — with Saul of Tarsus watching (7:58).

**Chapters 8–12 (Judaea and Samaria):** Saul's persecution scatters the Jerusalem church, inadvertently spreading the gospel. Philip evangelizes Samaria and the Ethiopian eunuch. Saul is converted on the Damascus Road (9:1–19) — the event recounted three times in Acts (chs. 9, 22, 26), underlining its centrality. Peter's vision at Joppa and encounter with Cornelius (chs. 10–11) break the Jewish-Gentile barrier decisively. The church at Antioch becomes the new mission hub — where believers are first called "Christians" (11:26).

**Chapters 13–28 (Uttermost Parts):** Paul's three missionary journeys extend the gospel west into Asia Minor, Macedonia, Greece, and finally to Rome itself:
- **First Journey** (13–14): Cyprus → Pisidian Antioch → Iconium → Lystra → Derbe → return
- **Second Journey** (15:36–18:22): Syria → Galatia → Troas (Macedonian vision) → Philippi → Thessalonica → Berea → Athens → Corinth (18 months) → return
- **Third Journey** (18:23–21:17): Ephesus (3 years) → Macedonia → Greece → Troas → Miletus → Jerusalem

Paul's arrest in Jerusalem (21:27), trials before the Sanhedrin, Felix, Festus, and Agrippa (chs. 22–26), and his dramatic sea voyage and shipwreck en route to Rome (chs. 27–28) fill the final section. The book ends with Paul under house arrest in Rome — "preaching the kingdom of God and teaching those things which concern the Lord Jesus Christ, with all confidence, no man forbidding him" (28:31). The open ending is deliberate: the mission continues.

## Notes & Reflections

- Acts 1:8 is not merely the commission — it is the structural outline of the entire book: the geographical progression from Jerusalem → Judaea/Samaria → uttermost parts is exactly the narrative arc of Acts 1–28
- The Holy Spirit appears 70+ times — "Acts of the Apostles" is a misleading title; the Spirit appoints, commissions, restrains, and empowers at every turn; Origen and Chrysostom both noted the Spirit is the real author of the mission
- Luke records three tellings of Paul's conversion (chs. 9, 22, 26), each with slightly different emphasis — a deliberate literary device to underscore its importance, not an error
- The "we" passages (16:10–17; 20:5–21:18; 27:1–28:16) — Luke abruptly shifts to first person, indicating he was physically present; this provides strong eyewitness authentication for those sections
- The abrupt ending (Paul preaching in Rome under house arrest with no verdict mentioned) is best explained by Acts being completed before Paul's trial outcome was known — ~AD 62; this also establishes a terminus ante quem for both Luke-Acts
- Gamaliel's principle (5:38–39) — "if this counsel or this work be of men, it will come to nought; but if it be of God, ye cannot overthrow it" — is presented neutrally; Luke may intend the reader to see its irony given subsequent history
- The Jerusalem Council (ch. 15) is the most consequential institutional decision in church history: the verdict (Gentiles accepted without circumcision, except for four concessions) determined the character of Christianity as a universal rather than Jewish-ethnic religion
