# Ephesians

> "For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: Not of works, lest any man should boast. For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus unto good works, which God hath before ordained that we should walk in them." — Ephesians 2:8–10 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Author:** [[Paul (Apostle)|Paul]], with [[Tychicus]] as the letter-carrier
- **Date Written:** ~AD 60–62, from Rome during Paul's first imprisonment
- **Key Themes:** The church as the body and bride of Christ, spiritual blessings in heavenly places, the mystery of Jew-Gentile unity, the armor of God, marriage as a picture of Christ and the church
- **Chapters:** 6
- **Source Text:** [[Ephesians (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Ephesians is the most elevated and cosmic of Paul's letters — often called the "Queen of the Epistles." It is also the least crisis-driven: unlike Galatians (fighting legalism), Corinthians (addressing chaos), or Colossians (countering a specific heresy), Ephesians has no specific problem to solve. It is a considered, majestic exposition of the church — its origin in eternity, its nature as the body of Christ, its calling in the world, and its resources for spiritual warfare.

Early manuscripts of the letter lack "in Ephesus" (1:1), suggesting it may have been a circular letter sent to multiple churches in the Lycus Valley or Asia Minor — Ephesus being the first major stop. This explains the relatively impersonal tone and the absence of the specific greetings characteristic of Paul's other letters.

**Chapters 1–3 are doctrinal** — a meditation on the church's identity "in Christ." The letter opens with a triple doxology: the Father chose us before the foundation of the world (1:4), the Son redeemed us through his blood (1:7), the Spirit sealed us as a down payment of inheritance (1:13–14). Paul prays for the eyes of the readers' hearts to be opened to understand the hope, the riches, and the surpassing power available to them (1:15–23). Chapter 2 moves from individual salvation (dead in trespasses, made alive by grace through faith — 2:1–10) to corporate salvation: Jew and Gentile, once divided by the "middle wall of partition," are now one new humanity in Christ (2:11–22). This "mystery" — hidden for ages and now revealed — is Paul's great theme in ch. 3: the church is the vehicle through which God's manifold wisdom is displayed to "principalities and powers in heavenly places" (3:10).

**Chapters 4–6 are ethical** — the "walk" that flows from the "wealth." Chapter 4 opens with the great imperative: "Walk worthy of the calling with which you were called." Then: unity in the body; the ascended Christ gave gifts (apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors-teachers) to equip the saints. Put off the old man, put on the new. Chapter 5 extends this to light vs. darkness, careful living, Spirit-filling, and the marriage theology: husbands love your wives as Christ loved the church; wives submit to husbands as to the Lord. Chapter 6 addresses children and parents, servants and masters, and then the great closing: put on the full armor of God for the spiritual warfare against "principalities, powers, rulers of darkness, and spiritual wickedness in high places."

## Notes & Reflections

- The letter's structure is the classic Pauline pattern of *indicative* (chapters 1–3: what God has done) before *imperative* (chapters 4–6: therefore live this way) — grace before ethics, identity before behavior
- "Principalities and powers in heavenly places" (3:10; 6:12) — Ephesians has the most developed angelology and demonology of Paul's letters; the spiritual realm is not incidental but the background of all human history
- The "mystery" (*mystērion*) of ch. 3 is not a secret now withheld but a secret now revealed — God's plan to unite Jew and Gentile in one body was *hidden* in OT times and is now disclosed through the gospel
- Ephesians 5:22–33 is the NT's most developed theology of marriage; the husband-wife relationship is explicitly likened to Christ's relationship with the church — making marriage a sacramental picture, not merely a social arrangement
- "Be filled with the Spirit" (5:18) — Greek present imperative: "keep on being filled" — an ongoing process, not a one-time event
