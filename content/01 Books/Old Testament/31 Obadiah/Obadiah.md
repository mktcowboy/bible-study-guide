# Obadiah

> "The pride of thine heart hath deceived thee, thou that dwellest in the clefts of the rock, whose habitation is high; that saith in his heart, Who shall bring me down to the ground?" — Obadiah 1:3 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Obadiah]] — the name means "servant of Yahweh"; the most common name in the OT (12 individuals with this name); no further biographical details are given
- **Date Written:** ~586 BC — the description of Edom gloating over Jerusalem's fall (vv. 10–14) fits the Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem; possibly the most immediately post-587 BC prophetic text in the OT
- **Key Themes:** Divine judgment on pride, the sin of betrayal between brothers, the Day of the LORD as universal reckoning, the principle of divine reciprocity ("as thou hast done, so shall it be done"), the restoration of Israel
- **Chapters:** 1
- **Source Text:** [[Obadiah (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Obadiah is the shortest book in the OT — 21 verses, a single chapter — and one of the most historically specific: it addresses Edom's betrayal of Judah during the Babylonian siege and sack of Jerusalem in 587–586 BC. The oracle is entirely directed at Edom, with a concluding promise of Israel's restoration.

The Edom-Israel relationship is one of the oldest and bitterest in biblical history. Edom is the nation descended from Esau; Israel from Jacob — twin brothers whose rivalry began in the womb (Genesis 25:22–23) and continued for over a millennium. The Edomites occupied the rugged territory southeast of Judah, in the Negev and the cliffs of Petra — hence "clefts of the rock" (v. 3). Despite this shared ancestry ("thy brother Jacob," v. 10), Edom stood by, gloated, and actively participated when Babylon destroyed Jerusalem.

**Verses 1–9:** Pride is the opening charge. Edom believed its mountain strongholds made it impregnable — "Who shall bring me down?" The LORD answers: though you soar as high as the eagle, I will bring you down (v. 4). Even her allies will turn against her; her wise men will be destroyed.

**Verses 10–14:** The specific indictment. On the day of catastrophe, Edom "stood on the other side" (*amad minneged*, v. 11) — a spectator to Jerusalem's fall. But the guilt escalates: Edom "rejoiced" (v. 12), "entered into the gate" of Jerusalem (v. 13), "stood in the crossway to cut off those that did escape" (v. 14), and "delivered up those that did remain" to Babylon (v. 14). The brotherly betrayal is complete.

**Verses 15–21:** The principle of reciprocity: "As thou hast done, it shall be done unto thee" (v. 15). The Day of the LORD will swallow all nations, including Edom. Meanwhile, the house of Jacob will be fire, the house of Joseph a flame, and the house of Esau will be stubble. The final verse promises the kingdom: "And saviours shall come up on mount Zion to judge the mount of Esau; and the kingdom shall be the LORD's" (v. 21).

Historically, Edom was absorbed into the Nabataean Arab kingdom by the 4th century BC and ceased to exist as a distinct people by the Roman period — exactly as Obadiah predicted.

## Notes & Reflections

- Obadiah is the only OT book entirely focused on Edom — 21 verses devoted to one nation's sin; the focus gives the book intensity
- The parallel with Jeremiah 49:7–16 is striking — several verses are nearly identical; scholars debate whether Obadiah drew from Jeremiah, Jeremiah from Obadiah, or both from a common oracle tradition
- "Stood on the other side" (v. 11) — passivity in the face of a brother's suffering is treated as active sin; neutrality is not innocent
- The six specific charges of vv. 12–14 are structured as "do not" commands in Hebrew — either prohibitions against doing it again, or a vivid way of saying "you did this, and this, and this"
- The final verse ("the kingdom shall be the LORD's") is one of the OT's most compact eschatological declarations — history is not random but moving toward divine sovereignty
