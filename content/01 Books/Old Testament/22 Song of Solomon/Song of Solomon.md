# Song of Solomon

> "Set me as a seal upon thine heart, as a seal upon thine arm: for love is strong as death; jealousy is cruel as the grave: the coals thereof are coals of fire, which hath a most vehement flame." — Song of Solomon 8:6 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Solomon]] — attributed in the opening line ("The song of songs, which is Solomon's" — 1:1); Solomon appears as a character within the poem; whether he is the sole author, the principal character, or the patron under whose name the poems were collected is debated
- **Date Written:** ~950 BC (Solomonic era) — if by Solomon; some scholars date a later compilation of older love poetry
- **Key Themes:** The goodness and beauty of human love within the covenant of marriage, desire as God's gift, the exclusivity of committed love, beauty and embodied creation as gifts, the allegorical dimension of God's love for Israel and Christ's love for the church
- **Chapters:** 8

## Summary

The Song of Solomon (also called Song of Songs, or Canticles) is the most misunderstood book in the canon — and perhaps the most avoided. It is a love poem between a bride (the Shulammite woman) and her beloved (the king/shepherd), written in some of the most sensuous, imagistic, and beautiful poetry in the ancient world. There is no explicit mention of God. There are no historical events. There is no law, prophecy, or instruction. It is, on its face, an erotic poem in praise of physical love between a man and a woman.

This is why it almost didn't make the Hebrew canon. Rabbi Akiba (c. AD 100) had to argue for its inclusion at the Council of Jamnia: "All the Writings are holy, but the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies." The canonical debate itself points to the book's intensity and the risk of reading it too nakedly.

**Two major interpretive traditions:**

*Allegorical:* Jewish tradition reads the poem as God's love for Israel; Christian tradition (going back to Origen in the 3rd century) reads it as Christ's love for the church or the soul. These traditions are ancient and not to be dismissed — the imagery of marriage as a metaphor for the God-people relationship runs throughout the OT (Hosea, Ezekiel 16, Isaiah 54, Jeremiah 2) and NT (Ephesians 5:25–33, Revelation 19:7–9).

*Literal:* The Song celebrates physical love within marriage as a divine gift. The detailed bodily praise (*wasf* poetry — a Near Eastern genre of physical praise), the mutual desire, the garden imagery (connecting to Eden), and the structure of courtship-consummation-commitment all suggest a concrete celebration of married love. The literal and allegorical readings are not mutually exclusive; the literal grounds and validates the allegorical.

**Structure (follows courtship to commitment):**
- **Chapters 1–3:** Initial longing, searching, the wedding procession
- **Chapters 4–5:** The wedding night; mutual praise; the bride's midnight search
- **Chapters 6–8:** Reunion, mature love, the great declaration

The woman's voice dominates the poem — she speaks more than the man, initiates, searches, and articulates desire with confidence. This is remarkable in an ancient Near Eastern context. Her voice is not passive or subordinate; she is an agent in the relationship.

## Notes & Reflections

- The threefold refrain "I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine" appears at 2:16, 6:3, and 7:10 — each time the formulation shifts slightly; at 6:3 it is "I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine"; at 7:10 it becomes "I am my beloved's, and his desire is toward me" — the focus shifts from mutual possession to the man's desire; many scholars see this as the poem's spiritual progression toward mature, secure love
- The garden imagery (4:12–16; 5:1; 6:2) deliberately echoes Genesis 2 — the enclosed, private garden of the bride is the restored Eden where man and woman can be fully known without shame; the Song presents sexual intimacy within marriage as the recovery of the pre-Fall state
- Rabbi Akiba's defense of the Song ("All the Writings are holy, but the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies") suggests he was reading it allegorically — the Holy of Holies is the place of most intimate divine presence; the Song is the text of most intimate divine-human love
- The woman's agency throughout the poem is notable — she initiates in 1:2–4, searches in 3:1–4 and 5:6–8, and makes the ultimate declaration in 8:6–7; she is not a passive object of the man's desire but an active participant; this is consistent with the pre-Fall equality of Genesis 2:23
- The allegorical and literal readings are not in competition; the literal reality of human love is the ground on which the allegorical truth stands; if human love is not genuinely good, it cannot function as a metaphor for divine love — and the Song insists it is very good
