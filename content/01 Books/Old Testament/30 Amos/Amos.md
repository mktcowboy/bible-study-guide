# Amos

> "But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream." — Amos 5:24 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Amos (Prophet)|Amos]] of Tekoa — a herdsman and fig cultivator from Tekoa in Judah (~10 miles south of Jerusalem); he explicitly denies being a professional prophet: "I was no prophet, neither was I a prophet's son; but I was an herdman, and a gatherer of sycomore fruit" (7:14)
- **Date Written:** ~760–750 BC — during the reign of Uzziah of Judah and Jeroboam II of Israel; "two years before the earthquake" (1:1), a seismic event confirmed by archaeology (~760 BC at Hazor)
- **Key Themes:** Social justice for the poor, the hollowness of worship divorced from ethics, the coming Day of the LORD as judgment (not celebration), the remnant and Davidic restoration, God's sovereign rule over all nations
- **Chapters:** 9
- **Source Text:** [[Amos (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Amos is the great social justice prophet of the OT — a farmer from the south sent north to Israel during the reign of Jeroboam II, when the northern kingdom was at the peak of its political and economic prosperity. Behind that prosperity lay systemic injustice: the poor were sold for a pair of sandals (2:6), the grain was trampled (5:11), dishonest scales cheated the needy (8:5–6), and the rich reclined on beds of ivory drinking wine in bowls (6:4) while the afflicted went unnoticed. Meanwhile, the people came to Bethel and Gilgal for worship — and Amos delivers God's verdict: *he despises it*.

**Chapters 1–2: Eight oracles against the nations.** Amos begins with a device calculated to win his northern audience: he pronounces judgment on the surrounding nations (Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon, Moab), then on Judah — before turning on Israel itself with the longest and most devastating oracle of all. The literary trap is sprung: Israel has been nodding agreement through the condemnation of her neighbors, and suddenly she is the target.

**Chapters 3–6: Three "hear this word" oracles against Israel.** The specific sins of Israel are indicted: selling the poor for sandals, sexual exploitation at the altar (2:7–8), rejecting the Nazirites, silencing the prophets (2:12). Chapter 4 lists five specific disciplines God sent — famine, drought, blight, locusts, plague — and after each: "yet have ye not returned unto me, saith the LORD." Chapter 5 contains the liturgical heart of Amos: "I hate, I despise your feast days, and I will not smell in your solemn assemblies... But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream" (5:21, 24). Chapter 6 indicts "them that are at ease in Zion" — the complacent ruling class blind to the coming disaster.

**Chapters 7–9: Five visions.** Locusts (7:1–3), fire (7:4–6), the plumb line (7:7–9), summer fruit (8:1–3), and the LORD at the altar (9:1). Between the third and fourth visions, Amaziah the priest of Bethel tells Amos to go home to Judah and prophesy there — Amos replies that he was not a professional prophet; God called him from the flock. The vision of summer fruit (8:1–3) plays on the Hebrew word *qayits* (summer fruit) and *qets* (end) — "The end is come upon my people Israel." The fifth vision (9:1) sees the LORD himself at the altar commanding judgment with no escape.

**The closing restoration (9:11–15):** Like all the prophets, Amos ends with hope. The "tabernacle of David that is fallen" will be raised up; Israel will be restored to her land and the nations will bear God's name. This passage is quoted by James at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15:16–17) as scriptural warrant for Gentile inclusion in the church.

## Notes & Reflections

- Amos is the prototype of the "lay prophet" — he insists he has no prophetic credentials, family, or school; God simply took him from the flock and said "go"; this validates prophetic authority as divine appointment, not institutional affiliation
- "You only have I known" (3:2) — the word *yada* (know) here is covenantal knowledge, not mere awareness; election is the greatest privilege and the greatest accountability; the more light given, the more responsibility
- Amos 5:21–24 is the classic OT text on the relationship between worship and ethics — not that worship is wrong, but that worship without corresponding justice is an offense; the language is visceral: "I *hate*, I *despise*, I will *not smell*"
- The earthquake "two years before" (1:1) is likely the same earthquake mentioned in Zechariah 14:5; archaeology at Hazor has found an earthquake destruction layer dated to ~760 BC, confirming the historical accuracy of the book's opening date reference
- Amos 9:11–12 at the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15): James's exegesis is significant — the "tabernacle of David" being rebuilt is interpreted as the inclusive community of Jesus followers drawn from all nations; the Davidic restoration is happening now through the gospel
