# Malachi

> "Behold, I will send my messenger, and he shall prepare the way before me: and the Lord, whom ye seek, shall suddenly come to his temple, even the messenger of the covenant, whom ye delight in: behold, he shall come, saith the LORD of hosts." — Malachi 3:1 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Malachi]] — the name means "my messenger" (cf. 3:1); may be a title rather than a personal name; the Septuagint renders it as "his messenger"; no biographical details beyond the name
- **Date Written:** ~450–430 BC — the latest of the OT prophets; the temple is rebuilt (1:10), the governorship is established (1:8), and the same abuses Nehemiah addresses (intermarriage, neglected tithes) are present; likely contemporary with or just before Nehemiah's second term (~433 BC)
- **Key Themes:** Covenant faithfulness when religion has become routine, the corruption of the priesthood, divorce and marriage covenant, the tithe and material devotion, the coming Day of the LORD, the forerunner Elijah, the "sun of righteousness" with healing in his wings
- **Chapters:** 4
- **Source Text:** [[Malachi (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Malachi is the last prophetic voice of the OT — the final word before 400 years of prophetic silence. He addresses a community that has returned from exile, rebuilt the temple, and... drifted into spiritual complacency. The crisis is not acute persecution or heresy but something more mundane and harder to resist: going through the motions. The priests offer blind and lame animals (1:8). The people have stopped bringing tithes (3:8–10). Men are divorcing their Jewish wives to marry foreign women (2:14–16). Prayer has become mechanical. Doubt has crept in: "What is the use of serving God?" (3:14).

Malachi's signature literary device is the **disputation oracle** — a question-and-answer format in which God states a truth, the people dispute it ("But ye say, Wherein?"), and God answers with specific evidence. This back-and-forth appears six times, making Malachi the most dialogic of the prophets. It creates a confrontational intimacy: God is not delivering distant oracles but answering actual objections.

**The six disputations:**
1. "I have loved you" → "Wherein hast thou loved us?" (1:2–5) — the Edom/Esau contrast proves it
2. "Ye have despised my name" → "Wherein have we despised?" (1:6–2:9) — defiled offerings and corrupt priesthood
3. "Ye have wearied the LORD" → "Wherein have we wearied him?" (2:17–3:5) — saying evil is good; God will come to judge
4. "Return unto me" → "Wherein shall we return?" (3:6–12) — the tithes; test God in this
5. "Your words are stout against me" → "What have we spoken against thee?" (3:13–4:3) — "It is vain to serve God"; but the faithful will be vindicated
6. (Implicit disputation resolution) — Remember the Law; Elijah is coming (4:4–6)

**Chapters 1–2** concentrate on priestly failure. The priests offer polluted bread and defective animals they would never dare present to a Persian governor (1:8). The Levitical covenant has been broken (2:8). Divorce has become casual (2:14–16: "For the LORD, the God of Israel, saith that he hateth putting away" — one of the OT's clearest statements on divorce).

**Chapter 3** opens with the great promise: before the Day of the LORD, God will send a messenger to prepare the way (3:1) — quoted by all four Gospels as referring to John the Baptist. The Lord will "suddenly come to his temple." The tithes passage (3:8–10) contains the only explicit invitation in Scripture to "test God": "Bring ye all the tithes into the storehouse... and prove me now herewith, saith the LORD of hosts, if I will not open you the windows of heaven."

**Chapter 4** closes the OT with a two-sided image: the proud and wicked will be stubble; the sun of righteousness will rise with healing in his wings for those who fear God's name. And the final command: remember the Law of Moses. And the final promise: Elijah will come before the great and terrible day of the LORD, to turn the hearts of fathers to children and children to fathers — or the land will be struck with a curse.

The last word of the OT in the Hebrew Bible is "curse" (*cherem*). The next voice in the story is John the Baptist.

## Notes & Reflections

- Malachi 1:11 is one of the OT's most remarkable texts — "my name shall be great among the Gentiles" and "pure offering" offered to God in every place; Justin Martyr and Irenaeus read this as a prophecy of the Eucharist (the universal Christian worship offering); it is at minimum a prophecy of Gentile worship of the God of Israel
- The "messenger" of 3:1 and "Elijah" of 4:5 are identified in all four Gospels and Acts as John the Baptist — one of the clearest predictive fulfillments in the entire NT; Jesus himself makes the identification in Matthew 11:14
- Malachi 3:10 — "prove me now herewith" — is the only place in Scripture where God explicitly invites testing; the usual prohibition against testing God (Deuteronomy 6:16) concerns testing his power or patience; here he invites the test of his financial faithfulness
- The Hebrew OT closes at 4:6 with the word *cherem* (curse/destruction); the Greek LXX rearranges the minor prophets so Malachi is not last; the Hebrew order, with this ominous ending, leaves the OT in suspension — "or else" — resolved only by the coming of the one who bore the curse (Galatians 3:13)
- *Segullah* (3:17) — "special treasure" or "prized possession" — is a covenant term used for Israel in Exodus 19:5 and Deuteronomy 7:6; Malachi reassures the faithful remnant that even when serving God seems pointless, God has not forgotten them

- **Preceded by:** [[Zechariah]] in canonical order

- **Bridge to NT:** Malachi ends with the promise of Elijah; Matthew begins with John the Baptist — the OT closes and the NT opens on the same hinge
