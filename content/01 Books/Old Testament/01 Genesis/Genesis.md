# Genesis

> "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." — Genesis 1:1 (KJV)

## Overview

**Author:** Traditionally attributed to [[Moses]], though the question of Mosaic authorship has been debated by scholars. Jewish and Christian tradition uniformly ascribe the book to Moses, who may have compiled earlier sources and oral traditions.

**Date of Writing:** Approximately 1400s BC (during the Exodus period), though some scholars propose a later date. The events recorded span from Creation to the settlement of the Israelites in Egypt — thousands of years.

**Original Title:** *Bereshit* (בְּרֵאשִׁית) — Hebrew for "In the beginning," taken from the first word of the text. The title "Genesis" comes from the Greek *Genesis* (γένεσις), meaning "origin," "birth," or "creation," used in the Septuagint.

**Key Themes:**
- **Creation** — God as the sovereign Creator of all things
- **Fall and Sin** — The entrance of sin into the world and its devastating consequences
- **Covenant** — God's gracious promises to redeem humanity through chosen individuals
- **Providence** — God's sovereign care and direction of human history
- **Judgment and Mercy** — God's justice against sin alongside His compassion
- **Seed of the Woman** — The proto-evangelium (first gospel promise) in Genesis 3:15
- **Electing Grace** — God's choice of individuals and nations for His redemptive purposes
- **Family and Lineage** — The genealogies tracing the line of promise from Adam to Israel

## Summary

[[Genesis]] is the foundational book of both the Hebrew Bible and the Christian Old Testament, serving as the introduction to the entire biblical narrative. The book's name means "beginnings," and it covers the origins of the cosmos, humanity, sin, nations, and the nation of Israel.

The first eleven chapters (often called the "Primeval History") deal with universal themes: the [[Creation]] of the heavens and earth, the [[Fall of Man]] and introduction of sin, the escalating wickedness of humanity, the judgment of [[The Flood]], the scattering of nations at the [[Tower of Babel]], and the genealogies connecting Adam to Abraham.

Chapters 12–50 shift focus to the patriarchs — the founding fathers of Israel. The narrative follows [[Abraham]], called out of [[Ur]] by God's promise to make him a great nation; his son [[Isaac]], through whom the covenant continues; [[Jacob]] (renamed Israel), who receives the twelve tribes; and [[Joseph]], whose wisdom and faithfulness save the family during famine, setting the stage for Israel's sojourn in [[Egypt]].

Throughout, Genesis establishes the pattern of God's redemptive work: despite human failure and rebellion, God faithfully fulfills His covenant promises, preparing the way for the coming Messiah.

## Study Notes

- Genesis contains both historical narrative and genealogical records, blending theology with history
- The book is structured around ten "toledot" (generations/account of) sections, each introduced by the phrase "these are the generations of..."
- The covenant theme runs throughout: Noahic covenant (9:8–17), Abrahamic covenant (12, 15, 17), and reaffirmations with Isaac and Jacob
- Genesis establishes the pattern of divine election: Seth over Cain, Noah over his generation, Shem over Ham, Abraham over Lot, Isaac over Ishmael, Jacob over Esau, Joseph over his brothers, Judah as the royal line
- The book ends with Israel in Egypt, setting the stage for Exodus — demonstrating that God's promises endure even in difficult circumstances
