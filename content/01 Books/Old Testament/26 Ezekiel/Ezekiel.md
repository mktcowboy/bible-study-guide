# Ezekiel

> "A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you: and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you an heart of flesh." — Ezekiel 36:26 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Ezekiel (Prophet)|Ezekiel]] son of Buzi — a priest as well as a prophet (1:3); exiled to Babylon with the first deportation in 597 BC; received visions beside the river Chebar (the Grand Canal near Nippur); his wife died as a sign act (24:15–18); active 593–571 BC
- **Date Written:** ~593–571 BC — the most precisely dated prophetic book in the OT; 14 dates are given in the text, spanning 22 years
- **Key Themes:** The mobile glory of God (not confined to any building), idolatry as spiritual adultery, individual moral accountability, the new covenant (heart of flesh), the dry bones resurrection, the eschatological return of God's glory, the new temple and the river of life
- **Chapters:** 48
- **Source Text:** [[Ezekiel (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Ezekiel is the most visionary, theatrically bizarre, and symbolically dense of all the prophetic books. A priest in exile who becomes a prophet, Ezekiel is commanded to perform strange sign acts (lying on his side for 430 days, cooking food over dung, shaving his head with a sword), receives the most elaborate heavenly vision in the OT (the chariot-throne, *merkabah*, of chapters 1 and 10), and delivers some of the most emotionally intense oracles in all of Scripture. At the center of everything is a single driving concern: the glory of God (*kavod Yahweh*).

The book's architecture is built around that glory's movement: in chapters 8–11, the glory of God departs from the temple (corrupted by idolatry) — step by step, reluctantly, east and east again. The judgment narratives of chapters 1–24 and the oracles against the nations of chapters 25–32 fill the space of its absence. Then in chapters 40–48, the glory returns — streaming back from the east into the new eschatological temple, filling it, and sending out from it a river that grows deeper and deeper, healing even the Dead Sea.

**Chapters 1–3 (The Inaugural Vision and Commission):** Ezekiel sees God's throne-chariot — four living creatures (human/lion/ox/eagle faces), spinning wheels within wheels full of eyes, a crystal expanse, a sapphire throne, and on it the appearance of a man surrounded by fire and rainbow — "the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the LORD" (1:28). He falls face down. God commissions him as a watchman: whether the people hear or refuse, they will know a prophet was among them.

**Chapters 4–24 (Judgment Oracles and Sign Acts):** The most sustained sequence of prophetic sign acts in any biblical book — siege models, lying on sides, rationed food, shaved head, baggage carried out through a hole in the wall, not mourning the death of his wife. These enacted parables communicate the reality of coming siege and exile with an intensity that words alone cannot carry. The famous extended allegories of Jerusalem as an unfaithful wife (ch. 16) and as two sisters (Oholah and Oholibah — Israel and Judah, ch. 23) are the most explicit in the prophets. Chapters 8–11 show the divine glory departing from the temple in stages — the most theologically significant narrative in the book.

**Chapters 25–32 (Oracles Against the Nations):** Ammon, Moab, Edom, Philistia (25), Tyre — the longest and most elaborate oracle against a foreign city in the prophets (26–28), including the "prince of Tyre" lament that has been read as a depiction of Satan's original glory and fall (28:11–19); Egypt (29–32). These establish God's sovereignty over all nations, not just Israel.

**Chapters 33–39 (The Restoration Oracles):** The book pivots. Chapter 33 restates the watchman's role now that Jerusalem has fallen (the news arrives at 33:21). Chapters 34–37 are the great restoration promises: God will be Israel's shepherd (34 — "I will search for my sheep"), the mountains of Israel will flourish (35–36), a new heart and new spirit will be given (36:26), and the dry bones will live again (37:1–14). The valley of dry bones — "can these bones live?" — is the book's most famous passage: a national resurrection vision that becomes in the NT the paradigm of spiritual regeneration and bodily resurrection. Chapters 38–39 describe the Gog-Magog apocalyptic battle.

**Chapters 40–48 (The New Temple Vision):** In precise architectural detail Ezekiel is taken on a tour of the eschatological temple — its gates, courts, chambers, altar. The glory of God returns (43:1–5) by the same eastern gate through which it departed. The river of life flows eastward from under the threshold (47:1–12), growing deeper as it goes, healing the waters of the sea, producing trees whose leaves are "for medicine" (healing of the nations). The final verse: "the name of the city from that day shall be, the LORD is there" (48:35, *Yahweh-Shammah*).

## Notes & Reflections

- "Son of man" is used 93 times in Ezekiel — more than in all other OT books combined; Jesus adopts this as his own primary self-designation (used 80+ times in the Gospels), drawing on both the Ezekiel usage (human frailty before divine majesty) and Daniel 7:13 (the son of man who receives universal dominion)
- The departure of God's glory (chs. 8–11) is one of the most theologically significant — and most heartbreaking — sequences in the OT; the glory does not leave suddenly but reluctantly, pausing at each threshold as if hoping the people will repent; the absence of divine presence is the greatest tragedy of the exile
- Ezekiel 37 (dry bones) is often read individualistically ("our God can raise me from the dead") but its explicit meaning is national: "These bones are the whole house of Israel... I will open your graves... and bring you into the land of Israel" (37:11–12); it is the vision of Israel's national resurrection, which the NT applies typologically to spiritual regeneration and bodily resurrection
- The new temple vision (chs. 40–48) has been interpreted three ways: (1) a literal rebuilt temple for the millennial kingdom (dispensational); (2) an ideal temple that functions symbolically like OT tabernacle instructions — showing what perfect worship looks like (Calvin); (3) a vision of the church and/or new Jerusalem (already fulfilled); the New Jerusalem of Revelation 21–22 draws explicitly from Ezekiel 47–48 while transforming the temple imagery ("I saw no temple therein" — Revelation 21:22)
- Ezekiel's *merkabah* (throne-chariot) vision became the foundation of Jewish mystical speculation (Merkabah mysticism, Kabbalistic traditions); the rabbis debated whether it was safe to study publicly; its influence on Revelation 4–5 and Paul's "third heaven" experience (2 Corinthians 12:2) is widely recognized
