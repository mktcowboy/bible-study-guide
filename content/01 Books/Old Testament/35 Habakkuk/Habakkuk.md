# Habakkuk

> "Although the fig tree shall not blossom, neither shall fruit be in the vines; the labour of the olive shall fail, and the fields shall yield no meat; the flock shall be cut off from the fold, and there shall be no herd in the stalls: Yet I will rejoice in the LORD, I will joy in the God of my salvation." — Habakkuk 3:17–18 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Habakkuk (Prophet)|Habakkuk]] — the name may derive from a Hebrew root meaning "to embrace" or possibly from an Akkadian plant name; no biographical details are given beyond the title "the prophet"
- **Date Written:** ~605 BC — the oracle about Babylon rising as a judgment instrument fits the period immediately after the Battle of Carchemish (605 BC), when Babylon decisively defeated Assyria and Egypt and became the dominant Near Eastern power
- **Key Themes:** The theodicy of divine justice, the mystery of God using a more wicked nation to judge a less wicked one, faith as the proper response to unanswered questions, the sovereignty of God over history, the final triumph of divine glory
- **Chapters:** 3
- **Source Text:** [[Habakkuk (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Habakkuk is unlike any other prophetic book. Most prophets speak God's word *to* the people; Habakkuk speaks the people's complaint *to* God and records God's answer. It is structured as a dialogue — a bold, honest, back-and-forth wrestling with the justice of God that reads more like Job than like Isaiah. The book is short (three chapters, 56 verses) but contains one of the most quoted verses in the NT: "The just shall live by his faith" (2:4).

**The first complaint (1:2–4):** Habakkuk's opening question is pastoral: Why do you not hear when I cry? Why do you tolerate injustice and violence within Judah? The law is paralyzed; the wicked surround the righteous. The prophet is not asking about abstract theodicy — he is watching Judah collapse morally and crying out for divine intervention.

**God's first answer (1:5–11):** The answer is worse than the question. God says he is about to act — by raising up the Babylonians (*Chaldeans*). They are described in terrifying terms: swift as leopards, fierce as evening wolves, who gather captives like sand. "Their might is their god" (1:11). The instrument God will use to judge Judah is a nation far more violent, far more wicked than Judah.

**The second complaint (1:12–2:1):** Now the theodicy gets sharper. Habakkuk acknowledges that God is eternal and holy and that he uses Babylon as his instrument — but how can he look on the treacherous and remain silent while the wicked swallows up one more righteous than himself (1:13)? He stations himself on the rampart, waiting to see what God will say (2:1).

**God's second answer (2:2–20):** Write the vision plainly; it awaits its appointed time. "The just shall live by his faith" (2:4). The proud will fail; the righteous will endure by trusting God even when they cannot see what he is doing. Five "woe" oracles follow (2:6–20), indicting Babylon's specific sins: plunder, covetousness, bloodshed, debauchery, and idolatry. The section closes with the most majestic silence in all of prophecy: "But the LORD is in his holy temple: let all the earth keep silence before him" (2:20).

**Habakkuk's psalm (chapter 3):** The dialogue ends; the prophet responds not with a second question but with a prayer-psalm. He recalls God's past mighty acts (the Exodus, the wilderness, Sinai) in trembling awe. The closing verses (3:17–19) are among the most remarkable in the OT: even if everything fails — crops, livestock, harvest — "Yet I will rejoice in the LORD, I will joy in the God of my salvation." This is not blind optimism; it is faith that has wrestled through the dark night and emerged trusting anyway.

The phrase "the just shall live by faith" (2:4) is quoted three times in the NT (Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11, Hebrews 10:38) — the foundation of the Reformation doctrine of *sola fide*.

- Isaiah 26:20–21 — "The just shall live" and God's judgment of the earth
- Deuteronomy 32:35 — "Vengeance is mine" — God's justice over nations (Hab. 2:6–20 root)

**New Testament:**
- Romans 1:17 — Paul quotes Habakkuk 2:4 as the thesis of his letter; "the just shall live by faith" — the righteousness from God received through faith
- Galatians 3:11 — Habakkuk 2:4 used to show the law does not justify; justification is by faith
- Hebrews 10:38 — Habakkuk 2:4 applied to endurance: "my just one shall live by faith; if he shrinks back, my soul has no pleasure in him"
- Acts 13:41 — Paul quotes Habakkuk 1:5 ("I work a work in your days which ye shall in no wise believe") to warn his synagogue audience

## Notes & Reflections

- Habakkuk is the only prophetic book structured as a dialogue between prophet and God — the questions are not doubts to be rebuked but honest wrestling that God himself engages and honors
- "The just shall live by his faith" (2:4) — in Hebrew, *emunah* means faithfulness, steadfastness, reliability — not merely intellectual assent; the meaning is "the righteous one will be kept alive through his steadfast trust"; Paul's use in Romans and Galatians extends this to justification by faith
- Habakkuk 2:20 — "Let all the earth keep silence before him" — is the silence of awe before divine sovereignty; having asked his two questions and received his answers, the prophet has nothing left to say before the holy God; this silence is resolution
- The five woes of chapter 2 against Babylon function as a foretaste of Babylon's destruction — the very instrument of God's judgment will itself be judged; the pattern repeats in Revelation 17–18
- Chapter 3 is one of the most powerful examples of *lament-to-trust* movement in the Bible — Habakkuk does not explain how God's plan makes sense; he simply chooses to trust the God whose past acts he has rehearsed; faith is not the end of questions but the decision to trust God despite unanswered ones
