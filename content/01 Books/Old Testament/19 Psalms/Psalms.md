# Psalms

> "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want." — Psalm 23:1 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Multiple — [[David]] (73 attributed psalms), [[Asaph]] (12), [[Sons of Korah]] (11), [[Solomon]] (2: Psalms 72, 127), [[Moses]] (1: Psalm 90 — the oldest), [[Heman]] (1: Psalm 88), [[Ethan]] (1: Psalm 89), anonymous (49)
- **Date Written:** ~1400–400 BC — spanning the entire OT period from Moses to the post-exilic restoration; the final compilation was likely completed in the Persian period (~400 BC); the Psalter is therefore a 1,000-year anthology
- **Key Themes:** The full range of human experience before God; honest lament as prayer; the messianic king; Torah as delight; Zion as God's dwelling; the universal praise of all creation; death and resurrection hope
- **Chapters:** 150
- **Source Text:** [[Psalms (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Psalms is Israel's prayer book and hymnal — 150 poems spanning the full emotional and theological spectrum of human life before God. It is the most quoted OT book in the NT (cited 400+ times), the book Jesus knew most intimately, and the collection the church has never stopped praying.

The Hebrew title is *Tehillim* ("praises"), but the collection contains far more lament than praise — approximately 67 lament psalms. This is canonical theology: God has included speech of complaint, despair, and even accusation in his inspired Scripture. The Psalms teach that honesty before God — including anger, doubt, abandonment, and desperation — is not unfaith. It is prayer. Brueggemann's categories of orientation, disorientation, and reorientation map the Psalter's emotional arc: psalms that affirm an ordered world, psalms that shatter it, and psalms that find a new order on the other side of loss.

**The Five Books (mirroring the Pentateuch):** The Psalter is divided into five books, each ending with a doxology. The arc moves from covenant crisis and individual lament (Books 1–3) through penitential confession (Book 4) to a crescendo of universal praise (Book 5, ending in five pure Hallelujah psalms 146–150). If the Torah is God speaking to Israel, the Psalms are Israel speaking back — together they form the covenant dialogue.

**The Anatomy of a Lament Psalm:** Most individual lament psalms share a recognizable structure. Psalm 13 is a textbook example in only 6 verses: (1) *Address and complaint* — "How long, O LORD? wilt thou forget me for ever?" (13:1); (2) *Petition* — "Consider and hear me, O LORD my God" (13:3); (3) *Motivation* — why God should act ("lest I sleep the sleep of death," 13:3); (4) *Confession of trust* — "But I have trusted in thy mercy" (13:5); (5) *Vow of praise* — "I will sing unto the LORD, because he hath dealt bountifully with me" (13:6). The movement from lament to praise is not cheap resolution — it is the journey through honest suffering to renewed trust, without skipping the pain.

## Hebrew Poetic Devices

| Device | Description | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Synonymous Parallelism** | Second line restates the first in different words | "The heavens declare the glory of God; / and the firmament sheweth his handywork" (19:1) |
| **Antithetic Parallelism** | Second line contrasts the first | "For the LORD knoweth the way of the righteous: / but the way of the ungodly shall perish" (1:6) |
| **Synthetic Parallelism** | Second line extends or completes the first | "I will praise thee, O LORD, with my whole heart; / I will shew forth all thy marvellous works" (9:1) |
| **Chiasm** | A-B-C-B'-A' inverted structure | Psalm 51:1 — "Have mercy upon me, O God, / according to thy lovingkindness: / according unto the multitude of thy tender mercies / blot out my transgressions" |
| **Acrostic** | Each verse or section begins with successive Hebrew letters | Psalms 9–10; 25; 34; 37; 111–112; 119; 145 — Psalm 119 uses all 22 letters, 8 verses each |
| ***Selah*** | Musical/liturgical notation; meaning uncertain — may indicate pause, crescendo, or change in musical mode; appears 71 times | Throughout Psalms; densely in Pss. 3–9, 24, 32, 46 |
| **Inclusio** | A psalm begins and ends with the same phrase | Psalm 8: "O LORD our Lord, how excellent is thy name in all the earth!" (8:1 and 8:9) |

## Cross-References

**Old Testament:**
- 2 Samuel 22 — David's song nearly identical to Psalm 18; the same poem appears in both narrative and liturgical form
- Exodus 15 — The Song of the Sea; antecedent to the Hallelujah psalms and Enthronement psalms
- Jeremiah 11; 15; 17; 20 — Jeremiah's laments (*confessions*) use the lament psalm structure and often echo specific psalms
- Isaiah 40–55 — The Servant Songs echo Psalms 22 and 69; Isaiah 53 and Psalm 22 describe the same suffering

**New Testament:**
- Luke 24:44 — "All things must be fulfilled which were written in the law of Moses, and in the prophets, and in the psalms, concerning me" — Jesus explicitly names the Psalms as messianic Scripture
- Matthew 27:35–46 — The crucifixion narrative fulfills Psalm 22 in sequence: forsakenness (22:1), scorned (22:7–8), lots cast (22:18), bulls of Bashan surrounding (22:12)
- Matthew 26:30 — Jesus sings the Hallel (Psalms 113–118) with his disciples at the Last Supper before Gethsemane
- Luke 23:46 — Jesus's last words quote Psalm 31:5: "Into thine hand I commit my spirit"
- Acts 2:25–35 — Peter's Pentecost sermon is built on Psalms 16 and 110; the resurrection of Jesus is proved from the Psalter
- Romans 3:10–18 — Paul strings together six psalm quotations to prove universal human sinfulness
- Hebrews 1 — Four psalm quotations in seven verses (1:5 = Ps. 2:7; 1:6 = Ps. 97:7; 1:7 = Ps. 104:4; 1:8–9 = Ps. 45:6–7; 1:10–12 = Ps. 102:25–27; 1:13 = Ps. 110:1) to establish Christ's superiority to angels
- Revelation 4–5 — The heavenly worship draws from Psalms 47, 93, 96–99; "Worthy is the Lamb" echoes Psalms 145–150

## Notes & Reflections

- **The Psalms are the most-quoted OT book in the NT** — 400+ citations or allusions; every NT author draws from the Psalter; Paul quotes it more than any other OT book in Romans alone; the Psalms are foundational to NT theology of Christ, the church, and the new creation
- **Psalm 22 is the most prophetically precise chapter in the OT:** Written by David c. 1000 BC, it describes in sequence: being forsaken by God (22:1 → Matthew 27:46); scorned and mocked ("he trusted in God" — 22:8 → Matthew 27:43); surrounded by enemies described as bulls and dogs (22:12–13, 16); hands and feet pierced (22:16); bones out of joint, heart melted (22:14); garments divided by lot (22:18 → John 19:24); dying of thirst (22:15 → John 19:28). Crucifixion was not an existing execution method when David wrote this
- **Psalm 110 is the single most-cited OT verse in the NT** — quoted or alluded to 33 times; Jesus uses it to silence the Pharisees' question about whose son the Messiah is (Matthew 22:41–46); it establishes the Priest-King as both David's descendant and David's Lord — the theological paradox only the Incarnation resolves
- **The imprecatory psalms** (35; 55; 58; 69; 83; 109; 137; 140) are a pastoral and theological challenge: these psalms call down divine judgment on enemies in graphic, even violent, language. Three responses: (1) they are sub-Christian and should be excised (rejected by the church); (2) they express honest human anger that God receives and redirects — the alternative is that we suppress rage, not pray it; (3) they are ultimately messianic — the righteous sufferer calling for God to vindicate justice, reaching fullest expression at the cross. The imprecatory psalms should be prayed Christologically: the one who was unjustly condemned is the one through whom God vindicates all righteousness
- **Psalm 88 stands alone** — the only psalm that ends in darkness with no resolution; "darkness is my closest friend" (88:18) is the final word; Heman's unresolved cry has never been explained away by the canon that preserved it; it tells the truth that some seasons of faith end without resolution, and that this too is prayer
- ***Selah* appears 71 times** — its meaning is debated; most likely a musical instruction to pause, perhaps for a moment of reflection or an instrumental interlude; its placement at high-tension moments (after a striking declaration or at a pivot between lament and trust) suggests it marks where the speaker or listener should pause and let the weight land
- **The Songs of Ascent (Psalms 120–134)** are a distinct pilgrimage collection — 15 psalms sung by Israelites as they traveled up to Jerusalem for the three annual feasts (Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles); each begins with "A Song of Ascents"; they move from the anxiety of pilgrimage (120: "woe is me that I sojourn in Mesech") to the joy of arrival (122: "I was glad when they said unto me, Let us go into the house of the LORD") to confident rest (131) and priestly blessing (134); they are a spiritual journey compressed into 15 poems
- **Psalm 119 is the most elaborate literary structure in the Bible** — 176 verses, 22 sections (one per Hebrew letter), each section containing exactly 8 verses, every verse using one of 8 Hebrew synonyms for God's word (*Torah*, *dabar*, *mitzvah*, *choq*, *mishpat*, *piqqud*, *edah*, *imrah*); the entire poem is a sustained argument that Torah is not burden but treasure, lamp, honey, and life — meditation on Deuteronomy 6:4–9 extended across a lifetime
- **The Psalter's arc is theological:** The movement from lament-heavy Books 1–3 to praise-crescendo Books 4–5 is not accidental editorial arrangement — it is a theological statement about the direction of history and prayer; lament is not the final word; praise is; the canon itself is oriented toward Psalm 150

## Sequential Connections

- **Preceded by:** [[Job]] in canonical order — both are wisdom/poetry; Job addresses suffering without resolution; Psalms gives suffering a voice and a direction (toward God)
- **Companion to:** [[Proverbs]] — together they form the devotional core of OT wisdom; Psalms = vertical (prayer to God), Proverbs = horizontal (wisdom for life among people)
- **The Wisdom corpus:** With [[Job]] and [[Ecclesiastes]], forms the complete OT wrestling with life's hardest questions
- **Most quoted in:** Every NT book draws from Psalms; especially [[Matthew]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]], [[Romans]], [[Hebrews]], [[Revelation]]
- **Liturgical use:** Songs of Ascent (120–134) sung on pilgrimage to Jerusalem; Hallel (113–118) at Passover and the Last Supper; Psalms 95–100 at Sabbath morning services; Psalm 23 at funerals and the Lord's Supper across church history
