# Micah

> "He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good; and what doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?" — Micah 6:8 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Micah]] of Moresheth-Gath — a rural town in the Shephelah (lowlands) of Judah, ~25 miles southwest of Jerusalem; his rural, peasant background contrasts with Isaiah's urban, court-connected ministry
- **Date Written:** ~735–700 BC — during the reigns of Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah (1:1); a contemporary of [[Isaiah]] and [[Hosea]]
- **Key Themes:** Social justice for the oppressed poor, the corruption of religious and political leadership, the Bethlehem Messiah, the universal reign of God from Zion, the incomparable mercy of God
- **Chapters:** 7

## Summary

Micah is a prophet from the countryside indicting the city — specifically Jerusalem and Samaria — for the sins of their leadership. He is a rural prophet who understands what urban exploitation looks like from the ground: landlords who seize fields in the night (2:1–2), priests who teach for hire, prophets who preach for money (3:11), merchants who use dishonest scales (6:10–11). His famous Micah 6:8 is not an abstraction; it emerges from a specific social crisis where justice is absent, mercy has been commodified, and pride has replaced humility before God.

**Chapters 1–3: Judgment oracles.** Micah opens with God himself coming out of his holy temple — a theophany that melts mountains and fills valleys. The cause? The transgressions of Jacob and Judah. Samaria will become a heap of rubble (1:6 — fulfilled 722 BC); Jerusalem will face the same (1:12 — fulfilled in Micah's time by Sennacherib's invasion and later by Babylon). Chapters 2–3 are Micah's direct confrontation with those who devour his people: the greedy landowners, the false prophets who tailor their messages to whoever pays (2:11: "If a man walking in wind and falsehood says 'I will preach of wine and beer,' he would be the preacher for this people"), and the rulers who "hate good and love evil." The devastating climax of chapter 3: "Therefore shall Zion for your sake be plowed as a field, and Jerusalem shall become heaps" — the only pre-exilic prophet to explicitly predict Jerusalem's destruction.

**Chapters 4–5: Messianic hope.** In the great reversal that characterizes Hebrew prophecy, judgment is immediately followed by promise. Micah 4:1–3 is nearly identical to Isaiah 2:2–4 — the mountain of the Lord exalted, nations streaming to it, swords beaten into plowshares. Chapter 5 delivers the most specific Messianic location prophecy in the OT: "But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah, though thou be little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of thee shall he come forth unto me that is to be ruler in Israel; whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting" (5:2). This is the text Herod's chief priests quoted when answering where Christ was to be born (Matthew 2:5–6).

**Chapters 6–7: The covenant lawsuit.** God brings a case (a *rib*, or legal dispute) against Israel, calling the mountains and hills as witnesses. "What have I done to you? How have I wearied you?" (6:3). The response is an escalating list of what the people think God wants — burnt offerings, thousands of rams, ten thousands of oil, even the firstborn child. Micah's answer: none of that. Just justice, mercy, humility (6:8). Chapter 7 ends with one of the most moving confessions of trust in the OT: "I will bear the indignation of the LORD, because I have sinned against him, until he plead my cause" (7:9) — and the great declaration of God's incomparable forgiveness: "Who is a God like unto thee, that pardoneth iniquity...He will have compassion upon us; he will subdue our iniquities; and thou wilt cast all their sins into the depths of the sea" (7:18–19).

## Cross-References

**Old Testament:**
- Isaiah 2:2–4 — "Swords into plowshares" passage is nearly identical to Micah 4:1–3; either one quotes the other or both draw from a common prophetic tradition
- Isaiah 9:6–7; 11:1–5 — Parallel Messianic prophecies (Micah 5:2–5)
- Jeremiah 26:18 — Micah's Jerusalem destruction prediction (3:12) is explicitly cited by the elders when defending Jeremiah; one of the few times one prophet quotes another by name
- Genesis 22 — Abraham; God's faithfulness to the Abrahamic covenant (Micah 7:20)

**New Testament:**
- Matthew 2:5–6 — Herod's priests quote Micah 5:2 to identify Bethlehem as Messiah's birthplace
- Matthew 10:35–36 — "A man's foes shall be they of his own household" (Micah 7:6)
- Luke 1:72–73 — Zechariah's Benedictus echoes Micah 7:20 (Abraham's covenant)

## Notes & Reflections

- Micah 6:8 is one of the most frequently quoted OT verses — it functions as a summary of prophetic ethics; it does not replace the law but distills its essence; notably, the word for "mercy" (*hesed*) is covenantal loyalty, not mere sentimentality
- Micah 5:2 is the most geographically specific Messianic prophecy in the OT — not merely a Davidic dynasty promise but a specific town (Bethlehem Ephratah, distinguishing it from a Bethlehem in Zebulun); the phrase "goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting" (*miqqedem mimei olam*) implies pre-existence
- Micah 3:12 is significant historically: it is explicitly cited in Jeremiah 26:18 — the elders invoke Micah's prediction to save Jeremiah's life; this is one of the clearest examples of one prophet's words preserving another prophet from death
- The swords-to-plowshares vision (4:3) appears in nearly identical form in Isaiah 2:4; both prophets were contemporaries — they either quoted each other or shared a common liturgical or prophetic tradition
- Micah 7:19 — "He will cast all our sins into the depths of the sea" — is the theological root of the Jewish *Tashlich* ceremony (Rosh Hashanah), where sins are symbolically cast into moving water
