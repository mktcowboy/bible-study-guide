# Hosea

> "How shall I give thee up, Ephraim? how shall I deliver thee, Israel?... my heart is turned within me, my repentings are kindled together." — Hosea 11:8 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Hosea (Prophet)|Hosea]] son of Beeri — from the northern kingdom of Israel; the only writing prophet from the north to leave a book; his name means "salvation"
- **Date Written:** ~753–722 BC — the longest prophetic ministry in the minor prophets; spanning the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah in Judah, and Jeroboam II through Hoshea in Israel; concluded with the Assyrian exile (722 BC)
- **Key Themes:** God's covenant love (*hesed*) for an unfaithful people, marriage as a metaphor for the God-Israel relationship, the tragedy of spiritual adultery (Baal worship), judgment as discipline not rejection, the promise of a new betrothal and restoration
- **Chapters:** 14

## Summary

Hosea is the most emotionally intimate of the prophetic books. God commands Hosea to marry a woman of promiscuity — Gomer — and then to love her and take her back even after her infidelity. This is not merely an illustration: Hosea is called to *embody* God's own experience with Israel. Through his own marriage, divorce, and redemption of his wife, Hosea knows from the inside what God feels toward his unfaithful people. The book is, at its core, a love story with a broken heart.

The theological vocabulary of the book clusters around two Hebrew words: *hesed* (covenant love, steadfast faithfulness — often translated "lovingkindness" or "mercy") and *da'at Elohim* (knowledge of God — not intellectual assent but intimate knowing, as in a marriage). Israel has forsaken both. She has exchanged Yahweh for the Baals, run after her lovers (the agricultural fertility gods), and credited them with the grain, wine, and oil that actually came from God (2:8).

**Chapters 1–3 (the biographical narrative):** Hosea marries Gomer; she bears three children whose names are theological judgments: Jezreel (the site of Ahab's crimes; judgment is coming there), Lo-Ruhamah ("not loved" — God will no longer have compassion on Israel), and Lo-Ammi ("not my people" — the covenant formula inverted). Chapter 2 is a legal divorce proceeding in which God addresses Israel (Gomer) directly, strips her of the gifts he gave her, and then — in one of the most startling pivots in the prophets — promises to woo her back into the wilderness and speak tenderly to her and restore the betrothal. Chapter 3: Hosea buys back Gomer from slavery for fifteen pieces of silver and a measure of barley, and tells her to wait for him.

**Chapters 4–14 (oracles of judgment and restoration):** The remainder of the book alternates between accusation and hope. Chapter 4 opens the indictment: "There is no truth, nor mercy, nor knowledge of God in the land" (4:1). The priests bear particular blame — they have profited from the people's sin and failed to instruct them (4:6: "my people are destroyed for lack of knowledge"). Chapter 6 contains one of the most poignant statements of repentance desire in the OT: "Come, and let us return to the LORD... After two days will he revive us: in the third day he will raise us up" (6:1–2) — a text early Christians read as a resurrection prophecy. But God's response: "Your goodness is as a morning cloud... I desired mercy [*hesed*] and not sacrifice, and the knowledge of God more than burnt offerings" (6:4–6).

The oracles move through Israel's history — the wilderness, Baal-Peor, Gibeah, Jacob the supplanter — seeing in each episode the pattern of faithlessness and divine grief. Chapter 11 is the emotional climax: God recalls calling Israel out of Egypt as a son, teaching him to walk, stooping to feed him — and then the anguish: "How shall I give thee up, Ephraim?" (11:8). This is not the language of a distant judge; it is the language of a parent who cannot let go.

The book closes with a final call to repentance (14:1–3) and a promise of healing and restored blessing (14:4–8), ending with the exhortation: "Who is wise, and he shall understand these things? prudent, and he shall know them? for the ways of the LORD are right, and the just shall walk in them: but the transgressors shall fall therein" (14:9).

## Notes & Reflections

- Hosea's marriage is one of the most debated in the OT — was Gomer already a prostitute at marriage (1:2) or did she become one afterward? The text is ambiguous; either reading makes theological sense as a picture of Israel at the moment of the covenant (already worshipping idols) or Israel's subsequent unfaithfulness
- Hosea 11:1 — "Out of Egypt I called my son" — is applied to the infant Jesus in Matthew 2:15; this is typological interpretation: Israel is the son; Jesus is the true Israel who relives and recapitulates Israel's history faithfully
- The three children's names (1:4–9) form a descending arc of judgment — Jezreel (discipline), Lo-Ruhamah (removal of mercy), Lo-Ammi (covenant broken) — which is then reversed in 2:22–23 (Jezreel becomes abundance, Lo-Ruhamah becomes Ruhamah, Lo-Ammi becomes "my people")
- Hosea 6:2 — "after two days will he revive us; in the third day he will raise us up" — is a communal restoration formula in context, but its language (two days/third day resurrection) is so precise that many early Christian interpreters read it as predictive of Christ's resurrection
- *Hesed* appears 6 times in Hosea — more than in any other minor prophet; it is the theological center of the book; the failure of Israel is the failure of *hesed* (loyalty, covenant love) and *da'at* (intimate knowledge of God)
