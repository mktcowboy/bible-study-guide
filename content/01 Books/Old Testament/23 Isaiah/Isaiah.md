# Isaiah

> "But he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." — Isaiah 53:5 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Isaiah (Prophet)|Isaiah]] son of Amoz — Jerusalem-based court prophet; married to "the prophetess" (8:3); two sons with symbolic names (Shear-jashub, "a remnant shall return"; Maher-shalal-hash-baz, "speed the spoil, hasten the prey"); 2 Isaiah authorship debate: many scholars propose "Deutero-Isaiah" (chs. 40–55) and "Trito-Isaiah" (chs. 56–66) based on historical referents; the NT and the Dead Sea Scrolls (Isaiah Scroll, 1QIsa-a) treat the book as unified
- **Date Written:** ~740–681 BC — Isaiah's ministry spans the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah; the later chapters address the Babylonian exile (which Isaiah predicted) from God's perspective
- **Key Themes:** The Holy One of Israel, the remnant theology, the Servant of the LORD, universal salvation through a Davidic deliverer, the new exodus, the new creation
- **Chapters:** 66
- **Source Text:** [[Isaiah (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Isaiah is the most quoted OT book in the NT (cited over 400 times), the longest of the prophetic books, and the most comprehensively Messianic. Early Christian interpreters called it "the fifth Gospel." Its 66 chapters mirror the 66 books of the Protestant Bible — the first 39 (like the OT) dealing primarily with judgment and the covenant, the final 27 (like the NT) dealing primarily with comfort, redemption, and the new creation.

The book opens with a courtroom scene: "Hear, O heavens, and give ear, O earth: for the LORD hath spoken, I have nourished and brought up children, and they have rebelled against me" (1:2). Israel stands indicted. The book moves across judgment and hope in alternating currents — never allowing either to dissolve into the other.

**The architecture of Isaiah:**

**Chapters 1–12 (Judgment and Promise for Judah and Israel):** Isaiah's call vision (ch. 6) — the seraphim, "Holy, holy, holy," the live coal on the prophet's lips — establishes both God's transcendence and his holiness as the ground of everything that follows. The "Book of Immanuel" (chs. 7–12) contains the great Messianic oracles: the sign of Immanuel/virgin birth (7:14), the child whose name is "Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace" (9:6), the Branch from Jesse's root (11:1–9), and the eschatological peace where "the wolf also shall dwell with the lamb" (11:6).

**Chapters 13–23 (Oracles Against the Nations):** Comprehensive indictments of every major power — Babylon (13–14), Assyria (14:24–27), Philistia, Moab, Damascus, Ethiopia, Egypt, Edom, Arabia, Jerusalem itself (ch. 22), Tyre. The Oracle of Babylon (chs. 13–14) contains the famous taunt against the "son of the morning" (14:12) — traditionally associated with Satan's fall — followed by Israel's restoration over her oppressor.

**Chapters 24–27 ("The Little Apocalypse"):** A cosmic expansion of judgment-to-redemption — the whole earth laid waste, the kings of heaven judged, the dead raised, "He will swallow up death in victory" (25:8 — quoted by Paul in 1 Corinthians 15:54). The section ends with the great eschatological song of the redeemed.

**Chapters 28–35 (Woes and Warnings):** Six "woe" oracles against various forms of false security — relying on Egypt, arrogant self-sufficiency, secret plans against God. Chapter 35 provides the first glimpse of the new exodus: "the ransomed of the LORD shall return, and come to Zion with songs and everlasting joy" (35:10).

**Chapters 36–39 (Historical Interlude):** Nearly verbatim parallel with 2 Kings 18–20. Sennacherib's siege of Jerusalem (701 BC); Hezekiah's prayer; 185,000 Assyrians destroyed by the angel of the LORD; Hezekiah's illness and recovery; his foolish display of treasures to Babylonian envoys — which seals the prophecy of Babylonian exile.

**Chapters 40–55 ("Deutero-Isaiah" / The Book of Comfort):** Begins with the most famous pastoral opening in the prophets: "Comfort ye, comfort ye my people, saith your God" (40:1). God speaks to the exiles in Babylon as if they are already there, promising a new exodus home. The "servant songs" (42:1–9; 49:1–13; 50:4–11; 52:13–53:12) form the theological heart of this section — describing the Servant of the LORD who will accomplish what Israel could not, and whose suffering will bear the sins of many. Isaiah 53 alone contains more specific Christological fulfillment than any other chapter in the OT. Cyrus is named a century and a half before his birth (44:28; 45:1) as God's "anointed" who will rebuild Jerusalem.

**Chapters 56–66 ("Trito-Isaiah" / The New Creation):** The final section expands the salvation promises to the nations, addresses the proper keeping of the Sabbath and the covenant, condemns empty fasting (ch. 58 — the passage Jesus reads in Luke 4:18), celebrates the year of the LORD's favor (ch. 61), and culminates in the glorious vision of new heavens and a new earth (65:17–25) — directly quoted in Revelation 21.

## Notes & Reflections

- Isaiah is the most quoted OT book in the NT — Matthew alone quotes or alludes to Isaiah over 40 times; Paul quotes it more than any other OT book; John 12:39–41 says Isaiah saw Christ's glory
- Isaiah 53 — The fourth Servant Song is the most Christologically dense passage in the OT: 700 years before Calvary it describes the rejection, silence, substitutionary suffering, burial with the rich, resurrection, and intercessory work of the Servant; the Dead Sea Scrolls confirm this text was not modified after the fact
- The "Deutero-Isaiah" debate (whether chs. 40–66 are by a different author because they describe Babylon as already the enemy) rests primarily on the assumption that predictive prophecy is impossible; the internal evidence of the book's unified theology, the Dead Sea Isaiah Scroll (which has no break at chapter 39), and the NT's consistent attribution to one "Isaiah" favor unified authorship
- Isaiah 40:3 is quoted in all four Gospels as fulfilled by John the Baptist — the most extensively multi-Gospel OT quotation in the NT
- Isaiah 7:14 — The word translated "virgin" is *almah* in Hebrew (young woman of marriageable age); the LXX (Greek translation) translates it *parthenos* (virgin); Matthew's use of *parthenos* follows the LXX; the theological weight rests on Matthew's authoritative interpretation
