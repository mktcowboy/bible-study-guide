# Exodus

> "And God spake unto Moses, and said unto him, I am the LORD: And I appeared unto Abraham, unto Isaac, and unto Jacob, by the name of God Almighty, but by my name JEHOVAH was I not known to them." — Exodus 6:2–3 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Moses]] — the narrative is written from Moses' perspective; he is both the subject and, by tradition, the author; the Pentateuch's Mosaic authorship is attested in the OT (Joshua 8:31; 1 Kings 2:3), the NT (Mark 12:26 references "the bush" passage), and by Jesus (John 5:46)
- **Date Written:** ~1446–1406 BC — during the wilderness period; the traditional early-date Exodus places the event c. 1446 BC (1 Kings 6:1 backward from Solomon's fourth year); late-date proponents suggest c. 1270 BC
- **Key Themes:** Redemption from bondage, the divine name (YHWH), the Passover as the paradigm of substitutionary atonement, covenant at Sinai, the Tabernacle as the pattern of divine dwelling, Moses as mediator
- **Chapters:** 40
- **Source Text:** [[Exodus (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Exodus (Greek: "departure") is the pivotal book of the Old Testament — the event referenced more than any other in Scripture. Israel's salvation from Egypt establishes the paradigm that all subsequent salvation language draws from, culminating in Christ's Passover death and resurrection. The book divides into two equal and theologically paired halves.

**Chapters 1–18 (Deliverance):** Israel's 400-year bondage in Egypt has become crushing. God tells Moses his name — "I AM THAT I AM" (*Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh*, 3:14; the four Hebrew letters YHWH, "the LORD") — and commissions him as deliverer. Ten plagues escalate in severity, each targeting a specific Egyptian deity and culminating in the death of every firstborn. The Passover — blood on the doorposts, a roasted lamb, unleavened bread, bitter herbs, loins girded — is the defining act: death passes over the blood-covered household. Israel departs, crosses the Red Sea on dry ground as the waters drown Pharaoh's army, and receives manna, water from the rock, and victory over Amalek in the wilderness.

**Chapters 19–40 (Covenant and Tabernacle):** Israel arrives at Sinai — the mountain burns with fire, trembles, and thunders. God descends. The Ten Commandments are given (ch. 20), then the Book of the Covenant (21–23), then the detailed Tabernacle instructions (25–31). The golden calf rebellion (ch. 32) — Israel's apostasy at the very moment God is delivering the blueprint for worship — ruptures the covenant. Moses intercedes (32:11–14), descends, breaks the tablets, and returns to beg God's presence: "If thy presence go not with me, carry us not up hence" (33:15). God renews the covenant. Moses' face shines. The Tabernacle is constructed exactly as instructed, and the glory of the LORD fills it (40:34–38) — God has come to dwell among his people.

## Notes & Reflections

- **The Exodus is the central saving event of the OT** — referenced 120+ times across the Hebrew Bible; every subsequent act of divine salvation is interpreted through its categories; Isaiah promises a "new exodus," and Jesus performs one
- **The divine name YHWH** ("LORD" in most English translations) is revealed for the first time in its full theological weight at 3:14: *Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh* — "I AM WHO I AM" or "I WILL BE WHAT I WILL BE"; the name denotes both absolute self-existence and covenantal faithfulness; Jesus's "I am" declarations in John all echo this
- **The hardening of Pharaoh's heart** is both God's action and Pharaoh's own — the early plagues say Pharaoh hardened his own heart (8:15; 8:32); later God is said to harden it (9:12; 10:20); the theological point: God gives people up to what they have already chosen (Romans 1:24–28; 9:17–18); Pharaoh is an example of judicial hardening
- **The Passover (ch. 12) is the theological center of Exodus:** The lamb must be without blemish, slain at twilight, its blood applied to the doorposts and lintel, its flesh eaten with unleavened bread and bitter herbs, with loins girded for departure. Paul (1 Cor. 5:7), Peter (1 Pet. 1:18–19), and John (John 1:29; Rev. 5:6) all identify Christ as the Passover lamb. The Eucharist is explicitly connected to Passover at the Last Supper
- **The Tabernacle (chs. 25–40) is not peripheral:** It occupies more chapters than the creation narrative. The detailed instructions — ark, mercy seat, lampstand, bread of presence, incense altar, priestly garments — are not architectural curiosity but theology in three dimensions. Hebrews 9 reads every element as a "shadow" of Christ's priestly work. John 1:14 (*eskēnōsen*) is the New Covenant Tabernacle

- **Preceded by:** [[Genesis]] — the patriarchal promises (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob) that Exodus begins to fulfill

- **NT fulfillment:** [[John]] 1 (Tabernacle in flesh), [[1 Corinthians]] 5 (Passover), [[Hebrews]] 3 and 9 (Moses and the sanctuary), [[Revelation]] 15 (song of Moses and the Lamb)
- **Followed by:** [[Leviticus]] — Tabernacle regulations and the holiness of God
