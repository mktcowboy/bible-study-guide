# Haggai

> "Thus speaketh the LORD of hosts, saying, This people say, The time is not come, the time that the LORD's house should be built. Then came the word of the LORD by Haggai the prophet, saying, Is it time for you, O ye, to dwell in your cieled houses, and this house lie waste?" — Haggai 1:2–4 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Haggai]] — the name means "festival" or "festal one"; possibly born during a festival; no other biographical details given
- **Date Written:** 520 BC — precisely dated in the text to the second year of Darius (1:1; 2:1, 10, 20); the most precisely dated prophetic book in the OT, with four discrete messages all delivered within four months (August–December 520 BC)
- **Key Themes:** Misplaced priorities and their consequences, the call to rebuild the temple, divine presence as the source of true blessing, the shaking of heaven and earth, Zerubbabel as the Messianic signet ring
- **Chapters:** 2
- **Source Text:** [[Haggai (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Haggai is the most historically focused and practically urgent of the minor prophets. The setting is precise: 538 BC, Cyrus of Persia had issued his decree allowing the Jewish exiles to return to Judah and rebuild the temple. Zerubbabel led the first wave of returnees; they laid the foundation and then stopped — for 16 years. By 520 BC, they had built comfortable houses for themselves while the temple lay in ruins. Haggai delivers four sharp messages across four months that directly address this problem, and with remarkable success: the people obeyed within 23 days (1:14–15).

**First message (1:1–15) — "Consider your ways."** Haggai diagnoses the economic problem the returnees had been blaming on bad timing. Crops have failed, water runs dry, wages disappear. The diagnosis: "Ye looked for much, and, lo, it came to little; and when ye brought it home, I did blow upon it. Why? saith the LORD of hosts. Because of mine house that is waste, and ye run every man unto his own house" (1:9). The phrase "consider your ways" (*simu l'vavkem al-darkekem*) appears four times in the book — it is Haggai's refrain, a call to diagnostic self-examination. The people respond, the Spirit stirs them, and within 23 days they are back at work on the temple.

**Second message (2:1–9) — "Be strong."** The old men who had seen Solomon's temple were weeping — the new temple was visibly inferior (2:3: "Who is left among you that saw this house in her first glory? and how do ye see it now? is it not in your eyes in comparison of it as nothing?"). God's response: "Be strong... I am with you, saith the LORD of hosts... my spirit remaineth among you: fear ye not" (2:4–5). Then the great shaking promise: God will shake the heavens, the earth, the sea, and all nations, and "the desire of all nations shall come" (2:7) — the precious things of all nations will fill this house, and "the glory of this latter house shall be greater than of the former" (2:9). This is either an immediate economic promise or (in its NT reading) a Messianic prophecy about the greater glory that will come through Jesus.

**Third message (2:10–19) — Holiness and blessing.** Using a question to the priests about ritual purity (holiness is not contagious from an object; but impurity is — 2:12–14), Haggai diagnoses the nation's spiritual condition. Before the foundation was re-laid, there was no blessing. From this day forward, God will bless (2:19).

**Fourth message (2:20–23) — Zerubbabel the signet ring.** On the same day as the third message, God addresses Zerubbabel specifically: "I will make thee as a signet: for I have chosen thee" (2:23). The signet ring (*chotam*) was the supreme symbol of royal authority — the instrument through which a king's official decrees were sealed. Jehoiachin (Zerubbabel's grandfather) had been told "though thou wert the signet upon my right hand, yet would I pluck thee thence" (Jeremiah 22:24) — a curse. Haggai reverses it: Zerubbabel is confirmed as the Davidic line's continuation, appearing in the genealogy of Jesus (Matthew 1:12; Luke 3:27).

## Notes & Reflections

- Haggai achieves something almost unparalleled among the prophets: the people *immediately obeyed* (1:12); within 23 days they were at work; Haggai is the most practically successful prophet in terms of visible response
- The "shaking of the nations" (2:6–7) is quoted in Hebrews 12:26 and interpreted as referring to the new covenant's inauguration — the ultimate "shaking" was not Persia's campaigns but the death and resurrection of Christ and the establishment of an unshakeable kingdom
- Zerubbabel's role as "signet ring" (2:23) reverses the curse of Jeremiah 22:24 on Jehoiachin — a remarkable piece of prophetic reversal; the Davidic line was not dead; it was waiting to produce the ultimate descendant (Matthew 1:12)
- "The glory of this latter house shall be greater" (2:9) — the second temple was architecturally inferior to Solomon's, but Jesus walked in it; God incarnate stood in Herod's expansion of the second temple; the presence of Christ was the "greater glory"
- The book uses the divine name "LORD of hosts" (*Yahweh Tzevaot*) 14 times in 38 verses — the name emphasizes God's commanding authority over all angelic and natural armies; in a discouraging situation, this name is Haggai's constant reminder of who is actually in charge
