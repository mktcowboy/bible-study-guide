# Jeremiah

> "For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith the LORD, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end." — Jeremiah 29:11 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Jeremiah]] (with [[Baruch]] as scribe)
- **Date Written:** ~627–586 BC (23 years ministry, through fall of Jerusalem)
- **Key Themes:** Judgment, new covenant, repentance, false prophets, suffering, hope, exile
- **Chapters:** 52
- **Source Text:** [[Jeremiah (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

[[Jeremiah]] is the most personal, emotional book of the prophets — the "weeping prophet" who delivered 40 years of judgment messages to a people who refused to listen. Called as a youth, Jeremiah watched Jerusalem fall, the temple burn, and his people scatter. Yet through the darkest judgment, God promised a new covenant — "I will put my law in their mind, and write it in their hearts."

The book covers:
- **Chapters 1–25: Call & Messages** — Jeremiah's commission, warnings to Judah
- **Chapters 26–45: Symbolic Acts & Letters** — Yoke, pot, letter to exiles
- **Chapters 46–51: Oracles Against Nations** — Babylon, Egypt, Moab, Ammon
- **Chapter 52: Fall of Jerusalem** — Historical appendix, parallel to 2 Kings 24–25

## Notes & Reflections

- Jeremiah was called before birth — divine election
- The new covenant (31:31-34) is the most important OT covenant passage after Genesis 3:15
- Jeremiah wept for his people — compassion amid judgment
- The burning scroll shows God's word can't be destroyed
- Buying a field during siege = hope in darkest hour
- "Thoughts of peace" (29:11) was written to exiles, not rebels
- Jeremiah's isolation mirrors Christ's loneliness
- The book ends with purchase — redemption, not destruction
