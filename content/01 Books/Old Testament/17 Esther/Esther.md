# Esther

> "And who knoweth whether thou art come to the kingdom for such a time as this?" — Esther 4:14 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Unknown (traditionally [[Mordecai]] or a chronicler)
- **Date Written:** ~430 BC
- **Key Themes:** Divine providence, courage, identity, reversal of fortune, deliverance, hidden sovereignty
- **Chapters:** 10

## Summary

[[Esther]] is the only book in the Bible where God's name is never mentioned — yet His providence is everywhere. Set during the Persian Empire under [[Ahasuerus]] (Xerxes I), the story follows a Jewish orphan girl who becomes queen and risks her life to save her people from genocide.

The plot moves with the pace of a thriller: a beauty pageant, a drunken king, a villainous plot, a sleepless night, a feast, a counter-feast, and a dramatic reversal. Every "coincidence" is divine orchestration — Esther is in the right place at the right time because God placed her there.

## Notes & Reflections

- God's name never appears — yet His providence is everywhere
- Esther 4:14 — "for such a time as this" — God places us where we need to be
- Every "coincidence" is divine orchestration: the king's sleepless night, Vashti's deposition, Esther's selection
- Haman is an Agagite, while Mordecai's Benjamite/Kish line echoes [[Saul]] and [[Saul Spares Agag]]
- The story of Ruth and Esther bookend the Historical books: a Moabite saves the line of David, an Agagite tries to destroy it
- Esther's courage costs her everything — "if I perish, I perish"
- Purim celebrates divine deliverance without mentioning God — He works behind the scenes
- The reversal theme: Haman builds a gallows, hangs on it; Mordecai is honored; Jews are elevated
- Esther shows that God preserves His people even when they're silent
