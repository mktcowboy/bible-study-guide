# 1 Kings

> "Give thy servant an understanding heart to judge thy people, that I may discern between good and bad." - 1 Kings 3:9 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Anonymous compiler drawing on royal and prophetic records
- **Date Written:** After the division of the kingdom and before the exile, traditionally within the Deuteronomistic history
- **Key Themes:** Wisdom, temple worship, covenant faithfulness, royal failure, divided kingdom, prophetic confrontation
- **Chapters:** 22
- **Source Text:** [[1 Kings (KJV Source)]]

## Summary
[[1 Kings]] begins with the transfer of power from [[David]] to [[Solomon]], rises to the high point of the temple in [[Jerusalem]], and then descends into fracture, idolatry, and prophetic judgment. The book moves from [[Solomon's Succession]] and [[Solomon's Wisdom]] through [[Temple Construction]] and [[Temple Dedication]], then breaks open through [[Solomon's Downfall]], [[Division of the Kingdom]], [[Jeroboam's Golden Calves]], and the fiery ministry of [[Elijah]] against [[Ahab]] and [[Jezebel]].

The book divides into three movements:
- **Chapters 1-11: Solomon's Reign** - succession struggle, wisdom, temple, international glory, and eventual compromise
- **Chapters 12-16: Kingdom Division and Early Northern Sin** - [[Rehoboam]], [[Jeroboam]], rival shrines, and repeated dynastic collapse
- **Chapters 17-22: Elijah and the House of Ahab** - drought, [[Mount Carmel Showdown|Mount Carmel]], covenant prosecution, and Ahab's death

## Notes and Reflections
- [[1 Kings]] moves from royal splendor to covenant exposure; the temple and the kingdom rise together and then begin to fracture.
- [[Solomon]] shows that wisdom, wealth, and even sacred vocation cannot preserve a divided heart.
- [[Jeroboam]] turns political insecurity into liturgical innovation, and that move poisons the north for generations.
- [[Elijah]] is not merely dramatic; he is the covenant prosecutor who names what the kings refuse to name.
- The book's recurring judgment formulas make clear that history here is theological, not merely political.
