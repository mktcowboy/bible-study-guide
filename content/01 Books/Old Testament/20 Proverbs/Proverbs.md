# Proverbs

> "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom: and the knowledge of the holy is understanding." — Proverbs 9:10 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Multiple — [[Solomon]] (chs. 1–24, with "the men of Hezekiah" copying 25–29), [[Agur]] son of Jakeh (ch. 30), [[King Lemuel]] (31:1–9, "the words which his mother taught him"); the collection spans ~250 years of royal wisdom
- **Date Written:** ~950–700 BC — Solomon composed the core (~950 BC); Hezekiah's scribes copied and assembled additional material (~700 BC)
- **Key Themes:** The fear of the LORD as the foundation of all wisdom, the two-ways contrast (wisdom vs. folly, life vs. death), wisdom as a way of life not merely information, the practical dimensions of integrity — speech, money, work, marriage, leadership
- **Chapters:** 31
- **Source Text:** [[Proverbs (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Proverbs is the most practically focused book in the OT wisdom literature — a collection of ~900 sayings, longer discourses, and poetic pieces covering the full range of human life. It is not a systematic theology; it is an education in how to *live*. The book's audience is primarily "my son" (used 15 times in chs. 1–9) — a young person at the beginning of adult life who must choose between two paths.

The key word of the book is *chokmah* (wisdom) — which in Hebrew usage is not primarily intellectual but *practical*: the skill that enables one to live well in God's world in accordance with the order he built into creation. The book's foundational thesis appears at 1:7 and 9:10: "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom." This is not a minor preamble; it is the hermeneutical key to the entire collection. Every proverb is a specific application of this root.

**Chapters 1–9 (The Prologue — extended discourses):** Unlike the pithy one-verse proverbs of chapters 10–31, this section contains long wisdom speeches in the form of a father's instruction to his son. The centerpiece is the personification of **Lady Wisdom** (chs. 8–9) — a figure who speaks in the first person, claims to have been present at creation ("when he appointed the foundations of the earth: then I was by him, as one brought up with him" — 8:29–30), and invites all to her table. In direct contrast, **Lady Folly** sits at her door inviting passersby to stolen water and bread eaten in secret — "he knoweth not that the dead are there" (9:18). The choice between wisdom and folly is the choice between life and death.

**Chapters 10–29 (The proverb collections):** The classic format — short, antithetic couplets ("A wise son maketh a glad father: but a foolish son is the heaviness of his mother" — 10:1). These cover speech (15:1, 18:21), work and laziness (6:6–11, 12:24), money and poverty (10:4, 22:7), friendship (17:17, 27:6), anger (15:18, 29:11), sexual temptation (5:1–23, 7:1–27), honesty (11:1, 12:17), and leadership (14:34, 29:2). Chapter 22:6 — "train up a child in the way he should go" — and chapter 22:7 — "the borrower is servant to the lender" — are among the most frequently cited proverbs.

**Chapter 30 (Agur):** One of the most humble passages in Wisdom literature — "I am more brutish than any man, and have not the understanding of a man. I neither learned wisdom, nor have the knowledge of the holy. Who hath ascended up into heaven, or descended? what is his name, and what is his son's name, if thou canst tell?" (30:2–4). The numerical proverbs (three things... four things) characterize this section.

**Chapter 31 (Lemuel/The Virtuous Woman):** The book closes with a mother's advice to her royal son (31:1–9) and then the famous *Eshet Chayil* — the Virtuous Woman poem (31:10–31), an acrostic where each verse begins with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet. She is the incarnation of Lady Wisdom from chapter 8 — a woman of strength, intelligence, commerce, generosity, and dignity whose "price is far above rubies."

## Notes & Reflections

- Proverbs 8:22–31 — Lady Wisdom present at creation — is the most debated passage in the book; the early church used it in Christological debates (Arius cited it to argue Christ was a created being); the orthodox response is that Wisdom here is a personification, but the NT (John 1, Colossians 1:16–17, 1 Corinthians 1:24) shows that what Proverbs personified, the gospel declares in the person of Jesus
- Proverbs are not promises but patterns — they describe *generally* how wisdom works, not ironclad laws; "a soft answer turns away wrath" (15:1) is true as a general pattern, not a guarantee in every case; this is why the book must be read alongside Job (which addresses the exceptions) and Ecclesiastes
- The Virtuous Woman (31:10–31) is a Hebrew acrostic (*Eshet Chayil*) — sung by Jewish husbands to their wives on Shabbat; the woman described is not a domestic servant but an entrepreneur, manager, landowner, merchant, and provider for the poor — a full embodiment of practical wisdom
- The book reflects a form of wisdom shared across the ancient Near East — parallels exist with Egyptian (Instruction of Amenemope, which 22:17–23:11 closely parallels) and Mesopotamian wisdom literature; this is not a problem for inspiration but shows God's common grace in human wisdom
- Proverbs 3:5–6 is the most memorized verse in the book and one of the most cited in all of Scripture — the two imperatives (trust / don't lean on your own understanding) and two promised results (he will direct your paths) are the relational epistemology of the entire book in compressed form
