# 2 Samuel

> "And thine house and thy kingdom shall be established for ever before thee: thy throne shall be established for ever." - 2 Samuel 7:16 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Nathan]] and [[Gad]], with material flowing from the Samuel narratives
- **Date Written:** ~970 BC
- **Key Themes:** David's reign, the [[Davidic Covenant]], sin and consequences, divine mercy, rebellion, covenant kingship
- **Chapters:** 24
- **Source Text:** [[2 Samuel (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

[[2 Samuel]] chronicles the reign of [[David]] from the aftermath of [[Death of Saul & Jonathan]] to the closing years of his kingship. The book traces David's rise from king of Judah at [[Hebron]] to king over all Israel in [[Jerusalem]], then follows the fracture lines that spread through his house after his sin with [[Bathsheba]] and the murder of [[Uriah the Hittite]].

The structure follows David's reign in three broad movements:
- **Chapters 1-5: Rise to Unified Kingship** - [[Death of Saul Reported]], [[David King over Judah]], the conflict with [[Ishbosheth]], [[Abner]], and [[Joab]], and finally [[David King over All Israel]] with the capture of [[Jerusalem]].
- **Chapters 6-10: Covenant and Consolidation** - [[Ark Brought to Jerusalem]], the [[Davidic Covenant]], mercy to [[Mephibosheth]], and the victories that secure the kingdom.
- **Chapters 11-24: Sin, Judgment, and Mercy** - [[David & Bathsheba]], [[Nathan's Confrontation]], the fall of David's house through [[Amnon]], [[Tamar]], and [[Absalom]], then the closing mercy of [[David's Census]] at [[Araunah's Threshing Floor]].

[[2 Samuel]] is both royal history and theological warning. [[David]] is shown at his best and worst, and the book insists that even the greatest king needs grace. At the same time, the promise of an everlasting throne points beyond David to the true son of David.

## Notes & Reflections

- [[Davidic Covenant]] is one of the central royal promises of Scripture and points directly toward Christ
- [[David & Bathsheba]] makes clear that David is not excused or sanitized; grace does not erase consequences
- The book shows how private sin can become public disaster in a king's house
- [[Absalom's Rebellion]] is a political crisis born from unresolved family evil
- [[Joab]] often saves David's throne while acting with moral violence that David never fully restrains
- [[Mercy to Mephibosheth]] stands beside the darkest narratives to show David at his most covenant-faithful
- [[David's Census]] ends the book with judgment stopped by mercy at a place later tied to worship
