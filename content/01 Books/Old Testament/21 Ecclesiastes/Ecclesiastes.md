# Ecclesiastes

> "Vanity of vanities, saith the Preacher, vanity of vanities; all is vanity." — Ecclesiastes 1:2 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Qoheleth]] — the Hebrew title, translated "Preacher" or "Teacher"; the book identifies the author as "the son of David, king in Jerusalem" (1:1), traditionally understood as Solomon; modern scholarship debates whether this is a pseudepigraphical attribution to Solomonic wisdom rather than direct authorship
- **Date Written:** ~950 BC (if Solomonic) or ~450–350 BC (if post-exilic composition in Solomon's persona); the language has late features that complicate the early date
- **Key Themes:** The limits of human wisdom and achievement under the sun, the sovereignty of God who holds what humans cannot grasp, enjoyment as a genuine but fragile divine gift, the fear of God as the beginning and end of wisdom, judgment and accountability
- **Chapters:** 12
- **Source Text:** [[Ecclesiastes (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

Ecclesiastes is the most philosophically daring book in the OT — a sustained meditation on the meaning of human existence that refuses easy answers. The teacher (Qoheleth) begins with a thesis — "Vanity of vanities, all is vanity" (*hebel habalim*, literally "vapor of vapors") — and then systematically examines every human attempt to find lasting meaning "under the sun": wisdom, pleasure, work, wealth, legacy, fame. Each fails on its own terms. Each dissipates like breath (*hebel* = vapor, breath, mist). The book is profoundly honest, and its honesty is why it exists in the canon: it tells the truth about life without God at the center.

The phrase "under the sun" (used 29 times) is the key hermeneutical marker. Qoheleth is not saying life is meaningless in an absolute sense; he is saying that *life viewed from the horizontal plane, without reference to God*, is meaningless. Every conclusion about vanity is reached from a vantage point that excludes the eternal — and the book repeatedly punctures that vantage point with reminders that God exists, that he acts in time, and that there will be judgment.

**Chapter 1–2 (The experiments):** Qoheleth tries wisdom, then pleasure, then great works. He built houses, planted gardens, gathered servants and possessions, amassed silver and gold. "Whatsoever mine eyes desired I kept not from them, I withheld not my heart from any joy; for my heart rejoiced in all my labour: and this was my portion of all my labour. Then I looked on all the works that my hands had wrought... and, behold, all was vanity" (2:10–11). The conclusion is not nihilistic; it is a stripping away of false foundations.

**Chapter 3 (Times and seasons):** The famous poem of 14 pairs of opposites — "a time to be born, and a time to die... a time to love, and a time to hate" (3:2–8). This is commonly misread as permission to do anything in its proper time. It is actually a meditation on divine sovereignty: God has set the times; humans cannot discover them, control them, or transcend them. The section ends: "He hath made every thing beautiful in his time: also he hath set the world in their heart, so that no man can find out the work that God maketh from the beginning to the end" (3:11).

**Chapters 4–10 (Observations):** A series of observations about social injustice, political power, wealth, religious vows, wisdom's value and limits, and the fundamental unpredictability of life. Notable: the admonition about worship (5:1–7) — "keep thy foot when thou goest to the house of God, and be more ready to hear than to give the sacrifice of fools"; the observation that the righteous sometimes perish and the wicked sometimes prosper (7:15; 9:11); "the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong, neither yet bread to the wise... but time and chance happeneth to them all" (9:11).

**Chapters 11–12 (The conclusion):** Cast your bread upon the waters — act in uncertainty; God will bring results in his time. Chapter 12 opens with a famous allegory of aging (the sun darkened, the keepers of the house trembling, the strong men bowed, the grinders ceasing — all images of the body's decline). The book reaches its conclusion: "Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment, with every secret thing, whether it be good, or whether it be evil" (12:13–14).

## Notes & Reflections

- *Hebel* ("vanity") literally means "breath" or "vapor" — the same word used for Abel's name in Hebrew; it is a word of *insubstantiality*, not *worthlessness*; Qoheleth is not saying life is bad, but that it is fragile, passing, and cannot bear the weight of ultimate meaning
- The repeated phrase "under the sun" (29×) is Ecclesiastes' hermeneutical key: all the observations about futility are made from a *this-world-only* perspective; the book persistently ruptures that perspective with reminders of God, eternity, and judgment
- The "enjoyment texts" (2:24; 3:12–13; 5:18–20; 8:15; 9:7–9) are not nihilistic concessions — they are genuine theology; enjoyment of food, work, and marriage is a *gift from God*, not an escape from meaninglessness
- Ecclesiastes 3:11 — "set *eternity* in their heart" (*olam* — the Hebrew) — is perhaps the deepest anthropological statement in the OT; humans are uniquely constructed to sense transcendence but unable to grasp it; this creates the restlessness that Augustine described as "our heart is restless until it rests in Thee"
- Paul's use of *mataiotēs* (futility/vanity) in Romans 8:20 almost certainly echoes Ecclesiastes' *hebel*; creation has been subjected to futility not by accident but by God's sovereign design, awaiting liberation

## Sequential Connections

- **Preceded by:** [[Proverbs]] in canonical order — Solomon's confident practical wisdom; Ecclesiastes is the more searching, testing counterpart
- **Wisdom triad:** [[Job]], [[Proverbs]], and Ecclesiastes form the OT wisdom corpus — Job addresses suffering, Proverbs addresses ordinary wisdom, Ecclesiastes addresses the limits of both
- **Wisdom trajectory:** Proverbs presents wisdom's rewards; Ecclesiastes tests them; the NT resolves both in Christ "in whom are hid all the treasures of wisdom" (Colossians 2:3)
- **Followed by:** [[Song of Solomon]]
