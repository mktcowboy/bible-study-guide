# Nahum

> "The LORD is good, a strong hold in the day of trouble; and he knoweth them that trust in him." — Nahum 1:7 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Nahum]] of Elkosh — the location of Elkosh is uncertain (proposed sites include Galilee, Judah, and even Mesopotamia); his name means "comfort" or "consolation"
- **Date Written:** ~663–612 BC — after the fall of Thebes/No-amon (~663 BC, mentioned as past in 3:8) and before the fall of Nineveh (612 BC, which Nahum predicts)
- **Key Themes:** The just wrath of God against unrepentant evil, the fall of Nineveh as divine judgment, God as both judge of nations and refuge for his people, the contrast between pride and trust
- **Chapters:** 3

## Summary

Nahum is the companion book to Jonah: both are about Nineveh, the capital of the Assyrian Empire. In Jonah (~760 BC), Nineveh repented at an Israelite prophet's preaching and was spared. A century later, the repentance had long faded. Assyria was now the most brutal empire in the ancient Near East — a byword for cruelty, with military tactics of mass deportation, flaying, impalement, and the systematic destruction of conquered peoples. Nahum (~630 BC) announces that Nineveh's time has come. There is no call to repent here; the verdict is final.

This makes Nahum one of the most theologically bracing books in the OT. It is entirely devoted to the destruction of a single city — and it celebrates that destruction. To modern readers this can seem disturbing; in its context, it was the cry of every victim of Assyrian terror. Nahum's "good news" (the Hebrew *baser*, which provides the NT word *euangelion* via 1:15) is the announcement of Nineveh's fall to those who have suffered under her.

**Chapter 1** opens with a two-part theological statement about God's character: he is a jealous avenger who does not acquit the wicked, whose wrath is fierce as a storm and fire (1:2–6) — AND he is good, a stronghold in trouble, one who knows those who trust him (1:7). These are not contradictory; they are the two faces of moral justice. Verses 9–15 then apply these truths: the enemy plotting against the LORD will be cut off; but to Judah — "O Judah, keep thy solemn feasts" — the oppressor's yoke will be broken.

**Chapter 2** is vivid battle poetry describing Nineveh's siege and fall in present-tense urgency. The shields glow red, the chariots rage in the streets, the river gates are opened and the palace dissolves. The lion's den — Nineveh, who filled her caves with prey — is now empty. Where is the lion now?

**Chapter 3** piles up charges against Nineveh: the harlot-city that sold nations through witchcraft; the military superpower that inflicted endless cruelty. Like the fall of Thebes (No-amon) — which the Assyrians themselves had destroyed — Nineveh will be shamed and defeated. Her leaders are like swarms of locusts that will scatter at dawn. "There is no healing of thy bruise; thy wound is grievous: all that hear the bruit of thee shall clap the hands over thee" (3:19).

Nineveh fell in 612 BC, exactly as Nahum prophesied, to a coalition of Babylonians and Medes. The city was so thoroughly destroyed that it was lost to history for centuries — Alexander the Great marched past its ruins without knowing what city had once stood there.

## Notes & Reflections

- Nahum 1:15 is nearly verbatim with Isaiah 52:7 — either Nahum quotes Isaiah, Isaiah quotes Nahum, or both draw from a common liturgical formula; Paul quotes both in Romans 10:15, applying the "good tidings" to the gospel
- The book is not sadistic celebration of violence — it is the comfort of victims. Every nation that suffered under Assyrian brutality heard Nahum's oracle as deliverance
- Nahum 1:7 stands in deliberate contrast to the surrounding judgment passages — "the LORD is good" is not an afterthought but the theological center; he distinguishes between the guilty and the trusting
- Nineveh's historicity: Nineveh was rediscovered by British archaeologists in 1842–1846 (Sir Austen Henry Layard), revealing the palace of Sennacherib and the library of Ashurbanipal — confirming details in Nahum, Kings, and the prophets
- The book's literary quality is exceptional — particularly the battle sequence of chapter 2, which is among the most vivid military poetry in the Bible
