# Jonah

> "O LORD, was not this my saying, when I was yet in my country? Therefore I fled before unto Tarshish: for I knew that thou art a gracious God, and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repentest thee of the evil." — Jonah 4:2 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Anonymous — tradition attributes authorship to Jonah himself; [[Jonah (Prophet)|Jonah]] son of Amittai was a historical prophet from Gath-hepher (2 Kings 14:25) in the northern kingdom during Jeroboam II's reign (~793–753 BC)
- **Date Written:** ~760 BC if written by Jonah; some scholars propose a later date (~500 BC) based on the third-person narration and linguistic features
- **Key Themes:** The breadth of God's mercy, prophetic reluctance and nationalism, genuine repentance, God's sovereign use of unlikely instruments, the question of whether Israel wants God's compassion extended to her enemies
- **Chapters:** 4

## Summary

Everyone knows the fish — but the great fish is not the point of Jonah. The fish takes up five verses (1:17–2:10); the real drama occupies the rest of the book. Jonah is the most ironic of all the prophetic books: a prophet who runs *from* God's mission, whose audience repents while he sulks, who is angrier about a withered plant than about the fate of 120,000 people. The book ends not with resolution but with a question — "Shouldest not I spare Nineveh?" — that hangs over every subsequent reader.

**Chapter 1:** God commissions Jonah to preach to Nineveh (the capital of Assyria, Israel's worst enemy). Jonah immediately boards a ship in the opposite direction — to Tarshish, which is approximately as far west as Nineveh is east. God sends a storm. The pagan sailors pray to their gods and then reluctantly throw Jonah overboard at his own request. The storm ceases. The sailors offer sacrifices to Yahweh. The irony begins: pagan sailors respond to God better than the prophet does.

**Chapter 2:** Inside the great fish, Jonah prays — a psalm of thanksgiving, not repentance. He acknowledges deliverance and vows to fulfill what he has promised. The fish vomits him out.

**Chapter 3:** The second commission. This time Jonah goes. He walks a single day's journey into the city and delivers the shortest sermon in prophetic history: "Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown" (3:4). The entire city — from king to livestock — repents in sackcloth and ashes. The king's decree is remarkable (3:9): "Who can tell if God will turn and repent, and turn away from his fierce anger, that we not perish?" God does relent. The greatest revival in the OT occurs at the preaching of a man who didn't want to preach it.

**Chapter 4:** Jonah is furious. "It displeased Jonah exceedingly, and he was very angry" (4:1). His explanation (4:2) is theologically astonishing: he already *knew* God was gracious and merciful — that's why he fled. He quotes Exodus 34:6, the core declaration of God's character, as a complaint. He wanted Nineveh destroyed. He sits east of the city watching, perhaps hoping God will still destroy it. God provides a gourd for shade; Jonah rejoices excessively. God provides a worm that kills the gourd; Jonah is bereft. God's closing question (4:10–11) is the book's point: Jonah pitied the plant (which cost him nothing) — shouldn't God pity 120,000 people who do not know their right hand from their left?

The book never tells us how Jonah answered.

- 2 Kings 14:25 — Jonah son of Amittai mentioned as a historical prophet; confirms he is a real person
- Exodus 34:6 — "Gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness" — Jonah quotes this as his reason for fleeing; it is the great self-declaration of God's character
- Psalm 139:7–12 — "Where can I flee from your presence?" — Jonah tried

## Notes & Reflections

- Jonah is unique among the prophets: he flees his commission, prays inside a fish, delivers the barest possible sermon, and then sulks when it succeeds; he is the anti-model prophet, which makes the book a meditation on the gap between the prophet's limitations and God's sovereign purpose
- Jesus's use of "the sign of Jonah" (Matt. 12:40) links Jonah's three days in the fish to his own three days in the tomb — not merely an illustration but a typological fulfillment; Jesus is the greater Jonah, willingly offered, not thrown overboard
- The Ninevites' repentance is genuine — sackcloth, fasting, the king's decree — and God honors it; but Nahum ~130 years later shows it did not persist; the book demonstrates that repentance has to be sustained, not just initiated
- Jonah quotes Exodus 34:6 (God's own self-declaration) as a *complaint* — this is one of the Bible's darkest ironies; the very character of God that Israel celebrated became, for Jonah, the reason for resentment when applied to enemies
- The book's ending is unresolved — Jonah never answers God's question; the reader is left to answer it; this is probably intentional; are *you* as angry as Jonah when God is merciful to people you consider enemies?
