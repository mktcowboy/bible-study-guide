# Zechariah

> "Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion; shout, O daughter of Jerusalem: behold, thy King cometh unto thee: he is just, and having salvation; lowly, and riding upon an ass, and upon a colt the foal of an ass." — Zechariah 9:9 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Zechariah]] son of Berechiah, son of Iddo (1:1) — a priest-prophet (Iddo was a priestly family, Nehemiah 12:4); contemporary of [[Haggai]]; the name means "the LORD remembers"
- **Date Written:** 520–518 BC (chs. 1–8, precisely dated); chapters 9–14 are undated and may be from a later period of Zechariah's ministry or even a later hand ("Deutero-Zechariah" is a significant scholarly debate); the NT cites both halves as authoritative
- **Key Themes:** The rebuilding of the temple as a foretaste of God's renewed presence, eight night visions explaining God's cosmic purposes, the Branch (Messiah) as priest-king, Israel's future and the final Day of the LORD, the most detailed cluster of Messianic prophecies in the OT after Isaiah
- **Chapters:** 14

## Summary

Zechariah is the most visionary prophet of the post-exilic period and one of the most Messianic books in all of the OT — the NT quotes or alludes to it more than any other minor prophet. Like his contemporary Haggai, he prophesied to encourage the rebuilding of the temple in 520 BC; but where Haggai is practical and earthbound, Zechariah is cosmic. He uses eight night visions, two oracular burdens, symbolic acts, and apocalyptic imagery to set the temple reconstruction within the framework of God's ultimate purposes for Israel and the nations.

**The book divides naturally into two halves:**

**Chapters 1–8 (dated, 520–518 BC):** After a call to return to God (1:1–6), Zechariah receives eight night visions in rapid succession — a visual theology of God's program for Jerusalem and the nations:

1. **The horsemen** (1:7–17) — riders report that the nations are at rest; God will return to Jerusalem with mercy
2. **Four horns and four craftsmen** (1:18–21) — the nations that scattered Israel will be terrified
3. **The measuring line** (2:1–13) — Jerusalem will be too full for walls; God will be a wall of fire around her
4. **The cleansing of Joshua** (3:1–10) — Satan accuses; God rebukes; Joshua reclothed; the Branch introduced
5. **The golden lampstand and olive trees** (4:1–14) — "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6); Zerubbabel will complete the temple
6. **The flying scroll** (5:1–4) — judgment on thieves and perjurers
7. **The woman in the ephah** (5:5–11) — wickedness transported to Babylon
8. **Four chariots** (6:1–8) — God's patrol of the earth; the north country quieted

The series concludes with the symbolic crowning of Joshua (6:9–15) — the priest wearing a crown, combining the two offices; the one called "the Branch" will build the true temple and be a priest upon his throne.

**Chapters 9–14 (undated):** These chapters are apocalyptic and Messianic in a different register — less sequential vision, more sustained oracle. The first burden (9–11) includes the Palm Sunday prophecy (9:9), the thirty pieces of silver (11:12–13), and the broken shepherd. The second burden (12–14) covers the siege of Jerusalem, the mourning over the "pierced one" (12:10), the refining of the remnant (13:9), and the climactic Day of the LORD when God's feet stand on the Mount of Olives, it splits in two, and living waters flow from Jerusalem.

## Notes & Reflections

- Zechariah contains more explicit Messianic prophecy than any OT book except Isaiah — Palm Sunday entry (9:9), betrayal price (11:12–13), the piercing (12:10), the smitten shepherd (13:7) are all fulfilled specifically in the Passion narrative
- The "Deutero-Zechariah" debate (whether chs. 9–14 are by a different author) is significant in scholarship — the stylistic shift is real, but the thematic continuity and NT attribution (quoting both halves as Zechariah) favor unified authorship or a later phase of the same prophet's ministry
- "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6) was addressed specifically to Zerubbabel about temple completion — but has been applied by every generation to the impossibility of God's work being accomplished through human resources alone
- Matthew 27:9 attributes the "thirty pieces of silver" quotation to Jeremiah, not Zechariah — a famous textual difficulty; several explanations exist (ancient lectionary practice of naming sections by the first prophet; Jeremiah's field purchase; scribal error)
- Zechariah 12:10 — "They shall look upon me whom they have pierced" — in the Hebrew, the speaker is Yahweh ("upon *me*"); this is used in John 19:37 and Revelation 1:7 to identify the pierced one as Jesus — and by extension, as Yahweh incarnate; it is one of the strongest OT arguments for the deity of Christ
