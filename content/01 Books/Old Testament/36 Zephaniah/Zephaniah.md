# Zephaniah

> "The LORD thy God in the midst of thee is mighty; he will save, he will rejoice over thee with joy; he will rest in his love, he will joy over thee with singing." — Zephaniah 3:17 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Zephaniah]] son of Cushi, son of Gedaliah, son of Amariah, son of Hezekiah — the unusually long genealogy (four generations) likely points to King Hezekiah as an ancestor, making Zephaniah a member of the royal family
- **Date Written:** ~630–625 BC — during the reign of King Josiah of Judah (640–609 BC), before Josiah's reform in 621 BC; Zephaniah may have helped catalyze that reform
- **Key Themes:** The comprehensive Day of the LORD (judgment on Judah, Jerusalem, all nations), radical judgment of religious syncretism and social injustice, the call to seek the LORD while there is time, the remnant of the humble, God rejoicing over his people
- **Chapters:** 3

## Summary

Zephaniah opens with one of the most sweeping statements of divine judgment in the OT: "I will utterly consume all things from off the land, saith the LORD" (1:2–3). Everything — man and beast, birds and fish — will be swept away. From this cosmic opening, the scope narrows progressively: from creation, to Judah, to Jerusalem, to specific social classes in Jerusalem (priests, officials, merchants), to the surrounding nations, and then back to Jerusalem for a final word of restoration.

The book's historical setting is crucial: Josiah came to the throne at age 8 after the long, idolatrous reigns of Manasseh and Amon. The temple had been desecrated; Baal worship, Asherah poles, and astral cults had infiltrated Jerusalem itself. Zephaniah's preaching during this period of religious chaos — before Josiah's reforms — gives the opening chapters their fierce specificity. He names who is being addressed: those who "swear by the LORD, and swear by Malcam" (1:5) — the syncretists who worship Yahweh and the Ammonite god simultaneously. He indicts the "princes and king's children, and all such as are clothed with strange apparel" (1:8) — the ruling class who have adopted foreign fashion along with foreign religion.

**Chapter 1** is dominated by the Day of the LORD (used 20+ times in the book). It is "a day of wrath... a day of distress and anguish, a day of wasteness and desolation, a day of darkness and gloominess" (1:15). The famous verse 1:14 — "The great day of the LORD is near, it is near, and hasteth greatly" — became the basis for the medieval Latin hymn *Dies irae* (Day of Wrath), used in Requiem Masses for centuries.

**Chapter 2** calls the nation to seek the LORD before judgment falls (2:1–3), then pronounces doom on the surrounding nations: Philistia (west), Moab and Ammon (east), Ethiopia (south), and Assyria (north) — all four compass points, signaling universal scope.

**Chapter 3** returns to Jerusalem to pronounce woe on her corrupt officials, prophets, and priests (3:1–8). Then the pivot: "For then will I turn to the people a pure language, that they may all call upon the name of the LORD" (3:9). God will purge and purify. The chapter ends with one of the OT's most tender passages: God himself will rejoice over his people with singing (3:17). The warrior-God who sweeps away everything is also the Father who sings over his children.

## Notes & Reflections

- Zephaniah 3:17 is one of the most remarkable verses in the OT — the warrior-God who opens chapter 1 sweeping away all creation ends the book singing a lullaby over his people; the emotional range of the book is extraordinary
- "I will utterly consume all things" (1:2–3) echoes the un-creation language of Genesis in reverse — the cosmic scope of the Day of the LORD exceeds any local judgment
- *Dies irae* (Day of Wrath) — the medieval Latin sequence sung at Catholic Requiem Masses is based on Zephaniah 1:14–15 ("the great day of the LORD is near"); it was set to music by Mozart, Verdi, Berlioz, and hundreds of others
- The *anawim* (humble poor, meek of the earth) in 2:3 and 3:12 become a major theme in the Psalms, Isaiah 61, and the Beatitudes of Jesus (Matthew 5:3–5) — those too poor and powerless to resist are the ones God particularly protects
- Zephaniah's royal lineage is significant: a prince of the blood speaking against the religious syncretism of the royal court; the indictment was from within, not from outside
