# Judges

> "And there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in his own eyes." — Judges 21:25 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Traditionally [[Samuel]] (internal evidence suggests a prophet-priest wrote it)
- **Date Written:** ~1050 BC (before the monarchy, after the conquest)
- **Key Themes:** Apostasy, oppression, deliverance, cycle of sin, divine patience, moral decline
- **Chapters:** 21
- **Source Text:** [[Judges (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

[[Judges]] covers roughly 350 years — from the death of [[Joshua (Son of Nun)]] to the death of [[Samuel]], bridging the conquest era and the monarchy. The book exposes the spiritual rot beneath Israel's surface: they inherited the land but failed to drive out the Canaanites, and within a generation they had abandoned the LORD for the gods of the peoples around them.

The central pattern of Judges is a devastating cycle:
1. **Apostasy** — Israel forsakes the LORD
2. **Oppression** — God hands them over to an enemy
3. **Crying out** — Israel repents and calls to God
4. **Deliverance** — God raises up a judge to save them
5. **Rest** — Peace during the judge's lifetime
6. **Repeat** — After the judge dies, the cycle begins again

Each cycle descends deeper into moral chaos. The book ends with four appendix stories (chapters 17–21) that show the nation at rock bottom — idolatry, violence, and sexual immorality run rampant. The refrain "there was no king in Israel" is both a diagnosis and a plea: Israel needs righteous leadership.

## Notes & Reflections

- Judges is arguably the darkest book of the Old Testament — it shows what happens when a covenant people abandon their covenant God
- The moral decline is measurable: each cycle gets worse, each judge more flawed
- Gideon starts with faith but ends with idolatry (the ephod) — even heroes can fall
- Samson has divine power but zero self-control — strength without wisdom is dangerous
- The appendix stories (17–21) aren't random — they show the end result of "every man doing what's right in his own eyes"
- The book argues FOR a king — not just any king, but a righteous, covenant-keeping king (ultimately Christ)
- Deborah is the only female judge — God uses whom He wills
- Jephthah's story is a caution against rash vows
- The book ends with Benjamin getting wives through violence — the cycle of sin has no clean resolution
- Judges proves that human leadership, however inspired, cannot save a broken people — they need a perfect King
