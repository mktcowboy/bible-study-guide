# Joshua

> "Be strong and of a good courage: for unto this land wilt thou bring the people... only be thou very courageous, that thou mayest observe to do according to all the law, which Moses my servant commanded thee." — Joshua 1:6-7 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Traditionally [[Joshua (Son of Nun)]], completed by [[Eleazar]] after Joshua's death (Joshua 24:29-31)
- **Date Written:** ~1400s BC
- **Key Themes:** Conquest, covenant faithfulness, divine warfare, rest, obedience, land distribution
- **Chapters:** 24
- **Source Text:** [[Joshua (KJV Source)]]

## Summary

[[Joshua]] picks up where [[Deuteronomy]] left off — [[Moses]] is dead, and a new generation stands at the edge of the Promised Land. God commissions [[Joshua (Son of Nun)]], Moses' aide and successor, to lead Israel across the [[Jordan River]] and into the land God promised to [[Abraham]], [[Isaac]], and [[Jacob]].

The book divides into four major sections:
- **Chapters 1–4: Crossing the Jordan** — God's commission of Joshua, the spy mission to [[Jericho]], the miraculous crossing of the Jordan on dry ground (mirroring the Red Sea crossing in [[Exodus]]).
- **Chapters 5–12: Conquest of Central & Southern Canaan** — Renewal of the covenant at Gilgal, the fall of [[Jericho]], the disaster at [[Ai]] (caused by [[Achan]]'s theft), the victory at Ai, the Gibeonite deception, the southern campaign, and the northern campaign.
- **Chapters 13–21: Division of the Land** — The territory east of the Jordan given to Reuben, Gad, and Manasseh; the western territory divided among the remaining nine-and-a-half tribes by lot; cities of refuge appointed; Levitical cities designated.
- **Chapters 22–24: Closing Events** — Dismissal of the eastern tribes, the great altar controversy, covenant renewal at Shechem, Joshua's farewell, and his death at 110.

Joshua is a book of fulfillment — the promises made in Genesis are now being realized. But it's also a warning: partial obedience is disobedience, and compromise with the Canaanite cultures will bring future trouble.

## Notes & Reflections

- Joshua is both a military leader and a covenant mediator — he leads conquest AND reads the Law
- The book shows divine warfare: God fights for Israel, but Israel must obey
- The pattern is clear: obedience = victory, disobedience = defeat (Ai is the textbook example)
- Joshua's personal faithfulness contrasts with the nation's future unfaithfulness (Judges)
- The "rest" of Joshua is a type of the ultimate rest in Christ (Hebrews 4)
- Rahab — a Canaanite harlot — becomes an ancestor of Christ, showing grace extends beyond Israel
- The unconquered territories left at the end foreshadow the compromise that will plague Israel in Judges
- Caleb's faithfulness at age 85+ shows that age is no barrier to serving God
- The covenant renewal at Shechem mirrors Deuteronomy — same choice: blessing or curse
