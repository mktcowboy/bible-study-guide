# 2 Kings

> "My father, my father, the chariot of Israel, and the horsemen thereof." - 2 Kings 2:12 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** Anonymous compiler working with royal and prophetic records
- **Date Written:** During or after the exile, after the events of 586 BC and the closing release of Jehoiachin
- **Key Themes:** Prophetic authority, covenant consequences, reform and relapse, Assyrian judgment, Babylonian exile, restrained hope
- **Chapters:** 25
- **Source Text:** [[2 Kings (KJV Source)]]

## Summary
[[2 Kings]] carries the divided-kingdom story from the close of [[Elijah]]'s ministry to the destruction of [[Jerusalem]]. The book opens with [[Ahaziah and Baal-zebub]], moves through [[Elijah Taken to Heaven]] and the long miracle cycle of [[Elisha]], turns through [[Jehu's Revolution]], and then narrows toward the fates of Israel and Judah in [[Fall of Samaria]] and [[Fall of Jerusalem]].

The book divides naturally into four movements:
- **Chapters 1-8: Prophetic succession and mercy** - [[Elijah]], [[Elisha]], household miracles, military deliverance, and foreign healing
- **Chapters 9-17: Dynastic upheaval and northern collapse** - [[Jehu]], unfinished reform, and the fall of Israel
- **Chapters 18-23: Judah's final bright lights** - [[Hezekiah]] and [[Josiah]] stand out amid accelerating judgment
- **Chapters 24-25: Babylon and exile** - Jerusalem falls, the temple is destroyed, and [[Jehoiachin]] survives in captivity

## Notes and Reflections
- [[2 Kings]] is not merely a sequence of kings; it is a sustained record of how God's word outlasts every throne.
- [[Elisha]]'s miracle cycle keeps mercy visible even while the kingdoms are decaying.
- The northern kingdom never truly recovers from the legacy of [[Jeroboam]].
- [[Hezekiah]] and [[Josiah]] shine brightly, but both reigns also show that reform inside history does not automatically erase accumulated judgment.
- The release of [[Jehoiachin]] matters because the book refuses to end with annihilation alone.
