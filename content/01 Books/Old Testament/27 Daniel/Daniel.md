# Daniel

> "I saw in the night visions, and, behold, one like the Son of man came with the clouds of heaven, and came to the Ancient of days, and they brought him near before him. And there was given him dominion, and glory, and a kingdom, that all people, nations, and languages, should serve him." — Daniel 7:13–14 (KJV)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Author:** [[Daniel (Prophet)|Daniel]] — a Jewish youth of noble lineage deported to Babylon in 605 BC under Nebuchadnezzar; given the Babylonian name Belteshazzar; served in the courts of Babylon and Persia for over 60 years; both a statesman and a prophet; contemporary of Ezekiel and Jeremiah
- **Date Written:** ~605–536 BC — the book covers Daniel's career from the first deportation (605 BC) to the third year of Cyrus (536 BC); the Maccabean dating hypothesis (165 BC) rests on the assumption that predictive prophecy is impossible; the Dead Sea Scrolls confirm the text was not late-authored
- **Key Themes:** God's sovereignty over all kingdoms, faithful witness under pressure, the succession of world empires ending in God's eternal kingdom, apocalyptic visions of the end, the Son of Man who receives universal dominion
- **Chapters:** 12

## Summary

Daniel is unique among the Major Prophets — half court narrative, half apocalyptic vision — and stands as the theological backbone of NT apocalyptic, including Revelation. A Judean noble taken captive in 605 BC, Daniel spends his entire career proving by deed and vision that the God of Israel, not the gods of Babylon, rules history.

**Bilingual structure:** Daniel is written in two languages with theological intentionality. The Hebrew sections (1:1–2:3; 8:1–12:13) address the Jewish people directly — Daniel's personal situation and Israel's future. The Aramaic section (2:4–7:28) — the international language of the ancient Near East — addresses the Gentile nations: God's message to the empire as a whole. The Aramaic section is itself a chiasm: chapters 2 and 7 (four kingdoms), 3 and 6 (furnace and lions' den — faithfulness under death threat), 4 and 5 (two kings humbled).

**Chapters 1–6 (The Court Narratives):** Daniel and his three friends (Hananiah, Mishael, Azariah — renamed Shadrach, Meshach, Abednego) are trained in Babylon's finest academy but refuse to compromise on dietary law (ch. 1), idolatry (ch. 3), and prayer (ch. 6). Each test produces divine vindication. Daniel interprets Nebuchadnezzar's statue dream (ch. 2) and the writing on the wall (ch. 5), and survives the lions' den under Darius (ch. 6). These are not primarily heroic tales but theological declarations: God's sovereignty is not diminished by exile.

**Chapters 7–12 (The Visions):** Four visions escalate in detail toward the end times. The four beasts (ch. 7) parallel the four-kingdom statue (ch. 2) but from God's perspective — what looks like power from below looks like predatory beasts from heaven. The Ancient of Days sits in judgment. A figure "like a Son of Man" approaches on the clouds and receives universal, eternal dominion — the most significant messianic vision in the OT. Jesus adopts "Son of Man" as his primary self-designation, grounding it in both Daniel 7:13–14 and Ezekiel's "son of man" (human frailty before divine majesty).

The Ram and Goat (ch. 8) precisely predicts the Medo-Persian and Greek empires and the career of Antiochus IV Epiphanes. The Seventy Weeks (ch. 9) — the most debated eschatological passage in the OT — maps out 490 years of Israel's history culminating in the cutting off of Messiah and the desolation of the sanctuary. Daniel 10–12 presents the longest single vision in the Bible: the detailed history of the Ptolemaic and Seleucid kingdoms, the career of Antiochus IV (a "type" of the final Antichrist), and the climactic resurrection of the dead.

## Notes & Reflections

- **The bilingual structure is theological:** The Aramaic section (2:4–7:28) is addressed to the Gentile world — God's word to the nations. The Hebrew sections address Israel directly. The Aramaic section is itself a chiasm: chs. 2 and 7 (four kingdoms), 3 and 6 (death-threat faithfulness), 4 and 5 (two kings humbled) — art built into the architecture
- **Daniel 7:13–14 is the most significant messianic vision in the OT:** Jesus uses "Son of Man" as his primary self-designation in the Gospels (80+ times); he draws from both Daniel 7 (universal dominion) and Ezekiel (human frailty before divine majesty); at his trial he explicitly applies Daniel 7:13 to himself, which the high priest considered blasphemy (Mark 14:62)
- **The Seventy Weeks (9:24–27) is the most debated end-times passage in Scripture:** Four major interpretive views: (1) entirely fulfilled by Antiochus IV and the Maccabean crisis; (2) fulfilled by Christ's first coming and AD 70 (partial preterist); (3) a "gap" between the 69th and 70th week — the church age — with the 70th week still future (dispensational); (4) fulfilled progressively through Christ's first coming, death, and second coming. The "Messiah cut off" (9:26) is almost universally agreed to refer to Christ's death
- **Nebuchadnezzar's conversion (ch. 4)** is a remarkable narrative — a Gentile king, through the humiliation of madness, comes to acknowledge and praise the God of Israel; "those that walk in pride he is able to abase" (4:37) — the book's thesis stated by its most unlikely witness
- **The fourth figure in the furnace (3:25)** — Nebuchadnezzar says "like a son of the gods" (*bar-elahin*); early church fathers uniformly interpreted this as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (Christophany); the Hebrew readers would hear "son of God" as divine agent
- **Daniel 12:2 is the clearest statement of individual bodily resurrection in the OT:** "Many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt" — Jesus alludes to this in John 5:28–29; it establishes that resurrection includes both the righteous and the wicked
