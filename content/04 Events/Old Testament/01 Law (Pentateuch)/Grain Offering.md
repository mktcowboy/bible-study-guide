# Grain Offering

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Leviticus]]
- **Chapters:** Leviticus 2; 6:14-23
- **Summary:** A non-blood offering of grain, oil, and frankincense expressing thanksgiving and dedication of one's labor to God.

## Details
The grain offering (Hebrew: *minchah*, "gift" or "tribute") was made of fine flour, either raw with oil and frankincense, baked in an oven, cooked on a griddle, or fried. No leaven or honey was permitted; salt was required. A handful was burned on the altar as a memorial portion; the remainder went to Aaron and his sons as part of the priestly provision.

Unlike the animal offerings, the grain offering required no blood — it represented the fruit of human labor and agriculture. It was rarely offered alone; it typically accompanied the burnt offering and peace offering, completing the act of worship by presenting both animal and agricultural produce to God.

The grain offering expressed that all human productivity belongs to God. The first fruits brought to the Tabernacle were themselves a form of grain offering acknowledging that the harvest came from the LORD.
