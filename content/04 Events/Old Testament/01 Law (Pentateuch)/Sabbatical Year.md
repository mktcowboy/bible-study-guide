# Sabbatical Year

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Leviticus]]
- **Chapters:** Leviticus 25; Deuteronomy 15
- **Summary:** Every seventh year the land was to rest; debts were to be released; and every fiftieth year (Jubilee) land reverted to its original family owners.

## Details
Leviticus 25 extends the Sabbath principle from days to years. Every seventh year (the Sabbatical Year or Shemitah) the land was to lie fallow — no sowing, pruning, or deliberate harvesting. What grew on its own could be eaten by owners, servants, hired workers, and animals alike. The law was grounded in theology: the land belongs to God, and its fertility is a gift, not an entitlement.

Deuteronomy 15 adds the dimension of debt release: every seven years Israelites were to cancel debts owed by fellow Israelites. This provision was to prevent the permanent enslavement of the poor. The warning in Deuteronomy 15:9 — don't harden your heart against a needy brother because the seventh year approaches — acknowledged the temptation to stop lending as Shemitah neared.

Every seventh Sabbatical Year (the 50th year overall) was the Jubilee: all land reverted to its ancestral family, all Israelite debt-slaves were freed. The Jubilee was grounded in Israel's memory of their own slavery in Egypt and redemption by God: "for they are my servants, which I brought forth out of the land of Egypt: they shall not be sold as bondmen" (25:42).

## Notes
- Leviticus 25:10 is inscribed on the Liberty Bell in Philadelphia.
- Jesus' sermon in Luke 4 uses Isaiah 61, which draws on the Jubilee language, to describe his own ministry as a proclamation of liberation.
- No historical evidence confirms the Jubilee was ever systematically observed, but the prophetic critique of unjust landholding (Isaiah 5, Micah 2) assumes its ongoing moral authority.
