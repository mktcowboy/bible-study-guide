# Tabernacles

## Overview
- **Testament:** Both
- **Book:** [[Leviticus]], [[John]]
- **Chapters:** Leviticus 23:33-43; John 7
- **Summary:** The joyful seven-day harvest feast celebrating Israel's wilderness sojourn; celebrated in booths; the most festive of all the appointed feasts.

## Details
The Feast of Tabernacles (Hebrew: *Sukkot*, "booths") began on the fifteenth of Tishri — five days after the Day of Atonement — and lasted seven days with an eighth day holy convocation. The people built and lived in temporary booths (sukkot) made of leafy branches to remember the wilderness journey when Israel lived in such dwellings. It was the great harvest festival, celebrating the ingathering of grain, wine, and oil, and was known simply as "the feast" in some texts (1 Kings 8:2; Nehemiah 8:14).

John 7 is set during Sukkot. On the last great day of the feast, when a water-pouring ceremony (not in the Mosaic text but established by tradition) was performed at the temple, Jesus stood and cried out: "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink." The announcement was deliberately timed to claim fulfillment of the feast's deepest meaning — the living water that the wilderness water-rock and the temple ceremonies both pointed toward.

Zechariah 14:16-19 prophesies that all nations will come to Jerusalem to celebrate Sukkot in the messianic age, making it the feast of the ingathering of all peoples.

## Notes
- The booths (sukkot) are read theologically as reminders of dependence — Israel survived in the wilderness not by their own strength but by God's provision.
- Jesus' declaration in John 7 during Tabernacles connects to the rock in the wilderness (1 Corinthians 10:4) and the living water promised to the Samaritan woman (John 4).
- Zechariah 14 gives Tabernacles the most explicitly universal eschatological significance of all the feasts.
