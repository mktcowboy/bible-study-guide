# Daughters of Zelophehad

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Numbers]]
- **Chapters:** Numbers 27:1-11; 36:1-12
- **Summary:** Five daughters of Zelophehad successfully petitioned Moses and God for their father's inheritance rights when he died without sons.

## Details
Zelophehad, a man from the tribe of Manasseh, died in the wilderness having no sons — only five daughters: Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah. Under existing inheritance customs, his name and property would pass out of his family entirely. The five daughters came before Moses, Eleazar the priest, and the entire congregation at the door of the tabernacle and made a reasoned case: their father was not among Korah's rebels and died for his own sin; why should his name disappear simply because he had no son?

Moses brought their case before God, who ruled in their favor: "The daughters of Zelophehad speak right: thou shalt surely give them a possession of an inheritance among their father's brethren." God then turned this individual case into a precedent for Israelite inheritance law — daughters could inherit when there were no sons, and a chain of secondary heirs was established for other cases.

In Numbers 36, the case was revisited: if the daughters married outside their tribe, the inheritance would transfer at Jubilee to their husbands' tribe and be permanently lost to Manasseh. God ruled they must marry within their tribe. The daughters complied, marrying their cousins.

## Notes
- This is one of the most explicit OT examples of legal precedent being established through appeal to God — a landmark in biblical law.
- The daughters were not asking for special treatment; they were appealing for equal standing under a principle their father had not violated.
- Joshua 17:3-6 records the actual fulfillment of the ruling when the land was divided.
