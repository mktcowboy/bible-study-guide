# Departure from Sinai

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Numbers]]
- **Chapters:** Numbers 10:11-36
- **Summary:** Israel broke camp at [[Mount Sinai]] and set out in the second year, the cloud leading the way by day; a system of silver trumpets signaled each movement.

## Details
Numbers 10 marks the end of the Sinai period. On the twentieth day of the second month of the second year after the Exodus, the cloud lifted from the Tabernacle and Israel marched out in prescribed tribal order. The journey from [[Mount Sinai]] to the promised land should have taken a matter of days; the wilderness wandering of forty years had not yet begun — that would come soon after at [[Kadesh-barnea]].

Before the march, God gave [[Moses]] instructions for making two silver trumpets. These were used to call the congregation, to signal the breaking of camp, to alarm for battle, and to mark appointed feasts. Different signals — short bursts versus continuous blasts, different numbers of trumpets — conveyed different meanings to the assembled people.

Moses invited his father-in-law [[Jethro]] (here called Hobab son of Reuel) to join Israel on the march and serve as eyes for them in the wilderness. The text preserves two brief liturgical prayers of Moses at the setting out and the stopping of the Ark: "Rise up, LORD, and let thine enemies be scattered" and "Return, O LORD, unto the many thousands of Israel."

## Notes
- Psalm 68 is widely read as a poem shaped around the march of the Ark: "Let God arise, let his enemies be scattered" echoes Moses' departure prayer.
- The trumpet system is the earliest description of a military/liturgical signal code in the Bible — an integration of worship and warfare that characterizes the wilderness narrative.
