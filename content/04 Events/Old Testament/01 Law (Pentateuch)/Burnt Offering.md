# Burnt Offering

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Leviticus]], [[Numbers]]
- **Chapters:** Leviticus 1; Numbers 28-29
- **Summary:** The wholly consumed offering of voluntary dedication and atonement; the entire animal was burned on the altar as a pleasing aroma to God.

## Details
The burnt offering (Hebrew: *olah*, "that which goes up") was the most complete of the Levitical offerings — the entire animal was burned on the altar, with nothing reserved for the worshipper or priest except the hide. It could be a bull, sheep, goat, or bird depending on the worshipper's means, making it accessible at every economic level.

The worshipper brought the animal to the Tabernacle entrance, laid his hand on its head (signifying identification), and slaughtered it. The priests applied the blood to the altar, arranged the pieces, and burned the whole offering. The act expressed total consecration — holding nothing back from God.

The burnt offering was also the daily offering (Numbers 28): one lamb in the morning and one in the evening formed the backbone of Israel's regular worship calendar. Additional burnt offerings marked Sabbaths, new months, and all the appointed feasts.
