# Clean and Unclean

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Leviticus]]
- **Chapters:** Leviticus 11–15
- **Summary:** Leviticus 11–15 defines clean and unclean foods, skin diseases, bodily discharges, and mold; the system marked the boundary between the holy and the common.

## Details
Leviticus 11 lists clean and unclean animals — land animals with split hooves and chewing cud are clean; sea creatures with fins and scales are clean; most birds of prey and insects are unclean, with the exception of certain locusts. The categories are not explained in terms of hygiene but in terms of holiness: "I am the LORD your God: ye shall therefore sanctify yourselves, and ye shall be holy; for I am holy" (11:44).

Chapters 12-15 address other forms of uncleanness: childbirth (Leviticus 12), skin diseases loosely translated as "leprosy" (13-14), and bodily discharges (15). Each form of uncleanness required a period of isolation and purification rituals before the person could re-enter the community and approach the Tabernacle. Priests were responsible for diagnosing and certifying cleanness.

The system served multiple functions: it created a boundary between Israel and surrounding cultures, maintained the distinction between holy (approaching God) and common (ordinary life), and provided a framework in which physical conditions could have spiritual significance. In the New Testament, Jesus regularly crosses clean/unclean boundaries — touching lepers, speaking to the bleeding woman, raising the dead — as a sign that the Kingdom of God overturns ritual exclusion.

## Notes
- Peter's vision in Acts 10 of "unclean" animals with a command to eat directly applies Leviticus 11 language to the Gentile inclusion question.
- The clean/unclean system is not abolished by Jesus but fulfilled — he touches the unclean and makes them clean rather than becoming unclean himself.
- Modern interpreters debate whether the categories had hygienic, symbolic, or social boundary functions — likely all three simultaneously.
