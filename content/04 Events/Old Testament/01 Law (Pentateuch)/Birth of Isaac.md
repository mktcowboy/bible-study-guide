# Birth of Isaac

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Genesis]]
- **Chapters:** Genesis 17:15-21; 18:1-15; 21:1-7
- **Summary:** God fulfilled his promise: the barren Sarah bore Isaac to Abraham when Abraham was 100 years old and Sarah was 90 — the child of laughter and the child of the covenant.

## Details
God had first promised Abraham a son through whom his covenant would continue in Genesis 15, reaffirmed it with the covenant of circumcision in Genesis 17, and repeated it at the oaks of Mamre through three angelic visitors in Genesis 18. On that last occasion, when the LORD declared "Sarah thy wife shall have a son," Sarah — overhearing from inside the tent — laughed to herself, thinking "After I am waxed old shall I have pleasure, my lord being old also?" The LORD responded: "Is any thing too hard for the LORD?" It was at this encounter that the promise was given a timeframe: "I will certainly return unto thee according to the time of life; and, lo, Sarah thy wife shall have a son."

In Genesis 21, exactly as God had promised, Sarah conceived and bore a son to Abraham in his old age. Abraham was one hundred years old. Abraham called his son's name Isaac — which in Hebrew (Yitzhak) means "he laughs" — a name given by God himself (17:19). Sarah declared: "God hath made me to laugh, so that all that hear will laugh with me." She also expressed the wonder: "Who would have said unto Abraham, that Sarah should have given children suck? for I have born him a son in his old age."

The birth of Isaac is the hinge of the patriarchal narrative — all the promises to Abraham were contingent on this one child born against biological impossibility. It demonstrates that God's covenant does not operate through natural human capacity but through divine creative power, establishing the pattern of the "miraculous birth" that runs through scripture to the virginal conception of Jesus.

## Notes
Paul uses the birth of Isaac as a theological illustration of justification by faith in Romans 4 — Abraham's faith in God "who quickeneth the dead" was credited as righteousness. In Galatians 4, Paul uses Isaac vs. Ishmael as an allegory of promise vs. law, spirit vs. flesh. The pattern — a barren woman, a divine promise, an impossible birth — recurs with Rebekah, Rachel, Manoah's wife (Samson's mother), Hannah, Elizabeth, and ultimately Mary, forming a typological chain pointing to the Incarnation.
