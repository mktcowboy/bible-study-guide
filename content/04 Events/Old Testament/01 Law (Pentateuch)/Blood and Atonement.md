# Blood and Atonement

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Leviticus]]
- **Chapters:** Leviticus 17
- **Summary:** Leviticus 17 establishes that blood is sacred because it is the life of the creature, and that God has appointed it as the means of atonement on the altar.

## Details
Leviticus 17 contains two interconnected laws. First, all slaughter of animals was to take place at the Tabernacle entrance — not in the open field — to prevent sacrifices being offered to "devils" (goat-idols, v.7) in the wilderness. This centralized all killing of animals that could be used for sacrifice, though this regulation likely applied in modified form once Israel spread through Canaan.

The theological core of the chapter is verse 11: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the altar to make an atonement for your souls: for it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul." Blood is not merely a bodily fluid but the seat of the creature's life — given by God. Because God owns all life, only God can appoint how life may atone for life.

The prohibition on eating blood (vv.10-14) follows directly from this theology. To eat blood would be to consume what belongs to God and violates the boundary between common nourishment and sacred atonement.

## Notes
- Hebrews 9 builds its entire argument about Christ's once-for-all sacrifice on the logic of Leviticus 17:11 — life for life, blood for blood.
- The prohibition on eating blood was among the regulations retained for Gentile believers in Acts 15, bridging Mosaic food law and the new covenant community.
- The theology of this chapter makes sacrifice rational rather than magical: blood atones because God has appointed it, not because blood has power in itself.
