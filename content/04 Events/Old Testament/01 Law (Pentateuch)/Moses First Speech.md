# Moses' First Speech

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Deuteronomy]]
- **Chapters:** Deuteronomy 1–3
- **Summary:** On the plains of Moab, [[Moses]] reviewed Israel's wilderness journey from Horeb to the eastern Jordan, recalling both failures and victories.

## Details
Deuteronomy opens with Moses addressing all Israel on the plains of Moab, forty years after leaving Egypt. His first speech (chapters 1-3) is essentially a selective historical review from Horeb to the present moment, designed to prepare the new generation to enter the land their parents had forfeited.

Moses recalled God's command to leave Horeb and take the land, the appointment of leaders to help him judge (based on Jethro's counsel in Exodus), and then the critical failure at Kadesh-barnea: the twelve spies brought back a discouraging report, the people rebelled and refused to enter, and God decreed that the first generation would not see the promised land — not even Moses, because of an unspecified failure at Meribah (though Deuteronomy 1:37 blames the people's sake). [[Joshua (Son of Nun)]] and [[Caleb]] were the only exceptions.

Moses then reviewed the wilderness years and the recent victories over the Amorite kings [[Sihon]] and [[Og]] east of the Jordan — the first territories taken as a down payment on the promise. He concluded by recounting the assignment of Transjordan to Reuben, Gad, and half of Manasseh, and his commissioning of Joshua to lead what he himself could not complete.

## Notes
- Deuteronomy's genius is that Moses retells the story with a theological lens — the point is not mere history but covenant exhortation for the new generation.
- His attribution of his own exclusion from the land to "your sakes" (1:37) reflects pastoral self-sacrifice in preaching rather than shifting blame.
- The conquest of Sihon and Og (chapters 2-3) is presented as proof that what God promised for Canaan, he had already begun to deliver east of the Jordan.
