# Giving of the Law

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Primary Text:** Exodus 19-20
- **Related Texts:** Exodus 24; Exodus 31-34; Deuteronomy 5
- **Summary:** At Mount Sinai, the LORD descends in fire, speaks the Ten Commandments, establishes covenant order with Israel, and gives Moses the covenant instructions that shape Israel's worship and life.

## Details

The giving of the law occurs after the exodus from Egypt. Grace precedes command: the LORD first redeems Israel, then calls Israel to covenant obedience. At Sinai, God declares that Israel is to be his treasured possession, a kingdom of priests, and a holy nation if they will obey his voice and keep his covenant.

The scene is filled with holy terror: thunder, lightning, thick cloud, trumpet sound, smoke, fire, and a quaking mountain. The people must consecrate themselves and keep boundaries because the holy God is drawing near. The law is therefore not presented as casual moral advice; it is the covenant word of the God who delivered Israel and now claims their whole life.

The Ten Commandments begin with grace and identity: "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt." The commands order worship, speech, time, family, life, marriage, property, truth, and desire. They reveal what covenant faithfulness looks like before God and neighbor.

Exodus 24 records covenant ratification with blood and a covenant meal. Moses ascends the mountain for forty days and receives the tablets, tabernacle instructions, and priestly arrangements. The golden calf interrupts the story almost immediately, showing Israel's inability to keep covenant even while the covenant is being given. Moses intercedes, the covenant is renewed, and the LORD proclaims his name as merciful, gracious, longsuffering, and just.

The New Testament does not treat the law as evil. It reveals sin, teaches holiness, and points toward fulfillment in [[Jesus Christ]]. The law reaches its proper goal in Christ, who fulfills it and forms a people who walk by the Spirit.

## Notes
- The law is given after redemption, not as a way to earn the exodus.
- Sinai teaches both access and distance: God draws near, but only on his terms.
- The golden calf shows that Israel needs more than tablets; the prophets later promise a law written on the heart.
- The law exposes sin, orders covenant life, and points toward Christ's fulfillment.
