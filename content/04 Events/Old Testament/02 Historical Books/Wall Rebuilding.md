# Wall Rebuilding

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nehemiah]]
- **Chapters:** Nehemiah 3-4
- **Summary:** Jerusalem's wall was rebuilt section by section by priests, Levites, merchants, nobles, and families from different towns, despite the mockery and military threats of Sanballat, Tobiah, and Geshem; workers labored armed and ready to fight.

## Details
Nehemiah 3 provides a detailed record of the wall repair: 42 groups working on specific sections from the Sheep Gate clockwise around the city. Priests rebuilt the Sheep Gate and consecrated it. Different families from Jericho, Tekoa, Gibeon, and other towns worked on adjacent sections. Goldsmiths, perfumers, merchants, and political officials all participated alongside priestly and Levitical families. The participation of people from their own houses (several times mentioned as working "over against his house") gave personal stakes to the project.

As the work progressed, Sanballat was furious and mocked: "What do these feeble Jews? will they fortify themselves? will they sacrifice? will they make an end in a day? will they revive the stones out of the heaps of the rubbish which are burned?" Tobiah added: "Even that which they build, if a fox go up, he shall even break down their stone wall." Nehemiah prayed and the people kept building. When the wall was half-built and the work progressed, Sanballat, Tobiah, the Arabians, Ammonites, and Ashdodites plotted to fight against Jerusalem. Nehemiah prayed, set a watch, and armed the workers: half worked while half held spears, shields, bows, and armor. Those who carried materials worked with one hand while holding a weapon with the other. A trumpeter stood by Nehemiah to sound the alarm for rallying.

## Notes
Nehemiah 3's detailed roster of builders is one of the most specific administrative records in the OT. The diversity of builders — priests, tradespeople, civic officials, women (Nehemiah 3:12 mentions Shallum's daughters) — illustrates a whole-community mobilization. The simultaneous work and armed defense (chapter 4) became a metaphor in Christian tradition for the posture of ministry in a hostile world: building with one hand, weapons ready in the other.
