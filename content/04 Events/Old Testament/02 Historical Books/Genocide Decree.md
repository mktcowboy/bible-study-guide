# Genocide Decree

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Esther]]
- **Chapters:** Esther 3
- **Summary:** Haman the Agagite obtained a royal decree from King Ahasuerus to destroy all Jews in the Persian empire on a date determined by lot; Mordecai and the Jews mourned and Mordecai urged Esther to act.

## Details
King Ahasuerus elevated Haman the Agagite to a position above all other princes and commanded that all bow down and pay homage to him. Mordecai refused, consistently and on principle. The other royal officials reported this to Haman, who was enraged and, learning that Mordecai was a Jew, determined to destroy not just Mordecai but all Jews throughout the empire. To find an auspicious date, Haman cast Pur (the lot) — thus the festival that commemorates these events is called Purim — and the lot fell on the 13th of Adar, nearly a year away. Haman then went to the king and, without naming the targeted people, framed them as a people who did not keep the king's laws and who were therefore a threat. He offered ten thousand talents of silver to fund the operation. The king gave Haman his signet ring to seal the decree and told him to do as he pleased with the people.

The decree was written and sent to all provinces: to destroy, kill, and annihilate all Jews — young and old, women and children — on a single day, the 13th of Adar, and to plunder their goods. The city of Susa was thrown into confusion. Mordecai tore his clothes, put on sackcloth and ashes, and wept bitterly outside the king's gate. Jews throughout the provinces mourned with fasting and weeping. Esther learned of Mordecai's grief and sent to inquire; he sent her a copy of the decree and urged her to go to the king and beg mercy for her people.

## Notes
Haman is identified as an "Agagite" — a descendant of Agag, king of the Amalekites whom Saul spared contrary to God's command (1 Samuel 15). The story thus has deep historical roots: the Amalekites were ancient enemies of Israel (Exodus 17), and this final confrontation between a Benjaminite (Mordecai) and an Amalekite descendant (Haman) echoes Saul's incomplete obedience. Mordecai's victory corrects what Saul failed to finish. The casting of lots (Pur) being the method of date selection ironically becomes the name of the festival celebrating deliverance — the very instrument of planned destruction becomes the label for salvation.
