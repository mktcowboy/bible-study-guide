# Intermarriage Crisis

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Ezra]]
- **Chapters:** Ezra 9-10
- **Summary:** Ezra learned that the returned exiles had married foreign women contrary to the Law; he mourned and prayed publicly in grief; a covenant assembly was called and the foreign marriages were dissolved.

## Details
Shortly after Ezra's arrival, the leaders reported to him that the people of Israel, the priests, and the Levites had not separated themselves from the peoples of the lands but had intermarried with them — Canaanites, Hittites, Perizzites, and others. The holy seed had "mingled themselves with the people of those lands." Ezra tore his garment and robe, pulled hair from his head and beard, and sat down appalled until the evening sacrifice. At the evening sacrifice he fell on his knees, spread his hands to the LORD and prayed an anguished confessional prayer (Ezra 9:6-15) — acknowledging Israel's history of sin, their undeserved mercy, and the gravity of the present violation.

A large assembly of men, women, and children gathered around Ezra and wept bitterly. One of the people, Shecaniah, acknowledged the sin but proposed a remedy: make a covenant with God to put away all the foreign wives and their children. Ezra rose and made the leading priests, Levites, and all Israel swear to do so. Proclamation went out to all the returned exiles: gather in Jerusalem within three days or forfeit all property and be expelled from the assembly. In the month Kislev they assembled in the open square before the temple, trembling in rain, and agreed to the covenant. Over three months the priests investigated each case; the list of those who had foreign wives is provided in Ezra 10:18-44.

## Notes
This episode is theologically and ethically complex. The prohibition on intermarriage was rooted not in ethnic purity but in the danger of religious syncretism — the historical pattern of foreign wives leading Israelite men into idolatry (Deuteronomy 7:3-4; 1 Kings 11:1-4). The dissolution of the marriages is harsh by modern standards but reflects the covenant community's recognition that their survival as a distinct people worshipping the true God was at stake. Ezra's prayer is one of the most powerful penitential prayers in the OT — he identifies himself fully with the people's sin despite not being personally guilty.
