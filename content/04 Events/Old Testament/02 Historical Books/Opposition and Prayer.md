# Opposition and Prayer

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nehemiah]]
- **Chapters:** Nehemiah 4
- **Summary:** As Jerusalem's wall rose, [[Sanballat]] and [[Tobiah]] escalated from mockery to conspiracy; [[Nehemiah]] responded with prayer and armed guards, and the work continued.

## Details
When [[Sanballat]] heard the wall was being rebuilt, he was furious and mocked publicly before his associates and the army of Samaria: "What do these feeble Jews?" [[Tobiah]] added his own taunt. [[Nehemiah]] responded with prayer — not a gentle petition but a sharp imprecatory plea: "Hear, O our God; for we are despised: and turn their reproach upon their own head."

The wall rose to half its height (4:6), which was the point of maximum vulnerability. Sanballat, Tobiah, and their allies began conspiring to attack Jerusalem and create confusion. The Jews were exhausted from the pace of work and felt the threat from all directions. Nehemiah set a watch, armed the builders, and stationed groups of people by families in the exposed places of the wall.

The organizational response was practical and memorable: half the people worked on the wall while the other half stood guard armed with spears, shields, bows, and armor. The workers building the wall used one hand for the trowel and one hand held a weapon. A trumpeter stood with Nehemiah ready to signal if attack came from any point, and the people were asked to sleep in Jerusalem rather than return to their towns.

## Key People Involved
- [[Nehemiah]] — Prayed and organized the defense
- [[Sanballat]] — Led the external opposition and mockery
- [[Tobiah]] — Joined Sanballat in opposition

## Notes
- Nehemiah's combination of prayer and practical action ("pray and post a guard") is one of the most cited examples of faith integrated with prudence in the OT.
- The "one hand on the trowel, one hand on the sword" image is deeply embedded in Christian vocabulary for engaged ministry in a resistant world.
