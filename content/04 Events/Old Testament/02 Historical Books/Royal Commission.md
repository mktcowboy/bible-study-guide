# Royal Commission

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nehemiah]]
- **Chapters:** Nehemiah 2:1-10
- **Summary:** [[Nehemiah]] asked King Artaxerxes for permission to return to Jerusalem and rebuild; the king granted letters of safe passage, timber, and authority.

## Details
Four months after hearing of Jerusalem's broken walls and praying (Nehemiah 1), Nehemiah was serving wine to King Artaxerxes when the king noticed his sadness. Though Nehemiah was "very sore afraid," he answered honestly: the city of his fathers' graves lay in ruins. When the king asked what he wanted, Nehemiah prayed briefly before answering — and then made a comprehensive request: letters of safe passage, letters to the keeper of the king's forest for timber, and unspoken authority that would soon become clear.

The king granted everything Nehemiah asked — "the good hand of my God upon me." He also sent army officers and cavalry with Nehemiah, an escort that the text contrasts favorably with Ezra's experience (Ezra 8:22).

The commission immediately drew opposition from [[Sanballat]] the Horonite and [[Tobiah]] the Ammonite, who were "grieved exceedingly" that someone had come to seek the welfare of Israel's children. The stage was set for the conflict that would shadow the entire rebuilding project.

## Key People Involved
- [[Nehemiah]] — Asked for and received the commission
- Artaxerxes — Persian king who granted permission and support
- [[Sanballat]] — Immediately opposed the commission
- [[Tobiah]] — Allied with Sanballat in opposition

## Notes
- Nehemiah's four-month gap between hearing and asking (Nehemiah 1:1; 2:1) reflects the combination of prayer, preparation, and waiting for the right moment that marks mature leadership.
- The commission's provision of timber and escort represents extraordinary royal favor — the kind that Nehemiah attributes directly to God's hand.
