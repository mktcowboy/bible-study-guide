# Ezra's Arrival

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Ezra]]
- **Chapters:** Ezra 7-8
- **Summary:** In the seventh year of Artaxerxes I (458 BC), Ezra the scribe arrived in Jerusalem leading a second wave of returnees; he bore a royal commission to appoint judges and teach the Law of God throughout the province.

## Details
Ezra son of Seraiah, a descendant of Aaron through the high priestly line, was a scribe skilled in the Law of Moses. The narrator introduces him with the summary: "he had prepared his heart to seek the law of the LORD, and to do it, and to teach in Israel statutes and judgments." In the seventh year of Artaxerxes I, Ezra led a second group of returnees from Babylon, the journey taking four months. He gathered the people at the river Ahava, fasted and prayed for protection on the journey (embarrassed to ask for a military escort after having declared the LORD's protection), and then set out.

Artaxerxes gave Ezra a remarkable royal commission: silver and gold from the king and his counselors, authority to draw on the royal treasury, and the power to appoint magistrates and judges to govern all the people beyond the river who knew the laws of God. All who did not know these laws were to be taught. The letter also exempted all priests, Levites, and temple servants from taxation. Ezra arrived in Jerusalem in the fifth month of the seventh year of Artaxerxes and delivered the silver and gold offerings to the temple treasury.

## Notes
Ezra arrives in Jerusalem roughly 58 years after the temple completion. He represents a third wave of reforming activity — after Zerubbabel's physical restoration and before Nehemiah's administrative reform. The three leaders form a triad: Zerubbabel (temple), Ezra (Law), Nehemiah (walls/governance). Ezra's description as a "ready scribe in the law of Moses" (7:6, KJV) positions him as the father of what later tradition calls the scribal movement — the transmission, study, and interpretation of Torah that shaped Second Temple Judaism.
