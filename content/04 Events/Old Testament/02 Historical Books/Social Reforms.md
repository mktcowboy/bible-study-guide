# Social Reforms

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nehemiah]]
- **Chapters:** Nehemiah 5
- **Summary:** Nehemiah confronted nobles and officials for charging exorbitant interest on loans from impoverished Jews; they publicly agreed to restore fields, vineyards, houses, and cancel debts; Nehemiah also refused personal enrichment as governor.

## Details
A great outcry arose among the people during the wall building. Poor families were mortgaging fields, vineyards, and houses to buy grain during the famine. Others were borrowing money at interest to pay the king's tax, with their children becoming debt-slaves. The nobles and officials were lending money and grain at usury to fellow Jews — contrary to the Law (Leviticus 25:36-37; Deuteronomy 23:19-20). Nehemiah was "very angry" when he heard these things and rebuked the nobles: "We after our ability have redeemed our brethren the Jews, which were sold unto the heathen; and will ye even sell your brethren?"

Nehemiah called a great assembly and confronted the nobles publicly: "The thing that ye do is not good: ought ye not to walk in the fear of our God because of the reproach of the heathen our enemies?" He demanded they restore the lands, vineyards, olive yards, and houses, plus the hundredth part of the money and grain and wine. The nobles agreed immediately and swore an oath before the priests. Nehemiah shook his robe and said: "So God shake out every man from his house, and from his labour, that performeth not this promise, even thus be he shaken out, and emptied." The people said "Amen" and the nobles did as they promised.

Nehemiah also recounted that he himself, as governor, did not take the governor's food allowance — which previous governors had taken as a burden on the people. He fed 150 Jews and officials at his table daily at his own expense, refusing to seek personal gain.

## Notes
This reform addresses the economic dimension of covenant faithfulness that the prophets had consistently emphasized. The jubilee provisions of Leviticus 25 — debt release, return of land — form the legal background. Nehemiah's self-description as a governor who did not enrich himself stands as a model of public servant integrity. His symbolic act of shaking his robe echoes prophetic gesture-prophecy. The rapid agreement of the nobles to restore everything suggests the public pressure of the assembled community was effective.
