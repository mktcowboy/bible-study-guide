# Ezra Reads the Law

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nehemiah]]
- **Chapters:** Nehemiah 8
- **Summary:** At the Water Gate, Ezra read the Law aloud to all Israel from early morning until midday; Levites circulated among the crowd to explain the meaning; the people wept at hearing God's word, then rejoiced when told the day was holy.

## Details
On the first day of the seventh month, all the people gathered as one man in the open square before the Water Gate and asked Ezra the scribe to bring the Book of the Law of Moses. Ezra stood on a wooden platform made for the purpose, with six men on his right and seven on his left. He opened the book in sight of all the people — all could see him — and when he opened it the people stood. He blessed the LORD, and all the people answered "Amen, Amen," lifting their hands and bowing their heads to the ground in worship.

Ezra and the Levites "read in the book in the law of God distinctly, and gave the sense, and caused them to understand the reading" — a description of what later became known as the targum (Aramaic paraphrase/explanation). The reading lasted from early morning until midday. All the people wept when they heard the words of the Law. But Nehemiah the governor, Ezra the priest, and the Levites told the people: "This day is holy unto the LORD your God; mourn not, nor weep" — "neither be ye sorry; for the joy of the LORD is your strength." They were to eat, drink, and send portions to those who had nothing. The people went away to eat and drink with great rejoicing, for they understood the words that were declared to them. The following day the Law was read again and they found the instruction about the Feast of Tabernacles — they immediately built booths and celebrated it with great joy for seven days.

## Notes
This assembly is one of the most significant scenes of biblical worship in the OT — essentially a founding moment for synagogue-style Torah reading and exposition. The fact that the Levites gave "the sense" (the meaning) is the earliest description of biblical interpretation as a community practice. The shift from weeping to rejoicing is theologically important: the Law rightly understood does not end in guilt but in the joy of knowing God's word. Ezra's public ministry of the word represents the culmination of his stated life purpose (Ezra 7:10).
