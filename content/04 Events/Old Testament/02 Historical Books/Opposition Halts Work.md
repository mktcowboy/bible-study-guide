# Opposition Halts Work

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Ezra]]
- **Chapters:** Ezra 4
- **Summary:** The peoples of the surrounding region wrote to King Artaxerxes accusing the Jews of building a rebellious city; an official stop-work order was issued and temple construction ceased for years.

## Details
When the enemies of Judah and Benjamin heard that the returning exiles were building a temple, they offered to help — claiming to worship the same God since the days of Esarhaddon. Zerubbabel, Jeshua, and the other heads of families refused: "You have nothing to do with us to build an house unto our God." The adversaries then set out to discourage and frustrate the builders, hiring counselors against them throughout the reign of Cyrus and into the reign of Darius.

Ezra 4 inserts a chronological note about later opposition that clarifies the pattern: in the days of Ahasuerus the adversaries wrote an accusation against the inhabitants of Judah. During the reign of Artaxerxes, Bishlam, Mithredath, and others wrote a letter in Aramaic arguing that Jerusalem was a rebellious city and that if it were rebuilt it would stop paying tribute to Persia. They warned that the king should search the records and discover the city's history of insurrection. Artaxerxes replied that the records confirmed Jerusalem's past, and he ordered construction stopped. The adversaries went immediately and stopped the work by force and authority.

## Notes
Ezra 4 uses a telescoping literary technique, presenting opposition from multiple Persian reigns in a single chapter to make the thematic point: the adversaries consistently sought to frustrate God's work. The Aramaic section (4:8-6:18) reflects the official Persian administrative correspondence preserved in the source documents. The work remained stopped until the second year of Darius I (520 BC), when the prophets Haggai and Zechariah stirred the community to recommence.
