# Esther Becomes Queen

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Esther]]
- **Chapters:** Esther 2:1-18
- **Summary:** After a kingdom-wide search for Vashti's replacement, the Jewish orphan Esther — raised by her cousin Mordecai — was chosen as queen; on Mordecai's instruction she concealed her Jewish identity.

## Details
When the king's anger subsided and he remembered Vashti, his servants proposed gathering beautiful young virgins from across the empire; the woman who pleased the king most would become queen. Esther, a Jewish girl named Hadassah, was among those brought to the palace. She was the adopted daughter of her cousin Mordecai of the tribe of Benjamin, who had raised her after the death of her parents. She had a beautiful figure and was lovely to look at.

Esther was brought to the king's harem under the care of Hegai, the keeper of the women. She won his favor immediately, and he provided her with beauty treatments, special food, seven maids, and the best rooms. On Mordecai's instruction, she did not reveal her people or her kindred. Every day Mordecai walked before the court of the women's quarters to learn how Esther fared. After twelve months of preparation — six months with oil of myrrh and six months with spices — each young woman had one night with the king. When Esther's turn came, she asked nothing beyond what Hegai advised, and she won favour in the sight of all who saw her. The king loved Esther more than all the women and set the royal crown upon her head and made her queen in place of Vashti. He proclaimed a great feast in Esther's honor and granted a holiday throughout the provinces.

## Notes
Mordecai's instruction for Esther to hide her identity (2:10, 20) is a detail that will drive the tension of the story — her true identity, when revealed, is what makes her the essential person to intercede for her people. The book's complete absence of explicit divine language forces the reader to see Providence working through Esther's beauty, Hegai's favour, and the king's preference. The concealment of Esther's Jewishness is a classic story of identity in diaspora — a recurring theme in Jewish literature.
