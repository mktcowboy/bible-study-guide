# Jonah's Anger and God's Question

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Jonah]]
- **Chapters:** Jonah 4
- **Summary:** Furious at God's mercy toward Nineveh, Jonah reveals he always knew God would relent — quoting Exodus 34:6 as a complaint — then grieves the death of a sheltering gourd more than he cares for 120,000 souls, provoking God's closing unanswered question: "Shouldest not I spare Nineveh?"

## Details

The fourth chapter is the theological heart of Jonah, and it is structured around a single devastating contrast. The chapter opens: "But it displeased Jonah exceedingly, and he was very angry" (4:1). The mission was a complete success by every external measure — city-wide repentance, royal decree, God's mercy confirmed. Jonah's response is fury. His explanation to God is remarkable in its candor and in its theology: "Was not this my saying, when I was yet in my country? Therefore I fled before unto Tarshish: for I knew that thou art a gracious God, and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repentest thee of the evil" (4:2). The words Jonah quotes — "gracious... merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness" — are a direct citation of Exodus 34:6, the great self-declaration God made to Moses at Sinai, the theological center of the entire OT's portrait of God. Jonah does not quote it in worship. He quotes it as a complaint. He ran not because he doubted God's judgment would come, but because he *knew* God's mercy would follow repentance, and he did not want [[Nineveh]] to receive it.

Jonah then requests his own death: "Therefore now, O LORD, take, I beseech thee, my life from me; for it is better for me to die than to live" (4:3). God's response is a question — "Doest thou well to be angry?" (4:4) — which Jonah does not answer. He goes east of the city, builds a shelter, and sits watching, apparently hoping God will still destroy it. God appoints a gourd to grow over Jonah's shelter, providing shade, and "Jonah was exceeding glad of the gourd" (4:6). The next day, God appoints a worm to smite the gourd, then a vehement east wind and scorching sun, until Jonah again faints and asks again to die: "It is better for me to die than to live" (4:8).

God's question is now made explicit: "Thou hast had pity on the gourd, for the which thou hast not laboured, neither madest it grow; which came up in a night, and perished in a night: And should not I spare Nineveh, that great city, wherein are more than sixscore thousand persons that cannot discern between their right hand and their left hand; and also much cattle?" (4:10–11). The gourd cost Jonah nothing; he did not plant it or tend it. Yet he mourned its loss with genuine grief. How much more should God — who made, sustains, and loves every person in the city — care about 120,000 people who are spiritually without orientation? The "sixscore thousand" who cannot discern their right hand from their left may describe young children, or may describe the entire spiritually ignorant city. Either way, the implication is the same: they are not morally sophisticated enemies but confused, needy human beings — and cattle, too, are in God's care.

The book ends on that unanswered question. Jonah never replies. The reader is left to supply the answer — or to recognize themselves in Jonah's silence.
