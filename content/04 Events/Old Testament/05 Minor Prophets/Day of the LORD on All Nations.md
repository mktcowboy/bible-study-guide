# Day of the LORD on All Nations

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Obadiah]]
- **Chapters:** Obadiah 1:15-21
- **Summary:** Obadiah expands the judgment on Edom into a universal horizon — the Day of the LORD falls on all nations, the house of Jacob becomes fire and the house of Esau stubble, and the concluding declaration is that "the kingdom shall be the LORD's."

## Details

The oracle against Edom pivots at verse 15 from the particular to the universal: "For the day of the LORD is near upon all the heathen: as thou hast done, it shall be done unto thee: thy reward shall return upon thine own head" (1:15). What began as a specific prophecy against Edom now opens onto the full eschatological landscape — the Day of the LORD, which the prophets consistently deploy as the moment when divine justice catches up with history, when the accumulated ledger of human violence and pride is settled. Edom is not merely a nation that will be punished; it is the representative of all nations that have lifted themselves against the LORD and his people. The particular fate of Edom becomes the paradigm case for what awaits all who have done to others as they pleased with no fear of recompense.

The cup imagery of verse 16 is significant: "For as ye have drunk upon my holy mountain, so shall all the heathen drink continually, yea, they shall drink, and they shall swallow down, and they shall be as though they had not been." The image of the cup of divine wrath appears across the prophets (Isaiah 51:17; Jeremiah 25:15-17; Habakkuk 2:16; Revelation 14:10) and represents the inescapable moral consequence of a universe governed by a righteous God — what the nations have dispensed to others they will themselves drink. Edom celebrated on the mountain of Jerusalem (the "holy mountain") when it fell; now the nations, as Edom, will drink on their own holy mountains. The poetic justice of the cup reciprocates exactly what was poured out.

The imagery shifts to fire and stubble in verse 18: "And the house of Jacob shall be a fire, and the house of Joseph a flame, and the house of Esau for stubble, and they shall kindle in them, and devour them; and there shall not be any remaining of the house of Esau; for the LORD hath spoken it." The reunited northern (Joseph) and southern (Jacob) houses of Israel become the instrument of Edom's final destruction. This is not simple vengeance narrative — it is the prophetic vision of restored Israel as a participant in divine justice, a theme running through the prophets from Isaiah to Zechariah. The people who had been devoured become the devouring fire; the people who had been scattered now possess the possessions of the nations (1:19-20).

The geography of 1:19-20 is a restoration map: the Negev, the Shephelah, Ephraim, Samaria, Gilead — territories of both the northern and southern kingdoms are repossessed. The vision looks beyond the immediate post-exilic return to something comprehensive, a reconstitution of the full land of promise. Obadiah thus ends where the prophets consistently end: "And saviours shall come up on mount Zion to judge the mount of Esau; and the kingdom shall be the LORD's" (1:21). The final note is not Israel's victory but the LORD's kingdom — the whole cascade of judgment and restoration has its telos not in national pride vindicated but in divine sovereignty manifested. The Day of the LORD on all nations is the Day the LORD's kingship becomes visibly, universally undeniable.
