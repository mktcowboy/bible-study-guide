# Social Justice Indictment

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Amos]]
- **Chapters:** Amos 2–8
- **Summary:** Amos delivers sustained indictments of Israel's systemic exploitation of the poor — selling the righteous for silver, trampling the needy, using dishonest scales — while the ruling class reclines on ivory beds and performs elaborate worship that God declares he despises.

## Details

The social indictment of Amos spans the heart of the book and constitutes the most comprehensive OT critique of economic injustice within a covenant people. The charges begin in Amos 2:6–8 — selling "the righteous for silver, and the poor for a pair of shoes" — and gather momentum through chapters 4, 5, 6, and 8. What makes the indictment theologically striking is its context: Israel under Jeroboam II was at the peak of its political and military power, with expanding borders and material prosperity. The prosperity was real, but it was built on exploitation, and the worship that accompanied it was therefore an abomination.

The "cows of Bashan" in chapter 4 — a phrase addressed to the wealthy women of [[Samaria]] who pressure their husbands to bring wine while the poor are crushed (4:1) — reveals the social texture of the indictment. Luxury and callousness are intertwined. Chapter 5 contains the most famous passage: "I hate, I despise your feast days, and I will not smell in your solemn assemblies. Though ye offer me burnt offerings and your meat offerings, I will not accept them" (5:21–22). The worship at [[Bethel]] and [[Gilgal]] had not stopped; the problem was not the cessation of religion but religion's continued practice alongside economic exploitation. Amos does not call for less worship but for justice: "But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream" (5:24). This is not an argument against worship but a declaration that worship divorced from justice is not acceptable to God.

Chapter 6 indicts the ruling class in Samaria who are "at ease in Zion" — reclining on beds of ivory, stretching themselves on couches, eating lambs from the flock and calves from the stall, inventing instruments of music, drinking wine from bowls, and anointing themselves with chief ointments — "but they are not grieved for the affliction of Joseph" (6:6). The specific charge is not wealth but the willful blindness of luxury in the face of social collapse. Chapter 8 returns to the merchants who falsify measures, make the ephah small and the shekel great, and sell "the refuse of the wheat" to the poor (8:5–6). The Day of the LORD, these merchants assume, will be a good day for Israel; Amos announces it will be darkness and not light (5:18–20).

## Key People Involved
- [[Amos (Prophet)|Amos]] — the Tekoite shepherd who delivers this indictment, himself from outside the ruling class
- [[Jeroboam II]] — king under whose prosperous reign the injustice flourished unchallenged
- [[Amaziah]] — priest of Bethel who attempts to silence Amos in response to these indictments (7:10–17)

## Key Places
- [[Samaria]] — the capital where the wealthy ruling class resided; target of the "cows of Bashan" oracle
- [[Bethel]] — the royal sanctuary where worship was performed alongside exploitation
- [[Gilgal]] — another cultic site condemned alongside Bethel in the indictment (5:5)

## Important Verses
> "I hate, I despise your feast days, and I will not smell in your solemn assemblies. Though ye offer me burnt offerings and your meat offerings, I will not accept them: neither will I regard the peace offerings of your fat beasts." — Amos 5:21–22

> "But let judgment run down as waters, and righteousness as a mighty stream." — Amos 5:24

> "That sell the righteous for silver, and the poor for a pair of shoes; that pant after the dust of the earth on the head of the poor, and turn aside the way of the meek." — Amos 2:6–7
