# The Messenger and the Sun of Righteousness

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Malachi]]
- **Chapters:** Malachi 3:1; 4:2, 5
- **Summary:** Malachi's closing oracles predict a forerunner messenger who will prepare the way of the LORD, a "sun of righteousness" arising with healing, and the return of Elijah before the great Day of the LORD—all fulfilled in John the Baptist's ministry and Christ's coming.

## Details
The final chapter and a half of Malachi constitute the last words of the Old Testament prophetic canon, and they face forward with unusual precision. Three distinct but interlocking promises appear in close proximity. The first is in Malachi 3:1: "Behold, I will send my messenger, and he shall prepare the way before me: and the LORD, whom ye seek, shall suddenly come to his temple, even the messenger of the covenant, whom ye delight in: behold, he shall come, saith the LORD of hosts." Two figures appear in this verse: a forerunner messenger who prepares the way, and the LORD himself who suddenly comes. The structure closely parallels Isaiah 40:3 ("The voice of him that crieth in the wilderness, Prepare ye the way of the LORD").

The second promise appears in Malachi 4:2: "But unto you that fear my name shall the Sun of righteousness arise with healing in his wings." The solar imagery portrays the coming one as bringing light and health to those who have lived in covenant faithfulness. "Wings" may refer to the rays of the sun or to the hem of the priestly garment. The verse concludes with striking energy: those who feared God's name will "go forth, and grow up as calves of the stall"—released from confinement to joyful freedom.

The third promise is Malachi 4:5: "Behold, I will send you Elijah the prophet before the coming of the great and dreadful day of the LORD." This final promise links the forerunner of 3:1 with the specific figure of Elijah, creating the expectation that bore fruit in first-century Jewish expectation about the Messiah's precursor. Jesus himself resolved the question directly in Matthew 11:14: "And if ye will receive it, this is Elias, which was for to come," referring to John the Baptist. In Mark 1:2-3, the Gospel opens by quoting both Malachi 3:1 and Isaiah 40:3 as the framework for John's ministry. John himself, when asked if he was Elijah, said he was not (John 1:21)—but Jesus' own testimony interpreted the typological fulfillment. The "Elijah spirit and power" (Luke 1:17) of John the Baptist brought to completion the last promise of the Hebrew prophets across the four-century silence between Testaments.
