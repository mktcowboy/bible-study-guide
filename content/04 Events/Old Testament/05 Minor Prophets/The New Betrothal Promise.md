# The New Betrothal Promise

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Hosea]]
- **Chapters:** Hosea 2:14–23
- **Summary:** Following the covenant lawsuit and the stripping of Israel's gifts, God promises to woo Israel back into the wilderness, speak tenderly to her, and betroth her anew in righteousness, mercy, and faithfulness, renewing what the Baal worship had destroyed.

## Details

The pivot from judgment to promise in Hosea 2 is one of the most dramatic reversals in all of prophetic literature. After the severe stripping of covenant blessings described in 2:1–13, the tone shifts entirely at verse 14: "Therefore, behold, I will allure her, and bring her into the wilderness, and speak comfortably unto her" (2:14). The Hebrew verb for "speak comfortably" is *dibber al-lev* — literally "speak to the heart." The wilderness, which might seem like a place of punishment, becomes the setting for courtship, recalling the intimacy of Israel's early relationship with God following the Exodus, before the corruption of Canaan.

The gifts that were stripped in the lawsuit are now re-given as covenant blessings: "And I will give her her vineyards from thence, and the valley of Achor for a door of hope" (2:15). The valley of Achor — the place of Achan's sin and judgment in Joshua 7 — becomes "a door of hope," the OT's most radical reversal of a place of shame. Israel will respond "as in the days of her youth, and as in the day when she came up out of the land of Egypt" (2:15). The Baal names will be removed from her lips; she will call God "Ishi" (my husband) instead of "Baali" (my lord/master) — a shift from legal title to intimate relationship (2:16).

The betrothal language of 2:19–20 is the theological summit of the entire book. God declares: "And I will betroth thee unto me for ever; yea, I will betroth thee unto me in righteousness, and in judgment, and in lovingkindness, and in mercies. I will even betroth thee unto me in faithfulness: and thou shalt know the LORD." The fourfold list — righteousness, judgment, lovingkindness, mercies — names the covenant virtues that Israel has failed; God now pledges to provide them from his own side as the foundation of the new relationship. The promise ends with the restoration of the three children's names: Jezreel (restored as abundance), Lo-Ruhamah reversed to Ruhamah ("she obtained mercy"), and Lo-Ammi reversed to Ammi ("my people") — the full arc of judgment undone. Paul quotes from this passage in Romans 9:25–26 to support the inclusion of Gentiles among the people of God.
