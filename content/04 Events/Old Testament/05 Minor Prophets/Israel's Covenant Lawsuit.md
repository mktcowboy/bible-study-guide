# Israel's Covenant Lawsuit

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Hosea]]
- **Chapters:** Hosea 2; 4
- **Summary:** God brings a formal legal indictment against Israel as an unfaithful wife, charging her with abandoning covenant love and the knowledge of God in favor of the Baals, and stripping her of the gifts she credited to her false lovers.

## Details

Hosea 2 opens as a legal proceeding — a *rib* (lawsuit) in the form of a covenant complaint — in which God addresses Israel (figured as Gomer) directly. The opening charge is abandonment: Israel has pursued her lovers (the Baal deities of Canaan) and credited them with the grain, wine, and oil that actually came from God (2:8). The Baal cult was an agricultural fertility religion; Israel worshipped at the local high places, believing the Baals controlled the rain and the harvest. God's indictment reveals the irony: "She did not know that I gave her corn, and wine, and oil, and multiplied her silver and gold, which they prepared for Baal" (2:8). The covenant husband has been mistaken for a foreign god.

The legal consequence is divestiture. God declares he will "return and take away my corn in the time thereof, and my wine in the season thereof, and will recover my wool and my flax given to cover her nakedness" (2:9). This stripping of gifts mirrors the formal divorce proceedings of the ancient Near East, where a dismissed wife was returned to the state in which she entered the marriage. The gifts — grain, wine, oil, wool, flax — were given by God as covenant blessings (Deuteronomy 7:13) and are now revealed to be under his sovereign control. The festivals and sabbaths will be ended; the vines and fig trees destroyed; the appointed feasts ceased (2:11–13).

Hosea 4 extends the lawsuit to encompass the entire moral and religious life of the nation: "Hear the word of the LORD, ye children of Israel: for the LORD hath a controversy with the inhabitants of the land, because there is no truth, nor mercy, nor knowledge of God in the land" (4:1). These three absences — truth (*emet*), mercy (*hesed*), and knowledge of God (*da'at Elohim*) — form the theological core of the indictment. The first two are covenant virtues that ought to characterize human relationships; the third describes the intimate relational knowing that ought to characterize Israel's relationship with God. In their absence, bloodshed, stealing, adultery, and violence break out (4:2). The priests bear particular blame: "My people are destroyed for lack of knowledge: because thou hast rejected knowledge, I will also reject thee, that thou shalt be no priest to me" (4:6). The indictment does not merely describe individual sins but a systemic spiritual failure rooted in the loss of the knowledge of God.
