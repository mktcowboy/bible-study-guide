# Habakkuk's Complaint

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Habakkuk]]
- **Chapters:** Habakkuk 1:1-4
- **Summary:** The prophet Habakkuk cries out to God over the unchecked violence and injustice in Judah, asking how long God will allow evil to triumph, and receives the startling answer that God is raising up the Chaldeans as his instrument of judgment.

## Details
Habakkuk opens with a cry that distinguishes it from nearly every other prophetic book: it is not proclamation to the people but complaint to God. "O LORD, how long shall I cry, and thou wilt not hear! even cry out unto thee of violence, and thou wilt not save!" The prophet is not doubting God's existence but is wrestling with his apparent inactivity in the face of the wickedness he sees around him in Judah. The situation on the ground is dire: "the law is slacked, and judgment doth never go forth: for the wicked doth compass about the righteous; therefore wrong judgment proceedeth." Courts are corrupt, the Torah is ignored, and the righteous are surrounded by the unrighteous without recourse.

This opening lament frames Habakkuk as a unique prophetic dialogue—a back-and-forth between the prophet and God that occupies the first two chapters before resolving in a psalm of faith in chapter three. God's first answer to Habakkuk's complaint is deeply unsettling: he is indeed doing something about the injustice in Judah, but the instrument of his action will be the Chaldeans (Babylonians). "For, lo, I raise up the Chaldeans, that bitter and hasty nation, which shall march through the breadth of the land, to possess the dwellingplaces that are not theirs" (Habakkuk 1:6). This answer produces Habakkuk's second complaint: how can a holy God use a nation even more wicked than Judah to punish Judah?

The dialogue format makes Habakkuk one of the most theologically probing books in the Old Testament. Its questions—Why does God allow evil? Why does he seem silent? How can he use a worse nation to judge a sinful but less wicked one?—are questions every believer encounters. The book provides not a philosophical answer but a relational one: God is sovereign, he is acting even when unseen, and the righteous are called to live by faith in that conviction. The complaint of Habakkuk 1:1-4 is the necessary starting point for one of Scripture's most sustained reflections on theodicy and trust.
