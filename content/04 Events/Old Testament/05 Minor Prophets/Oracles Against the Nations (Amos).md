# Oracles Against the Nations (Amos)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Amos]]
- **Chapters:** Amos 1–2
- **Summary:** Amos delivers eight oracles of judgment against the surrounding nations — Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon, Moab, then Judah — before turning with the longest and most devastating oracle against Israel itself, springing the rhetorical trap on his northern audience.

## Details

Amos begins with a thunderclap: "The LORD will roar from Zion, and utter his voice from Jerusalem; and the habitations of the shepherds shall mourn, and the top of Carmel shall wither" (1:2). From his position as a shepherd from [[Tekoa]] in Judah, Amos proceeds to deliver eight oracles in rapid succession, each introduced with the formula "For three transgressions of [nation], and for four, I will not turn away the punishment thereof." The "three for four" formula is a wisdom idiom indicating not a precise count but a series of accumulated offenses that have reached and exceeded the limit of divine patience.

The condemned nations form a geographic ring around Israel: Damascus (Syria) is judged for threshing Gilead with iron (1:3); Gaza for delivering captives to Edom (1:6); [[Tyre]] for breaking a brotherly covenant and delivering captives (1:9); [[Edom]] for pursuing his brother with the sword, his anger tearing perpetually (1:11); Ammon for ripping open pregnant women in Gilead to enlarge territory (1:13); Moab for burning the bones of the king of Edom into lime (2:1). Each oracle ends with fire, destruction, and exile for the offending nation. A northern Israelite audience hearing these oracles would have been nodding enthusiastically — these are Israel's enemies, and God is condemning them.

Then the rhetoric turns. Judah is condemned (2:4–5) — for rejecting the law of the LORD and following lies. The audience may still have been comfortable; Judah was the rival southern kingdom. But the oracle against Israel in 2:6–16 is the longest and most devastating of all eight, and it arrives without a change in formula or tone. Israel is condemned not for the atrocities of foreign wars but for domestic injustice: selling the righteous for silver and the poor for a pair of shoes (2:6), sexual exploitation at the altar (2:7), drinking wine from the condemned in the house of their god (2:8), and silencing the prophets and Nazirites God had sent (2:12). The rhetorical trap is complete. Amos has drawn Israel into the role of moral judge over the nations and then placed Israel in the dock.

The final verses (2:13–16) describe the inescapability of judgment — the swift, the strong, the horseman, the bowman, none will escape when God acts. The literary structure of the eight oracles — each building on the last, the circle tightening from the furthest nations to Israel's closest neighbor to Israel itself — is one of the most carefully constructed rhetorical sequences in the OT.

## Key People Involved
- [[Amos (Prophet)|Amos]] — the Tekoite shepherd-prophet who delivers the oracles
- [[Jeroboam II]] — king of Israel during whose reign the prosperity and injustice Amos addresses flourished

## Key Places
- [[Tekoa]] — Amos's hometown; the base from which he is called to prophesy in Israel's north
- [[Bethel]] — the northern cultic site, later confrontation point for Amos (7:10–17)
- [[Samaria]] — the capital of the northern kingdom, ultimate target of judgment
- [[Edom]] — among the nations condemned; also the betraying brother nation condemned in [[Obadiah]]
- [[Tyre]] — condemned for covenant betrayal; trading Israelites as slaves

## Important Verses
> "For three transgressions of Israel, and for four, I will not turn away the punishment thereof; because they sold the righteous for silver, and the poor for a pair of shoes." — Amos 2:6

> "The LORD will roar from Zion, and utter his voice from Jerusalem; and the habitations of the shepherds shall mourn, and the top of Carmel shall wither." — Amos 1:2
