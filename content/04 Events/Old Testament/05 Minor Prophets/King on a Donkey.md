# King on a Donkey

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Zechariah]]
- **Chapters:** Zechariah 9:9-10
- **Summary:** Zechariah prophesies the coming of Jerusalem's king—just, having salvation, lowly, and riding upon a donkey—a prediction fulfilled at Christ's Triumphal Entry into Jerusalem on Palm Sunday.

## Details
Zechariah 9:9 stands among the most precisely fulfilled messianic prophecies in all of Scripture. Written roughly 500 years before the events of the first Palm Sunday, it specifies not only the nature of the coming king but the mode of his entrance: "Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion; shout, O daughter of Jerusalem: behold, thy King cometh unto thee: he is just, and having salvation; lowly, and riding upon an ass, and upon a colt the foal of an ass." The fourfold description of the king is remarkable: he is just (righteousness is his character), having salvation (not merely promising it but possessing or bringing it), lowly (humble, not arriving with military pomp), and riding on a donkey's colt rather than a war horse.

In the ancient Near East, a conqueror arrived on a horse; a donkey was a symbol of peace. Solomon had ridden a mule to his own coronation at the king's request (1 Kings 1:33). The king of Zechariah 9 comes not in the posture of military triumph but in the posture of peaceful accessibility. The verse immediately following (9:10) extends the scope of his reign: "And I will cut off the chariot from Ephraim, and the horse from Jerusalem, and the battle bow shall be cut off: and he shall speak peace unto the heathen: and his dominion shall be from sea even to sea, and from the river even to the ends of the earth." War will be abolished and universal dominion established—the same eschatological scope as Micah 4 and Isaiah 2.

The fulfillment is recorded in all four Gospels (Matthew 21:1-11; Mark 11:1-11; Luke 19:28-44; John 12:12-19). Matthew explicitly quotes Zechariah 9:9 and describes Jesus deliberately arranging for a donkey and colt, an arrangement that made the prophetic allusion unmistakable to the Jerusalem crowd. John notes that the disciples did not understand the significance at the time but remembered after the resurrection that these things had been written of him. The crowd's cry of "Hosanna" and "Blessed is he that cometh in the name of the Lord" drew on Psalm 118:25-26, weaving together multiple strands of prophetic and psalmic expectation. The Triumphal Entry thus presents Jesus consciously enacting Zechariah's script, claiming the identity of Zion's long-awaited king.
