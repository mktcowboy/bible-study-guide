# Day of Wrath (Zephaniah)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Zephaniah]]
- **Chapters:** Zephaniah 1–2
- **Summary:** Zephaniah proclaims an all-encompassing Day of the LORD in which God's wrath falls on Judah and surrounding nations, with neither silver nor gold able to deliver the guilty in that day.

## Details
The book of Zephaniah opens with one of the most sweeping announcements of judgment in the Old Testament: "I will utterly consume all things from off the land, saith the LORD. I will consume man and beast; I will consume the fowls of the heaven, and the fishes of the sea, and the stumblingblocks with the wicked; and I will cut off man from off the land, saith the LORD." The comprehensiveness of the declared destruction recalls the language of un-creation, reversing the order of Genesis. Zephaniah prophesied in the reign of Josiah, likely in the period before that king's great reform, which gives the oracles a context: Judah had sunk deeply into the idolatry of Manasseh's long reign, and the prophet's voice carried the urgency of imminent reckoning.

Chapter one focuses intensely on Judah and Jerusalem: priests who bow down to Baal, those who worship the host of heaven from their rooftops, those who have turned back from following the LORD. The social indifference of the prosperous is indicted: "those that say in their heart, The LORD will not do good, neither will he do evil." Zephaniah 1:14-16 builds to one of Scripture's most concentrated descriptions of the Day of the LORD: "The great day of the LORD is near, it is near, and hasteth greatly, even the voice of the day of the LORD: the mighty man shall cry there bitterly. That day is a day of wrath, a day of trouble and distress, a day of wasteness and desolation, a day of darkness and gloominess, a day of clouds and thick darkness." The verse "Neither their silver nor their gold shall be able to deliver them in the day of the LORD's wrath" emphasizes that no human resource can purchase immunity from divine justice.

Chapter two extends the oracle outward to the surrounding nations—Philistia to the west, Moab and Ammon to the east, Ethiopia to the south, and Assyria to the north. This fourfold compass direction signals a universal judgment. The oracle against Nineveh (Zephaniah 2:13-15) is particularly notable as a companion oracle to Nahum's longer treatment: "And he will stretch out his hand against the north, and destroy Assyria; and will make Nineveh a desolation." The passage's power to evoke the terror of divine wrath inspired the medieval Latin hymn Dies Irae ("Day of Wrath"), which became the Requiem Mass sequence and one of the most influential liturgical texts in Western Christianity.
