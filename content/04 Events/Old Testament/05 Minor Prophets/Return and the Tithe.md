# Return and the Tithe

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Malachi]]
- **Chapters:** Malachi 3:7-12
- **Summary:** God calls wayward Israel to return to him, identifies their robbery in withholding tithes and offerings as the specific form of their unfaithfulness, and invites them to test him by giving faithfully, promising that the windows of heaven will be opened.

## Details
Malachi 3:7 opens with a statement of Israel's long pattern of departure: "Even from the days of your fathers ye are gone away from mine ordinances, and have not kept them. Return unto me, and I will return unto you, saith the LORD of hosts." The call to return (Hebrew shub) is the classic covenant call to repentance, used throughout the prophets. But when the people ask "Wherein shall we return?", God's answer is surprisingly specific: "Will a man rob God? Yet ye have robbed me. But ye say, Wherein have we robbed thee? In tithes and offerings."

The accusation that the whole nation has been "robbing God" is striking in its directness. The tithe—the bringing of a tenth of produce, flocks, and income to the storehouse of the LORD—was a core obligation of the Mosaic covenant (Leviticus 27:30-32; Deuteronomy 14:22-29). Malachi 3:10 specifies the storehouse: "Bring ye all the tithes into the storehouse, that there may be meat in mine house." The storehouse provision supported the Levites and maintained the temple's ongoing ministry. Withholding it was not merely financial negligence but a direct breach of covenantal loyalty—treating God as someone from whom one could take services without giving what was owed.

The extraordinary element of this passage is the invitation God extends: "Prove me now herewith, saith the LORD of hosts, if I will not open you the windows of heaven, and pour you out a blessing, that there shall not be room enough to receive it." God essentially offers himself as the subject of a test—not out of insecurity but as an expression of how certain and reliable his covenantal generosity is. The promise extends beyond the tithe itself to the protection of the harvest ("I will rebuke the devourer for your sakes") and to international testimony ("all nations shall call you blessed"). The passage does not reduce to a mere prosperity formula; it is a summons back to covenantal integrity with the assurance that faithfulness toward God will be met with faithfulness from God.
