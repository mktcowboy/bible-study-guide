# Locust Plague and Lament

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Joel]]
- **Chapters:** Joel 1–2
- **Summary:** An unprecedented locust invasion strips Judah bare, silencing the temple offerings and compelling Joel to call the entire nation — from drunkards to priests — to fast, mourn, and cry out to God, for the day of the LORD is at hand.

## Details

Joel opens with an appeal to communal memory: "Hath this been in your days, or even in the days of your fathers?" (1:2). The locust invasion is without historical parallel — four successive waves described with four different Hebrew words (*gazam, arbeh, yeleq, chasil*), each term suggesting a stage of locust development or a successive swarm, the cumulative effect being total agricultural destruction: "That which the palmerworm hath left hath the locust eaten; and that which the locust hath left hath the cankerworm eaten; and that which the cankerworm hath left hath the caterpiller eaten" (1:4). No segment of the harvest escapes.

Joel summons different groups to lament in turn — the drunkards, because the wine is cut off (1:5); the nation as a whole, "for a nation is come up upon my land, strong, and without number" (1:6); the farmers who watch their wheat and barley fail (1:11); and especially the priests, because the grain offering and the drink offering have been cut off from the house of the LORD (1:9). This last detail is the theological key: the locust plague is not merely an agricultural disaster but a covenantal rupture. When the offerings cease, the daily communication between God and his people — the ritual vocabulary of covenant relationship — falls silent. Joel calls the priests to "sanctify a fast, call a solemn assembly, gather the elders and all the inhabitants of the land into the house of the LORD your God, and cry unto the LORD" (1:14).

The second chapter amplifies the locust plague into an eschatological vision: "a great people and a strong; there hath not been ever the like, neither shall be any more after it, even to the years of many generations" (2:2). The locust army moves with military precision — "they shall run like mighty men; they shall climb the wall like men of war" (2:7) — and before them the earth quakes and the heavens tremble (2:10). The call to repentance that follows is the book's most famous passage: "Therefore also now, saith the LORD, turn ye even to me with all your heart, and with fasting, and with weeping, and with mourning: And rend your heart, and not your garments" (2:12–13). The image of tearing garments was the standard ancient Near Eastern mourning ritual; the prophet insists on the inward reality rather than the outward performance. God's character is cited as the ground of hope: "for he is gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repenteth him of the evil" (2:13).

## Key People Involved
- [[Joel (Prophet)|Joel]] — the prophet who interprets the plague as a divine call to repentance and leads the nation in lament
- The Priests and Elders — summoned by Joel to lead the national fast and assembly before the LORD

## Key Places
- [[Edom]] — mentioned later in Joel 3 as one of the nations to be judged; the locust plague begins a sequence culminating in that universal reckoning
