# Doom of Edom

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Obadiah]]
- **Chapters:** Obadiah 1
- **Summary:** God pronounces total judgment on Edom for its pride in its mountain strongholds and its betrayal of Judah during the Babylonian sack of Jerusalem, declaring "as thou hast done, it shall be done unto thee" and announcing the reversal of Esau's ancient pride.

## Details

Obadiah is entirely addressed to [[Edom]], the nation descended from Esau, whose territory lay in the rugged highlands south and east of the Dead Sea — the cliff-faces and gorges of what is today southern Jordan, including the rose-red city of Petra carved from sandstone cliffs. The Edom-Israel relationship stretches back to the womb: Esau and Jacob were twin brothers (Genesis 25:22–26), and the hostility between their descendants had accumulated over more than a millennium by the time Obadiah writes. The opening charges against Edom are accordingly personal in their anguish: "For thy violence against thy brother Jacob shame shall cover thee, and thou shalt be cut off for ever" (v. 10).

The pride charge opens the oracle: "The pride of thine heart hath deceived thee, thou that dwellest in the clefts of the rock, whose habitation is high; that saith in his heart, Who shall bring me down to the ground?" (v. 3). Edom's mountain fortresses gave it a genuine military advantage — its territory was notoriously difficult to invade. This geographical security had curdled into arrogance, the belief that elevation conferred invulnerability. God's answer is direct: "Though thou exalt thyself as the eagle, and though thou set thy nest among the stars, thence will I bring thee down, saith the LORD" (v. 4). Even the allies who have made treaties with Edom will deceive and prevail against her; her wise men will be destroyed; her mighty men dismayed (vv. 7–9).

The central section (vv. 10–14) provides six specific charges for what Edom did on "the day that strangers carried away captive his forces, and foreigners entered into his gates, and cast lots upon Jerusalem" (v. 11). Edom (1) stood on the other side — passive spectatorship treated as moral failure; (2) rejoiced over Judah's destruction; (3) spoke proudly in the day of distress; (4) entered Jerusalem's gate; (5) stood at the crossroads to cut off those who tried to escape; (6) handed over survivors to Babylon. The progression from spectator to accomplice is complete. Obadiah's verdict — "as thou hast done, it shall be done unto thee: thy reward shall return upon thine own head" (v. 15) — is the OT's most compact formulation of divine reciprocity. Historically, Edom was overrun by the Nabataean Arabs by the 4th century BC and ceased to exist as a distinct people by the Roman period, when they were absorbed as Idumaea.
