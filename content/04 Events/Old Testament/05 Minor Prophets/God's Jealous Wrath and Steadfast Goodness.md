# God's Jealous Wrath and Steadfast Goodness

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nahum]]
- **Chapters:** Nahum 1:2-8
- **Summary:** Nahum's theological introduction presents the two-sided character of God — fierce jealous wrath that levels mountains and pursues his adversaries to darkness, and steadfast goodness that makes him a stronghold for those who trust him — establishing the moral foundation that makes Nineveh's coming judgment not arbitrary but necessary.

## Details

Before Nahum delivers a single line of battle poetry against Nineveh, he spends the opening verses of his oracle on theology — specifically, on the character of the God who is about to speak. This is deliberate and essential. Without this theological grounding, Nineveh's destruction might look like the victory of one nation over another, the turning of fortune's wheel, the accident of military history. Nahum insists from the outset that what is about to happen to Nineveh is a moral event, the action of a specific kind of God whose nature makes this judgment not only intelligible but inevitable.

The opening declaration is arresting: "God is jealous, and the LORD revengeth; the LORD revengeth, and is furious; the LORD will take vengeance on his adversaries, and he reserveth wrath for his enemies" (1:2). The Hebrew word rendered "jealous" here is *qanna* — the same word used of God at Sinai when he prohibits the worship of other gods (Exodus 20:5; 34:14). The jealousy of God is not petty resentment but the fierce, exclusive devotion of a covenant God who will not share his glory with what is not God and will not permit the suffering of his people to go unaddressed forever. This is love's other face — the wrath that belongs to anyone who has a genuine commitment to justice and an unbreakable attachment to those in their care. Nineveh has spent generations preying upon nations, including Israel. The God who made covenant with Israel is a jealous God, and that jealousy is now moving against Nineveh.

The cosmic dimension of this wrath is then rendered in storm imagery: "The LORD hath his way in the whirlwind and in the storm, and the clouds are the dust of his feet. He rebuketh the sea, and maketh it dry, and drieth up all the rivers: Bashan languisheth, and Carmel, and the flower of Lebanon languisheth. The mountains quake at him, and the hills melt, and the earth is burned at his presence, yea, the world, and all that dwell therein" (1:3b-5). The geography here — Bashan's fertile upland, Carmel's great headland, Lebanon's famous cedars — represents the most impressive natural landmarks in the Israelite world. And before the LORD they wilt. The mountains and hills, which throughout the ancient Near East were conceived as the seats of divine power, quake and melt before this God. Nineveh's famous walls are not mentioned; they do not need to be. If mountains melt, walls are beside the point.

Then comes the pivot, one of the most compressed theological summaries in all of Scripture: "The LORD is good, a strong hold in the day of trouble; and he knoweth them that trust in him" (1:7). In four Hebrew words of attribute and two of consequence, Nahum establishes the other side of this God's character. The same God whose fury makes mountains quake is the stronghold — the very word used for a fortified mountain refuge — for those who trust him. The contrast is not contradiction but completion: God's wrath against his adversaries and God's goodness to those who trust him are two expressions of the same character, the two sides of the same holiness. What makes him a terror to Nineveh makes him a refuge to Judah. "He knoweth them that trust in him" — in the Hebrew idiom, to "know" someone is to be in covenant relationship with them, to be bound to them with the full weight of personal commitment. God is not good in the abstract; he is good to and for specific people whom he knows by name.
