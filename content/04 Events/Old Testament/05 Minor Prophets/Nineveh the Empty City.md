# Nineveh the Empty City

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nahum]]
- **Chapters:** Nahum 2:1-13
- **Summary:** Nahum's vivid battle poetry describes the siege and sack of Nineveh with near-eyewitness precision — red shields, raging chariots, river gates thrown open, the lion's den emptied — fulfilled in 612 BC when Babylon and the Medes destroyed the city so thoroughly it was lost for centuries.

## Details

Nahum 2 is among the most arresting pieces of prophetic war poetry in all of Scripture. The scene opens on the attackers: "He that dasheth in pieces is come up before thy face: keep the munition, watch the way, make thy loins strong, fortify thy power mightily" (2:1). The irony is merciless — this verse is addressed to Nineveh, urging it to defend itself, and yet the next verses make clear that every defensive measure will fail. The sarcastic call to fortify is immediately undercut by what follows: the description of the attacker's forces in battle array, their armor the color of blood and fire, their appearance unstoppable.

The battle imagery that follows is breathtaking in its specificity: "The shield of his mighty men is made red, the valiant men are in scarlet: the chariots shall be with flaming torches in the day of his preparation, and the fir trees shall be terribly shaken. The chariots shall rage in the streets, they shall jostle one against another in the broad ways: they shall seem like torches, they shall run like the lightnings" (2:3-4). The red of the Median and Babylonian shields, the scarlet of their warriors, the torches on the chariots — these details are so precise that scholars have debated whether Nahum wrote before or after 612 BC. The consensus remains that this is genuine prophecy, but the vividness is unmistakable. The attacking forces look like lightning. Nineveh's own commanders stumble in their hurry to man the walls; the defense mechanism fails even as it is activated (2:5).

The fall of the river gates is the strategic turning point: "The gates of the rivers shall be opened, and the palace shall be dissolved" (2:6). Ancient sources confirm that the Tigris and Khosr rivers, which ran near and partially through Nineveh, flooded disastrously in 612 BC, weakening the walls and allowing the besiegers to enter. The "palace dissolved" — Nineveh's famous royal complex — disappears not through dramatic conquest but through structural failure from within. Nahum then describes the pillaging: silver and gold carried away, the city "void, and waste, and desolate" (2:10). The city that had plundered the ancient world is stripped bare.

The closing image is among the most memorable in prophetic literature: "Where is the dwelling of the lions, and the feeding place of the young lions, where the lion, even the old lion, walked, and the lion's whelp, and none made them afraid? The lion did tear in pieces enough for his whelps, and strangled for his lionesses, and filled his holes with prey, and his dens with ravin" (2:11-12). Nineveh is the lion's den — Assyria's kings used the lion as a royal symbol, carving it on palace walls, hunting it in royal enclosures, stamping it on their seals. Nineveh was the predator city that had fed on nations for generations. And now: where are the lions? The LORD declares "I am against thee" (2:13) and the den is empty. The city that consumed others has been consumed. Historically, Nineveh fell to the combined forces of Nabopolassar of Babylon and Cyaxares the Mede in 612 BC. The fall was so complete, the destruction so total, that by the time of Alexander the Great two centuries later, men marched past the ruins not knowing what great city had stood there. The city was not rediscovered until the 19th century, when archaeologists excavated it beneath the mounds across the river from modern Mosul.
