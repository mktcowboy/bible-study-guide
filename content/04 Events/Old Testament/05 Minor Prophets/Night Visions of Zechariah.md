# Night Visions of Zechariah

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Zechariah]]
- **Chapters:** Zechariah 1–6
- **Summary:** In a single night, Zechariah receives eight symbolic visions—ranging from horsemen among the myrtle trees to four chariots going out to the earth—all communicating God's active engagement in restoring Jerusalem and his people.

## Details
Zechariah's eight night visions form one of the most elaborate prophetic sequences in the Old Testament, comparable in scope and symbolic density to Ezekiel and Daniel. They arrive in the second year of Darius (520 BC), the same period as Haggai's ministry, and serve as an extended encouragement to the returned community as they rebuild the temple. Each vision communicates through image rather than direct address, requiring angelic interpretation that Zechariah consistently receives.

The first vision (Zechariah 1:7-17) shows a rider on a red horse standing among myrtle trees in a glen, with other horses behind him. These are reported to have "walked to and fro through the earth" and found it at rest. The LORD responds to the angel's intercession with "good words and comfortable words" of jealousy for Jerusalem and anger at the nations that have added to her affliction. The second vision (1:18-21) presents four horns—representing the nations that scattered Judah—and four craftsmen sent to destroy them. The third vision (chapter 2) is of a man with a measuring line going to measure Jerusalem, answered by the declaration that Jerusalem shall be "as towns without walls" because of the multitude in it, and that the LORD himself will be "a wall of fire round about her."

The fourth vision (chapter 3) stands apart as the most theologically concentrated: Joshua the high priest before the Angel of the LORD, accused by Satan, his filthy garments replaced with clean ones (see [[Joshua the High Priest Clothed]]). The fifth vision (chapter 4) presents a golden lampstand with seven lamps and two olive trees—interpreted as "the two anointed ones that stand by the LORD"—with the oracle that Zerubbabel will complete the temple "Not by might, nor by power, but by my spirit, saith the LORD of hosts." The sixth vision (chapter 5:1-4) is a flying scroll of enormous dimensions carrying a curse over the land. The seventh (5:5-11) is an ephah basket containing a woman named Wickedness, transported by two winged women to the land of Shinar—a symbolic removal of iniquity. The eighth and final vision (chapter 6) is four chariots issuing from between two bronze mountains, going out to the four winds of heaven—divine activity extending over the whole earth.

Together the eight visions form a comprehensive picture: Israel's enemies will be judged, Jerusalem will be restored and overflow with inhabitants, the priestly office will be purified, the work of rebuilding will succeed by the Spirit, iniquity will be removed, and God's sovereignty extends over all the earth.
