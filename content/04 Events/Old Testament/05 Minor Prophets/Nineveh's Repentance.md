# Nineveh's Repentance

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Jonah]]
- **Chapters:** Jonah 3
- **Summary:** Jonah delivers the briefest recorded prophecy — "Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown" — and the entire city from king to cattle repents in fasting and sackcloth, God relents from the threatened destruction, and the greatest revival in the Old Testament occurs through the most reluctant prophet in Scripture.

## Details

After the great fish deposits Jonah on dry land, the word of the LORD comes to him a second time: "Arise, go unto Nineveh, that great city, and preach unto it the preaching that I bid thee" (3:2). This time Jonah goes. The text records his obedience without elaboration — there is no recorded inner struggle, no extended preparation. He simply goes. The detail about [[Nineveh]] being "an exceeding great city of three days' journey" (3:3) has been a source of historical and interpretive discussion; whether this refers to the city's circumference, the length of Jonah's preaching tour, or the size of the greater Nineveh administrative district, it underscores the scale of what is about to happen.

Jonah enters the city a single day's journey and delivers the shortest sermon in the prophetic canon: "Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown" (3:4). Five words in Hebrew (*od arba'im yom w'Nineveh nehpaketh*). No call to repentance is explicit in the preached text. There is no "thus saith the LORD" framing. The message is announcement, not exhortation. Yet "the people of Nineveh believed God, and proclaimed a fast, and put on sackcloth, from the greatest of them even to the least of them" (3:5). The repentance is total and immediate. When the word reached the king of Nineveh, he rose from his throne, laid off his robe, covered himself with sackcloth, and sat in ashes (3:6). He then issued a royal decree commanding citywide fasting and mourning — including cattle (3:7–8) — and articulated a theology of repentance that rivals anything in the Hebrew prophets: "Let man and beast be covered with sackcloth, and cry mightily unto God: yea, let them turn every one from his evil way, and from the violence that is in their hands. Who can tell if God will turn and repent, and turn away from his fierce anger, that we perish not?" (3:8–9).

The king's question — "Who can tell if God will turn?" — is precisely the theology of Joel 2:14 ("Who knoweth if he will return and repent?"), and both reflect a genuine understanding that divine mercy is not automatic but is responsive to authentic repentance. God's response is immediate: "And God saw their works, that they turned from their evil way; and God repented of the evil, that he had said that he would do unto them; and he did it not" (3:10). The verb "repented" (*nacham*) describes God's responsive relenting — not that God was wrong to announce judgment, but that genuine repentance changes the situation. Jesus later cites this repentance as a rebuke to his own generation: "The men of Nineveh shall rise in judgment with this generation, and shall condemn it; because they repented at the preaching of Jonas; and, behold, a greater than Jonas is here" (Matthew 12:41).
