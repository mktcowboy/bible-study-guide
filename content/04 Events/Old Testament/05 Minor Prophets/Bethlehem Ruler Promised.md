# Bethlehem Ruler Promised

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Micah]]
- **Chapters:** Micah 5:2-5
- **Summary:** The LORD declares through Micah that the coming ruler over Israel shall come out of Bethlehem Ephratah, a small and seemingly insignificant town, whose origins are from everlasting.

## Details
Among the most precisely detailed messianic predictions in the entire Old Testament, Micah 5:2 names the specific town from which the promised ruler will come. Bethlehem Ephratah was distinguished from another Bethlehem in Zebulun by the addition of Ephratah, tying the prophecy to the clan of Judah and the line of David. The verse reads, "But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah, though thou be little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of thee shall he come forth unto me that is to be ruler in Israel; whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting." The paradox of greatness from smallness runs throughout the oracle: the town is explicitly called little, yet it is chosen to produce the one whose origins reach into eternity.

The phrase "whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting" carries unmistakable weight. The Hebrew term translated "everlasting" (olam) in this context points beyond any merely human dynasty to one who existed before time. Christian interpreters have consistently understood this as a declaration of the Messiah's pre-existence and divine nature. The ruler would not merely descend from David historically; he would be one whose being precedes all history.

The fulfilment is recorded in Matthew 2:1-6, where the chief priests and scribes cite this very passage when Herod inquires where the Christ is to be born. The magi's arrival and the scribes' answer confirm that Micah 5:2 was understood in the first century as pointing to the birth of the Messiah. Luke 2:4-7 records that Joseph and Mary traveled from Nazareth to Bethlehem precisely because of the census, an ordinary civic event that Providence used to bring the child into the world in the prophesied city. The prophecy also extends to a shepherd-king who will "feed his flock in the strength of the LORD" and whose greatness will reach "unto the ends of the earth," and under whom "this man shall be the peace."
