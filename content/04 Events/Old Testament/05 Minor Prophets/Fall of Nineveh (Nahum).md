# Fall of Nineveh (Nahum)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Nahum]]
- **Chapters:** Nahum 1–3
- **Summary:** Nahum delivers a complete oracle of destruction against Nineveh, the Assyrian capital, proclaiming its utter ruin approximately 150 years after Jonah's preaching there, fulfilled precisely in 612 BC.

## Details
The book of Nahum is entirely consumed with the coming judgment on Nineveh, the capital of the Assyrian Empire and the city Jonah had once reluctantly urged to repentance. The opening verse identifies itself as "the book of the vision of Nahum the Elkoshite" and "the burden of Nineveh." Where Jonah's preaching produced repentance and God relented, Nahum's oracle comes after Assyria has returned to its brutal ways—and there is no call to repentance. The message is entirely one of certain doom.

The first chapter establishes the theological foundation: God is described as a jealous God who takes vengeance on his adversaries and is slow to anger but will by no means clear the guilty. "The LORD is slow to anger, and great in power, and will not at all acquit the wicked." Yet this same God is "a stronghold in the day of trouble" for those who trust in him. The character of God—both wrathful toward his enemies and protective toward his people—undergirds every oracle that follows. The passage then turns directly to Nineveh: "What do ye imagine against the LORD? he will make an utter end: affliction shall not rise up the second time."

The second and third chapters provide vivid battle poetry describing the siege and sack of Nineveh in detail so precise that interpreters have read them almost as an eyewitness account written after the fact, though prophetic literature commonly employs this forward-looking vividness. Horses, chariots, soldiers, plunder, captive women, and fleeing officials are all described. The famous lament, "Woe to the bloody city! it is all full of lies and robbery; the prey departeth not," summarizes Assyria's long history of imperial cruelty. Nahum 3:7 asks rhetorically, "who will bemoan her? whence shall I seek comforters for thee?" The answer is that none will weep over Nineveh. In 612 BC, the combined forces of the Medes and Babylonians besieged and destroyed Nineveh. The city fell so completely that for centuries its location was lost and scholars doubted it had existed at all, until archaeology confirmed the ruins in the 19th century.
