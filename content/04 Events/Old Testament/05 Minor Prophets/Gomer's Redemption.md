# Gomer's Redemption

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Hosea]]
- **Chapters:** Hosea 3
- **Summary:** Hosea, commanded by God to love his wife again, purchases Gomer back from bondage for fifteen pieces of silver and a measure of barley, enacting in his own life the cost of God's grace toward faithless Israel.

## Details

Hosea 3 is the briefest and most concentrated chapter in the book, yet it carries the full weight of the book's theology in five verses. God speaks directly to Hosea: "Go yet, love a woman beloved of her friend, yet an adulteress, according to the love of the LORD toward the children of Israel, who look to other gods, and love flagons of wine" (3:1). The parallel is explicit — Hosea's love for Gomer is to mirror the LORD's love for Israel. The command is not merely to take her back but to *love* her, which places the act far beyond legal obligation or social propriety.

The transaction itself is described plainly: "So I bought her to me for fifteen pieces of silver, and for an homer of barley, and an half homer of barley" (3:2). The sum — fifteen pieces of silver and grain — is less than the standard slave-price of thirty shekels (Exodus 21:32), which may indicate Gomer's diminished value on the market, or it may be the limit of Hosea's financial means at the time. Either way, the act of purchase enacts redemption in its most concrete form: a price is paid, a person is freed. Hosea then addresses her: "Thou shalt abide for me many days; thou shalt not play the harlot, and thou shalt not be for another man: so will I also be for thee" (3:3). The waiting period imposed on Gomer mirrors the long period of Israel's discipline — many days without king, prince, sacrifice, or ephod — before the return to David her king.

The redemption of Gomer stands as one of the OT's most direct typological images of God's redemptive love. It anticipates the NT's language of being "bought with a price" (1 Corinthians 6:20). The price paid is real; the condition of the redeemed is one of prior bondage; the initiating motive is love, not merit. There is no indication that Gomer asked to be redeemed, sought Hosea out, or deserved restoration. The act is entirely from his side — a unilateral grace that reflects the character of divine *hesed* as Hosea has been preaching it. The cost to Hosea is real, personal, and financial. Grace, the book insists, is never cheap.
