# Psalm 150 - Let Everything Praise

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Psalms]]
- **Psalm Number:** 150
- **Type:** Praise / Doxology
- **Author:** Anonymous
- **Summary:** The grand concluding doxology of the Psalter, summoning all creation to praise the LORD with every instrument and in every place, culminating in the call for every breathing thing to offer praise.

## Content and Significance
Psalm 150 functions as the final resolution of the entire Psalter. The book of Psalms opens with the quiet blessing of the meditative individual (Psalm 1) and closes with a thunderous, universal explosion of praise. The final five psalms (146-150) form a doxological cluster, each beginning and ending with "Praise ye the LORD" (Hallelu-Yah), and Psalm 150 is their crescendo — nine imperatives to praise in a mere six verses, building to a single, all-encompassing final declaration.

The psalm begins by establishing where praise is to be offered: "Praise God in his sanctuary: praise him in the firmament of his power." The sanctuary refers to the earthly dwelling place of God among his people — the temple — and the firmament to the vast heavens that declare his glory (Psalm 19:1). Praise is to ascend from below and echo across the expanse above. There is no location exempt from the call to worship.

The next couplet establishes why praise is due: "Praise him for his mighty acts: praise him according to his excellent greatness." The two grounds are the historical works of God among his people (the exodus, the conquest, the deliverances, the covenants) and the intrinsic, infinite greatness of his character. The acts of God spring from and display his greatness; his greatness is made known through his acts. Both dimensions call forth worship.

The longest section of the psalm catalogs the instruments of praise: trumpets, psalteries and harps, timbrels with dancing, stringed instruments and organs (flute), loud cymbals, and high-sounding cymbals. The catalog represents virtually every category of ancient musical instrument — brass winds, strings, percussion, and winds. The inclusion of dance reminds that the body itself participates in worship. The full orchestration of Israel's musical heritage is pressed into service for the praise of the LORD.

The final verse transcends instrumentation entirely: "Let every thing that hath breath praise the LORD. Praise ye the LORD." The scope widens from Israel to every instrument to every creature that breathes. Breath is the gift of God from the moment of creation (Genesis 2:7), and its proper use is the praise of the Giver. This is the Psalter's final word on the purpose of creation: all that lives is summoned to worship. The New Testament's vision of universal worship in Revelation — "every creature which is in heaven, and on the earth, and under the earth, and such as are in the sea, and all that are in them" (Revelation 5:13) — is the eschatological fulfillment of Psalm 150's call.

Psalm 150 also functions as the literary and theological resolution of the entire Psalter. The Psalms contain every human experience — praise and lament, faith and doubt, triumph and terror, confession and petition. All of it presses toward this: a creation without reservation, with every voice and every instrument, lifted in the worship of the God who created, redeemed, and will ultimately restore all things. The Psalter ends not with a question but with a declaration — Hallelujah.

## Key Themes
- Universal, comprehensive praise summoned from all places and all creation
- The two grounds of praise: God's mighty acts and his excellent greatness
- Every instrument and the human body enlisted in worship
- Breath as God's gift and its proper use as praise
- The Psalter's grand doxological resolution
