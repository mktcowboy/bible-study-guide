# Psalm 8 - What Is Man

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Psalms]]
- **Psalm Number:** 8
- **Type:** Praise / Wisdom
- **Author:** David
- **Summary:** A meditation on God's majestic glory displayed in creation alongside the astonishing dignity he has granted to humanity, crowned with glory and given dominion over the earth.

## Content and Significance
Psalm 8 opens and closes with the same doxology — "O LORD our Lord, how excellent is thy name in all the earth!" — forming a literary frame that situates the entire psalm within the context of worship. The psalm moves from the grandeur of heaven to the smallness of man and back again, but it does so not to diminish humanity but to magnify the grace of God who exalts the lowly.

The first movement contrasts God's glory set above the heavens with the praise that rises from the mouths of babes and sucklings. This unexpected detail — that strength is ordained from the mouths of the weakest and most vulnerable — confounds the enemy and the avenger. The New Testament will see this verse fulfilled when Jesus quotes it in response to the children crying "Hosanna" in the Temple (Matthew 21:16), connecting authentic praise with those the world regards as insignificant.

The central question of the psalm is one of awe: "When I consider thy heavens, the work of thy fingers, the moon and the stars, which thou hast ordained; what is man, that thou art mindful of him? and the son of man, that thou visitest him?" The contemplation of the vast created order — stars ordained by divine fingers — throws the smallness of humanity into relief. The question is not rhetorical despair but astonishment at grace. That the infinite Creator is "mindful" of man and "visits" him implies ongoing personal attention and care from the sovereign God.

The answer to the question is remarkable: God has made man "a little lower than the angels" (or, in the Hebrew, a little lower than Elohim — God), crowning him with glory and honour and giving him dominion over all created things. The language deliberately echoes the creation mandate of Genesis 1:26-28, where humanity is made in God's image and given rule over the earth. The psalm thus recapitulates the theology of the image of God and celebrates it in lyrical praise.

The New Testament, especially the book of Hebrews, sees the "son of man" in Psalm 8 as ultimately fulfilled only in Jesus Christ. Hebrews 2:6-9 quotes the psalm and argues that while "we see not yet all things put under him" regarding humanity in general, "we see Jesus, who was made a little lower than the angels for the suffering of death, crowned with glory and honour." The true Man who perfectly bears the image and exercises the dominion is Christ — crucified in apparent weakness, now exalted in glory, the firstborn of a new humanity. Paul similarly connects Christ's universal lordship to the subjection of all things under his feet (1 Corinthians 15:27; Ephesians 1:22).

## Key Themes
- The majesty of God revealed in creation
- The paradox of human smallness and human dignity
- The dominion mandate and the image of God
- Praise arising from the lowly and weak
- Christological fulfillment in the exalted Son of Man

## Important Verses
> "O LORD our Lord, how excellent is thy name in all the earth! who hast set thy glory above the heavens." — Psalm 8:1

> "What is man, that thou art mindful of him? and the son of man, that thou visitest him?" — Psalm 8:4

> "For thou hast made him a little lower than the angels, and hast crowned him with glory and honour. Thou madest him to have dominion over the works of thy hands; thou hast put all things under his feet." — Psalm 8:5-6
