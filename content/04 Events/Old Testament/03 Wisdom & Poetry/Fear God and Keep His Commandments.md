# Fear God and Keep His Commandments

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Ecclesiastes]]
- **Chapters:** Ecclesiastes 12:13-14
- **Summary:** Qoheleth's final conclusion after his comprehensive inquiry — after all investigation of wisdom, pleasure, labor, and futility, the answer is startlingly simple: fear God and keep his commandments, for God will bring every work into judgment.

## Details
The closing verses of Ecclesiastes represent the resolution of one of Scripture's most searching philosophical investigations. After twelve chapters of rigorous inquiry into the profit of human toil, the nature of wisdom, the fate of the righteous and the wicked, the limits of pleasure and knowledge, and the pervasiveness of hebel (vanity/vapor), Qoheleth arrives at a conclusion of remarkable simplicity and weight: "Let us hear the conclusion of the whole matter: Fear God, and keep his commandments: for this is the whole duty of man. For God shall bring every work into judgment, with every secret thing, whether it be good, or whether it be evil."

The phrase "the whole duty of man" is actually a translator's addition to smooth the English; the Hebrew is simply "this is the whole of man" (zeh kol-ha'adam) — meaning this is the entirety of what it means to be human. Everything else the book has surveyed — wealth, wisdom, pleasure, legacy, reputation — is hebel without this. The fear of God is not one item in a list of human purposes but the comprehensive framework within which every other human activity finds or loses its meaning. This is the answer to the opening question of verse 1:3 — "What profit hath a man of all his labour which he taketh under the sun?" — the profit exists only for the one who lives in the fear of God.

The fear of God called for here connects Ecclesiastes to the broader wisdom tradition of the Old Testament. Proverbs declared the same axiom at its outset: "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (1:7; 9:10). Job's great wisdom poem concluded similarly: "Behold, the fear of the Lord, that is wisdom; and to depart from evil is understanding" (Job 28:28). What is striking in Ecclesiastes is how this conclusion arrives not as the first word of instruction to a young student but as the hard-won conclusion of an exhaustive inquiry by someone who tested every alternative. The Teacher has not simply inherited this answer from tradition; he has verified it through experience.

The grounding of the conclusion in divine judgment is crucial: "For God shall bring every work into judgment, with every secret thing, whether it be good, or whether it be evil." One of Ecclesiastes' most persistent burdens has been the apparent absence of justice — the righteous perishing and the wicked prospering, with no visible correction. The book has struggled honestly with this without resolution in the realm of observation "under the sun." The conclusion relocates justice where it belongs: in the sovereign and all-knowing God who judges not merely the visible work but every secret thing. Nothing escapes his scrutiny; no injustice persists beyond his final reckoning. The fear of God is rationally grounded in the certainty of divine judgment.

The New Testament announces that the judgment Ecclesiastes anticipates has a specific form: it is the judgment of the risen Christ. Acts 17:31 declares that God "hath appointed a day, in the which he will judge the world in righteousness by that man whom he hath ordained; whereof he hath given assurance unto all men, in that he hath raised him from the dead." Revelation 20:12 pictures the final judgment before the great white throne, where "the dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works." Qoheleth's conclusion that God will bring every secret work into judgment is fulfilled in the revelation that the Judge is the crucified and risen Son of God — the one who bore the judgment so that those who fear God and keep his commandments might stand in that day.
