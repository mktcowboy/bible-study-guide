# Psalm 23 - The Lord Is My Shepherd

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Psalms]]
- **Psalm Number:** 23
- **Type:** Confidence / Trust
- **Author:** David
- **Summary:** David's beloved psalm of confidence in the LORD as his shepherd and host, guiding through every valley and providing abundantly even in the presence of enemies.

## Content and Significance
Psalm 23 is perhaps the most memorized and beloved passage in all of Scripture, carried in the hearts of believers across generations through grief, fear, illness, and death. Its opening declaration is also its thesis: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want." The choice of shepherd imagery is significant — David, himself a shepherd before he was a king, writes from personal knowledge of what it means to provide, guide, protect, and restore a flock. The LORD is not a distant sovereign but an intimate shepherd who attends to every need.

The first movement of the psalm pictures pastoral provision and guidance. Green pastures and still waters speak to rest and refreshment; paths of righteousness point to moral direction and guidance — "for his name's sake," meaning the shepherd's faithfulness is grounded in his own character and reputation. The movement is purposeful: the shepherd leads rather than merely drives. Even when the path leads through "the valley of the shadow of death" — the darkest and most threatening terrain — the psalmist declares "I will fear no evil: for thou art with me." The comfort is not the absence of danger but the presence of the shepherd. The rod and the staff — instruments of protection and guidance — are themselves a comfort.

The psalm then shifts metaphors from shepherd to host. The scene changes from open fields to a prepared banquet table: "Thou preparest a table before me in the presence of mine enemies: thou anointest my head with oil; my cup runneth over." This is hospitality language, and it is bold — the table is set not after the enemies have been defeated and fled but in their very presence. The host's honor is at stake, and the guest is fully provided for even while threat remains. The anointing of the head with oil and the overflowing cup picture abundance and honor beyond what is merely sufficient.

The closing verse moves from present experience to future confidence: "Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life: and I will dwell in the house of the LORD for ever." The word translated "follow" carries the sense of pursuing — goodness and mercy are not incidental companions but active attendants chasing after the psalmist all his life. The psalm ends not in the field or at the table but in the house of the LORD — with the prospect of dwelling in God's presence permanently. This doxological ending lifts the psalm above mere reflection on past experience into eschatological hope.

The New Testament takes up the shepherd imagery and applies it to Jesus. In John 10 Jesus declares "I am the good shepherd: the good shepherd giveth his life for the sheep" — claiming to be precisely the divine shepherd of Psalm 23 who guides, protects, and provides even at the cost of his own life. Hebrews 13:20 calls him "that great shepherd of the sheep." Revelation 7:17 pictures the Lamb himself leading the redeemed to living fountains of waters and wiping away their tears — a final fulfillment of the green pastures and still waters of the psalm. Psalm 23 is thus not merely comfort poetry but prophecy of the Shepherd who would lay down his life to fulfill every promise it contains.

## Key Themes
- The LORD as attentive, providing, guiding shepherd
- Confidence in God's presence through the darkest valleys
- Divine hospitality and abundance in the presence of enemies
- Goodness and mercy as active companions throughout life
- Dwelling in God's presence as the ultimate destination
