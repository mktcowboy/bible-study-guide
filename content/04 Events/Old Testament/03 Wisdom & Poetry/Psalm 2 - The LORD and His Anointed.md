# Psalm 2 - The LORD and His Anointed

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Psalms]]
- **Psalm Number:** 2
- **Type:** Royal / Messianic
- **Author:** David (Acts 4:25 ascribes it to David)
- **Summary:** The nations rage against the LORD and His Anointed, but God laughs at their conspiracy and declares the eternal decree installing His Son as king on Zion, warning all rulers to submit or perish.

## Content and Significance
Psalm 2 opens with a scene of international turbulence: "Why do the heathen rage, and the people imagine a vain thing?" The kings of the earth conspire together against the LORD and against His anointed (Hebrew: Mashiach, Messiah), chafing at divine sovereignty. The word "vain" signals from the outset that their rebellion is not merely wicked but futile — they are plotting against the one who upholds all things.

The divine response to the conspiracy is not alarm but derision: "He that sitteth in the heavens shall laugh: the LORD shall have them in derision." This laughter is not indifference but sovereign contempt — the absolute security of the Almighty in the face of creaturely revolt. God's answer is declaration: "Yet have I set my king upon my holy hill of Zion." The enthroned king on Zion is the LORD's chosen representative, and in the ancient Near Eastern context, such a king embodies the victory of divine order over chaos.

The heart of the psalm is the divine decree spoken to the king: "Thou art my Son; this day have I begotten thee." This language of divine sonship marks the king as uniquely related to God — adopted and installed in a position of cosmic authority. The Father promises the nations as his inheritance and the uttermost parts of the earth as his possession, with authority to "break them with a rod of iron" and dash them in pieces like a potter's vessel. This imagery of absolute authority over the nations far exceeds any historical Israelite king and anticipates a figure of eschatological significance.

The psalm closes with an urgent call to wisdom and submission: "Kiss the Son, lest he be angry, and ye perish from the way, when his wrath is kindled but a little. Blessed are all they that put their trust in him." The kiss is a gesture of homage and allegiance. The warning carries gravity — delay is dangerous — but the final word is the same beatitude that opened Psalm 1: blessed are those who take refuge in the LORD's Anointed.

The New Testament applies Psalm 2 to Jesus more than any other psalm. The voice at Jesus' baptism and transfiguration ("This is my beloved Son") echoes Psalm 2:7. Acts 4:25-28 applies the raging nations to Herod and Pilate. Acts 13:33 applies the begetting declaration to the resurrection. Hebrews 1:5 and 5:5 apply it to Christ's divine sonship and high priesthood. Revelation 2:27 and 12:5 apply the rod of iron to Christ's ultimate reign. Psalm 2 thus stands as a foundational messianic psalm, not merely royal poetry applied secondarily to Jesus but a prophecy that the New Testament treats as directly fulfilled in him.

## Key Themes
- The futility of rebellion against God's sovereign purposes
- Divine installation of the Messianic King on Zion
- Divine sonship and the eternal decree
- Universal dominion of the Anointed One
- Wisdom consists in submission and trust in God's King
