# A Time for Everything

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Ecclesiastes]]
- **Chapters:** Ecclesiastes 3:1-8
- **Summary:** Qoheleth's celebrated poem of paired opposites declares that every human experience has its appointed time, suggesting beneath apparent randomness a divine ordering that transcends human control.

## Details
Ecclesiastes 3:1-8 is one of the most recognized pieces of poetry in world literature, consisting of fourteen paired opposites that together encompass the full range of human experience: "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven: a time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to pluck up that which is planted..." The poem continues through war and peace, mourning and dancing, gathering and casting away, silence and speech, love and hate. Each pairing presents polar opposites, and together they exhaust the spectrum — whatever a human being will ever face falls within this inventory.

The literary effect of the poem is complex and somewhat unsettling in its original context. One might read it as a peaceful affirmation that everything has its place, but Qoheleth's intent is more philosophically pointed. In the verses immediately following (vv. 9-11), he returns to his question: "What profit hath he that worketh in that wherein he laboureth?" The poem of times is not a comfort but a problem — if there is an appointed time for everything and humans cannot determine when each time arrives or departs, then human planning and striving are at the mercy of forces beyond their control. The cycles control us; we do not control them.

Yet the poem simultaneously gestures toward something beyond mere fatalism. Verse 11 is pivotal: "He hath made every thing beautiful in his time: also he hath set the world in their heart, so that no man can find out the work that God maketh from the beginning to the end." Two complementary truths are held in tension: God has made everything beautiful in its time (implying purposive order, not random chaos), and God has set "the world" (Hebrew: olam, sometimes translated "eternity") in the human heart — an awareness of transcendence and permanent significance that finite experience can never fully satisfy. Humans are creatures stretched between time and eternity, able to sense that there is a larger pattern without being able to see it from within.

This passage has become one of the most widely quoted biblical texts in secular culture, often detached from its context and read as comfortable affirmation of natural cycles. But Qoheleth's point is sharper: the same rhythms that give life its structure also render human autonomy an illusion. The response he commends is not resignation but the enjoyment of one's portion with gratitude (3:13), and ultimately the fear of God before whom all times stand (3:14). The proper posture within the appointed times is trust in the God who appoints them — the one who stands above the times and holds all things in his purpose.

The New Testament builds on this vision of appointed times. Paul writes in Galatians 4:4 that "when the fulness of the time was come, God sent forth his Son" — the incarnation as the decisive moment that arrived at the precise kairos appointed in divine counsel. Acts 17:26 declares that God "hath determined the times before appointed, and the bounds of their habitation." The appointed times of Ecclesiastes 3 are real, and the God who appoints them is the same God who at the appointed fullness of time shattered the cycle with the resurrection — the one event that does not merely repeat but fulfills and transforms all that came before it.
