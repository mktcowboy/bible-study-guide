# The Beloved's Voice (Song of Songs 2)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Song of Solomon]]
- **Chapters:** Song of Solomon 2:8-17
- **Summary:** The beloved hears her lover's voice bounding toward her over the mountains in springtime, calling her to come away — "Rise up, my love, my fair one, and come away; for, lo, the winter is past" — a passage of desire, pursuit, and mutual belonging that has been read as creation's spring, Israel's call from exile, and Christ's summons to the Church.

## Details

The scene opens with sound before sight: "The voice of my beloved! behold, he cometh leaping upon the mountains, skipping upon the hills" (2:8). The beloved does not say "I see my beloved" but "The voice of my beloved!" — the ear catches him before the eye. This is the phenomenology of intense love: the beloved's voice is recognizable at distance, before the beloved is visible, distinguishable from all other voices. It is, in the typological reading, the pattern of the sheep who know the Shepherd's voice among all other voices (John 10:4). The lover comes with the energy of a young stag, "leaping upon the mountains, skipping upon the hills" — all the great obstacles of terrain that might separate two people are nothing to this love; it runs over mountains.

He stands at the wall, gazing through the lattice (2:9), and calls: "Rise up, my love, my fair one, and come away. For, lo, the winter is past, the rain is over and gone; the flowers appear on the earth; the time of the singing of birds is come, and the voice of the turtle is heard in our land; the fig tree putteth forth her green figs, and the vines with the tender grape give a good smell. Arise, my love, my fair one, and come away" (2:10-13). The invitation is framed by the transformation of the world itself — it is spring, the season of renewal, and the lover summons the beloved not merely to a meeting but into the world made new. Every sense is engaged: sight (flowers, figs, grapes), sound (birds, the turtledove), smell (the vines in bloom). Spring is not background scenery; it is the sermon the creation is preaching in support of the lover's invitation.

The ancient Jewish allegorical reading heard in this summons the voice of God calling Israel out of Egypt — the "winter" of bondage in Pharaoh's house is past, the "rain" of suffering is over, and the beloved is called into the great spring of the Exodus, the season when the LORD revealed himself at Sinai and made covenant with his people as with a bride. The rabbinic literature developed this reading extensively, seeing the entire Song as the love poetry of the Sinai covenant. A second allegorical moment heard the call as God summoning Israel back from Babylonian exile — the winter of judgment is over, the spring of restoration has come, and the beloved is called to arise and return. In both readings the erotic spring imagery maps onto historical moments of divine initiative and human response, moments when God breaks into the dark season of his people's life with the summons: arise, come away.

The Christological reading that runs from Origen through Bernard of Clairvaux to the Puritan tradition hears in these verses the voice of the risen Christ calling the Church into the new creation spring of the gospel. The "winter" is the age of the law's condemnation, the long dark of human bondage to sin and death; the "rain" is over because the wrath has been poured out at the cross; the spring is the new creation inaugurated by the resurrection. "Rise up, my love" is then the call to every believer in every generation to arise from the death of sin into the life of the Spirit, to come away from the old world into the new. This reading does not replace but extends the literal meaning: the desire and delight of the poem are not evacuated but fulfilled — the longing of the Shulammite for her beloved becomes the figure for the longing of the soul for Christ.

The passage closes with the covenant formula: "My beloved is mine, and I am his: he feedeth among the lilies" (2:16). This is the Song's characteristic summary of the relationship — mutual possession, mutual belonging, the covenant bond expressed in the idiom of love. "My beloved is mine, and I am his" — the order will be reversed in 6:3 ("I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine"), a subtle theological movement that some interpreters read as the maturing of love from the beloved's possessive desire toward the self-giving that defines genuine covenant.

## Key People Involved
- The Shulammite — the beloved; the one who hears the voice, receives the invitation, declares the covenant formula
- The beloved (Solomon / the lover) — the one who comes leaping over mountains, calls the beloved into spring, receives her declaration

## Key Places
- The mountains and hills — the terrain the lover crosses, symbols of every obstacle that love overcomes
