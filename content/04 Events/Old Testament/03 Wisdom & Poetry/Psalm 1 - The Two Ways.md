# Psalm 1 - The Two Ways

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Psalms]]
- **Psalm Number:** 1
- **Type:** Wisdom
- **Author:** Anonymous (no superscription)
- **Summary:** The gateway psalm of the Psalter contrasts the blessed man who meditates on God's law with the wicked whose way perishes.

## Content and Significance
Psalm 1 functions as a deliberate introduction to the entire book of Psalms, setting before the reader two paths and two destinies. It opens with a beatitude — "Blessed is the man that walketh not in the counsel of the ungodly, nor standeth in the way of sinners, nor sitteth in the seat of the scornful" — tracing a downward progression from casual association with the wicked to settled habitation among the scornful. The movement from walking to standing to sitting pictures how moral compromise deepens through incremental stages.

The positive portrait of the blessed man centers not on his avoidance of evil alone but on his active delight in the law of the LORD: "his delight is in the law of the LORD; and in his law doth he meditate day and night." This meditation is not merely intellectual but devotional — a continual turning of the mind to God's revealed word. The result is likened to a tree planted by rivers of water, bringing forth fruit in due season, with its leaf never withering. The tree image speaks of rootedness, nourishment, vitality, and productive life sustained by a source outside itself.

In sharp contrast, the wicked are "like the chaff which the wind driveth away" — weightless, rootless, and driven by forces beyond their control. The image of threshing-floor chaff speaks to ultimate instability and worthlessness. The psalm then moves to eschatological consequence: "the ungodly shall not stand in the judgment, nor sinners in the congregation of the righteous." The two ways converge on a final reckoning where God knows and upholds the way of the righteous while the way of the wicked perishes.

Psalm 1 is widely recognized as a wisdom psalm placed editorially at the head of the Psalter to frame the entire collection as a book of instruction and to position the reader as a disciple who meditates on God's word. Its themes of the two ways echo Deuteronomy's calls to choose life or death, blessings or curses, and anticipate the Sermon on the Mount's Beatitudes and the teaching of Jesus about the broad and narrow ways (Matthew 7:13-14). The psalm is not merely an ethical checklist but a portrait of a person whose life is organically shaped by ongoing communion with God's word.

## Key Themes
- The two ways: righteousness and wickedness leading to opposite ends
- Meditation on God's law as the foundation of the blessed life
- Organic fruitfulness versus chaff-like instability
- Divine knowledge and judgment as the basis of moral order
- The Psalter as a book of the law to be meditated upon

## Important Verses
> "Blessed is the man that walketh not in the counsel of the ungodly, nor standeth in the way of sinners, nor sitteth in the seat of the scornful." — Psalm 1:1

> "But his delight is in the law of the LORD; and in his law doth he meditate day and night." — Psalm 1:2

> "And he shall be like a tree planted by the rivers of water, that bringeth forth his fruit in his season; his leaf also shall not wither; and whatsoever he doeth shall prosper." — Psalm 1:3

> "For the LORD knoweth the way of the righteous: but the way of the ungodly shall perish." — Psalm 1:6
