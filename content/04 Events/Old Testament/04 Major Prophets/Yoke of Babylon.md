# Yoke of Babylon

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Jeremiah]]
- **Chapters:** Jeremiah 27-28
- **Summary:** Jeremiah wore a wooden yoke to symbolize that nations must submit to Nebuchadnezzar's rule; the false prophet Hananiah publicly broke it and prophesied a quick end to Babylonian domination; Jeremiah replaced the wooden yoke with an iron one and prophesied Hananiah's death.

## Details
God commanded Jeremiah to make bars and yokes and put them on his neck, then send messages to the kings of Edom, Moab, Ammon, Tyre, and Sidon — and to King Zedekiah of Judah — declaring that God had given all these lands to Nebuchadnezzar. The nations that submitted to Babylon's yoke would be allowed to remain in their land; those that refused would face sword, famine, and pestilence. The false prophets who said the Babylonian yoke would be short were lying. The temple vessels already taken to Babylon would not return soon; more would follow.

In chapter 28, the prophet Hananiah son of Azzur confronted Jeremiah in the temple before the priests and all the people. He proclaimed that within two years God would break the yoke of Babylon, restore Jeconiah and the exiles and the temple vessels. He then took the yoke from Jeremiah's neck and broke it publicly, saying this was how God would break Nebuchadnezzar's yoke. Jeremiah's response was nuanced: "Amen: the LORD do so: the LORD perform thy words." But he pointed out that prophets of old prophesied war and disaster; only a prophet who prophesied peace, when it came to pass, could be recognized as truly sent by God.

Later, the word of the LORD came to Jeremiah: "Thou hast broken the yokes of wood; but thou shalt make for them yokes of iron." Hananiah had broken a wooden prop but the iron reality of Babylon's power was undiminished. God also said Hananiah had made this people trust in a lie; he would die within a year. Hananiah died in the seventh month of that year.

## Notes
The confrontation with Hananiah is one of the clearest illustrations in scripture of the problem of distinguishing true from false prophecy. Both men claimed to speak for God; their messages were contradictory; both were sincere. Jeremiah's criterion (28:9) — only verified fulfillment validates peace prophecy — points to the same test in Deuteronomy 18:21-22. The wooden/iron yoke progression is a chilling prophetic dynamic: Hananiah's theatrical destruction of the wooden symbol did not cancel the reality it represented but intensified it.
