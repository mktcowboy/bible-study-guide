# Oracles Against Nations

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Jeremiah]], [[Ezekiel]], [[Isaiah]], [[Amos]]
- **Chapters:** Jeremiah 46–51; Ezekiel 25–32; Isaiah 13–23; Amos 1–2
- **Summary:** Collections of prophetic oracles declaring God's judgment on surrounding nations — Egypt, Babylon, Moab, Edom, Ammon, Tyre, Philistia, and others.

## Details
All the major prophets contain collections of oracles directed at nations outside Israel. Jeremiah 46-51 addresses Egypt, Philistia, Moab, Ammon, Edom, Damascus, Kedar, Elam, and Babylon at length. Ezekiel 25-32 targets Ammon, Moab, Edom, Philistia, Tyre, Sidon, and Egypt. Isaiah 13-23 contains the famous "burden" oracles against Babylon, Moab, Damascus, Egypt, and others. Amos 1-2 opens with oracles against six surrounding nations before turning to Israel itself.

These oracles establish several important theological points. First, Israel's God is not merely the God of one nation but the sovereign over all nations — their history is in his hands. Second, the nations are held accountable for moral violations even without the Mosaic covenant: Amos 1-2 condemns the nations for war crimes, slave trading, and covenant violations without citing the Torah. Third, Babylon always occupies the climactic position in Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and Isaiah — the great empire faces the greatest judgment.

The oracles against Babylon in Jeremiah 50-51 are the longest in the collection and among the most vivid in the prophets. They envision a northern nation destroying Babylon just as Babylon destroyed Jerusalem — a vision that sustained hope during the exile.

## Notes
- The genre of "oracles against nations" was common in ancient Near Eastern prophetic traditions, which makes Israel's use of it a culturally intelligible claim to divine sovereignty over all nations.
- Amos's use of the form is rhetorically brilliant: he begins with six nations the Israelite audience would cheer against, before pivoting to Israel and Judah themselves.
