# Nebuchadnezzar's Dream of the Great Statue

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Daniel]]
- **Chapters:** Daniel 2:1-49
- **Summary:** Nebuchadnezzar is troubled by a dream he cannot remember and commands his wise men to both reveal and interpret it on pain of death. When [[The King's Court]]'s wise men confess they cannot, [[Daniel]] seeks God in prayer and receives the secret in a night vision. He describes a colossal statue with a head of fine gold, chest and arms of silver, belly and thighs of bronze, legs of iron, and feet of mingled iron and clay—struck and shattered by a stone cut without hands, which then grows into a mountain filling the whole earth. Daniel interprets the statue as a succession of world empires and the stone as the eternal kingdom God will establish.

## Significance
This vision is one of the most theologically and prophetically rich passages in the Old Testament, laying out a framework of successive Gentile world empires—commonly identified as Babylonian, Medo-Persian, Greek, and Roman—and declaring that God's kingdom will supersede them all without the agency of human power. The stone "cut without hands" is understood in Christian interpretation as a figure of the Messiah and [[The Kingdom of God]], whose dominion is not established by military or political force but by divine decree. The episode also establishes [[Daniel (Prophet)|Daniel]]'s role as a revealer of mysteries and links directly to the vision of [[The Ancient of Days]] in Daniel 7, where the same eternal kingdom is portrayed from heaven's perspective.

## Key Verses
> "Thou sawest till that a stone was cut out without hands, which smote the image upon his feet that were of iron and clay, and brake them to pieces." — Daniel 2:34 (KJV)
