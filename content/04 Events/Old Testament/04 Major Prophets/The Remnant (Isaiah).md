# The Remnant (Isaiah)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Both
- **Book:** [[Isaiah]], [[Romans]]
- **Chapters:** Isaiah 1:9; 10:20-22; 11:11-16; Romans 9:27-29; 11:1-6
- **Summary:** A theological concept threading throughout the prophets: even in the most severe judgment, God preserves a faithful remnant through whom the covenant promises continue; the promise does not fail even when the nation largely falls away.

## Details
Isaiah established the remnant concept in chapter 1: "Except the LORD of hosts had left unto us a very small remnant, we should have been as Sodom, and we should have been like unto Gomorrah" — the fact that any survived at all was divine mercy. In chapters 10-11, in the context of the Assyrian invasion, Isaiah prophesied: "And it shall come to pass in that day, that the remnant of Israel, and such as are escaped of the house of Jacob, shall no more again stay upon him that smote them; but shall stay upon the LORD, the Holy One of Israel, in truth. The remnant shall return, even the remnant of Jacob, unto the mighty God." His son was named Shear-jashub (Isaiah 7:3) — "a remnant shall return" — making the concept a living sign.

Across the prophets the remnant theme appears consistently: Jeremiah's letter to the exiles assumes a surviving remnant; Ezekiel addresses survivors; Micah promises gathering the remnant (4:7; 5:7); Zephaniah describes a humble remnant who trust in God (3:12-13); Zechariah envisions the gathered remnant (8:11-12).

Paul applies the remnant theology directly to the Jewish people in Romans 9-11. Quoting Isaiah's 10,000 figure for the exodus remnant and applying it to his own day: "Even so then at this present time also there is a remnant according to the election of grace" (Romans 11:5). Paul himself is proof: "Hath God cast away his people? God forbid. For I also am an Israelite, of the seed of Abraham." The remnant concept explains how God's word has not failed even though many in Israel did not believe.

## Notes
The remnant concept addresses one of the deepest theological problems: if God's covenant people fall into apostasy and judgment, has God's word failed? The remnant doctrine answers: No — God's election operates through a faithful core within the larger ethnic group, ensuring the covenant never becomes void. Paul's use of this concept in Romans 9-11 is the most systematic theological treatment, arguing that Jewish unbelief in Jesus does not nullify God's covenant faithfulness; a remnant of Jewish believers (including Paul himself) is proof of ongoing election. The concept also shapes ecclesiology: the church is always potentially a remnant people, a minority through whom God works in an unbelieving world.
