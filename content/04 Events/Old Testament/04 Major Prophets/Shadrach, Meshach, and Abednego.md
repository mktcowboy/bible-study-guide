# Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Daniel]]
- **Chapters:** Daniel 3
- **Summary:** Nebuchadnezzar erected a gold statue 90 feet tall and commanded all peoples to bow; the three Jewish men refused; they were thrown into a furnace heated seven times hotter than normal; a fourth mysterious figure appeared with them and they emerged unharmed.

## Details
King Nebuchadnezzar made a golden image 60 cubits high and 6 cubits wide and set it up in the plain of Dura in Babylon. A herald proclaimed that when the musical instruments sounded, all peoples must fall down and worship the golden image; whoever refused would immediately be cast into a burning fiery furnace. Certain Chaldeans accused Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego: they neither served the king's gods nor worshipped the golden image. Nebuchadnezzar in his rage summoned them and offered a second chance, asking rhetorically "and who is that God that shall deliver you out of my hands?"

Their answer is one of the great confessions of faith in scripture: "Our God whom we serve is able to deliver us from the burning fiery furnace... But if not, be it known unto thee, O king, that we will not serve thy gods, nor worship the golden image which thou hast set up." The "but if not" clause acknowledges that God may not deliver — and declares that even without deliverance, their allegiance would not change.

The king commanded the furnace heated seven times hotter than usual; the men who threw the three in were killed by the heat. The king then saw four men walking loose in the fire unharmed, and the fourth had the form "like the Son of God." He called the three out; they emerged with not a hair singed, their garments unchanged, no smell of fire on them. The king praised the God of Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego and decreed that no one could speak against him, "because there is no other God that can deliver after this sort."

## Notes
The identity of the "fourth figure" has been debated in church history. Many patristic writers identified it as a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (a christophany); others see it as an angel. Nebuchadnezzar's own description ("like the Son of God" in KJV; "like a divine being" or "like a son of the gods" in other translations) is ambiguous but pointed. The "but if not" confession has become a paradigm for mature faith that trusts God without demanding deliverance — distinguishing faith from presumption.
