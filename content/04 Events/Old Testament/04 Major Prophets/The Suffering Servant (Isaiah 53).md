# The Suffering Servant (Isaiah 53)

## Overview
- **Testament:** Both
- **Book:** [[Isaiah]], [[Acts]], [[Luke]]
- **Chapters:** Isaiah 42:1-9; 49:1-7; 50:4-11; 52:13-53:12; Acts 8:26-35; Luke 4:16-21
- **Summary:** Isaiah's four Servant Songs describe a mysterious figure anointed by God's Spirit who suffers vicariously for Israel and the nations; the NT identifies this servant as Jesus, fulfilled especially in his passion.

## Details
The four Servant Songs in Isaiah form a progressive portrait of an enigmatic figure: In the first song (42:1-9), the Servant is God's chosen one on whom the Spirit rests, who will bring justice to the nations gently — not breaking a bruised reed. He is appointed as a covenant to the people and a light to the nations. In the second song (49:1-7), the Servant is called from the womb and appointed not only to restore Israel but to be "a light to the Gentiles, that thou mayest be my salvation unto the end of the earth."

In the third song (50:4-11), the Servant has the tongue of the learned, wakes each morning to receive instruction, and submits to opposition — "I gave my back to the smiters, and my cheeks to them that plucked off the hair" — with quiet confidence that God would vindicate him. The fourth and climactic song (52:13-53:12) is the most explicitly sacrificial: the Servant's appearance was marred, he was despised and rejected, a man of sorrows. "Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows... he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed." He was led as a lamb to the slaughter, made an offering for sin, bore the sins of many, and made intercession for the transgressors.

When Philip met the Ethiopian eunuch reading Isaiah 53, the eunuch asked "of whom speaketh the prophet this? of himself, or of some other man?" Philip "began at the same scripture, and preached unto him Jesus."

## Notes
Isaiah 53 is the most frequently quoted OT passage in NT presentations of the crucifixion. Matthew (8:17), John (12:38), Acts (8:32-33), Romans (10:16), and 1 Peter (2:22-25) all cite it explicitly. The Hebrew term "asham" (guilt offering) in 53:10 is the technical Levitical term for the offering that made restitution for sin — indicating that the Servant's death is understood as a sacrificial substitution. The identity of the Servant has been disputed in Jewish interpretation (nation of Israel? Isaiah himself? a future king?) but the NT is uniformly clear: the servant is Jesus.
