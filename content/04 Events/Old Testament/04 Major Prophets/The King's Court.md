# The King's Court

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Daniel]]
- **Chapters:** Daniel 1
- **Summary:** Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah were selected from among Judean exiles for training in Nebuchadnezzar's court; they refused the king's food as a matter of covenant faithfulness and after ten days were found healthier than all who ate the royal diet.

## Details
After Nebuchadnezzar's conquest of Jerusalem in 605 BC, he instructed his chief official Ashpenaz to select young men from the nobility of Judah — those without physical defect, handsome, intelligent, quick to learn — to be educated in the language and literature of the Chaldeans for three years, after which they would serve the king. Among those selected were Daniel (renamed Belteshazzar), Hananiah (Shadrach), Mishael (Meshach), and Azariah (Abednego). They were assigned portions of the king's food and wine.

Daniel purposed in his heart not to defile himself with the king's food or wine and asked the chief official for permission to eat differently. God had granted Daniel favour with the official, who expressed sympathy but fear: "I am afraid of my lord the king, who hath appointed your meat and your drink: for why should he see your faces worse liking than the children which are of your sort? then shall ye make me endanger my head to the king." Daniel then proposed a ten-day test: vegetables and water only, compared against those eating the royal food. If they looked worse, he would comply.

At the end of ten days their countenances appeared fairer and fatter in flesh than the young men who ate the king's food. The guard removed their assigned food and wine and gave them only vegetables. God gave the four young men knowledge, skill in all learning and wisdom, and Daniel understanding of visions and dreams. At the end of three years they were presented to Nebuchadnezzar; he found none equal to them and they entered royal service. In all matters of wisdom and understanding the king found them ten times better than all the magicians and enchanters in his realm.

## Notes
Daniel 1 establishes the theme of the entire book: faithful Jews maintaining covenant identity in a foreign empire that seeks to assimilate them through culture, education, and naming. The renaming (giving Babylonian names) was a standard assimilation technique; the four maintained their internal identity while navigating the external demands. The food issue was likely connected to idolatrous dedication of the royal food (and possibly pork or blood) rather than merely dietary preference. Their supernatural health demonstrates that covenant faithfulness is not a disadvantage even in worldly terms.
