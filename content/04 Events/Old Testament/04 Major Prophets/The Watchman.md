# The Watchman

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Ezekiel]]
- **Chapters:** Ezekiel 3:17-21; 33:1-9
- **Summary:** God appointed [[Ezekiel]] as a watchman for Israel; if he failed to warn the wicked, their blood would be on his hands; if he warned and they refused, they bore their own guilt.

## Details
Twice in Ezekiel — at the beginning of his ministry (3:17-21) and at the beginning of the oracles of restoration (33:1-9) — God gave Ezekiel the image of the watchman (Hebrew: *tzofeh*). A watchman stands on the city wall scanning the horizon; when he sees the sword coming, he blows the trumpet. If he fails to blow it and the city falls, the blood of the citizens is on his hands. If he blows and they refuse to flee, they bear their own guilt.

God applies this image directly: Ezekiel is the watchman for the house of Israel. His responsibility is to speak the warning God gives him; the response belongs to his hearers. This dual accountability — prophet held responsible for speaking, hearer held responsible for responding — defines prophetic ministry throughout Ezekiel.

The watchman motif also appears in Habakkuk 2:1 and Isaiah 62:6, but nowhere is it developed as fully as in Ezekiel. In Ezekiel 33, it is paired with the principle that God takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked but desires repentance — the watchman's warning is always offered in hope, not merely in warning.

## Notes
- The watchman concept underlies Paul's claim in Acts 20:26-27 that he is "pure from the blood of all men" because he declared the whole counsel of God.
- The image integrates individual moral responsibility with communal accountability — a defining tension in Ezekiel's theology.
