# Is It Nothing to You

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Lamentations]]
- **Chapters:** Lamentations 1:1-22
- **Summary:** Jerusalem is personified as a widow weeping bitterly in the night, calling out "Is it nothing to you, all ye that pass by?" while simultaneously confessing "The LORD is righteous; for I have rebelled against his commandment" — lament and confession held together in the acrostic discipline of grief.

## Details

Lamentations opens with one of the most desolate words in the Hebrew Bible: *Ekhah* — "How!" It is the same word that opens the great funeral dirge, and it signals immediately that what follows is a death song. The city of Jerusalem, which had been the center of the world in the Israelite imagination — the place where heaven and earth met, where the LORD's name dwelt, the joy of the whole earth (Psalm 48:2) — is now a widow. "How doth the city sit solitary, that was full of people! how is she become as a widow! she that was great among the nations, and princess among the provinces, how is she become tributary!" (1:1). The rhetorical contrasts accumulate: great/tributary, full of people/solitary, princess/vassal. Everything that defined Jerusalem's status has been inverted.

The personification of Jerusalem as a weeping woman is one of Lamentations' most powerful devices. "She weepeth sore in the night, and her tears are on her cheeks: among all her lovers she hath none to comfort her: all her friends have dealt treacherously with her, they are become her enemies" (1:2). The "lovers" here are the nations and allies Israel had sought out — Egypt, Assyria, the surrounding nations — to whom Israel had repeatedly turned in moments of political crisis instead of to the LORD. All of them have abandoned her now. The political theology embedded in this verse is precise: Israel turned from the LORD to the nations for security; now the nations have turned from Israel, and there is no one left to comfort. The theological consequence of covenant unfaithfulness is political abandonment.

The chapter then delivers one of Scripture's most haunting verses: "Is it nothing to you, all ye that pass by? behold, and see if there be any sorrow like unto my sorrow, which is done unto me, wherewith the LORD hath afflicted me in the day of his fierce anger" (1:12). Jerusalem cries out to every passerby — anyone in earshot of history — to stop and look, to register the weight of this suffering, to bear witness. The cry "Is it nothing to you?" is a rebuke of indifference, the demand that suffering not be ignored or walked past without acknowledgment. The Church Fathers heard in this verse a proleptic voice of Christ on the cross — the one whose sorrows exceeded all sorrows, whose affliction was the affliction of God's fierce wrath, calling to every generation that passes: *behold*. The application does not evacuate the original historical reference but extends it to its fullest meaning.

What is theologically remarkable about Lamentations 1 is the simultaneous movement of lament and confession. Jerusalem does not simply protest her suffering as unjust; she admits its justice: "The LORD is righteous; for I have rebelled against his commandment: hear, I pray you, all people, and behold my sorrow: my virgins and my young men are gone into captivity" (1:18). In the same breath that she calls all nations to witness her sorrow, she declares the LORD righteous in bringing it. This is not the reconciled peace of one who has moved beyond grief — Jerusalem is still in agony, her enemies have prevailed, her children are gone. But the confession stands within the lament, not after it. Grief and theological honesty occupy the same moment. Lamentations refuses the comfort of either easy theodicy ("God did not really do this") or despair ("There is no justice in any of this"). It holds both — the LORD is righteous, and it is unbearable.

The literary form of the chapter reinforces this containment of grief: it is an acrostic poem, each verse beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet, from Aleph to Taw. The entire alphabet gives shape to the shapeless. Grief that could spill in any direction — that threatens to overwhelm every boundary of language and meaning — is given structure. The form itself is a theological statement: even in devastation, language holds; even in the worst that can happen, ordered speech remains possible, and the ordering of speech is the first act of maintaining one's humanity.
