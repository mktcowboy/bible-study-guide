# Siege & Fall

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Jeremiah]], [[2 Kings]]
- **Chapters:** Jeremiah 39; 52; 2 Kings 25
- **Summary:** Jerusalem was besieged by Nebuchadnezzar's army for 18 months; the wall was breached; King Zedekiah fled but was captured, blinded, and taken to Babylon; the city and temple were burned; key people were deported.

## Details
In the ninth year of Zedekiah (588 BC), Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon came against Jerusalem with all his army and besieged it. The siege lasted until the eleventh year of Zedekiah (586 BC) — approximately 18 months. In the fourth month, on the ninth day, famine was severe in the city with no bread for the people. The Babylonian army broke through the wall and the city fell. Zedekiah and all the soldiers fled at night through the gate between the two walls by the king's garden, heading toward the Arabah.

The Babylonian army pursued and overtook Zedekiah in the plains of Jericho. He was brought to Nebuchadnezzar at Riblah, where his sentence was carried out: Zedekiah watched his sons and all the nobles of Judah be executed; then his own eyes were put out; he was bound with bronze chains and carried to Babylon. Nebuzaradan, the captain of the guard, then burned the temple, the royal palace, and every great house in Jerusalem. The walls of Jerusalem were broken down. The remaining people were deported to Babylon; only the poorest were left to tend the vineyards and fields.

Jeremiah was specially treated: Nebuchadnezzar's orders were to deal well with him; he was released and given the choice to come to Babylon or stay in the land. He stayed with Gedaliah, the appointed governor.

## Notes
The fall of Jerusalem in 586 BC is the central trauma of the OT — the destruction of the temple, the city, and the Davidic monarchy. Lamentations was composed in its immediate aftermath. The book of Jeremiah is the most extensive prophetic engagement with this event: Jeremiah had spent 40 years predicting it and was still present to witness it. The irony of his release by the Babylonians — treated better by Israel's enemies than by his own people — is one of scripture's sharpest indictments of the prophetic rejection pattern that Jesus would later invoke (Matthew 23:37).
