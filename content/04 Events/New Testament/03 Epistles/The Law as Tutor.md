# The Law as Tutor

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Galatians]]
- **Chapters:** Galatians 3:1-29
- **Summary:** Paul's sustained argument that the law functioned as a temporary guardian pointing to Christ, and that with his coming all who believe — Jew and Gentile, slave and free, male and female — are equally children of God and heirs of Abraham's promise.

## Details
Galatians 3 opens with an exasperated apostle: "O foolish Galatians, who hath bewitched you, that ye should not obey the truth, before whose eyes Jesus Christ hath been evidently set forth, crucified among you?" (3:1). The Galatians had received the Spirit through hearing with faith, not through works of the law — Paul appeals to their own experience as evidence. He then reaches back to Abraham as the foundational test case, since the Judaizing teachers had almost certainly invoked the patriarch's circumcision as warrant for their demands. Paul reads Genesis differently: "Abraham believed God, and it was accounted to him for righteousness" (3:6, citing Genesis 15:6). Righteousness came by faith, before circumcision (cf. Romans 4), so the true children of Abraham are those who share his faith, not merely his physical descent.

The promise to Abraham that "In thee shall all nations be blessed" (3:8, citing Genesis 12:3) is read by Paul as a pre-announcement of the gospel — the gospel preached in advance to Abraham. The nations' blessing was always bound up with faith-righteousness, not law-keeping. The law, which came 430 years after the covenant promise to Abraham (3:17), cannot annul or supplement that covenant. Its purpose was different: "It was added because of transgressions, till the seed should come to whom the promise was made" (3:19). The law functioned to define and expose sin, to hold Israel under its custody, to press the need for the coming Seed — Christ — in whom the promise would find its fulfillment.

The controlling metaphor arrives in 3:24-25: "the law was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ, that we might be justified by faith. But after that faith is come, we are no longer under a schoolmaster." The Greek word is *paidagogos* — not the teacher, but the household slave tasked with escorting children to school and supervising their conduct. The *paidagogos* held authority over children, but his authority was custodial and temporary. When the children reached maturity, his supervisory role ended. So the law held Israel as a minor heir under supervision until the fullness of time, when Christ came and faith in him became the path to sonship. The law was always pointing forward; it was never the destination. To return to law-keeping as the basis for standing before God, after Christ has come, is to go back to the elementary stage that was meant to lead beyond itself.

The chapter closes with one of Paul's most sweeping declarations of Christian unity: "For ye are all the children of God by faith in Christ Jesus. For as many of you as have been baptized into Christ have put on Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither bond nor free, there is neither male nor female: for ye are all one in Christ Jesus. And if ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise" (3:26-29). The ethnic, social, and gender distinctions that structured the ancient world — and that the circumcision party was effectively reinstating as markers of spiritual hierarchy — are relativized by union with Christ. They do not cease to exist, but they no longer determine one's standing before God or one's place in the covenant community. All are heirs of the Abrahamic promise through the one Seed who summed up that promise in himself.
