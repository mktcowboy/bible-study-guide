# The Peace of God

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Philippians]]
- **Chapters:** Philippians 4:4-13
- **Summary:** The famous quartet of commands — rejoice, be anxious for nothing, pray with thanksgiving, think on what is good — crowned by Paul's testimony of learned contentment in all circumstances through Christ.

## Details
Philippians 4:4-13 is among the most memorized passages in the New Testament, yet it is frequently lifted from its context. Paul writes from prison, facing possible execution, in a letter full of joy (the word "joy" or "rejoice" appears sixteen times across four chapters). The commands here are not the cheerful advice of a comfortable man but the hard-won disciplines of an apostle who has faced shipwreck, beatings, hunger, and chains. That context gives the passage its weight.

The famous quartet unfolds in sequence. First: "Rejoice in the Lord alway: and again I say, Rejoice" (4:4) — the repetition signals this is not a mood but a command, and its object is not circumstances but the Lord. Second: "Be careful for nothing" (4:6) — *merimnao*, anxiety, the worried turning of the mind — is to be replaced by prayer. Third: present "your requests unto God" with thanksgiving. Fourth, the result: "the peace of God, which passeth all understanding, shall keep your hearts and minds through Christ Jesus" (4:7). The word "keep" (*phroureo*) is a military term — to stand guard over. Peace is not a feeling Paul generates but a garrison God posts.

The mental discipline of 4:8 is equally structured: "whatsoever things are true, whatsoever things are honest, whatsoever things are just, whatsoever things are pure, whatsoever things are lovely, whatsoever things are of good report; if there be any virtue, and if there be any praise, think on these things." The verb *logizomai* means to reckon, calculate, take account of — it is deliberate and sustained thought, not passive entertainment. This list defines the material with which the redeemed mind is to be furnished, functioning as the positive counterpart to the anxieties Paul has forbidden.

The contentment passage (4:11-13) carries a crucial qualifier that is often missed when 4:13 is quoted in isolation. "I have learned, in whatsoever state I am, therewith to be content" (4:11) — the word "learned" (*emathon*) indicates acquired knowledge, not natural temperament. Contentment is not Paul's personality; it is a discipline he has trained into himself through experience. He specifies the range: "I know both how to be abased, and I know how to abound" (4:12). The famous verse that follows — "I can do all things through Christ which strengtheneth me" (4:13) — refers specifically to this capacity to endure both poverty and plenty without losing equilibrium. It is a statement about contentment in economic suffering and abundance, not a general promise of athletic or professional achievement.
