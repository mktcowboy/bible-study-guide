# Tests of Fellowship

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[1 John]]
- **Chapters:** 1 John 2:3-27
- **Summary:** John establishes three recurring tests — moral, social, and doctrinal — by which genuine Christian fellowship can be recognized, while exposing the antichrist spirit that had already produced a schism in the community.

## Details

One of the distinctive structural features of 1 John is John's use of recurring tests that he applies throughout all five chapters. These tests are not a checklist imposed from outside but emerge naturally from the theological axioms of the letter: if God is light and God is love and Jesus is the Christ come in the flesh, then these realities must be verifiable in the life of those who claim fellowship with God. Chapter 2 is where John first brings these three tests into explicit focus. The first is the moral test — obedience to the commandments of God: "And hereby we do know that we know him, if we keep his commandments. He that saith, I know him, and keepeth not his commandments, is a liar, and the truth is not in him" (2:3-4). Knowledge of God, again in the Johannine sense, is not intellectual acquaintance but life-transforming encounter. If that encounter has genuinely occurred, the will moves toward obedience.

The second test is social — love of the brothers. John introduces what he calls "a new commandment" that is simultaneously "an old commandment" (2:7-8): love one another. It is old because it has been present from the beginning of the Christian message; it is new because Christ's love has brought it to a qualitatively new depth and made it newly applicable in the light of the end. The test is sharpened into a contrast: "He that saith he is in the light, and hateth his brother, is in darkness even until now. He that loveth his brother abideth in the light, and there is none occasion of stumbling in him" (2:9-10). Hatred of the brother — active ill-will and contempt — is the precise diagnostic marker of remaining in darkness, regardless of what theological claims are being made. And darkness has a disorienting effect: "he that hateth his brother is in darkness, and walketh in darkness, and knoweth not whither he goeth, because that darkness hath blinded his eyes" (2:11). This is not merely a moral failure but a cognitive one — hatred corrupts perception.

The third test is doctrinal — the confession that Jesus is the Christ. John introduces "antichrist" as the term for those who deny this confession, and then delivers one of the most significant ecclesiological observations in the NT: "They went out from us, but they were not of us; for if they had been of us, they would no doubt have continued with us: but they went out, that they might be made manifest that they were not all of us" (2:19). A schism had occurred in John's community. People had departed — almost certainly teachers promoting a proto-Gnostic Christology that denied the true humanity or messianic identity of Jesus. John's reading of the departure is searching: they left because they did not belong. Continued fellowship is itself a sign of genuine incorporation; departure reveals that the root was never real. The doctrinal question is then made explicit: "Who is a liar but he that denieth that Jesus is the Christ? He is antichrist, that denieth the Father and the Son" (2:22). The denial of Jesus's messiahship is not merely a Christological error — it carries with it a denial of the Father, because Jesus is the Father's definitive self-disclosure.

The passage concludes with a remarkable pneumatological note. The ordinary believers who remain faithful do not need the departed teachers, because they have received an anointing — the *chrisma* (literally "anointing") of the Holy Spirit — that functions as an inner teacher: "But the anointing which ye have received of him abideth in you, and ye need not that any man teach you: but as the same anointing teacheth you of all things, and is truth, and is no lie, and even as it hath taught you, ye shall abide in him" (2:27). This is not anti-intellectualism or a rejection of all teaching, but an affirmation that the community of ordinary Spirit-indwelt believers possesses a capacity for discernment that cannot be overridden by the claims of spiritual elites. The three tests — obedience, love, and right confession — function as the ongoing grammar of authentic Christianity throughout all five chapters of the letter, cycling and deepening as John returns to them again and again.
