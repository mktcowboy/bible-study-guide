# Armor of God

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Ephesians]]
- **Chapters:** Ephesians 6:10–18
- **Summary:** Paul's closing exhortation in Ephesians, calling believers to spiritual warfare by putting on the whole armor of God — each piece drawn from the equipment of a Roman soldier and recast as a spiritual virtue or weapon for standing against the schemes of the devil.

## Details
The armor passage concludes the ethical section of Ephesians (chapters 4-6), which applies the great theological heights of chapters 1-3 (election, redemption, reconciliation, the mystery of the church) to concrete Christian living. The final threat to be addressed is not human or social but cosmic: "For we wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places" (6:12). This statement presupposes the spiritual cosmology Paul sketched in Ephesians 1:21 and 3:10 — a hierarchy of angelic and demonic powers that stand behind the visible world's conflicts.

The armor is introduced with the call to "put on the whole armour of God" (6:11, 13) — the Greek word panoplia, a soldier's complete kit. The repetition is emphatic: partial armor leaves partial vulnerability. The first three pieces are described as things already worn or standing in (girding, putting on, shod), suggesting settled virtues of character: the belt of truth (truthfulness and the truth of the gospel as the soldier's stability), the breastplate of righteousness (the righteousness of Christ covering the vital organs of the heart), and feet shod with the preparation of the gospel of peace (readiness and mobility grounded in the good news). These constitute the soldier's standing posture.

The next three are taken up actively in conflict: the shield of faith, the helmet of salvation, and the sword of the Spirit. The shield (thureos in Greek — the large rectangular Roman scutum, not the small round parma) is capable of quenching "all the fiery darts of the wicked" (6:16). Roman soldiers could lock these shields together wall-like, suggesting the communal dimension of faith's defense. The helmet of salvation protects the mind — hope of salvation was the helmet in the parallel 1 Thessalonians 5:8 passage. The sword of the Spirit, identified as the word of God, is the single offensive weapon in the list, pointing to Scripture and Spirit-empowered speech as the means of advancing against darkness.

The passage concludes with the foundation beneath all the armor: "Praying always with all prayer and supplication in the Spirit" (6:18). Prayer is not a seventh piece of armor but the sustaining activity in which the armored soldier operates. Paul draws his imagery from Isaiah 59:17 (where God himself puts on righteousness as a breastplate and salvation as a helmet) and Isaiah 11:5 (where righteousness is the belt of the Messiah). The armor of God is, in the deepest sense, the character of God made available to his people.
