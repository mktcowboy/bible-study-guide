# Ministry of Reconciliation

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[2 Corinthians]]
- **Chapters:** 2 Corinthians 5:11-21
- **Summary:** Paul's most compact statement of the atonement, grounding the ministry of reconciliation in the great exchange — God made Christ to be sin so that believers might become the righteousness of God in him.

## Details
Second Corinthians 5:11-21 is preceded by Paul's meditation on the future body and the judgment seat of Christ (5:1-10), and the awareness of that coming accounting drives his present compulsion: "Knowing therefore the terror of the Lord, we persuade men" (5:11). He is not performing for the Corinthians but living coram Deo, before God. The controlling motive, however, is not fear alone but love: "For the love of Christ constraineth us; because we thus judge, that if one died for all, then were all dead: And that he died for all, that they which live should not henceforth live unto themselves, but unto him which died for them, and rose again" (5:14-15). The logic is substitutionary from the outset — one died for all, therefore all died in him; his resurrection life is now the basis for their new mode of living.

This new life produces a transformed way of seeing. Paul no longer regards anyone "after the flesh" — even his former estimation of Christ according to worldly categories is gone (5:16). What has replaced it is the category of new creation: "Therefore if any man be in Christ, he is a new creature: old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new" (5:17). The Greek is emphatic — *kainē ktisis*, a new creation, not merely a new chapter. This is eschatological language drawn from Isaiah's vision of the new heavens and new earth (Isaiah 65:17), applied to the individual believer's union with Christ. The old covenant order of evaluation — ethnic identity, status, law-keeping — has been superseded.

The ground of this new creation is stated immediately: "And all things are of God, who hath reconciled us to himself by Jesus Christ, and hath given to us the ministry of reconciliation" (5:18). Reconciliation moves in one direction — from God to humanity — because the enmity was on the human side. God was not estranged from himself; sinners were estranged from him. The mechanics of that reconciliation are then given in compressed form: "God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them" (5:19). The non-imputation of trespasses is not an arbitrary amnesty but is grounded in what 5:21 makes explicit — a double imputation, the center of the entire Pauline gospel.

"For he hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in him" (5:21). The verse has no verb of approximation — it does not say God treated Christ *as if* he were sin, but that he *was made* sin. The one who knew no sin in his own person bore sin representatively and substitutionally. The result is that believers are not merely forgiven but are constituted the very righteousness of God *in him* — a righteousness that belongs to them by virtue of union with Christ, not by moral achievement. This is the forensic core of justification by faith. Paul's statement in Romans 3:21-26 and Isaiah 53:6 ("the Lord hath laid on him the iniquity of us all") stand as the closest canonical parallels. The passage closes with the practical implication: those who have received this reconciliation are now ambassadors for Christ, as though God were pleading through them — "we pray you in Christ's stead, be ye reconciled to God" (5:20).
