# The Grace of God That Brings Salvation

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Titus]]
- **Chapters:** Titus 2:11-14
- **Summary:** One of the most compressed soteriological statements in the New Testament — grace as past appearing, present teacher, and future hope — producing a people purified and zealous for good works.

## Details
Titus 2:11-14 is embedded within a practical section of household and social instruction: older men, older women, younger women, younger men, and slaves are each given behavioral directives in 2:1-10. But Paul does not allow the ethics to float free of their theological foundation. Verse 11 introduces the grounding of all that has preceded: "For the grace of God that bringeth salvation hath appeared to all men." The practical instruction is because of the appearing of grace — the ethics flow from the event of Christ's coming, not from social convention or legal obligation.

The structure of the passage spans three tenses of salvation, making it one of the most complete soteriological statements in Paul's writings compressed into so few verses. The past tense: "hath appeared" (*epiphane*) — grace was made visible in a historical event, the incarnation and atoning work of Jesus Christ. The present tense: grace is "teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world" (2:12). Grace is not merely an event to look back on; it is an ongoing tutor reshaping present conduct. The future tense: "Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ" (2:13). The believer lives in the present between two appearings — the first coming that secured salvation and the second coming that will consummate it.

The title given to Christ in 2:13 is theologically significant: "the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ." The Greek construction (*tou megalou theou kai soteros hemon Iesou Christou*) most naturally refers to a single person rather than two — Jesus Christ is identified directly as "the great God and our Savior." This is one of the clearest divine titles applied to Christ in the Pauline corpus, and it stands parallel to John 1:1 and Colossians 2:9 as a direct affirmation of Christ's deity.

The purpose of Christ's self-giving is stated in 2:14: "Who gave himself for us, that he might redeem us from all iniquity, and purify unto himself a peculiar people, zealous of good works." Three purposes are layered: ransom (*lutrosetai* — to redeem by paying a price), purification, and the creation of a distinctive people. The phrase "peculiar people" does not mean eccentric but chosen — the word *periousios* echoes the Septuagint's description of Israel as God's "treasured possession" (Exodus 19:5, Deuteronomy 14:2). The new covenant people of God are defined not by ethnicity but by their zeal for good works — which circles back to the practical instructions of 2:1-10 and frames the entire ethical project of the letter.

## Important Verses
> "For the grace of God that bringeth salvation hath appeared to all men, Teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world; Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ; Who gave himself for us, that he might redeem us from all iniquity, and purify unto himself a peculiar people, zealous of good works." — Titus 2:11-14
