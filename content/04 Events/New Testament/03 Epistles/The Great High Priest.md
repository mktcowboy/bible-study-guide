# The Great High Priest

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Hebrews]]
- **Chapters:** Hebrews 4:14–10:18
- **Summary:** The theological heart of Hebrews: Jesus as the supreme and final High Priest after the order of Melchizedek, who offered himself once for all in the heavenly sanctuary, perfecting forever those who are sanctified — surpassing and terminating the entire Levitical sacrificial system.

## Details
The epistle to the Hebrews is organized around a series of "better than" comparisons demonstrating Jesus' superiority over every Old Testament institution. The priesthood section (4:14-10:18) is the longest and most intricate. It opens with a pastoral appeal: "Seeing then that we have a great high priest, that is passed into the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold fast our profession. For we have not an high priest which cannot be touched with the feeling of our infirmities; but was in all points tempted like as we are, yet without sin. Let us therefore come boldly unto the throne of grace" (4:14-16). The heavenly high priesthood of Jesus is simultaneously exalted and empathetic.

The Melchizedek argument (chapters 5-7) is the most theologically creative move in Hebrews. Melchizedek appears in Genesis 14 as a mysterious priest-king of Salem who receives tithes from Abraham — and in Psalm 110:4, God swears an oath to the Davidic king: "Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek." The author of Hebrews exploits what Genesis does not say: no genealogy, no birth, no death, no beginning or end of days recorded. Melchizedek thus serves as a type of the Son of God, who holds his priesthood permanently by the power of an indestructible life (7:16). The Levitical priesthood was validated by genealogy; Christ's by divine oath and resurrection.

The tabernacle comparison (chapters 8-9) develops the sanctuary typology. The earthly tabernacle was a shadow and copy of the heavenly sanctuary (8:5, citing Exodus 25:40 where God showed Moses "the pattern" on the mountain). The old covenant and its priesthood could not bring "perfection" — they were designed to point forward. Jesus entered "not into the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us" (9:24). He did not need to offer repeatedly, as the earthly high priest offered annually on the Day of Atonement; "but now once in the end of the world hath he appeared to put away sin by the sacrifice of himself" (9:26).

The climax in chapter 10 sets old covenant against new: the law was "a shadow of good things to come" and "can never with those sacrifices which they offered year by year continually make the comers thereunto perfect" (10:1). The very repetition of the sacrifices testified to their inadequacy — they were reminders of sins, not removers. But Christ, coming into the world to do the Father's will, offered one body once: "By the which will we are sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all" (10:10). The Levitical priest stood daily; Christ sat down at God's right hand, because his work was finished. "For by one offering he hath perfected for ever them that are sanctified" (10:14). The veil is torn; the way into the holiest is open; the sin problem is solved, not managed.
