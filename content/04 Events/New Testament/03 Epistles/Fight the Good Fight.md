# Fight the Good Fight

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[2 Timothy]]
- **Chapters:** 2 Timothy 4:6-8
- **Summary:** Paul's farewell from a Roman prison, expecting imminent death — a three-clause testimony of completion followed by the crown of righteousness, set against the starkly human detail of his abandonment and practical needs.

## Details
Second Timothy is almost certainly Paul's final letter, written from a Roman prison during his second imprisonment under Nero, probably 66-67 AD. Unlike his first imprisonment (Acts 28), which allowed relative freedom and ministry, this confinement is severe — he is chained like a criminal (2:9), abandoned by most of his colleagues (4:10-11, 4:16), and under no illusion about the outcome. The passage 4:6-8 is the emotional and spiritual summit of the letter, the final statement of a man who has run the full length of his course and can see the finish line.

The opening metaphor of 4:6 is striking in its double register. "For I am now ready to be offered" — the word *spendo* means to be poured out as a drink offering. Paul's death is not defeat but sacrifice, a cultic act within the economy of God's purposes. "The time of my departure is at hand" — *analusis*, the loosening of a tent or the casting off of a ship's moorings: departure as movement toward something rather than loss of something. Both images transform death from ending to transition.

The three-clause testimony of 4:7 forms a perfect chiasm of completion: "I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith." The fight (*agon*) is the athletic contest of the gladiatorial arena or the wrestling ground — the same metaphor Paul had urged on Timothy throughout the letter. The course (*dromos*) is the race track — the same image from Philippians 3:14 of pressing toward the goal. The faith (*pistis*) is both the body of Christian doctrine entrusted to Timothy and Paul's own personal faithfulness. He has kept what was given to him. All three are past-tense declarations of completion, not aspiration.

What follows in 4:8 is the expectation: "Henceforth there is laid up for me a crown of righteousness, which the Lord, the righteous judge, shall give me at that day: and not to me only, but unto all them also that love his appearing." The crown (*stephanos*) of righteousness is not earned by merit but given by the righteous Judge — and it is available to all who have loved Christ's appearing, not to Paul alone. The democratic breadth of the promise opens the farewell beyond autobiography into invitation. Immediately after this doxology, Paul writes that Demas has forsaken him, that only Luke remains, and that Timothy should bring his cloak and the books — especially the parchments. The contrast between the eternal crown and the winter cloak is one of the most humanizing moments in all of Scripture: the same man who surveys the sweep of his apostolic career also needs a coat and asks for his books.
