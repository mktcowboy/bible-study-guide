# Godly Sorrow and Repentance

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[2 Corinthians]]
- **Chapters:** 2 Corinthians 7:2-16
- **Summary:** Paul's account of Titus's return with good news from Corinth, and the crucial theological distinction he draws between godly sorrow — which works repentance unto salvation — and the sorrow of the world, which works death.

## Details
Second Corinthians 7 opens in the middle of an emotional current that runs across the whole letter. Paul had sent what scholars call the "severe letter" (likely the lost letter referenced in 2:3-4, possibly chapters 10-13) after a painful confrontation at Corinth, and then spent weeks in deep anxiety about how it would be received. He writes that even when he came to Troas he had no rest in his spirit (2:13), and that when he came into Macedonia his flesh had no rest — "without were fightings, within were fears" (7:5). The relief came through Titus: "Nevertheless God, that comforteth those that are cast down, comforted us by the coming of Titus" (7:6).

Titus's report was better than Paul had dared hope. The Corinthians had responded to the severe letter not with resentment but with repentance — with earnest desire to clear themselves, with indignation, with fear, with longing, with zeal, with vindication (7:11). Paul had been anxious about sending the letter; now he does not regret it. His relief, however, presses him toward a distinction of the first theological importance: "Now I rejoice, not that ye were made sorry, but that ye sorrowed to repentance: for ye were made sorry after a godly manner, that ye might receive damage by us in nothing" (7:9). The Corinthians were sorrowful — but the question is never the intensity of grief, nor even its sincerity in a general sense, but its orientation.

The decisive verse follows: "For godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of: but the sorrow of the world worketh death" (7:10). Paul distinguishes two griefs that may feel identical from the inside but move in opposite directions. Worldly sorrow is essentially self-referential — it grieves the exposure of sin, the loss of reputation, the consequences that follow, the embarrassment before others. It is fundamentally a sorrow about oneself. Godly sorrow is oriented differently: it grieves having sinned against a holy God, and because it is directed outward and upward rather than inward, it does not spiral into despair but issues in the turning that Scripture calls repentance. The Greek word is *metanoia* — a change of mind, of direction, of orientation — and Paul says this kind of repentance is "not to be repented of," meaning it is itself without regret. Worldly grief, by contrast, produces death: Judas's sorrow (Matthew 27:3-5) stands as the canonical counterexample to Peter's (John 21:15-19).

The pastoral weight of the passage lies in its precision as a diagnostic tool. Many people feel sorry after sin without truly repenting — the sorrow attaches to consequences rather than to the offense against God, and so it loops back to the same behavior once the consequences recede. Paul's language here gives the church one of the New Testament's most exact tests of whether grief over sin is the kind that saves or the kind that merely damages. The passage also reveals Paul's pastoral heart with unusual tenderness: his relief at their repentance, his confidence in them, his joy in Titus's joy — all of it paints a picture of what genuine apostolic care looks like when the gospel does its work in a community.
