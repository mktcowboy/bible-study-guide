# The Supremacy of Christ

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Colossians]]
- **Chapters:** Colossians 1:15-20
- **Summary:** The most comprehensive Christology in the New Testament, structured as an early hymn in two strophes — Christ as the agent of original creation and the head of the new creation in the church.

## Details
Colossians 1:15-20 stands as the highest Christological statement in all of Paul's letters and is widely recognized as an early hymn fragment incorporated into the letter, parallel in form and function to the Christ Hymn of Philippians 2:5-11. The immediate context is Paul's thanksgiving for the Colossians' faith (1:3-14), but the hymn escalates rapidly beyond thanksgiving into cosmic declaration. Its purpose is polemical as well as doxological: the Colossian "philosophy" (2:8) apparently elevated angelic intermediaries (*thrones, dominions, principalities, powers*) as necessary mediators between God and humanity — the hymn systematically subordinates all of these to Christ.

The first strophe (1:15-17) concerns creation. Christ is "the image of the invisible God" (*eikon tou theou tou aoratou*) — not a copy but the precise visible expression of the One who cannot be seen. "Firstborn of every creature" (1:15) does not mean Christ was created; *prototokos* is a title of supremacy and priority (as in Psalm 89:27, where God makes David "firstborn, higher than the kings of the earth") rather than a statement of origin. The scope of his creative agency is total: "all things... in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether they be thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him" (1:16). Not only through him but *for* him — Christ is both the agent and the goal of creation. The capstone: "he is before all things, and by him all things consist" (*sunistemi*, 1:17) — hold together. The universe's coherence and ongoing order depend on Christ's active sustaining.

The second strophe (1:18-20) concerns new creation. "He is the head of the body, the church" (1:18) — the same one who holds the cosmos together is the governing head of the community of redemption. He is "the beginning, the firstborn from the dead" — his resurrection inaugurates a new order of existence, making him preeminent in the age to come just as he is in the age that is. "For it pleased the Father that in him should all fulness dwell" (1:19) — *pleroma* (fullness) was likely a technical term in the Colossian philosophy referring to the totality of divine powers distributed among angelic beings; Paul's answer is that all of it — every last drop — dwells in Christ bodily (2:9).

The reconciliation that closes the hymn is cosmic in scope: "to reconcile all things unto himself... whether they be things in earth, or things in heaven, making peace through the blood of his cross" (1:20). The instrument of reconciliation is shocking — the cross, the instrument of shame and execution, is the mechanism of cosmic peace. This passage has shaped Christian theology of creation, incarnation, and atonement in every century, and its connections run through the Johannine Logos theology (John 1:1-14), the Christ Hymn in Philippians, and the opening of Hebrews (1:1-3).

## Important Verses
> "He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of every creature: For by him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible, whether they be thrones, or dominions, or principalities, or powers: all things were created by him, and for him." — Colossians 1:15-16

> "He is before all things, and by him all things consist. And he is the head of the body, the church: who is the beginning, the firstborn from the dead; that in all things he might have the preeminence." — Colossians 1:17-18
