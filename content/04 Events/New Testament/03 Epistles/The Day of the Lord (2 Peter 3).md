# The Day of the Lord (2 Peter 3)

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[2 Peter]]
- **Chapters:** 2 Peter 3:3-13
- **Summary:** Peter answers the scoffing objection to Christ's return with three decisive arguments, anchoring eschatological patience in the character of God and calling believers to holy living in light of the coming dissolution of all things.

## Details

The third chapter of 2 Peter pivots from the false teachers of chapter 2 to the scoffers of the last days, who represent a distinct but related danger. These mockers ask with calculated contempt: "Where is the promise of his coming? for since the fathers fell asleep, all things continue as they were from the beginning of the creation" (3:4). The objection is essentially uniformitarian — nature has always proceeded at the same pace, therefore no catastrophic divine intervention should be expected. It is a sophisticated form of practical atheism: the God who *might* act is indefinitely deferred, and in the meantime moral accountability dissolves. Peter identifies their core problem as willful ignorance: "this they willingly are ignorant of" (3:5). They choose not to remember.

Peter's first answer is historical: the scoffers forget that the world was once destroyed by water. The same divine word that "in the beginning" called the world into existence through water is the word that brought the flood over the ungodly world (3:5-6). There is no metaphysical barrier to divine catastrophic action — it has already happened once. And by that same word, "the heavens and the earth, which are now, by the same word are kept in store, reserved unto fire against the day of judgment and perdition of ungodly men" (3:7). The present stability of the cosmos is not evidence that God never acts; it is the patience of God holding the present order in reserve.

His second answer is theological: God's relationship to time is categorically different from ours. "But, beloved, be not ignorant of this one thing, that one day is with the Lord as a thousand years, and a thousand years as one day" (3:8). The apparent delay in Christ's coming is not a broken promise but an expression of divine longsuffering. The Lord is "not slack concerning his promise, as some men count slackness; but is longsuffering to us-ward, not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance" (3:9). This verse is one of the most theologically rich in the epistle: the delay of judgment is itself an act of mercy. Every day the Lord has not returned is another day the door of repentance remains open.

The third answer is prophetic certainty: the Day of the Lord *will* come, and when it comes it will be sudden and total. "But the day of the Lord will come as a thief in the night; in the which the heavens shall pass away with a great noise, and the elements shall melt with fervent heat, the earth also and the works that are therein shall be burned up" (3:10). The scope is cosmic — not merely political or national judgment, but the dissolution of the created order itself. This is the backdrop against which Peter draws his great practical conclusion: "Seeing then that all these things shall be dissolved, what manner of persons ought ye to be in all holy conversation and godliness, Looking for and hasting unto the coming of the day of God?" (3:11-12). The dissolution of the present order is not cause for despair but for reordered priorities and purified character. And beyond the fire lies the promise: "Nevertheless we, according to his promise, look for new heavens and a new earth, wherein dwelleth righteousness" (3:13) — the same vision that will dominate the closing chapters of Revelation and the hope of the [[New Jerusalem]].
