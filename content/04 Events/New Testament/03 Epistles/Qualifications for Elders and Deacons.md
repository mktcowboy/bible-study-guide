# Qualifications for Elders and Deacons

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[1 Timothy]]
- **Chapters:** 1 Timothy 3:1-16
- **Summary:** Paul's twin lists of qualifications for overseers and deacons, grounded in character and household management, closing with an early Christological hymn.

## Details
First Timothy 3 provides the most complete set of qualifications for church leadership in the New Testament, paralleled by Titus 1:5-9 for elders in Crete. Paul opens with an affirmation: "This is a true saying, If a man desire the office of a bishop, he desireth a good work" (3:1). The desire for leadership is not suspect — it is commended. What follows is not a job description but a character profile. The qualifications for the overseer (episkopos — also translated bishop or elder) are primarily relational and moral rather than professional or academic.

The list for overseers spans 3:2-7. The bishop must be "blameless, the husband of one wife, vigilant, sober, of good behaviour, given to hospitality, apt to teach; Not given to wine, no striker, not greedy of filthy lucre; but patient, not a brawler, not covetous; One that ruleth well his own house, having his children in subjection with all gravity" (3:2-4). The qualification "husband of one wife" has been interpreted variously — as requiring marriage, forbidding polygamy, or requiring marital faithfulness — but in any reading it points toward relational integrity. The rationale Paul provides for the household management requirement is a piece of practical ecclesiology: "For if a man know not how to rule his own house, how shall he take care of the church of God?" (3:5). The church is understood by analogy to a household, and the elder's domestic life is the observable proof of his fitness to lead a larger family.

The qualifications for deacons (3:8-13) are similarly character-focused: "grave, not doubletongued, not given to much wine, not greedy of filthy lucre; Holding the mystery of the faith in a pure conscience" (3:8-9). Deacons are to be tested first (3:10), and their spouses are also held to a standard of character (3:11). The promise to those who serve well is encouragement: "they that have used the office of a deacon well purchase to themselves a good degree, and great boldness in the faith which is in Christ Jesus" (3:13). The word "degree" (*bathmos*) implies standing or rank — faithful service produces a kind of spiritual gravitas.

The chapter closes unexpectedly with a poetic fragment of confession (3:16) — "great is the mystery of godliness" — followed by six balanced lines that scholars have identified as another early Christian hymn: "God was manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, seen of angels, preached unto the Gentiles, believed on in the world, received up into glory." The six lines may trace the arc of Christ's ministry across two realms — the first three earthly/spiritual, the last three moving toward universal proclamation and final glorification. Its placement after the leadership qualifications suggests that the mystery of Christ's person is the very thing the church's ordered leadership exists to guard and proclaim.
