# The Good Works of a Sound Church

## Overview

- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Titus]]
- **Chapters:** Titus 2:1-14
- **Summary:** The apostle [[Paul (Apostle)]] charges Titus to teach sound doctrine that shapes every member of the congregation — aged men, aged women, young women, young men, and servants — in godly living befitting the gospel. The passage culminates in a declaration of the grace of God that appeared in [[Jesus Christ]], teaching the whole church to deny ungodliness and to live soberly, righteously, and godly in this present world, in expectation of the blessed hope of his glorious appearing.

## Significance

This passage is the practical heart of [[Titus]], grounding Christian conduct not in mere moralism but in the appearance of God's grace in the person of Christ. Sound doctrine (*hugiainouse didaskalia*) is not merely correct belief but belief that produces health — whole, ordered, godly lives — throughout the entire body of the congregation. The "grace of God that bringeth salvation" is presented as the great teacher, instructing believers to renounce what the flesh craves and to live as those who await their Redeemer's return. The church's visible holiness is thus the fruit of the gospel, not its root.

## Key Verses

> "For the grace of God that bringeth salvation hath appeared to all men, Teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world; Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ; Who gave himself for us, that he might redeem us from all iniquity, and purify unto himself a peculiar people, zealous of good works." — Titus 2:11-14 (KJV)
