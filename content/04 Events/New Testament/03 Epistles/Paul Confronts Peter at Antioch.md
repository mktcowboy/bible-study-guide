# Paul Confronts Peter at Antioch

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Galatians]]
- **Chapters:** Galatians 2:11-21
- **Summary:** Paul's public confrontation of Peter at Antioch over table fellowship with Gentiles, demonstrating that the Galatian crisis is not merely theoretical — even an apostle could compromise the truth of justification by faith under social pressure.

## Details
Galatians 2:11-21 is one of the most dramatic scenes in the Pauline letters and one of the most theologically consequential. Paul had just recounted his visit to Jerusalem and his private conversation with James, Cephas, and John, who extended to him the right hand of fellowship (2:9). The sequence that follows shows how quickly that fellowship could be strained when ecclesial politics pressed against gospel truth. Peter had come to Antioch — the first great Gentile-majority church — and was eating with Gentile believers, which in a Jewish context meant sharing table fellowship without the distinctions of kosher law. But when certain men came from James in Jerusalem, Peter withdrew and separated himself, "fearing them which were of the circumcision" (2:12). Barnabas, Paul's own missionary partner, was also "carried away with their dissimulation" (2:13).

Paul's response was immediate and public: "But when I saw that they walked not uprightly according to the truth of the gospel, I said unto Peter before them all, If thou, being a Jew, livest after the manner of Gentiles, and not as do the Jews, why compellest thou the Gentiles to live as the Jews?" (2:14). The confrontation strikes modern readers as harsh; its logic is exacting. Peter's withdrawal was not merely a matter of cultural preference — it was a lived argument. By separating from Gentile believers at the table, Peter was implicitly communicating that Gentile faith in Christ was insufficient, that something further (table-law compliance, circumcision) was required for full standing before God. His behavior was a sermon, and it was a false one.

The rebuke pivots into what may be the earliest formulation of justification by faith in the Pauline corpus: "Knowing that a man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ, even we have believed in Jesus Christ, that we might be justified by the faith of Christ, and not by the works of the law: for by the works of the law shall no flesh be justified" (2:16). The repetition of the contrast — faith of Christ over against works of the law, stated three times in a single sentence — is emphatic to the point of incantation. Paul is not introducing a novel position; he says "we have believed," placing Peter in the company of those who already know this. The confrontation is thus an accusation of inconsistency: Peter knows the truth and has acted against it.

The passage reaches its summit in 2:20, one of the most compressed statements of union with Christ in Scripture: "I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me: and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God, who loved me, and gave himself for me." The "I" that lives in the flesh is not the same "I" that existed before — that self was co-crucified with Christ. What now lives is Christ's life in Paul, appropriated through ongoing faith in one who loved him personally and gave himself for him specifically. The universal gospel of 2:16 becomes intensely personal: "the Son of God, who loved *me*, and gave himself for *me*." The incident at Antioch thus produces Paul's deepest account of what is at stake whenever the gospel of free justification is compromised — it strikes at the very identity of those in whom Christ now lives.
