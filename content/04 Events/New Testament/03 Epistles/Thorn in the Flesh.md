# Thorn in the Flesh

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[2 Corinthians]]
- **Chapters:** 2 Corinthians 12:1-10
- **Summary:** Paul's account of being caught up to the third heaven and the subsequent thorn in the flesh given to keep him humble, culminating in God's answer that his grace is sufficient and his strength is made perfect in weakness.

## Details
Second Corinthians 12:1-10 is the climax of Paul's "fool's speech" — his ironic boasting in weakness that runs across chapters 11-12. His opponents at Corinth trafficked in visionary experiences and apostolic credentials, so Paul reluctantly enters the same arena, only to invert it. He describes a man (himself, writing in the third person for modesty's sake) "caught up to the third heaven" and "into paradise" fourteen years prior: "whether in the body, I cannot tell; or whether out of the body, I cannot tell: God knoweth" (12:2-3). What he heard there was "unspeakable words, which it is not lawful for a man to utter" (12:4). The vision surpasses anything his rivals could claim — yet Paul refuses to boast of it directly, insisting he will only boast of his infirmities.

The reason for this restraint is the thorn itself: "And lest I should be exalted above measure through the abundance of the revelations, there was given to me a thorn in the flesh, the messenger of Satan to buffet me, lest I should be exalted above measure" (12:7). The repetition of the clause "lest I should be exalted above measure" at both the beginning and end of the verse is deliberate — the thorn's entire purpose is humility. Its source is described with theological precision: given through Satan's agency, yet within God's sovereign purpose. Paul prayed three times for its removal, echoing Christ's three prayers in Gethsemane, and received not removal but a word: "My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness" (12:9).

The nature of the thorn has been debated across church history without resolution. Proposals include a physical ailment (poor eyesight, epilepsy, malaria), a persistent human adversary, or some form of spiritual torment. The grammatical phrase *skolops tē sarki* — a stake or splinter in the flesh — suggests something sharp and unwelcome, but "flesh" in Paul can refer to the physical body or to his human creaturely existence more broadly. The ambiguity appears intentional: the passage functions as Scripture's most universally applicable word on unanswered prayer precisely because readers from every background of suffering have been able to inhabit it. Whatever the thorn was, its effect was that Paul's ministry could not be explained by his own strength.

Paul's response to the divine answer is one of Scripture's great reversals: "Most gladly therefore will I rather glory in my infirmities, that the power of Christ may rest upon me. Therefore I take pleasure in infirmities, in reproaches, in necessities, in persecutions, in distresses for Christ's sake: for when I am weak, then am I strong" (12:9-10). The word translated "rest upon" (*episkēnoō*) means to pitch a tent or take up residence — the power of Christ dwells upon Paul precisely where his own strength gives out. The chiasm at the close — "when I am weak, then am I strong" — is the theological formula that governs the entirety of 2 Corinthians and brings the letter's central argument to its sharpest point.
