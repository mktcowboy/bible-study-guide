# Faith Without Works Is Dead

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[James]]
- **Chapters:** James 2:14–26
- **Summary:** James's sharp practical argument that genuine saving faith necessarily produces visible works, using Abraham and Rahab as proof — not contradicting Paul's doctrine of justification by faith but insisting that the faith that justifies is a living faith, not a mere intellectual assent.

## Details
James 2:14-26 is one of the most discussed and misunderstood passages in the New Testament, having been set against Paul's doctrine of justification by faith since at least Martin Luther, who called James "an epistle of straw." But the apparent tension resolves when the different contexts are recognized: Paul argues against earning righteousness by law-works before an unbelieving God; James argues against claiming righteousness while living without love before a watching church. Paul's opponent is the self-righteous law-keeper; James's opponent is the self-deceived orthodox professor.

James opens with a practical scenario: a brother or sister is naked and starving, and a fellow believer says "Depart in peace, be ye warmed and filled" without supplying bodily needs. "What doth it profit?" (2:16). The rhetorical question drives the point: words without corresponding action are empty. "Even so faith, if it hath not works, is dead, being alone" (2:17). The imagined interlocutor asserts that faith and works can be separated — "thou hast faith, and I have works: shew me thy faith without thy works, and I will shew thee my faith by my works" (2:18). James's counter is devastatingly witty: "Thou believest that there is one God; thou doest well: the devils also believe, and tremble" (2:19). Orthodox creedal assent shared with demons is not saving faith.

The appeal to Abraham is the centerpiece. James cites the same text as Paul — Genesis 15:6, "Abraham believed God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness" — but pairs it with a different event: the offering of Isaac on the altar (Genesis 22). "Seest thou how faith wrought with his works, and by works was faith made perfect?" (2:22). James does not read Genesis 22 as a second justification but as the full expression of the Genesis 15 faith — the moment when that earlier belief was demonstrated to be genuine, living, and active. Abraham's faith and works are not rivals; the works were the organic expression of the faith. "Ye see then how that by works a man is justified, and not by faith only" (2:24). The word "justified" here carries the sense of publicly vindicated or demonstrated to be righteous, as in Matthew 11:19 ("wisdom is justified of her children").

Rahab completes the illustration: the prostitute of Jericho who hid the spies "was she not justified by works, when she had received the messengers, and had sent them out another way?" (2:25). The action vindicated the faith. The closing verdict uses the body-spirit analogy: as a body without spirit is dead, "so faith without works is dead also" (2:26). A living faith is one that breathes — that moves, acts, loves, and helps. The passage is ultimately a call not to earn salvation but to take the self-examination of one's faith seriously.
