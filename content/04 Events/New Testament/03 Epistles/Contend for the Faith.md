# Contend for the Faith

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Jude]]
- **Chapters:** Jude 1:3-25
- **Summary:** Jude's urgent summons to contend for the apostolic faith against men who have secretly infiltrated the community marshals six OT examples of judgment, three types of apostasy, and closes with one of the most majestic doxologies in the NT.

## Details

Jude's letter is singular in the NT for its stated change of purpose. Jude tells us directly that he had intended to write about "the common salvation" — the shared inheritance of redemption — but was compelled by urgent circumstances to write instead a letter of warning and defense: "it was needful for me to write unto you, and exhort you that ye should earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints" (v.3). The phrase "once delivered" (*hapax paradotheise*) is theologically loaded: the faith has been delivered once, in a definitive act, and the apostolic deposit is not subject to ongoing revision by those who claim new revelation or spiritual authority. Contending for this faith is therefore not optional belligerence but faithful stewardship of what has been entrusted.

The threat is immediate and internal: "certain men crept in unawares" (v.4) — a deliberately covert language of infiltration. These are not outside attackers but insiders who have come in through the ordinary channels of community life, and who are now "turning the grace of our God into lasciviousness, and denying the only Lord God, and our Lord Jesus Christ" (v.4). The antinomian error — using the doctrine of grace as a license for moral abandon — is the specific form of their corruption. Jude then deploys three OT examples in rapid sequence: the generation of Israel that was saved from Egypt but destroyed in the wilderness for unbelief (v.5), the angels who "kept not their first estate, but left their own habitation" and are now held in everlasting chains under darkness awaiting judgment (v.6), and the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah who gave themselves over to sexual immorality and "are set forth for an example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire" (v.7). The escalating severity of these examples — a nation, supernatural beings, whole cities — demonstrates that no status, no past privilege, and no apparent power protects against divine judgment for willful apostasy.

Three OT individuals then appear as personal types of the false teachers' sins: "Woe unto them! for they have gone in the way of Cain, and ran greedily after the error of Balaam for reward, and perished in the gainsaying of Core" (v.11). Cain represents murderous contempt for the brother; Balaam represents the prostitution of prophetic ministry for financial gain; Korah represents organized rebellion against divinely appointed leadership. These three together constitute a comprehensive anatomy of the spirit animating the false teachers — envy, greed, and power-hunger expressed through the specific forms they take in a religious community. Jude's imagery for the teachers themselves reaches its most vivid and damning: "These are spots in your feasts of charity... clouds they are without water, carried about of winds; trees whose fruit withereth, without fruit, twice dead, plucked up by the roots; Raging waves of the sea, foaming out their own shame; wandering stars, to whom is reserved the blackness of darkness for ever" (v.12-13). Each image is of something that appears to offer what it cannot deliver: clouds with no rain, trees with no fruit, waves producing nothing but foam.

The letter's closing doxology is among the most celebrated in the NT and provides a profound tonal counterweight to all that has preceded it. Having described with unflinching precision the character and fate of those who corrupt the faith, Jude ends not in fear or despair but in soaring confidence in the God who holds his people: "Now unto him that is able to keep you from falling, and to present you faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy, To the only wise God our Saviour, be glory and majesty, dominion and power, both now and ever. Amen" (v.24-25). The word "faultless" (*amomous*) is the same word used in Ephesians 1:4 and elsewhere for the state of those presented before God — without blemish, without accusation. The preservation of the saints is not achieved by their own vigilance alone but by God's active keeping. The doxology does not replace the call to contend; it grounds it. We contend for the faith because the faith is worth keeping, and we keep it because God keeps us.
