# The Man of Sin

> One of Satan's chief agents — Paul's name for the final lawless one, whose "coming is after the working of Satan" (2 Thessalonians 2:9). See [[Satan (the Accuser)]], and the parallel figures [[The Beast]] and [[The Antichrists]].

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[2 Thessalonians]]
- **Chapters:** 2 Thessalonians 2:1–12
- **Summary:** Paul's portrait of the "man of sin" or "son of perdition" — a figure of supreme lawlessness revealed before the [[The Day of the LORD|Day of the Lord]], who exalts himself above every god, sits in the temple of God claiming to be God, and is destroyed by the brightness of Christ's coming.

## Background: Why Paul Wrote
The Thessalonians were shaken by a report that the Day of the Lord had already come (2 Thessalonians 2:2). Paul corrects the panic: that day cannot come until two things happen first — "a falling away" (the apostasy) and "that man of sin be revealed, the son of perdition" (2:3).

## The Portrait of the Lawless One
He "opposeth and exalteth himself above all that is called God, or that is worshipped; so that he as God sitteth in the temple of God, shewing himself that he is God" (2 Thessalonians 2:4; see [[The Temple]]). His self-deification is the climax of all human pride. His "coming is after the working of Satan with all power and signs and lying wonders" (2:9) — a satanic counterfeit of Christ's own mighty works — "and with all deceivableness of unrighteousness in them that perish; because they received not the love of the truth" (2:10).

## The Restrainer
Paul says something now holds the lawless one back: "only he who now letteth will let, until he be taken out of the way" (2 Thessalonians 2:7). "The mystery of iniquity doth already work" — lawlessness is present, but its full embodiment is delayed until the restraint is lifted. See [[The Restrainer]].

## His Destruction
The lawless one is no match for Christ: "then shall that Wicked be revealed, whom the Lord shall consume with the spirit of his mouth, and shall destroy with the brightness of his coming" (2 Thessalonians 2:8). His end is certain and effortless — undone by a word from the returning Lord. See [[The Second Coming]], [[Judgment]].

## Old Testament and Gospel Connections
Paul's "man of sin" draws on [[Daniel]]: the "little horn" who "shall... magnify himself above every god" (Daniel 7:8; 11:36) and the "abomination of desolation" set up in the sanctuary (Daniel 9:27; 11:31). Jesus cited that abomination directly in the [[Olivet Discourse]] (Matthew 24:15). The pattern of a ruler enthroning himself in God's place recurs in [[Nebuchadnezzar]], in Antiochus Epiphanes (see the [[Intertestamental Period]]), and in the Roman emperors who claimed divine honors.

## Key People Involved
- [[Paul (Apostle)]] — Who described this figure
- [[Satan (the Accuser)]] — The power behind his coming
- [[Jesus Christ]] — Who destroys him at his appearing

## Interpretive History
The identity of the man of sin has long been debated: some early interpreters saw Caligula or a future emperor; the Protestant Reformers identified the office of the papacy; premillennial interpreters expect a future personal Antichrist; others read a symbol of the spirit of lawlessness active in every age. The self-deifying pattern suggests both a recurring type and a final intensification.

## Notes
The "abomination of desolation" of Daniel 9:27 and 11:31, the "little horn" of Daniel 7–8, Paul's "man of sin," and Revelation's beast are widely read as related or identical figures in eschatology. Jesus cited Daniel's abomination in his Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24:15). The self-deification ("shewing himself that he is God") echoes the hubris of Nebuchadnezzar, Antiochus Epiphanes, and the Roman emperors — suggesting "the man of sin" describes both a recurring pattern and a final climax. Paul's pastoral point is comfort: however terrible the lawless one, the Lord destroys him with a breath.
