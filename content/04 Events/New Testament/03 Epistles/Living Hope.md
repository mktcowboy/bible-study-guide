# Living Hope

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[1 Peter]]
- **Chapters:** 1 Peter 1:3–9
- **Summary:** Peter's opening doxology, blessing God for the living hope that flows from Christ's resurrection — an inheritance incorruptible and undefiled reserved in heaven — and celebrating the joy of faith that holds fast through trials as gold is refined through fire.

## Details
First Peter is addressed to "strangers scattered throughout" the provinces of Asia Minor — communities of Christian believers experiencing social displacement and likely imperial hostility. Peter's response is not a political program but a theological reorientation: the readers are to understand their present suffering through the lens of a secure future and an already-accomplished redemption. The letter opens with a hymnic blessing (1:3-12) that stands as one of the most concentrated statements of Christian hope in the New Testament.

"Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, which according to his abundant mercy hath begotten us again unto a lively hope by the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead" (1:3). The phrase "begotten us again" (new birth) grounds hope not in human determination or accumulated virtue but in divine mercy and the objective event of resurrection. The hope is "lively" — living, not static — precisely because it rests on the living Christ. This distinguishes Christian hope from mere optimism or stoic resignation; it is anchored in history.

The inheritance toward which this hope reaches is described in a triple negative: "incorruptible, and undefiled, and that fadeth not away" (1:4) — three compound words in the Greek, each denying one form of earthly decay. What earthly inheritance is not eventually corrupted, defiled, or faded? This one is "reserved in heaven for you, who are kept by the power of God through faith unto salvation ready to be revealed in the last time" (1:4-5). Two layers of keeping: the inheritance kept in heaven, the believer kept on earth. The divine custody moves in both directions.

"Wherein ye greatly rejoice, though now for a season, if need be, ye are in heaviness through manifold temptations" (1:6). The word translated "manifold" (poikilos) means multi-colored or variegated — the same word used to describe the devil's schemes in Ephesians 6:11 and the many-colored wisdom of God in Ephesians 3:10. Trials come in as many varieties as life can produce. But they serve a purpose: the trial of faith, "being much more precious than of gold that perisheth, though it be tried with fire, might be found unto praise and honour and glory at the appearing of Jesus Christ" (1:7). The fire that refines gold is the image: the trials do not destroy faith but purify and demonstrate it. The passage closes with the remarkable description of a faith that loves and rejoices without yet seeing: "Whom having not seen, ye love; in whom, though now ye see him not, yet believing, ye rejoice with joy unspeakable and full of glory" (1:8).
