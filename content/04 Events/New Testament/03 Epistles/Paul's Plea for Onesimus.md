# Paul's Plea for Onesimus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Philemon]]
- **Chapters:** Philemon 1:8-21
- **Summary:** Paul's masterpiece of persuasion — a personal appeal for the runaway slave Onesimus, converted in prison, to be received back by his master Philemon not as a slave but as a brother beloved, with Paul absorbing any debt.

## Details
The letter to Philemon is the shortest of Paul's letters and the most personal. It is addressed to a wealthy believer in Colossae whose slave Onesimus had run away — and possibly stolen money in the process (v.18). Onesimus had somehow come into contact with the imprisoned Paul and had been led to faith in Christ ("my son, whom I have begotten in my bonds," v.10). Paul now sends him back to his master with this letter, which is a carefully constructed appeal that never commands but makes refusing nearly impossible. It is simultaneously one of the most rhetorically sophisticated and theologically rich documents in the New Testament.

The opening strategy is deliberate. Paul could, he says, "be much bold in Christ to enjoin thee that which is convenient" (v.8) — he has apostolic authority to command Philemon's obedience. But instead he appeals on the basis of love and his own condition: "yet for love's sake I rather beseech thee, being such an one as Paul the aged, and now also a prisoner of Jesus Christ" (v.9). He explicitly sets aside the power he could exercise, preferring to appeal rather than command. This rhetorical move mirrors precisely what he will ask Philemon to do with Onesimus — set aside the legal power of a master and receive him as a brother.

The wordplay in verse 11 is central to the letter's appeal. Onesimus means "useful" or "profitable" in Greek — a common name for slaves that signaled their utility. Paul writes: "Which in time past was to thee unprofitable, but now profitable to thee and to me." The slave who bore the name "Useful" had been useless; now, in Christ, he has become what his name always promised. The appeal reaches its peak in verse 16: "Not now as a servant, but above a servant, a brother beloved, specially to me, but how much more unto thee, both in the flesh, and in the Lord." Paul asks Philemon to re-categorize Onesimus entirely — from property to family member. The phrase "both in the flesh, and in the Lord" acknowledges both the earthly and spiritual dimensions of their new relationship.

The letter functions as a living parable of the gospel. Paul stands in as mediator between an offended party (Philemon/God the Father) and an offender who has fled and cannot make restitution (Onesimus/the sinner). Paul absorbs the debt personally: "If he hath wronged thee, or oweth thee ought, put that on mine account; I Paul have written it with mine own hand, I will repay it" (vv.18-19). This is the language of substitution — Paul assumes the liability of Onesimus so that Onesimus can be received freely. He even adds a gentle pressure: "albeit I do not say to thee how thou owest unto me even thine own self besides" (v.19) — Philemon himself had come to faith through Paul's ministry, making Paul the creditor in a deeper sense. The letter ends with Paul's confidence that Philemon will do "even more" than he asks (v.21), and a request to prepare a guest room — Paul expects release and a visit.
