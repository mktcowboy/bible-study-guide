# Trial Before Gallio

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 18:12-17
- **Summary:** While Paul was ministering in [[Corinth]] during his eighteen-month stay, the Jewish community brought him before Gallio, the proconsul of [[Achaia]], charging that he persuaded men to worship God contrary to the law. Before Paul could speak, Gallio dismissed the case outright, ruling that the dispute was a matter of Jewish religious law rather than Roman civil wrong. He drove them from the judgment seat, and the Greeks then beat Sosthenes the chief ruler of the synagogue without any intervention from Gallio.

## Significance
Gallio's dismissal established an important legal precedent for the early church's mission in the Roman world. By ruling that the dispute between Jews and Christians over law, names, and interpretation was outside Roman jurisdiction, Gallio effectively granted the gospel a measure of legal shelter under the tolerated status Judaism already enjoyed in the empire. The episode also anchors one of the firmest chronological fixed points in Pauline studies: an inscription at Delphi dates Gallio's proconsulship to approximately AD 51-52, making this trial among the most precisely dateable events in the entire New Testament.

## Key Verses
> "And Gallio said unto the Jews, If it were a matter of wrong or wicked lewdness, O ye Jews, reason would that I should bear with you: But if it be a question of words and names, and of your law, look ye to it; for I will be no judge of such matters. And he drave them from the judgment seat." — Acts 18:14-16 (KJV)
