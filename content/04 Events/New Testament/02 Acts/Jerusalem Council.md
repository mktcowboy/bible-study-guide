# Jerusalem Council

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 15:1-35
- **Summary:** A controversy over whether Gentile converts must be circumcised and keep the Law of Moses is brought before the apostles and elders at Jerusalem; after testimonies from Peter, Paul, and Barnabas, James presides over a decision that Gentiles need not be burdened with the whole Mosaic law but should observe four essential abstentions; a letter is sent to the Gentile churches, and the Gentile mission is formally validated.

## Details
The Jerusalem Council of approximately AD 49 is the most consequential ecclesiastical assembly in the New Testament, and its resolution shaped the entire subsequent trajectory of Christianity. The crisis arose from a specific doctrinal challenge: certain men had come down from Judea to Antioch and were teaching the Gentile believers that "except ye be circumcised after the manner of Moses, ye cannot be saved" (Acts 15:1). This was not a peripheral question — it struck at the heart of what the gospel required of Gentile converts. Paul and Barnabas had "no small dissension and disputation with them" before the church at [[Antioch (Syria)]] sent them to Jerusalem to settle the matter with the apostles and elders.

The Pharisaic party within the Jerusalem church made their position explicit: "It was needful to circumcise them, and to command them to keep the law of Moses" (Acts 15:5). The council that followed was genuine deliberation — "much disputing" (Acts 15:7) — before Peter rose to speak. His testimony was grounded entirely in the precedent of [[Cornelius's Vision]]: God had chosen that the Gentiles should hear the gospel through his mouth and believe, and God — who knows the heart — had "put no difference between us and them, purifying their hearts by faith" (Acts 15:9). To require circumcision was, Peter argued, to "put a yoke upon the neck of the disciples, which neither our fathers nor we were able to bear" (Acts 15:10). His conclusion was a precise statement of grace: "But we believe that through the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ we shall be saved, even as they" (Acts 15:11).

Paul and Barnabas then testified to what God had done through them among the Gentiles — "declaring what miracles and wonders God had wrought among the Gentiles by them" (Acts 15:12) — and the assembly fell silent. James, who presides as the leader of the Jerusalem church, then spoke. He cited Amos 9:11-12 — the prophecy of David's rebuilt tabernacle through which "the residue of men might seek after the Lord, and all the Gentiles, upon whom my name is called" (Acts 15:17) — as prophetic warrant for the Gentile inclusion the council had just witnessed. His judgment was authoritative and balanced: "Wherefore my sentence is, that we trouble not them, which from among the Gentiles are turned to God" (Acts 15:19). But he proposed four abstentions that Gentile believers should observe, likely chosen to enable table fellowship between Jewish and Gentile Christians and to avoid the most egregious paganism: abstain from things polluted by idols, from sexual immorality, from things strangled, and from blood (Acts 15:20).

The council's letter, carried by Judas Barsabbas and Silas along with Paul and Barnabas, addressed the Gentile churches with the famous phrase: "For it seemed good to the Holy Ghost, and to us, to lay upon you no greater burden than these necessary things" (Acts 15:28). The Gentile churches at Antioch, Syria, and Cilicia "rejoiced for the consolation" (Acts 15:31). The Jerusalem Council achieved several things simultaneously: it preserved the unity of a church that might otherwise have fractured over the circumcision question; it formally recognized the Gentile mission as God's own work; it established that salvation is by grace alone, not by works of the law; and it provided a practical framework for Jewish-Gentile coexistence in the early church.

Paul's account in Galatians 2:1-10 — which most scholars take as a parallel report of the same meeting — adds that the Jerusalem pillars (James, Cephas, and John) "gave to me and Barnabas the right hands of fellowship; that we should go unto the heathen, and they unto the circumcision" (Galatians 2:9). This formal division of apostolic labor — Gentile mission to Paul, Jewish mission to the Jerusalem apostles — gives the council its lasting structural significance for the history of Christian mission.

## Key People Involved
- [[Peter (Cephas)]] — delivers the decisive testimony from the Cornelius precedent; articulates salvation by grace alone
- [[Paul (Apostle)]] — reports miracles among Gentiles; defender of Gentile freedom from the law's burden
- [[Barnabas]] — partners with Paul in the testimony before the council
- James (the Lord's brother) — presides over the Jerusalem church; delivers the final verdict; cites Amos as prophetic warrant
- [[Silas]] — chosen with Judas Barsabbas to carry the council letter to Antioch; enters Paul's company here

## Key Places
- [[Jerusalem]] — the council's location; the mother church of the Christian movement
- [[Antioch (Syria)]] — where the crisis originated; where the letter was delivered and received with joy
