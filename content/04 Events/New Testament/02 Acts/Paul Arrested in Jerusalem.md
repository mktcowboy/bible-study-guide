# Paul Arrested in Jerusalem

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 21:17-23:35
- **Summary:** Paul arrives in Jerusalem, is urged to undergo a purification rite to quell rumors; Asian Jews recognize him in the Temple, spark a riot, and the Roman tribune rescues him from the mob; Paul addresses the crowd in Hebrew, then reveals his Roman citizenship to avoid flogging; a conspiracy of forty men vows to kill him, and he is transferred under heavy escort to Caesarea.

## Details
Paul's arrival in Jerusalem fulfills the trajectory he had set in Acts 19:21: "After I have been there, I must also see Rome." He arrived with Gentile delegates carrying the collection from the Gentile churches — a tangible symbol of Gentile-Jewish unity in the body of Christ. James and the Jerusalem elders received him warmly and glorified God for the report of Paul's ministry, but they also brought a concern to his attention: tens of thousands of Jewish believers in Jerusalem had heard a rumor that Paul "teachest all the Jews which are among the Gentiles to forsake Moses, saying that they ought not to circumcise their children, neither to walk after the customs" (Acts 21:21). To dispel this, they urged Paul to sponsor four men under a Nazirite vow and pay for their purification — a gesture that would demonstrate his personal observance of the law.

Paul agreed and entered the Temple. But seven days into the purification, "the Jews which were of Asia" — men who had opposed Paul in Ephesus and knew him — saw him in the Temple courts. They identified a Gentile (Trophimus the Ephesian) whom they had seen with Paul in the city and falsely concluded he had brought him into the inner courts of the Temple where Gentiles were forbidden. "This is the man, that teacheth all men every where against the people, and the law, and this place: and further brought Greeks also into the temple, and hath polluted this holy place" (Acts 21:28). The entire city was "moved" — Jerusalem was crowded for Pentecost — and a mob seized Paul, dragged him from the Temple, and was beating him to death when the Roman tribune (Claudius Lysias) arrived with soldiers. The mob stopped beating Paul at the sight of the soldiers; Paul was arrested and bound.

Paul's address to the mob from the steps of the Antonia Fortress — in Hebrew or Aramaic, which commanded astonished silence — is one of the most rhetorically dramatic moments in Acts. He narrated his Jewish credentials (born in Tarsus, educated under Gamaliel, a zealot for the law), his persecution of the church, his Damascus road encounter, and his subsequent vision in the Temple itself where the Lord commanded him: "Depart: for I will send thee far hence unto the Gentiles" (Acts 22:21). The word "Gentiles" broke the silence into riot again — the very subject that was the root of all opposition.

The tribune ordered Paul taken inside for examination by flogging to determine what he had done. Paul's declaration of Roman citizenship — "Is it lawful for you to scourge a man that is a Roman, and uncondemned?" (Acts 22:25) — stopped the proceeding immediately. Roman citizenship was a serious legal protection; the tribune himself was alarmed that he had bound a citizen without trial. The next day, Paul was brought before the Sanhedrin. His declaration that the dispute was over the resurrection of the dead — "And when he had so said, there arose a dissension between the Pharisees and the Sadducees: and the multitude was divided" (Acts 23:7) — fractured the council into such disorder that the tribune removed Paul again.

That night the Lord appeared to Paul: "Be of good cheer, Paul: for as thou hast testified of me in Jerusalem, so must thou bear witness also at Rome" (Acts 23:11). The divine assurance that Rome lay ahead reframes the entire arrest narrative: what appeared to be a catastrophic end to Paul's freedom was in fact the providential mechanism by which he would reach the imperial capital. The conspiracy that followed — forty men vowing neither to eat nor drink until they had killed Paul — was thwarted when Paul's nephew learned of it and reported it to the tribune. Claudius Lysias responded with a remarkable display of Roman efficiency: under cover of darkness, an escort of two hundred soldiers, seventy horsemen, and two hundred spearmen accompanied Paul to [[Caesarea Maritima]] and the governor Felix — a transfer that removed Paul from Jewish reach and placed him under the protection of Roman law.

## Key People Involved
- [[Paul (Apostle)]] — the arrested apostle; Roman citizen; preacher even under arrest; the protagonist of Acts 21-28
- James (the Lord's brother) — receives Paul and proposes the purification strategy; the Jerusalem church's presiding elder
- Claudius Lysias — the Roman tribune; arrests Paul to save him from the mob; later transfers him to Felix
- [[Felix]] — governor of Judea; receives Paul at Caesarea; the first of three Roman officials before whom Paul will appear

## Key Places
- [[Jerusalem]] — the Temple courts; the Antonia Fortress; the Sanhedrin chamber
- [[Caesarea Maritima]] — the destination of Paul's nighttime transfer; where he will spend two years imprisoned
