# Paul in Rome

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 28:1-31
- **Summary:** Paul winters on [[Malta]] where a viper bite leaves him unharmed and he heals Publius's father and many others; arriving in Rome, he is placed under house arrest; he immediately convenes the Jewish leaders, preaches Christ from the Scriptures, and ends with a final declaration that the salvation of God is sent to the Gentiles; Acts closes with Paul preaching "none forbidding him."

## Details
Acts 28 is both an ending and, in a deeper sense, a launching point. The book's final word in Greek is "akolutos" — "unhindered" — a single word that summarizes both Paul's immediate situation under house arrest and the larger theological claim of the entire narrative: the word of God cannot be chained (2 Timothy 2:9). That the gospel reaches Rome — the capital of the known world — is the fulfillment of the geographical promise of Acts 1:8 ("unto the uttermost part of the earth"), and it arrives not by political triumph but through a prisoner's testimony.

The winter at [[Malta]] extended the narrative with a final set of apostolic signs. When Paul gathered sticks for a fire and a viper fastened onto his hand, the locals immediately concluded he was a murderer whom justice would not let escape: "And he shook off the beast into the fire, and felt no harm" (Acts 28:5). Their conclusion shifted: "they said that he was a god" (Acts 28:6) — a pendulum that recalls the Lystra episode (Acts 14:11-18). Publius, the island's chief official, received the shipwreck survivors hospitably. His father lay sick with fever and dysentery; Paul "prayed, and laid his hands on him, and healed him" (Acts 28:8). Others on the island who were sick came and were healed as well. The three months on Malta were not idle waiting but active ministry — and the island's people honored Paul's party with gifts and provisions when they departed in the spring.

The approach to Rome was marked by an emotional reception. Roman Christians, hearing of Paul's arrival, came as far as the Forum of Appius and the Three Taverns to meet the apostle — approximately forty and thirty miles from the city respectively. "Whom when Paul saw, he thanked God, and took courage" (Acts 28:15). Even Paul, the bold apostle who had stood before governors and kings, was moved to gratitude by the sight of brothers coming to meet him. He was placed under house arrest — "in his own hired house" — with a soldier guarding him, but free to receive visitors and to teach.

Three days after arriving in Rome, Paul convened the leaders of the Jewish community — he always went to the Jew first — and gave them his account: "Men and brethren, though I have committed nothing against the people, or customs of our fathers, yet was I delivered prisoner from Jerusalem into the hands of the Romans" (Acts 28:17). He was careful to note that the Romans had found no cause of death and had been willing to release him, but Jewish opposition had forced his appeal to Caesar. He stressed that he was wearing his chain "for the hope of Israel" — a phrase that links his imprisonment to the central Jewish hope of resurrection and the Messianic age. The Roman Jewish leaders were cautious; they had received no letters about Paul from Judea, and they wanted to hear what he taught directly, knowing only that "this sect…is every where spoken against" (Acts 28:22).

The great dialogue that followed — a full day of Paul "expounding and testifying the kingdom of God, persuading them concerning Jesus, both out of the law of Moses, and out of the prophets" (Acts 28:23) — produced the familiar divided response: some believed, some did not. Paul's final word to the unbelieving was a citation from Isaiah 6:9-10 — the same passage Jesus had quoted about Israel's spiritual dullness (Matthew 13:14-15) — followed by the programmatic declaration that closes Acts: "Be it known therefore unto you, that the salvation of God is sent unto the Gentiles, and that they will hear it" (Acts 28:28). It is the third time in Acts this declaration has been made (Acts 13:46; 18:6 are the first two), and here it is made at Rome, the center of the Gentile world, giving it maximum rhetorical force.

Acts ends not with Paul's death — which is not recorded — but with his ongoing ministry: "And Paul dwelt two whole years in his own hired house, and received all that came in unto him, Preaching the kingdom of God, and teaching those things which concern the Lord Jesus Christ, with all confidence, no man forbidding him" (Acts 28:30-31). The Prison Epistles (Philippians, Colossians, Ephesians, Philemon) were written during this Roman house arrest, as was the letter to the Philippians which contains Paul's account of the progress of the gospel in Rome itself: "all the palace, and in all other places" have heard it (Philippians 1:13). Luke's decision to end here — open-ended, forward-looking, with Paul preaching freely — is not a literary deficiency but a theological statement: the story is not over. The word of God is unhindered.

## Key People Involved
- [[Paul (Apostle)]] — the prisoner who preaches; arrives at the end of the world as Acts defines it; the unhindered apostle
- [[Luke (Evangelist)|Luke]] — present throughout; the "we" narrative continues to Rome; Acts is his own eyewitness account
- [[Aristarchus]] — companion from [[Macedonia]], mentioned in Acts 27:2; present through the voyage and presumably Rome
- Publius — chief official of [[Malta]]; received and honored the shipwreck survivors; his father healed

## Key Places
- [[Malta]] — three months; viper; Publius's father healed; generous farewell by the island's people
- [[Rome]] — the destination of Acts 1:8 and 19:21 and 23:11; house arrest; two years of unhindered preaching
