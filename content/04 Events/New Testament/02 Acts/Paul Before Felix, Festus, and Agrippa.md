# Paul Before Felix, Festus, and Agrippa

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 24:1-26:32
- **Summary:** Paul makes his defense before three successive Roman rulers at Caesarea over approximately two years; Felix delays judgment while privately hoping for a bribe; Festus, inheriting the case, proposes a Jerusalem trial which prompts Paul's appeal to Caesar; Agrippa II and Bernice hear Paul's full autobiography and gospel proclamation, with Agrippa's famous reply — "Almost thou persuadest me to be a Christian" — before all agree Paul has done nothing worthy of death or imprisonment.

## Details
The judicial sequence of Acts 24-26 serves multiple purposes in Luke's narrative: it provides the most extensive first-person account of Paul's conversion and call that appears in Acts; it demonstrates the consistent Roman finding that Paul had committed no civil offense; and it showcases Paul preaching the gospel at the highest levels of Roman and Jewish political power, fulfilling the Lord's charge that he would bear witness before kings (Acts 9:15). Three separate hearings across approximately two and a half years chart both the paralysis of Roman justice and the sovereign advance of the gospel.

Before [[Felix]], the governor of Judea, the high priest Ananias brought the formal Jewish charges: Paul was "a pestilent fellow, and a mover of sedition among all the Jews throughout the world, and a ringleader of the sect of the Nazarenes: Who also hath gone about to profane the temple" (Acts 24:5). Paul's defense denied every charge and reframed the dispute as purely theological — a question of resurrection and the way he worshiped "the God of my fathers" (Acts 24:14). Felix, who "had a more perfect knowledge of that way" (Acts 24:22), adjourned the case while waiting for Claudius Lysias to appear. What followed was a series of private interviews: "he sent for Paul, and heard him concerning the faith in Christ" (Acts 24:24). When Paul "reasoned of righteousness, temperance, and judgment to come, Felix trembled, and answered, Go thy way for this time; when I have a convenient season, I will call for thee" (Acts 24:25). His trembling and his evasion are among the most poignant moments in Acts — a man confronted with the truth who repeatedly deferred. The convenient season never came. Felix also hoped Paul would offer a bribe for his release; when he did not, and when Felix's two-year term ended, he left Paul in prison as a political favor to the Jews.

[[Festus]], Felix's successor, inherited the case and immediately came under pressure from the chief priests and elders of Jerusalem to send Paul there for trial. His counter-proposal — that they come to Caesarea and present their charges — brought the Sanhedrin accusers to Caesarea for a hearing. When Festus, inclined toward a Jewish favor, asked Paul whether he was willing to be tried in Jerusalem, Paul made the decisive move: "I appeal unto Caesar" (Acts 25:11). The appeal to Caesar — available to all Roman citizens — was a legal right that Festus was bound to honor. Paul was going to Rome.

Before the departure, [[Agrippa]] II (the last of the Herodian dynasty, son of Agrippa I who had killed James) arrived at Caesarea with his sister Bernice to pay respects to the new governor. Festus briefed him on Paul's case, and Agrippa expressed interest in hearing Paul personally. The audience in Acts 26 — Paul before Agrippa, Bernice, the military tribunes, and the principal men of the city — is the most formally royal setting in the entire book. Paul's speech is his fullest autobiographical statement: his Pharisaic life, his persecution of the church, his Damascus road encounter (including the Lord's commission in Acts 26:16-18: "to open their eyes, and to turn them from darkness to light, and from the power of Satan unto God, that they may receive forgiveness of sins"), and his mission to Jews and Gentiles alike.

Festus interrupted him: "Paul, thou art beside thyself; much learning doth make thee mad" (Acts 26:24). Paul replied with complete composure that he spoke "the words of truth and soberness" (Acts 26:25) and turned directly to Agrippa, who as a Jew knew the prophets: "King Agrippa, believest thou the prophets? I know that thou believest" (Acts 26:27). Agrippa's famous reply — "Almost thou persuadest me to be a Christian" (Acts 26:28) — is one of the most discussed lines in Acts. Whether it is irony, deflection, sincere near-response, or something else, Paul's answer was a model of apostolic graciousness: "I would to God, that not only thou, but also all that hear me this day, were both almost, and altogether such as I am, except these bonds" (Acts 26:29). When Agrippa, Festus, and Bernice conferred afterward, their verdict was unambiguous: "This man doeth nothing worthy of death or of bonds" (Acts 26:31). And Agrippa said to Festus, "This man might have been set at liberty, if he had not appealed unto Caesar" (Acts 26:32). The appeal had made Paul's Roman future inevitable.

## Key People Involved
- [[Paul (Apostle)]] — the defendant who becomes the preacher in every judicial setting; his conversion and call narrated in fullest detail
- [[Felix]] — governor of Judea; hears Paul privately; trembles; delays and hopes for a bribe; leaves Paul imprisoned
- [[Festus]] — Felix's successor; legally correct but politically pressured; grants Paul's Caesar appeal
- [[Agrippa]] — Herod Agrippa II; last Herodian ruler; hears Paul's full testimony; "Almost thou persuadest me to be a Christian"
- Bernice — Agrippa's sister; present at the hearing; no record of her response

## Key Places
- [[Caesarea Maritima]] — the location of all three hearings; Paul's place of imprisonment for approximately two years
