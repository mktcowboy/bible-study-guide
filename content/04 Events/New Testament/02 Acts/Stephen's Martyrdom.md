# Stephen's Martyrdom

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 6:1-8:3
- **Summary:** Stephen, one of the seven deacons and a man full of faith and the Holy Spirit, is arrested by the Sanhedrin, delivers a sweeping defense recounting Israel's history of rejecting God's messengers, sees a vision of Christ standing at God's right hand, and is stoned to death — becoming the church's first martyr.

## Details
The account of Stephen's martyrdom begins in Acts 6 with an administrative dispute in the Jerusalem church. As the church grew, Greek-speaking Jewish believers (Hellenists) complained that their widows were being neglected in the daily distribution of food. The apostles, unwilling to leave the ministry of the word, called the congregation to appoint seven men "of honest report, full of the Holy Ghost and wisdom" to oversee the practical care of the community (Acts 6:3). Stephen is listed first among these seven, described as "a man full of faith and of the Holy Ghost" (Acts 6:5). He was evidently a Hellenistic Jewish believer — comfortable in Greek-speaking synagogue settings — and quickly moved well beyond table service: "Stephen, full of faith and power, did great wonders and miracles among the people" (Acts 6:8).

Opposition arose from members of various Hellenistic synagogues in Jerusalem — men from Cyrene, Alexandria, Cilicia, and Asia — who "disputed with Stephen" but "were not able to resist the wisdom and the spirit by which he spake" (Acts 6:10). Unable to defeat him in open argument, they suborned false witnesses who accused him of speaking blasphemously against Moses and the Temple. Before the Sanhedrin, all who looked at Stephen saw that "his face was as it had been the face of an angel" (Acts 6:15). The high priest asked whether the charges were true, and Stephen's answer — the longest speech recorded in Acts — is the most comprehensive biblical survey of Israel's history ever put in a single speech.

Stephen's defense (Acts 7:2-53) moves through the entire sweep of salvation history: Abraham's call and the patriarchal narratives; Joseph sold into Egypt and God's providence working through that rejection; Moses's early life, rejection by his own people, call at the burning bush, the Exodus, and Israel's persistent idolatry in the wilderness. The pattern Stephen drives home is relentless: again and again, Israel rejected the very deliverers and prophets God sent — "Which of the prophets have not your fathers persecuted?" (Acts 7:52). The Tabernacle and Temple were never intended to confine God — "Howbeit the most High dwelleth not in temples made with hands" (Acts 7:48) — a citation from Isaiah 66. Stephen brought the history to its climax with the accusation that the Sanhedrin had now murdered the "Just One" foretold by those prophets.

The council reacted with fury. "When they heard these things, they were cut to the heart, and they gnashed on him with their teeth" (Acts 7:54). In this moment Stephen looked up and saw a vision: "I see the heavens opened, and the Son of Man standing on the right hand of God" (Acts 7:56). This vision is theologically charged: Christ, who is normally described as seated (Psalm 110:1), is here standing — a posture many interpreters read as rising to receive his first martyr. The mob could endure no more. They rushed upon him, drove him outside the city, and stoned him. Stephen prayed two prayers in his dying moments that deliberately echo Christ's own words from the cross: "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit" (Acts 7:59) and "Lord, lay not this sin to their charge" (Acts 7:60).

A young man named Saul was present, guarding the cloaks of those who stoned Stephen and "consenting unto his death" (Acts 8:1). The persecution that erupted immediately afterward scattered the Jerusalem church throughout Judea and Samaria — which paradoxically fulfilled Christ's mandate in Acts 1:8 to be witnesses in those very regions. The blood of Stephen, the first martyr, watered the seeds of a mission that would carry the gospel to the ends of the earth. Tertullian's famous phrase — "the blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church" — finds its first biblical warrant here.

## Key People Involved
- [[Stephen]] — first Christian martyr; one of the seven deacons; Hellenistic Jewish believer
- [[Paul (Apostle)]] — present as the young Saul, consenting to the stoning; guarded the witnesses' cloaks
- The Sanhedrin — the Jerusalem council that condemned Stephen
- False witnesses — suborned to accuse Stephen of blasphemy against Moses and the Temple

## Key Places
- [[Jerusalem]] — the city where the Sanhedrin met and where the stoning took place outside the city walls
