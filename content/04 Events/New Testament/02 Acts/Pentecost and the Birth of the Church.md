# Pentecost and the Birth of the Church

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Primary Text:** Acts 2:1-47
- **Summary:** At Pentecost, the Holy Spirit descends on the gathered disciples, Peter preaches Christ crucified and risen, about three thousand are baptized, and the Jerusalem church begins its shared life.

## Details

Pentecost occurs after the resurrection and ascension of Jesus, as the disciples wait in Jerusalem for the promised power from on high. The feast of Pentecost, or Weeks, already belonged to Israel's calendar as a harvest festival. In Acts 2, it becomes the moment when the first great harvest of gospel witness begins.

The disciples are together when a sound like a rushing mighty wind fills the house, divided tongues like fire rest on them, and they are filled with the Holy Spirit. They begin speaking in other tongues as the Spirit gives utterance. Jewish pilgrims from many nations hear the wonderful works of God in their own languages.

Some are amazed; others mock. [[Peter]] stands with the eleven and explains the event through Joel's promise that God would pour out his Spirit. He then proclaims Jesus: attested by God, crucified by lawless hands, raised from the dead, exalted to God's right hand, and made both Lord and Christ.

The crowd is cut to the heart and asks what they should do. Peter calls them to repent and be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ for the remission of sins, with the promise of the Holy Spirit. About three thousand receive the word and are added.

Acts 2 ends with the newborn church devoted to the apostles' teaching, fellowship, breaking of bread, and prayers. They share possessions, gather in temple courts and homes, praise God, and enjoy favor as the Lord adds to their number.

Pentecost inaugurates the age of the Spirit, publicly vindicates the risen Christ, launches the church's witness, and reverses the scattering imagery of Babel through multilingual praise.

## Notes
- Pentecost is not the church inventing mission; it is the risen Christ empowering witness by the Spirit.
- The tongues signal mission across peoples and languages, not private spectacle detached from proclamation.
- Peter's sermon is deeply scriptural: Joel, Psalms, resurrection, exaltation, and repentance are held together.
- The life of the church in Acts 2 shows doctrine, worship, generosity, fellowship, and growth as one Spirit-formed pattern.
