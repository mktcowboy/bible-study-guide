# Cornelius's Vision

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 10:1-11:18
- **Summary:** A Roman centurion named Cornelius receives an angelic vision directing him to send for Peter; Peter receives a corresponding vision of a sheet of unclean animals; Peter travels to Caesarea and preaches to Cornelius's household, upon whom the Holy Spirit falls in a dramatic demonstration that Gentiles are fully included in God's salvation.

## Details
Acts 10 is, as many scholars have noted, a second Pentecost — often called the "Gentile Pentecost" — and it represents the most theologically explosive breakthrough in the entire book of Acts. For the early Jewish-Christian church, the question of Gentile inclusion was not merely sociological but bound up in centuries of covenant identity. Clean and unclean, Jew and Gentile, circumcised and uncircumcised — these were not casual categories. That God would breach them required unmistakably divine intervention, and Acts 10 records exactly that: a vision to a Gentile, a vision to an apostle, and a sovereign falling of the Spirit that outran Peter's own theological preparation.

Cornelius was a centurion of the Italian cohort stationed at [[Caesarea Maritima]], the administrative capital of Judea. Luke introduces him in terms that would have resonated with any Jewish reader: "a devout man, and one that feared God with all his house, which gave much alms to the people, and prayed to God alway" (Acts 10:2). He was a God-fearer — a class of Gentiles who had attached themselves to the synagogue and the God of Israel without undergoing full conversion and circumcision. At three in the afternoon (the hour of prayer), an angel appeared to him in a vision, addressed him by name, and told him his prayers and alms had "come up for a memorial before God" (Acts 10:4). He was commanded to send men to Joppa for one Simon Peter.

Simultaneously, Peter was on the rooftop of Simon the tanner's house in Joppa, hungry and praying, when he fell into a trance. He saw the sky open and a great sheet descend, held at its four corners, containing "all manner of fourfooted beasts of the earth, and wild beasts, and creeping things, and fowls of the air" (Acts 10:12) — a comprehensive collection of both clean and unclean creatures. A voice commanded: "Rise, Peter; kill, and eat" (Acts 10:13). Peter's instinctive refusal — "Not so, Lord; for I have never eaten any thing that is common or unclean" (Acts 10:14) — was met with the definitive divine reply: "What God hath cleansed, that call not thou common" (Acts 10:15). This happened three times, and then the sheet was withdrawn. Peter was "doubting in himself what this vision which he had seen should mean" (Acts 10:17) when the men from Cornelius arrived at the gate.

The Spirit directly commanded Peter to go with the men "doubting nothing: for I have sent them" (Acts 10:20). Peter traveled to Caesarea and entered Cornelius's house — an act that was itself a radical boundary-crossing — to find a household assembled and waiting. His opening sermon at Caesarea (Acts 10:34-43) contains perhaps the most famous statement of impartiality in the New Testament: "Of a truth I perceive that God is no respecter of persons: But in every nation he that feareth him, and worketh righteousness, is accepted with him" (Acts 10:34-35). Peter had not yet finished his sermon when "the Holy Ghost fell on all them which heard the word" (Acts 10:44). The Jewish Christians who had come with Peter were astonished: "because that on the Gentiles also was poured out the gift of the Holy Ghost. For they heard them speak with tongues, and magnify God" (Acts 10:45-46). Peter's conclusion was immediate and irresistible: "Can any man forbid water, that these should not be baptized, which have received the Holy Ghost as well as we?" (Acts 10:47). Cornelius and his household were baptized in the name of Jesus Christ.

When Peter returned to Jerusalem and was criticized by the circumcision party for eating with Gentiles, he recounted the entire sequence of events and concluded: "What was I, that I could withstand God?" (Acts 11:17). The Jerusalem church fell silent and glorified God: "Then hath God also to the Gentiles granted repentance unto life" (Acts 11:18). This episode becomes the critical precedent Peter himself cites at the [[Jerusalem Council]] (Acts 15:7-11), anchoring the council's decision to welcome Gentiles without requiring circumcision. Cornelius's Vision is not a peripheral episode; it is the theological foundation upon which the entire Gentile mission rests.

## Key People Involved
- [[Cornelius]] — Roman centurion; God-fearer; first recorded Gentile convert; received the Spirit before baptism
- [[Peter (Cephas)]] — receives the sheet vision; crosses the boundary to enter Cornelius's house; preaches; witnesses the Spirit's falling
- The angel — appears to Cornelius directing him to send for Peter

## Key Places
- [[Caesarea Maritima]] — Cornelius's station; where the Spirit fell on Gentiles for the first time
- Joppa — where Peter was staying when he received the rooftop vision
