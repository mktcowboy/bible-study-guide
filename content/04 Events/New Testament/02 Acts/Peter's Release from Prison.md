# Peter's Release from Prison

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 12:1-24
- **Summary:** Herod Agrippa I executes the apostle James and imprisons Peter; the church prays without ceasing; an angel awakens Peter in prison, his chains fall off, the iron gate opens of its own accord, and Peter walks free — while Herod, who accepted divine honors, is struck down by God and eaten of worms.

## Details
Acts 12 opens at a moment of intense political danger for the Jerusalem church. Herod Agrippa I — grandson of [[Herod the Great]] and a popular client king who cultivated Jewish loyalties — moved against the church with lethal intent: "he killed James the brother of John with the sword" (Acts 12:2). This is the only apostolic martyrdom Luke records in Acts, and its brevity makes it more jarring. James, the son of Zebedee and one of the inner circle of three (along with Peter and John), was executed without ceremony. When Herod saw that this pleased the Jewish authorities, he proceeded to arrest Peter as well, intending to bring him to public trial after the Passover.

Peter was held under maximum security: four squads of four soldiers each kept watch in rotating shifts; Peter slept between two soldiers, bound with two chains (Acts 12:4-6). The church had every reason for fear, having just lost James. And yet Luke inserts a detail that frames everything that follows: "but prayer was made without ceasing of the church unto God for him" (Acts 12:5). This persistent, corporate intercession is the hinge on which the miracle turns.

The night before Herod intended to bring Peter to trial, an angel of the Lord appeared in the cell, struck Peter on the side to wake him, and commanded him to "arise up quickly." The chains fell from his hands. "And a light shined in the prison: and he smote Peter on the side, and raised him up, saying, Arise up quickly. And his chains fell off from his hands" (Acts 12:7). Peter was directed to dress himself and follow, which he did — believing he was seeing a vision rather than experiencing a literal escape. They passed the first guard, the second, and came to the iron gate leading to the city, which "opened to them of his own accord" (Acts 12:10). They went out, walked the length of one street, and the angel departed. Peter, coming to himself, said, "Now I know of a surety, that the Lord hath sent his angel, and hath delivered me out of the hand of Herod, and from all the expectation of the people of the Jews" (Acts 12:11).

Peter went to the house of Mary the mother of [[John Mark]], where many had gathered in prayer. His knock at the gate was answered by a servant girl named Rhoda, who was so overwhelmed with joy at recognizing Peter's voice that she ran in to report without opening the gate — leaving Peter standing outside. The praying community's incredulity at their own answered prayer ("Thou art mad" — Acts 12:15) is one of the most humanly authentic moments in Acts: the church prays for a miracle and is astonished when it occurs. Peter was let in, explained what had happened, and charged them to report it to James (the Lord's brother) and the brethren before departing to another place.

The contrast Luke draws between Peter's miraculous deliverance and Herod's fate is deliberately sharp. Herod, dressed in royal robes, gave a speech to the people of Tyre and Sidon, who cried out, "It is the voice of a god, and not of a man" (Acts 12:22). Herod did not give God the glory, and immediately "the angel of the Lord smote him, because he gave not God the glory: and he was eaten of worms, and gave up the ghost" (Acts 12:23). The king who killed an apostle and imprisoned another was himself struck down. Luke's closing summary captures the inversion perfectly: "But the word of God grew and multiplied" (Acts 12:24). The chapter that began with the execution of James ends with the death of his executioner and the multiplication of the word Herod had tried to suppress.

## Key People Involved
- [[Peter (Cephas)]] — imprisoned; miraculously released; the church's primary leader in Jerusalem at this moment
- [[Agrippa]] — Herod Agrippa I; client king who executed James and imprisoned Peter; struck down by God at the chapter's close
- [[John Mark]] — his mother Mary's house was the prayer meeting venue; John Mark is present in Jerusalem at this time
- James (son of Zebedee) — executed by Herod at the chapter's opening; the loss that sets the crisis in motion
- Rhoda — servant girl whose joyful amazement is one of the chapter's most humanly memorable details
- The angel of the Lord — effects the deliverance; and later strikes down Herod

## Key Places
- [[Jerusalem]] — site of the imprisonment, the prayer meeting, and Peter's release
- The prison — four squads of guards; chains; the iron gate that opened of its own accord
