# Conversion of Saul

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 9:1-31 (cf. Acts 22:1-21; Acts 26:9-20)
- **Summary:** Saul of Tarsus, a fierce persecutor of the church, is arrested by a blinding light on the road to Damascus, hears the risen Christ speak to him, spends three days blind and fasting, is healed by the disciple Ananias, and immediately begins proclaiming Jesus in the synagogues of Damascus.

## Details
The conversion of Saul of Tarsus is one of the hinge points of the entire New Testament. Luke records it three times in Acts (chapters 9, 22, and 26), a feature that signals its enormous theological weight in the narrative. At the time of his Damascus road encounter, Saul was not a fringe persecutor but a man armed with official letters from the high priest, traveling to the synagogues of Damascus to arrest any followers of "the Way" and bring them bound to Jerusalem (Acts 9:2). He "yet breathed out threatenings and slaughter against the disciples of the Lord" (Acts 9:1) — a man of consuming religious zeal directed against the church he had helped put to flight after Stephen's stoning.

The encounter was sudden and total. "And as he journeyed, he came near Damascus: and suddenly there shined round about him a light from heaven: And he fell to the earth, and heard a voice saying unto him, Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me?" (Acts 9:3-4). The doubling of the name — "Saul, Saul" — echoes the divine address to Moses from the burning bush and to Abraham on Moriah. Saul's question reveals the theological core of the whole episode: "Who art thou, Lord?" The answer strikes with the force of revelation: "I am Jesus whom thou persecutest" (Acts 9:5). In this one sentence, the risen Christ identifies himself completely with his suffering church — to persecute the disciples is to persecute Jesus himself. The implication for Saul's entire worldview was catastrophic: the man he had been persecuting as a crucified blasphemer was, in fact, the risen Lord.

Saul was led blind into Damascus, where he neither ate nor drank for three days. Meanwhile, the Lord appeared in a vision to a disciple named Ananias and commanded him to go to Saul. Ananias's honest protest — "Lord, I have heard by many of this man, how much evil he hath done to thy saints at Jerusalem" (Acts 9:13) — makes his obedience all the more remarkable. The Lord overrode his fear with a single commissioning statement: "He is a chosen vessel unto me, to bear my name before the Gentiles, and kings, and the children of Israel: For I will shew him how great things he must suffer for my name's sake" (Acts 9:15-16). Here, before Saul has preached a single word, his entire future ministry is sketched — Gentiles, kings, Israel, and suffering.

Ananias came, laid his hands on Saul, and spoke words of restoration: "Brother Saul, the Lord, even Jesus, that appeared unto thee in the way as thou camest, hath sent me, that thou mightest receive thy sight, and be filled with the Holy Ghost" (Acts 9:17). Immediately something like scales fell from Saul's eyes, he received his sight, rose and was baptized, and "straightway he preached Christ in the synagogues, that he is the Son of God" (Acts 9:20). The amazement of those who heard was complete: "Is not this he that destroyed them which called on this name in Jerusalem?" (Acts 9:21). Saul's preaching grew in power to the point that Damascus Jews plotted to kill him; he escaped by being lowered in a basket through a breach in the city wall by night.

The conversion of Saul signals a decisive shift in Acts — from the Jerusalem-centered ministry of the Twelve to a Gentile-directed mission that will carry the gospel to Rome and beyond. Paul himself, reflecting on the event, describes it in Galatians 1 as a revelation of God's Son "in me" (Galatians 1:16), a language of total interior transformation. For the theology of grace, no conversion in Scripture is more dramatic: the chief persecutor becomes the chief apostle.
