# Macedonian Vision

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Acts]]
- **Chapters:** Acts 16:6-10
- **Summary:** While at [[Troas]], the apostle Paul received a night vision of a man from Macedonia pleading, "Come over into Macedonia, and help us." Obeying immediately, Paul and his companions sailed westward, marking the gospel's decisive entry into Europe. It is at this point that the narrative of Acts shifts to the first "we passage," indicating that [[Luke]] joined the travelling party.

## Significance
The Macedonian Vision is the hinge on which Paul's entire western mission turns. Prevented by the Holy Ghost from preaching in Asia and Bithynia, Paul found himself at the westernmost edge of Asia Minor with no clear direction until the vision resolved the uncertainty in a single night. The immediate obedience of the missionary band — "assuredly gathering that the Lord had called us" — established a pattern of Spirit-directed movement that would carry the gospel into Greece, and ultimately to Rome itself. The arrival of [[Luke]] as a companion, signalled by the shift to first-person plural, also gave Acts its eyewitness texture for the European chapters.

## Key Verses
> "And a vision appeared to Paul in the night; There stood a man of Macedonia, and prayed him, saying, Come over into Macedonia, and help us. And after he had seen the vision, immediately we endeavoured to go into Macedonia, assuredly gathering that the Lord had called us for to preach the gospel unto them." — Acts 16:9-10 (KJV)
