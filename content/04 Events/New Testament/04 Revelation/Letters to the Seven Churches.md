# Letters to the Seven Churches

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Revelation]]
- **Chapters:** Revelation 2:1–3:22
- **Summary:** Seven individual letters from the risen Christ to seven historical churches in the Roman province of Asia — each following a recognizable pattern of address, self-identification, commendation, rebuke, warning, call to repentance, and promise to the overcomer — functioning as a prophetic health-check on the church in every age.

## Details
The seven letters are delivered by the risen, glorified Christ to the apostle John on Patmos and carried by messenger to seven churches along a roughly circular postal route in western Asia Minor: Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, and Laodicea. The letters are real correspondence to historical congregations with real problems — false apostles, persecution, Nicolaitan teachings, sexual immorality, dead formalism, and self-deceptive prosperity. At the same time, the closing refrain "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches" addresses every church in every time.

Each letter follows a consistent template: (1) a title addressing the angel (messenger or leader) of the church; (2) a self-description of Christ drawing from the throne-vision of Revelation 1 (the one who holds the seven stars, whose eyes are like fire, who has the sharp two-edged sword, etc.); (3) commendation for what the church does well; (4) rebuke for what is wrong — absent for Smyrna and Philadelphia; (5) a warning or call to repentance; (6) the recurring refrain "He that hath an ear, let him hear"; and (7) a promise to "him that overcometh."

The spectrum of church conditions represented is deliberately comprehensive. Ephesus has doctrinal vigilance but has "left thy first love" (2:4) — orthodox but cold. Smyrna is materially poor but spiritually rich, facing coming persecution — "be thou faithful unto death, and I will give thee a crown of life" (2:10). Pergamum holds fast the name of Christ even where Satan's throne is, but tolerates Balaam-teaching and Nicolaitanism. Thyatira has love and service and faith, but permits the prophetess "Jezebel" to lead God's servants into immorality. Sardis has a reputation for life but is dead — "strengthen the things which remain, that are ready to die" (3:2). Philadelphia, with little strength, has kept the word and not denied the name — given an open door that none can shut. Laodicea is the most severe: wealthy, increased with goods, needing nothing — but "wretched, and miserable, and poor, and blind, and naked" (3:17), spewed out of Christ's mouth for lukewarmness.

The promises to overcomers form a remarkable sequence: eating of the tree of life (Paradise regained), the crown of life, the hidden manna and white stone with a new name, power over the nations and the morning star, white garments and a name in the book of life, a pillar in the temple of God never going out, and sitting with Christ on his throne (Revelation 3:21). Together they recapitulate Eden, the wilderness, the Davidic kingdom, and the new creation. The letters thus function as a theology of the church embedded in miniature, and as a diagnostic grid for self-examination that Christians have applied to individual congregations and their own hearts across two millennia.

## Important Verses
> "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches." — Revelation 2:7 (repeated seven times)

> "Be thou faithful unto death, and I will give thee a crown of life." — Revelation 2:10

> "I know thy works, that thou art neither cold nor hot: I would thou wert cold or hot. So then because thou art lukewarm, and neither cold nor hot, I will spue thee out of my mouth." — Revelation 3:15-16

> "Behold, I stand at the door, and knock: if any man hear my voice, and open the door, I will come in to him, and will sup with him, and he with me." — Revelation 3:20
