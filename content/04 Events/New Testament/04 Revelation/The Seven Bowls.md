# The Seven Bowls

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Revelation]]
- **Chapters:** Revelation 15:1–16:21
- **Summary:** Seven angels pour out the seven last plagues — the complete wrath of God — upon the unrepentant earth: painful sores, seas and rivers of blood, scorching heat, darkness on the beast's throne, the drying of the Euphrates, and a catastrophic earthquake culminating in the cry "It is done."

## Details
Revelation 15 frames the bowl plagues with a heavenly prologue of profound solemnity. The victorious saints stand on a sea of glass mingled with fire, holding harps and singing "the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb" (15:3) — a wedding of Exodus theology and new covenant praise. The song is quoted from Exodus 15 and the Psalms: "Great and marvellous are thy works, Lord God Almighty; just and true are thy ways, thou King of saints" (15:3). This worship scene establishes that the following judgments are not arbitrary cruelty but the expression of a just and holy God whose ways are true. The heavenly temple then fills with smoke "from the glory of God, and from his power; and no man was able to enter into the temple, till the seven plagues of the seven angels were fulfilled" (15:8) — an intercession is suspended; the time for judgment has fully come.

The seven bowls are poured out in rapid succession, and unlike the partial judgments of the seals and trumpets (which struck a third), the bowls are total in scope, representing the final and complete outpouring of God's wrath. Bowl one: "a noisome and grievous sore upon the men which had the mark of the beast" (16:2). Bowl two: the sea becomes as "the blood of a dead man: and every living soul died in the sea" (16:3). Bowl three: rivers and fountains of water become blood — the angel explains, "for they have shed the blood of saints and prophets, and thou hast given them blood to drink; for they are worthy" (16:6). The altar itself responds: "Even so, Lord God Almighty, true and righteous are thy judgments" (16:7).

Bowl four scorches mankind with great heat and fire — "and they blasphemed the name of God...and they repented not to give him glory" (16:9). Bowl five brings darkness on the throne of the beast and its kingdom — men gnaw their tongues in pain but "repented not of their deeds" (16:11). The pattern of hardened unrepentance mirrors Pharaoh in Exodus and reveals the nature of final judgment: not the production of repentance but the revelation of the heart's settled direction. Bowl six dries the Euphrates river, preparing the way for the kings of the east, while demonic spirits work signs to gather the kings of the earth to "the battle of that great day of God Almighty" (16:14) at Armageddon. Bowl seven produces a great voice from heaven: "It is done." The greatest earthquake in history levels cities, islands disappear, mountains fall, and great hail of a talent's weight falls on men — who still "blasphemed God" (16:21). Creation itself is being unmade.
