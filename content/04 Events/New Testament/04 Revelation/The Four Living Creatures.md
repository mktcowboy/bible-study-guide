# The Four Living Creatures

## Overview
- **Testament:** Both
- **Book:** [[Revelation]], [[Ezekiel]]
- **Chapters:** Revelation 4:6-11; Ezekiel 1:5-21; Isaiah 6
- **Summary:** Four living creatures around the divine throne — with the faces of a lion, ox, man, and eagle — who ceaselessly cry "Holy, holy, holy" and fall in worship before God; first described in Ezekiel's throne vision and reappearing prominently in Revelation.

## Details
In Revelation 4, John saw the throne of God in heaven. Around the throne were four living creatures (Greek: zoa) full of eyes in front and behind. The first was like a lion, the second like an ox (calf), the third had the face of a man, and the fourth was like a flying eagle. Each had six wings and was full of eyes around and within. Day and night they never ceased to say: "Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty, which was, and is, and is to come." When they gave glory and honour to him who sat on the throne, the twenty-four elders fell down and worshipped.

In Ezekiel 1, during the prophet's vision by the Chebar canal in Babylon, four living creatures appeared — each with four faces (lion, ox, man, eagle), four wings, and gleaming forms; beside each creature was a wheel within a wheel covered with eyes; the Spirit directed them. These creatures were identified later as cherubim (Ezekiel 10). In Isaiah 6, the seraphim around God's throne also cry "Holy, holy, holy, is the LORD of hosts" — connecting all three throne visions in a continuous liturgical tradition.

The four faces have been interpreted from early Christianity as representing the four Gospels: lion (Mark — the lion of Judah, strength), ox (Luke — sacrificial service), man (Matthew — the human lineage of Jesus), eagle (John — the high-soaring theology). The early church fathers and the iconographic tradition of the four evangelists grew from this association.

## Notes
The trisagion ("Holy, holy, holy") shared between Isaiah 6 and Revelation 4 has been called the highest and most ancient of all liturgical acclamations — the worship that never stops in the presence of God. Christian liturgy across traditions has incorporated these words. The fourfold nature of the creatures (four faces, four directions) likely symbolizes totality — all of creation in its highest expressions represented in unceasing worship. The eyes "full of eyes" suggest omniscient awareness and ceaseless watchfulness.
