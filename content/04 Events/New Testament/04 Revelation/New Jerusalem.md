# New Jerusalem

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Revelation]]
- **Chapters:** Revelation 21:1–22:5
- **Summary:** The culminating vision of Scripture: a new heaven and new earth replacing the first, and a holy city descending from God — where God himself dwells with his people, death and sorrow are abolished forever, and the river of life and tree of life restore what was lost in Eden.

## Details
Revelation 21-22 stands as the telos toward which all of Scripture has moved. The vision is introduced with five new things: a new heaven, a new earth, the first heaven and earth passing away, the sea no more, and the holy city descending from God out of heaven "prepared as a bride adorned for her husband" (21:2). The new creation is not the annihilation and replacement of the old but its transfiguration and renewal — the same world, purged and glorified. The sea's absence (which represented chaos and the abyss throughout Revelation) signals total victory over every hostile power.

The great voice from the throne announces the theological center of the new creation: "Behold, the tabernacle of God is with men, and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his people, and God himself shall be with them, and be their God" (21:3). This is the fulfillment of the covenant formula running from Abraham through the prophets — "I will be their God, and they shall be my people" — now realized without veil or distance. The five promises of 21:4 are all negatives: God "shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away." The throne then speaks: "Behold, I make all things new" and "It is done. I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the end" (21:5-6).

The city itself (21:9-21) is described in terms drawn from Ezekiel 40-48 (the prophet's temple-city vision) and from the wilderness tabernacle, but expanded to cosmic proportions. The holy Jerusalem is a perfect cube of 12,000 furlongs — evoking the Holy of Holies (1 Kings 6:20), but now of unimaginable scale. Its walls are jasper, its streets pure gold like transparent glass, its twelve foundations garnished with precious stones, its twelve gates of pearl each named for a tribe of Israel, its foundations bearing the names of the twelve apostles. "And I saw no temple therein: for the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it" (21:22). The entire city has become the Holy of Holies — the dwelling of God co-extensive with the dwelling of his people.

The river of the water of life flows from the throne of God and the Lamb (22:1), and on either side stands the tree of life, bearing twelve kinds of fruit monthly, its leaves for the healing of the nations (22:2) — a direct restoration of Eden's garden, but with the curse reversed: "And there shall be no more curse" (22:3). The servants of God serve him and "see his face" (22:4) — the beatific vision that Moses was denied (Exodus 33:20), now freely granted. They reign for ever and ever. The closing verses of Revelation 22 frame this vision with the testimony of Jesus ("Surely I come quickly"), the Spirit and bride's invitation ("Come"), and the final benediction. Scripture ends where it began — in a garden with God — but in a garden that is also a city, that is also a temple, that is also the bride.
