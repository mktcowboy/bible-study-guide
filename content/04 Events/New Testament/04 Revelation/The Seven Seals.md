# The Seven Seals

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Revelation]]
- **Chapters:** Revelation 6:1–8:5
- **Summary:** The Lamb opens seven seals on the scroll of history, unleashing the four horsemen of conquest, war, famine, and death; revealing the martyrs crying for justice; bringing cosmic upheaval; and sealing 144,000 — culminating in the great multitude before the throne and the awe-filled silence when the seventh seal is opened.

## Details
The seven seals form the first of three septets of judgment in Revelation (seals, trumpets, bowls), but they are preceded by the most important scene in the book: the Lamb who alone is worthy to open the sealed scroll (Revelation 5). The worthiness of Christ as the slain Lamb is the theological precondition for all that follows. What history discloses, Christ has authority to open. The seals thus represent not random catastrophe but the unfolding of God's sovereign purpose under the Lamb's direction.

The first four seals release the famous Four Horsemen drawn from Zechariah 1 and 6. The white horse of the first seal is debated — some identify it with conquest or empire, others with the gospel's advance, though most commentators favor a military or political conqueror figure. The red horse brings war and the taking of peace from the earth. The black horse brings famine: "A measure of wheat for a penny, and three measures of barley for a penny; and see thou hurt not the oil and the wine" (6:6) — the staple food of the poor rationed at a day's wage, while luxury goods remain. The pale horse carries Death, and Hades follows with him — authority to kill "with sword, and with hunger, and with death, and with the beasts of the earth" over a fourth of the earth (6:8).

The fifth seal shifts from judgment on the earth to the perspective of heaven: under the altar are the souls of those "slain for the word of God, and for the testimony which they held" (6:9). They cry, "How long, O Lord, holy and true, dost thou not judge and avenge our blood on them that dwell on the earth?" (6:10). They are given white robes and told to rest a little longer until their number is complete. This seal is the theological pivot of the sequence — the martyrs' prayer for justice is the motive force behind the judgments that follow.

The sixth seal brings cosmic catastrophe in the imagery of the prophets: earthquake, sun darkened, moon like blood, stars falling, sky rolled up, mountains and islands removed (6:12-14). The great and the mighty hide in caves, crying for the mountains to fall on them: "for the great day of his wrath is come; and who shall be able to stand?" (6:17). Revelation 7 then inserts the sealing of 144,000 — twelve thousand from each tribe of Israel, marked for God's protection — followed by the great multitude from every nation, tribe, people, and tongue standing before the throne in white robes. The seventh seal, when opened, produces only silence in heaven for about half an hour (8:1) — the most dramatic silence in Scripture, the awe-filled pause before the seven trumpets begin.
