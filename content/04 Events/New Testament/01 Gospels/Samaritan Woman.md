# Samaritan Woman

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[John]]
- **Chapters:** John 4:1-42
- **Summary:** Jesus' conversation with a Samaritan woman at Jacob's well; he revealed himself as the living water and the Messiah; she evangelized her entire town and many Samaritans believed.

## Details
Traveling through Samaria (a route most Jews avoided), Jesus stopped at Jacob's well near the Samaritan city of Sychar while his disciples went to buy food. A Samaritan woman came to draw water. Jesus asked her for a drink — already transgressing two social conventions: Jews did not associate with Samaritans, and Jewish men did not address women publicly. Their conversation moved from physical water to "living water" to eternal life. The woman misunderstood initially, thinking he meant running spring water; Jesus clarified that the water he offered would become "a well of water springing up into everlasting life."

Jesus then revealed supernatural knowledge of her personal life: she had five husbands and the man she was with was not her husband. Astonished, she identified him as a prophet and raised the Jewish-Samaritan dispute over proper worship — Jerusalem vs. Mount Gerizim. Jesus' answer transcended both: "the hour cometh, and now is, when the true worshippers shall worship the Father in spirit and in truth." When she mentioned the coming Messiah, Jesus made one of his most direct self-revelations in all the Gospels: "I that speak unto thee am he."

The disciples returned and were surprised to find him speaking to a woman. The woman left her water pot, went to the city, and told people "Come, see a man, which told me all things that ever I did: is not this the Christ?" The Samaritans came out to see Jesus; many believed because of her testimony, and they invited him to stay. He stayed two days, and many more believed "because of his own word."

## Notes
This is the longest recorded conversation Jesus had with any individual in the Gospels. It crosses every social boundary of his culture: gender, ethnicity, religious tradition, and moral status. The woman moves from skepticism to recognition to proclamation in a single conversation — making her effectively the first missionary to the Samaritans. The episode anticipates Acts 8:4-25 (Philip's Samaritan mission). Jesus' self-disclosure here ("I am he" — ego eimi) is one of several such absolute statements in John that echo the divine name.
