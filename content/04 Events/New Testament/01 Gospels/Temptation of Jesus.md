# Temptation of Jesus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]]
- **Chapters:** Matthew 4:1-11; Mark 1:12-13; Luke 4:1-13
- **Summary:** After his baptism, Jesus was led by the Spirit into the wilderness for 40 days where Satan tempted him three times; Jesus overcame each temptation by quoting scripture.

## Details
Immediately after his baptism, the Spirit led (Mark says "drove") Jesus into the wilderness, where he fasted for forty days and forty nights. After this prolonged fast, when Jesus was hungry, Satan approached with three temptations. The first was to command stones to become bread — testing whether Jesus would use his divine power for personal comfort and survival. Jesus replied: "Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of God" (Deuteronomy 8:3).

The second temptation (in Matthew's order) was for Jesus to throw himself from the pinnacle of the temple, so that angels would catch him — a test of presumption, forcing God's hand through spectacular display. Satan even quoted Psalm 91:11-12. Jesus countered: "Thou shalt not tempt the Lord thy God" (Deuteronomy 6:16). The third temptation was a panoramic offer of all the kingdoms of the world in exchange for one act of worship toward Satan. Jesus refused: "Thou shalt worship the Lord thy God, and him only shalt thou serve" (Deuteronomy 6:13). At this Satan departed, and angels came and ministered to Jesus.

Every temptation Jesus faced echoes Israel's failures in the wilderness: hunger and bread (Exodus 16), testing God (Exodus 17), and idolatry (Exodus 32). Where Israel failed over 40 years, Jesus succeeded in 40 days. He is the true Israel, perfectly obedient where his people were not, qualifying him as the spotless lamb of the new covenant.

## Notes
The Temptation narrative demonstrates that Jesus faced genuine human temptation and emerged victorious, not as a divine being above temptation but as the perfect man. His weapon throughout is not miraculous power but the written word of God — specifically Deuteronomy, which itself records Moses' instructions from Israel's wilderness period. This establishes a pattern for Christian spiritual warfare: "the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God" (Ephesians 6:17).
