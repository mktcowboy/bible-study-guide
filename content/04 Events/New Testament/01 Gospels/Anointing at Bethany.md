# Anointing at Bethany

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[John]]
- **Chapters:** Matthew 26:6-13; Mark 14:3-9; John 12:1-8
- **Summary:** At a dinner in Bethany, a woman — identified in John 12 as [[Mary (sister of Martha)]] — anoints Jesus with costly ointment of spikenard worth three hundred pence; Judas objects on grounds of waste; Jesus defends her, declaring she has anointed him "for my burial" and that her act will be told wherever the gospel is preached.

## Significance
The anointing stands at the threshold of the Passion narrative as a prophetic act Jesus himself interprets as preparation for burial. In honoring the act as a memorial, Jesus implicitly affirms that his death is imminent and purposeful. Mark and Matthew place it immediately before Judas's decision to betray — the extravagant love of the woman contrasts starkly with the transactional betrayal that follows. John's explicit identification of the woman as Mary of Bethany ties this scene to the Lazarus household and gives the act added resonance: the sister of the man Jesus raised is the one who prepares him for his own death.

## Key Verses
> "She hath done what she could: she is come aforehand to anoint my body to the burying. Verily I say unto you, Wheresoever this gospel shall be preached throughout the whole world, this also that she hath done shall be spoken of for a memorial of her." — Mark 14:8-9 (KJV)
