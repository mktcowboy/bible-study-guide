# The Prodigal Son

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Primary Text:** Luke 15:11-32
- **Type:** Parable / teaching
- **Summary:** Jesus tells of a younger son who wastes his inheritance, returns in shame, and is welcomed by his father, while the older brother resents the father's mercy.

## Details

The parable of the prodigal son is the third and longest story in Luke 15, following the lost sheep and the lost coin. Jesus tells these parables in response to Pharisees and scribes grumbling that he receives sinners and eats with them. The parable therefore speaks not only to obvious sinners, but also to religious resentment at mercy.

The younger son asks for his inheritance before his father's death, effectively treating the father as though he were already dead. He leaves for a far country, wastes his substance in reckless living, and falls into famine and humiliation. Feeding swine, he comes to himself and resolves to return, confessing that he has sinned against heaven and before his father.

The father sees him while he is still far off, runs to him, embraces him, kisses him, clothes him, restores him, and calls for celebration. The son asks to be received as a hired servant, but the father restores him as son. The robe, ring, sandals, and feast all announce grace beyond the son's prepared speech.

The older brother stands outside the celebration. Though outwardly obedient, he is angry at the father's generosity. He describes his service as slavery, distances himself from his brother, and resents mercy. The father pleads with him too, reminding him that all the father has is his and that it is fitting to rejoice because the lost son is alive again.

The parable ends open-ended. The listener is left standing with the older brother outside the feast, invited to join the father's joy.

## Notes
- "Prodigal" means wasteful; the younger son wastes the inheritance, but the father's mercy is lavish too.
- The older brother is not a side character; he is aimed directly at religious resentment.
- The parable makes repentance relational: return to the father, not merely regret over consequences.
- The open ending forces the listener to answer whether they will join heaven's joy over sinners who repent.
