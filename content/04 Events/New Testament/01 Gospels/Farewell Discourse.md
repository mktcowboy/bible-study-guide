# Farewell Discourse

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[John]]
- **Chapters:** John 14-17
- **Summary:** In the upper room on the night of his arrest, Jesus delivered an extended farewell teaching to his disciples: the promise of the Holy Spirit, "I am the vine," the command to abide in love, warning of persecution, and the high priestly prayer.

## Details
After the foot-washing and the departure of Judas, Jesus turned to comfort and instruct his remaining disciples. John 14 opened with "Let not your heart be troubled" and the promise of the Father's house with many rooms. Jesus declared "I am the way, the truth, and the life: no man cometh unto the Father, but by me" — the exclusive claim that defines Christian soteriology. He promised to send "another Comforter" (the Paraclete, the Holy Spirit) who would be with them forever — the Spirit of truth who would dwell in them, teach them all things, and bring to remembrance his words.

Chapter 15 introduced the "True Vine" discourse: Jesus is the vine, the Father is the husbandman, and the disciples are branches. Branches that do not bear fruit are removed; those that do are pruned for greater fruitfulness. The key command is to "abide in me" — maintaining the vital union with Christ that produces fruit. He commanded them to love one another as he loved them, defined as the willingness to lay down one's life for friends. He warned them that the world would hate them as it hated him. Chapter 16 promised that the Spirit would convict the world of sin, righteousness, and judgment, and would guide the disciples into all truth.

Chapter 17 is the longest recorded prayer of Jesus — his "high priestly prayer." He prayed for himself (to be glorified), for the eleven disciples (that they be kept in the Father's name, kept from the evil one, sanctified in truth), and for all future believers (that they be one, that they see his glory). The prayer reveals Jesus' intimate relationship with the Father and his overarching concern for the unity of his people.

## Notes
John 14-17 constitutes the most theologically dense section of the Gospels and has no parallel in the Synoptics. The discourse moves from comfort to command to prayer, functioning as both pastoral consolation and theological formation. The Paraclete promises (14:16-17, 14:26, 15:26, 16:7-15) form the basis for the NT theology of the Holy Spirit's ministry. The high priestly prayer (ch. 17) is structured in three movements mirroring the Aaronic high priest's entry into the Most Holy Place — Jesus intercedes in the presence of the Father for all his people.
