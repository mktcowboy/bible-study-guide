# Massacre of the Innocents

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Matthew]]
- **Chapters:** Matthew 2:16-18
- **Summary:** When [[Herod the Great]] perceived that the Magi had not returned as instructed, he sent soldiers to kill all male children two years old and under in Bethlehem and all its surrounding region, according to the time he had diligently enquired of the Magi. Matthew applies Jeremiah 31:15 to the event: "In Rama was there a voice heard, lamentation, and weeping, and great mourning, Rachel weeping for her children, and would not be comforted, because they are not" (Matthew 2:18). The infant Jesus had already been taken to [[Egypt]] by this time through the [[Flight to Egypt]].

## Significance
The Massacre of the Innocents reveals the depths of fallen human power's hostility to the advent of the true King, and places the infancy of Jesus in direct parallel with the infancy of Moses, whose birth also was threatened by a royal decree to kill Hebrew male children (Exodus 1:15-22). Matthew's quotation of Jeremiah 31:15 is laden with irony: the verse in its original context is immediately followed by a promise of restoration and return from exile, suggesting that even in this grief a reversal is coming. The blood of the innocents thus frames the Christ-child's arrival as a recapitulation of Israel's suffering and a foretaste of his own eventual death at the hands of political and religious authorities.
