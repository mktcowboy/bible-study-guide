# The Widow's Mite

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Mark]]
- **Chapters:** Mark 12:41–44; Luke 21:1–4
- **Summary:** Jesus watches the rich cast large sums into the Temple treasury and a poor widow drop in two small copper coins, then declares she has given more than all of them — for they gave from their abundance, but she gave all her living.

## Details
As Jesus sat opposite the treasury in the Temple during Passion Week, he watched the crowd put money in. Many rich people cast in much. Then a poor widow came and dropped in two mites — the smallest coins in circulation, worth a fraction of a penny. Jesus called his disciples to him and made a formal declaration: "Verily I say unto you, That this poor widow hath cast more in, than all they which have cast into the treasury: For all they did cast in of their abundance; but she of her want did cast in all that she had, even all her living."

The passage is often read as praise of sacrificial giving, and it is that. But its location in both [[Mark]] and [[Luke]] — immediately after Jesus's denunciation of the scribes who "devour widows' houses" (Mark 12:40) — gives it a sharper edge. The widow gives everything she has to an institution whose religious leaders have already been condemned for exploiting people like her. Some interpreters see in Jesus's observation not only praise but also sorrow and indictment: the same corrupt religious system that exploits widows also inspires this widow's total devotion. The juxtaposition is left for the reader to hold.

The measure of giving Jesus uses is not absolute amount but proportion to what the giver has. The rich gave from surplus — their standard of living did not change. The widow gave "all her living" — the Greek word *bios* means life itself, sustenance, all she had to live on. This is the logic of 2 Corinthians 8:12: "if there be first a willing mind, it is accepted according to that a man hath." The widow's gift was total, not because it was required, but because she held nothing back.
