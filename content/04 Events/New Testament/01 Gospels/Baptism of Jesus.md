# Baptism of Jesus

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]]
- **Chapters:** Matthew 3:13-17; Mark 1:9-11; Luke 3:21-22; John 1:29-34
- **Summary:** John baptized Jesus in the Jordan River; the Holy Spirit descended as a dove; and the Father's voice declared "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased."

## Details
When Jesus came from Galilee to the Jordan to be baptized by John, John initially protested, saying he needed to be baptized by Jesus and not the reverse. Jesus answered that it was fitting to "fulfil all righteousness" and John consented. As Jesus came up from the water, the heavens opened and the Spirit of God descended upon him like a dove and alighted on him. A voice from heaven declared: "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased."

John's Gospel adds the perspective of John the Baptist's testimony: he saw the Spirit descend and remain on Jesus, and this was the sign God had told him would identify the one who baptizes with the Holy Spirit. John declared "Behold the Lamb of God" and testified that Jesus was the Son of God.

The baptism of Jesus is a Trinitarian event par excellence — all three persons of the Godhead are simultaneously present and active: the Son being baptized, the Spirit descending, the Father speaking. Jesus' baptism was not for personal sin (he had none) but an act of identification with sinful humanity, publicly commissioning him for his messianic ministry and inaugurating the new covenant era.

## Notes
All four Gospels record or reference this event, making it one of the most attested moments in Jesus' ministry. Jesus' baptism functions as his public anointing — the Greek word for "Christ" (Christos) means "anointed one." Luke notes that Jesus was praying when the Spirit descended, a characteristic Lukan emphasis on Jesus' prayer life.
