# Washing Feet

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[John]]
- **Chapters:** John 13:1-17
- **Summary:** At the Last Supper, Jesus rose from the table, girded himself as a servant, and washed each disciple's feet; Peter initially refused; Jesus taught this as the model of servant leadership.

## Details
During the supper, knowing that his hour had come and that the Father had given all things into his hands, Jesus rose from the table, laid aside his garments, girded himself with a towel, poured water into a basin, and began washing the disciples' feet and wiping them with the towel. This was the task reserved for the lowest household servant in Jewish and Roman culture. When he came to Simon Peter, Peter protested emphatically: "Lord, dost thou wash my feet?" Jesus answered that Peter did not understand now but would understand afterward. Peter refused categorically: "Thou shalt never wash my feet." Jesus replied: "If I wash thee not, thou hast no part with me." Peter then swung to the opposite extreme: "Lord, not my feet only, but also my hands and my head." Jesus explained that one who has already bathed needs only his feet washed.

After completing the washing, Jesus put his garments back on and returned to the table. He asked them if they understood what he had done: "Ye call me Master and Lord: and ye say well; for so I am. If I then, your Lord and Master, have washed your feet; ye also ought to wash one another's feet. For I have given you an example, that ye should do as I have done to you. Verily, verily, I say unto you, The servant is not greater than his lord."

## Notes
John's Gospel does not include the institution of the Lord's Supper but gives this extended foot-washing narrative instead — the two events are theologically parallel: both are embodied enactments of self-giving service. The act demonstrates the Incarnation's logic: the one who is Lord became servant. Whether foot-washing is practiced literally as an ordinance (as in some traditions) or symbolically as a model of humble service has been debated in church history, but the command to servant leadership is unambiguous.
