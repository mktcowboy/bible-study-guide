# Feeding of the Five Thousand

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Primary Texts:** Matthew 14:13-21; Mark 6:30-44; Luke 9:10-17; John 6:1-14
- **Summary:** Jesus feeds five thousand men, besides women and children, with five loaves and two fish, and twelve baskets remain.

## Details

The feeding of the five thousand is the only miracle of Jesus, apart from the resurrection, recorded in all four Gospels. This fourfold witness marks it as a major sign of Jesus' identity and kingdom provision.

The setting is a desolate place. The crowds follow Jesus, and he has compassion on them. In Mark, they are like sheep without a shepherd, so Jesus teaches them many things. When the day grows late, the disciples suggest sending the crowds away to buy food. Jesus instead tells the disciples, "Give ye them to eat."

The available provision is laughably small: five loaves and two fish. John's Gospel identifies [[Andrew]] as the disciple who mentions the boy with five barley loaves and two small fish, while also asking what they are among so many. Jesus takes the loaves, gives thanks, breaks them, and gives them through the disciples to the people.

All eat and are filled. The twelve baskets of fragments show not bare survival but abundance. The miracle echoes wilderness manna, Elisha's feeding miracle, and shepherd imagery. It also prepares for Jesus' [[Bread of Life]] discourse in John 6, where the sign points beyond physical bread to Jesus himself as the true bread from heaven.

The crowd responds by recognizing Jesus as the prophet who should come into the world and, in John, tries to make him king by force. Jesus withdraws, refusing a political kingship detached from the way of the cross.

## Notes
- The miracle is both compassion and revelation: Jesus feeds real hunger while revealing deeper identity.
- The twelve baskets connect the abundance with Israel and the apostolic witnesses.
- Jesus uses the disciples in distribution, teaching them dependence before ministry.
- John 6 guards against misunderstanding the sign as merely political or material provision.
