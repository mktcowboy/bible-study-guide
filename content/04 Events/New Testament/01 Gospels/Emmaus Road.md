# Emmaus Road

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Luke]]
- **Chapters:** Luke 24:13-35
- **Summary:** Two disciples walking from Jerusalem to Emmaus were joined by the risen Jesus who opened the scriptures to them along the way; they recognized him at the breaking of bread, and he vanished from their sight.

## Details
On the afternoon of the resurrection, two disciples — one named Cleopas — were walking from Jerusalem to Emmaus, a village about seven miles away, discussing the events of the past days. Jesus himself drew near and walked with them, but they were prevented from recognizing him. He asked what they were discussing; they stopped, their faces downcast, and expressed their shattered hope: "We trusted that it had been he which should have redeemed Israel" — but now it was the third day since his crucifixion. They had heard reports from women that the tomb was empty and angels said he was alive, but they could not confirm it.

Jesus responded: "O fools, and slow of heart to believe all that the prophets have spoken: Ought not Christ to have suffered these things, and to enter into his glory?" Beginning with Moses and all the prophets, he expounded to them in all the scriptures the things concerning himself. As they approached Emmaus, they urged the stranger to stay with them, for the day was far spent. At table, he took bread, blessed it, broke it, and gave it to them — and their eyes were opened, and they recognized him. He immediately vanished from their sight. They said to one another "Did not our heart burn within us, while he talked with us by the way, and while he opened to us the scriptures?" They returned immediately to Jerusalem to tell the eleven.

## Notes
The Emmaus Road narrative is Luke's most artistically crafted resurrection appearance. The movement of the story — from hopelessness to recognition to mission — mirrors the disciples' broader journey from the cross to the proclamation of the gospel. The moment of recognition at the breaking of bread has been interpreted sacramentally: Christ is known in the Eucharist. The pattern of "scripture opened, then presence recognized" defines the Christian experience of worship: the Word and the Table. The phrase "heart burning within us" has become a standard description of the Spirit-illumined encounter with scripture.
