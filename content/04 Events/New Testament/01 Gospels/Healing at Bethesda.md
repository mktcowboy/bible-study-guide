# Healing at Bethesda

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[John]]
- **Chapters:** John 5:1-47
- **Summary:** Jesus healed a man who had been ill for 38 years at the pool of Bethesda on the Sabbath; the resulting conflict with Jewish authorities generated Jesus' extended discourse on his relationship with the Father.

## Details
On a feast day, Jesus went up to Jerusalem and passed by the pool of Bethesda near the Sheep Gate, which was surrounded by five covered colonnades. A great multitude of sick people lay there — blind, halt, withered — waiting, according to the popular belief, for the periodic stirring of the water. One man had been in that condition for 38 years. Jesus singled him out and asked: "Wilt thou be made whole?" The man explained he had no one to put him into the pool when the water was stirred, and someone always stepped in before him. Jesus commanded: "Rise, take up thy bed, and walk." Immediately the man was healed, took up his mat, and walked.

The Jewish leaders confronted the healed man for carrying his mat on the Sabbath; he explained that the man who healed him had told him to do so. They sought Jesus, whom they had not yet identified. Later Jesus found the man in the temple and warned him: "sin no more, lest a worse thing come unto thee." The man then identified Jesus to the authorities, who persecuted Jesus for doing these things on the Sabbath. Jesus answered: "My Father worketh hitherto, and I work." This made the leaders more determined to kill him, because he not only broke the Sabbath but called God his Father, making himself equal to God.

Jesus' ensuing discourse (5:19-47) constituted a comprehensive claim: the Son does only what he sees the Father do; the Father has given the Son authority to raise the dead and to execute judgment; those who honor the Son honor the Father; all who are in tombs will hear his voice and come forth.

## Notes
John's Gospel is structured around seven signs; this healing is the third. The 38-year duration echoes Israel's 38-year wandering in the wilderness after Kadesh-barnea (Deuteronomy 2:14). The discourse that follows the healing is one of the most explicit statements of Jesus' divine nature and equal authority with the Father in the Gospels. The claim "the Father judgeth no man, but hath committed all judgment unto the Son" (5:22) is the foundation for the NT doctrine of Christ as eschatological judge.
