# Last Supper

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Primary Texts:** Matthew 26:17-30; Mark 14:12-26; Luke 22:7-38; John 13-17; 1 Corinthians 11:23-26
- **Summary:** On the night before his crucifixion, Jesus shares Passover with the Twelve, identifies the betrayer, institutes the bread and cup of the new covenant, washes feet, and gives farewell teaching.

## Details

The Last Supper takes place in the Passover setting. Jesus sends disciples to prepare the meal in an upper room in Jerusalem. The meal stands at the turning point between Israel's exodus memorial and the new covenant accomplished through Jesus' death.

During the supper, Jesus announces that one of the Twelve will betray him. The disciples are sorrowful, and [[Judas Iscariot]] is exposed. In John's account, Judas receives the morsel and goes out into the night. The betrayal is therefore not outside the table fellowship; it happens in the very setting of covenant intimacy.

Jesus takes bread, gives thanks, breaks it, and gives it to the disciples as his body. He takes the cup and speaks of his blood of the new covenant, shed for many for the remission of sins. The bread and cup become the church's repeated proclamation of the Lord's death until he comes.

John's Gospel emphasizes additional dimensions of the same evening. Jesus washes the disciples' feet, teaching servant love. He gives the new commandment, teaches about the Father's house, the way, the truth, and the life, the vine and branches, the coming Spirit, the world's hatred, and his high priestly prayer.

The Last Supper is therefore meal, sign, covenant, warning, comfort, and commissioning. It prepares the disciples to understand the cross not as defeat but as covenant-giving sacrifice.

## Notes
- The Last Supper transforms Passover around Jesus' impending death.
- The Lord's Supper looks backward to the cross and forward until he comes.
- Judas's presence at the table intensifies the betrayal and warns against outward nearness without faithfulness.
- John's footwashing scene shows the meaning of Jesus' death in the pattern of humble love.
