# Restoration of Peter

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[John]]
- **Chapters:** John 21:15-23
- **Summary:** By the Sea of Tiberias, the risen Jesus asked Peter three times "Lovest thou me?" and commissioned him to feed his sheep; the threefold question directly echoes and reverses Peter's three denials.

## Details
After the miraculous catch of fish and breakfast on the shore of the Sea of Tiberias, Jesus drew Peter aside for a personal conversation. Three times — corresponding exactly to Peter's three denials in the high priest's courtyard — Jesus asked "Simon, son of Jonas, lovest thou me?" The first two times Jesus used the Greek agapao (unconditional love); Peter responded with phileo (affectionate love) each time. The third time Jesus used Peter's own word phileo, and Peter, grieved at being asked a third time, appealed to Jesus' omniscience: "Lord, thou knowest all things; thou knowest that I love thee."

Each time Peter affirmed his love, Jesus gave a commission: "Feed my lambs," "Feed my sheep," "Feed my sheep." The threefold restoration corresponds to the threefold denial (John 18:17, 25-27). Jesus also prophesied Peter's future: in his youth he had gone where he wished, but in old age another would gird him and lead him where he would not wish to go — referring to the manner of his death (crucifixion, according to church tradition). John adds: "This spake he, signifying by what death he should glorify God." Jesus then simply said "Follow me."

## Notes
The restoration is a masterpiece of narrative theology. The charcoal fire burning on the shore (John 21:9) deliberately echoes the charcoal fire in the high priest's courtyard where Peter warmed himself when he denied Jesus (John 18:18) — the only two uses of that specific word in the NT. The rehabilitation is complete: the same setting, the same number of questions, the same man — but this time love and commission replace denial and shame. Jesus' words "follow me" (21:19, 22) return to his first call to Peter, implying a fresh beginning.
