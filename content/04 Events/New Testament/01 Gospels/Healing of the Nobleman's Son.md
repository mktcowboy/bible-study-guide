# Healing of the Nobleman's Son

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[John]]
- **Chapters:** John 4:46-54
- **Summary:** A royal official from [[Capernaum]] begged Jesus at [[Cana]] to heal his dying son; Jesus healed the boy from a distance with a word, and the official's whole household believed — the second sign Jesus performed in Galilee.

## Details
When Jesus returned to [[Cana]] of Galilee, where he had turned the water into wine, a certain nobleman (a royal official, likely in the service of Herod Antipas) came to him from [[Capernaum]], about twenty miles away, because his son lay sick and at the point of death. He begged Jesus to come down and heal him. Jesus responded, "Except ye see signs and wonders, ye will not believe" — a gentle rebuke of sign-dependent faith. The official pressed on: "Sir, come down ere my child die." Jesus answered, "Go thy way; thy son liveth." The man believed the word Jesus spoke and set off home.

As he was on his way, his servants met him with the news that the boy was alive and recovering. When he asked at what hour the child began to mend, they said, "Yesterday at the seventh hour the fever left him." The father realized it was the same hour Jesus had said, "Thy son liveth." As a result he himself believed, and his whole household with him. John explicitly numbers this as "the second miracle that Jesus did, when he was come out of Judaea into Galilee" (John 4:54).

## Key People Involved
- [[Jesus Christ]] — Healed the boy from a distance by his word alone
- The royal official of Capernaum — a court official whose faith grew from desperation to settled belief

## Key Places
- [[Cana]] — Where the official found Jesus and the second sign was given
- [[Capernaum]] — The official's home, where his son lay sick and was healed

## Notes
This is the second of John's seven "signs." Its structure traces a deepening faith: the official first wants Jesus physically present, then trusts his bare word, and finally believes outright along with his household. The healing from a distance demonstrates that Jesus' authority is not bound by location — a theme John highlights by setting the cure in [[Capernaum]] while Jesus remained in [[Cana]]. It is commonly distinguished from the healing of the centurion's servant (Matthew 8; Luke 7), which shares the long-distance motif but differs in person, circumstance, and Gospel.
