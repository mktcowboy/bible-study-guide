# Healing at Bethsaida

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Book:** [[Mark]]
- **Chapters:** Mark 8:22–26
- **Summary:** Jesus heals a blind man near Bethsaida in two stages — the man first sees people "like trees walking," and then with a second touch sees clearly — the only two-stage miracle in the Gospels, placed deliberately before Peter's partial confession.

## Details
A blind man was brought to Jesus at Bethsaida, and the people begged Jesus to touch him. Taking the man by the hand, Jesus led him outside the village — private, away from the crowd, as in the healing of the deaf-mute — and spat on his eyes, laid his hands on him, and asked if he saw anything. The man's answer is extraordinary and unrepeated in Scripture: "I see men as trees, walking" — an image of something real but not yet fully resolved, shapes without definition, motion without clarity.

Jesus laid his hands on him a second time. This time the man "looked stedfastly," and his sight was restored fully so that he saw every man clearly.

This is the only two-stage miracle Jesus performs anywhere in the Gospels, and its placement in [[Mark]] is deliberate. It falls immediately before Peter's confession at Caesarea Philippi (Mark 8:27–30). The structural parallel is unmistakable: Peter, like the blind man, sees something true but not yet fully resolved — "Thou art the Christ" is correct but incomplete; he does not yet see the suffering and death that must accompany the Messiah's glory. Jesus will have to touch Peter's understanding again (at the resurrection and Pentecost) before full clarity comes.

Mark thus uses the two-stage healing as an enacted parable of spiritual sight: the disciples have moved beyond blindness (they see that Jesus is the Christ) but have not yet achieved full vision (they do not grasp the cross). The healing at Bethsaida warns the reader not to declare too quickly that they see clearly.
