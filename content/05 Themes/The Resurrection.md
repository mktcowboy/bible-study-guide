# The Resurrection

## Overview
The resurrection is God's triumph over death, the vindication of the righteous, and the central confession of the Christian faith. It runs as a thread from the earliest Scriptures to the final vision of the new creation.

## Canonical Development
In the Old Testament, Job confesses that his Redeemer liveth and that in his flesh he shall see God (Job 19:25-26); Isaiah promises that the dead shall live and that the earth shall cast out its dead (Isaiah 26:19); Ezekiel's valley of dry bones declares God's power to raise a whole nation to life (Ezekiel 37); and Daniel foretells a general resurrection to everlasting life or everlasting contempt (Daniel 12:2). In the New Testament, Jesus raises Lazarus as a sign of his own authority over death (John 11), declares himself the resurrection and the life, and then rises bodily on the third day — the firstfruits of them that slept (1 Corinthians 15:20). Acts proclaims the resurrection as the heart of apostolic preaching, and Revelation depicts the final resurrection and judgment that consummates all history.

## Notes
The resurrection is not merely a future hope but the hinge of all Scripture: without it, faith is vain and the dead are perished (1 Corinthians 15:17-18). Because Christ is risen, the resurrection of the body at the last day is certain for all who are in him.
