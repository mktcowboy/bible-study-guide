# Angels

## Overview
Angels are the created, spiritual messengers and servants of the LORD who appear throughout Scripture to announce, protect, judge, and worship — functioning as the living link between the heavenly throne and the human stage of redemptive history.

## Canonical Development
In the Old Testament angels appear at decisive moments: the [[Angel of the LORD at Bochim]] reproves Israel; the angels who visit Abraham hasten the [[Destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah]]; [[Jacob Wrestles with God]] with a divine figure whose identity merges angel and God; the burning bush and the Sinai encounter are mediated by the angel of the LORD (Exod. 3:2; Acts 7:38). Isaiah's vision fills the temple with seraphim crying "Holy, holy, holy" (Isa. 6:2–3), and Daniel receives Gabriel's interpretation of visions and sees Michael as the prince who fights on Israel's behalf (Dan. 8:16; 10:13). The Psalms picture angels as God's mighty agents who "encamp round about them that fear him" (Ps. 34:7). In the New Testament the angelic activity intensifies around the Incarnation: Gabriel announces the [[Birth of John the Baptist]] to Zechariah and the [[Annunciation]] to Mary; angels sing at Bethlehem and minister to Jesus after the temptation. After the Resurrection an angel rolls away the stone and announces "He is risen." [[Peter's Release from Prison]] comes through an angel's hand (Acts 12:7–10), and [[Cornelius's Vision]] is initiated by an angelic messenger. Hebrews frames Christ's supremacy by placing him above all angels (Heb. 1:4–14), and Revelation unveils the angelic hosts as the executors of God's final judgments.

## Notes
The consistent biblical pattern is that angels never draw worship to themselves — when John falls to worship an angel in Revelation the angel refuses: "See thou do it not... worship God" (Rev. 22:9). Their role is entirely derivative and ministerial, pointing always toward the LORD they serve and the purposes of the redemptive plan they execute.
