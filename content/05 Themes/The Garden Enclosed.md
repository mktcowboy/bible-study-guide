# The Garden Enclosed

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Song of Solomon]]
- **Chapters:** Song of Solomon 4:12–5:1
- **Summary:** The groom praises his bride as "a garden enclosed" — a sealed spring, a fountain of gardens — and invites the north and south winds to carry her fragrance abroad. She opens the garden to him; he enters and feasts. The passage uses Eden-garden imagery to portray exclusive marital intimacy as the recovery of paradise.

## Details

Song of Solomon 4:12–5:1 is the poem's pivot — the moment of consummation rendered in the language of garden and fragrance, of invitation and entry. The groom has been praising his bride's body in vivid detail throughout chapter 4 (the *wasf*, or physical-praise poem, of 4:1–11), and he arrives at the central metaphor: "A garden enclosed is my sister, my spouse; a spring shut up, a fountain sealed" (4:12). Three parallel images — enclosed garden, shut spring, sealed fountain — all speak of privacy, exclusivity, and the fact that this intimacy has not been opened to others. In the ancient Near East, enclosed gardens were royal spaces of pleasure and privacy; sealed springs were prized and protected. The bride is all of these: a space of delight that belongs only to her beloved.

The variety of the garden (4:13–14) expands into a catalogue of spices and fragrant plants — pomegranates, spikenard, saffron, calamus, cinnamon, myrrh, aloes — that together suggest abundance, richness, and the concentrated pleasures of the created world made available within the garden of this relationship. The garden contains what the whole world holds most beautiful, gathered and enclosed for the beloved alone.

The bride's response in 4:16 is her invitation — one of the most striking moments in the poem for its directness and agency: "Awake, O north wind; and come, thou south; blow upon my garden, that the spices thereof may flow out. Let my beloved come into his garden, and eat his pleasant fruits." She calls upon the great forces of creation to carry her fragrance outward, and she opens herself — "his garden," she says, already transferring possession — to his coming. The garden is hers, but she gives it to him. The mutual gift of the self that the whole poem has been building toward arrives here with simplicity and completeness.

The groom accepts the invitation immediately (5:1): "I am come into my garden, my sister, my spouse: I have gathered my myrrh with my spice; I have eaten my honeycomb with my honey; I have drunk my wine with my milk." He takes what she has given, in language of feasting and fullness. And then comes the only third-person voice in the poem — most likely the Daughters of Jerusalem (the chorus), or perhaps God himself — "Eat, O friends; drink, yea, drink abundantly, O beloved." Whatever this voice is, it blesses the consummation. The intimacy is affirmed, not merely permitted.

The theological dimension is unmistakable. The garden imagery is deliberately Edenic — the Song returns the reader to Genesis 2, to the garden where man and woman were naked and unashamed (Genesis 2:25), where there was no barrier between embodied persons in their intimacy. The "enclosed garden" is the recovery of that pre-Fall space, available again within the covenant of marriage. The spices and fragrant abundance recall the garden of Eden's richness; the free giving of the self to the beloved recalls the wholeness of the pre-Fall union.

The eschatological resonance reaches forward as well. Revelation's New Jerusalem contains no enclosed garden but rather an open garden city, with the river of life and the tree of life in the midst of the street (Revelation 22:1–2) — the private intimacy of the enclosed garden of the Song becomes the public, abundant life of the new creation. What was sealed and private in the old world will be opened and shared in the new.
