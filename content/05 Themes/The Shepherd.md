# The Shepherd

## Overview
The shepherd image runs from the patriarchs and Moses through the Davidic covenant to Jesus the Good Shepherd, revealing God as the one who seeks, protects, and leads His flock with both authority and sacrificial love.

## Canonical Development
The patriarchs and Moses were literal shepherds before God called them to shepherd His people, establishing a pattern in which humble pastoral labour prefigures providential leadership. The psalms crystallised this image in the declaration "The LORD is my shepherd" (Psalm 23:1), and David the shepherd-king became the type to which all later rulers were measured; Ezekiel 34 indicts Israel's false shepherds and promises that God himself will search out and shepherd His scattered flock. Jesus fulfilled both strands when He declared "I am the good shepherd: the good shepherd giveth his life for the sheep" (John 10:11), and the apostles continued the imagery by calling Jesus the "great shepherd of the sheep" (Hebrews 13:20) and the "chief Shepherd" who shall reward faithful under-shepherds (1 Peter 5:4). The theme reaches its consummation in Revelation, where the Lamb who is in the midst of the throne "shall feed them, and shall lead them unto living fountains of waters" (Revelation 7:17).

## Notes
The shepherd theme unites divine providence and redemptive sacrifice, showing that the same God who guided Israel through the wilderness as a flock is the Lamb who was slain and yet reigns as the eternal shepherd of all nations.
