# Love Is Strong as Death

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Song of Solomon]]
- **Chapters:** Song of Solomon 8:6-7
- **Summary:** The Song's climactic declaration — "love is strong as death; jealousy is cruel as the grave...many waters cannot quench love, neither can the floods drown it" — serves as the book's thesis statement, measuring love against the ultimate enemy of life and finding that love matches it, a claim the resurrection will vindicate in history.

## Details

Song of Solomon 8:6-7 is among the most theologically dense passages in the entire Bible, compressed into two verses that carry the weight of the whole book and reach forward into the New Testament's central affirmation. The passage begins with a request: "Set me as a seal upon thine heart, as a seal upon thine arm" (8:6a). The seal in the ancient world was the object most personally identified with a person — pressed into wax or clay, it carried the authority and identity of its owner. To wear someone as a seal upon the heart is to be indelibly identified with them, to carry them in the place of deepest selfhood and highest visibility. The beloved asks to be permanently inscribed upon the lover — not merely remembered, not merely cherished in private, but publicly and personally bound.

Then comes the declaration that is the Song's thesis: "for love is strong as death; jealousy is cruel as the grave: the coals thereof are coals of fire, which hath a most vehement flame" (8:6b). The comparison is not hyperbole for its own sake. Death in the biblical worldview is the great separator, the force that breaks every human bond, that reaches into every house, that takes what no power can restore. Love is measured against this — not against distance, not against difficulty, not against time, but against death itself, the ultimate enemy. And love matches it. The word translated "cruel" (or "unyielding," *qasha*) — applied to the grave — speaks of the grave's absolute refusal to release what it has taken. Love is equally unyielding, equally absolute in its grip. The "coals of fire" that are love's flame — the Hebrew adds *shalhevetyah*, which some scholars parse as "the flame of the LORD" or "a most vehement flame of God" — suggest that the fire of this love is not merely human passion but something with a divine source, the same fire that burns at the center of God's own being.

The second verse extends the metaphor into cosmic scale: "Many waters cannot quench love, neither can the floods drown it" (8:7a). Water is the primordial chaos in the Hebrew imagination, the deep that existed before creation, the flood that threatened to undo the world, the sea that is the emblem of all that overwhelms and destroys. Many waters cannot quench this love. Every flood that has ever come — personal catastrophe, historical disaster, the flood of death itself — cannot put out the flame. This is not optimistic sentiment; it is a claim about the nature of love when it is rightly ordered, when it participates in the divine love that underlies and generates all human love. The following verse completes the thought: "if a man would give all the substance of his house for love, it would utterly be contemned" (8:7b). Love cannot be purchased because it is not a commodity; it can only be given freely, and its value exceeds any price that could be named.

The theological resonance of this passage with the New Testament is unmistakable. Paul's declaration in Romans 8:38-39 that "neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers, nor things present, nor things to come, nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord" is the New Testament exposition of Song of Solomon 8:6-7. Paul is not merely echoing the sentiment; he is making the same claim about the same love, now historically grounded in the death and resurrection of Christ. The love that the Song declares to be as strong as death has, in Christ, gone into death and come out the other side still burning. The resurrection is the proof of the Song's thesis — love did not merely match death but overcame it, and the love that is stronger than death is now available to every person who is united to the one who rose.

In the *hesed* tradition running through Hosea, Ruth, and the Psalms, this love has always been God's covenant love pressing into the darkness of human unfaithfulness and historical catastrophe without being extinguished. The Song of Solomon, read in this tradition, is not finally about Solomon and the Shulammite alone. It is about a love that has been burning from before the creation of the world, that has survived every flood of human sin and historical disaster, that went into the deepest darkness of death on a Friday afternoon, and that proved on the third day that many waters cannot quench it.
