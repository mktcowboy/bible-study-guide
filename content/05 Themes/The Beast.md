# The Beast

> One of Satan's chief agents. The Beast is the political-religious embodiment of God-opposing power, empowered by [[The Dragon|the dragon]] — that is, [[Satan (the Accuser)]] — to wage war on the saints. See also his fellow agents [[The Man of Sin]] and [[The Antichrists]].

## Overview
The Beast is the great end-times persecutor of [[Revelation]] — the political and religious embodiment of rebellion against God, empowered by the dragon to demand worship, blaspheme God, and make war on the saints. The image gathers up the Old Testament's "beastly" empires and concentrates them into a single climactic figure who is finally destroyed at the return of Christ.

## Old Testament Roots
The imagery begins in [[Daniel]], where four great beasts rise from the sea as a vision of successive world empires (Daniel 7:3). The fourth is "dreadful and terrible," with ten horns and a boastful "little horn" with "a mouth speaking great things" (Daniel 7:7–8) that "made war with the saints, and prevailed against them" and "speak[s] great words against the most High" (Daniel 7:21, 25). Behind Daniel's beasts stand the real God-defying powers of Scripture — [[Egypt]], [[Babylon]] under [[Nebuchadnezzar]], Persia, Greece, and [[Rome]] — each a foreshadowing of the final beast, and all heirs of the rebellion begun at the [[Tower of Babel]]. See [[The Ancient of Days]], whose heavenly court strips the little horn of its dominion.

## The Beast in Revelation
John sees a beast rise from the sea with seven heads and ten horns, "and upon his heads the name of blasphemy" (Revelation 13:1), combining the lion, bear, and leopard of Daniel into one. "And the dragon gave him his power, and his seat, and great authority" (Revelation 13:2). One of its heads has a mortal wound that is healed, and the world wonders after it, asking, "Who is like unto the beast? who is able to make war with him?" (13:4) — a blasphemous parody of the worship due to God. It is given authority for "forty and two months," to blaspheme God, and "to make war with the saints, and to overcome them" (13:5–7).

## The Second Beast and the Mark
A second beast rises from the earth, with "two horns like a lamb," but he "spake as a dragon" (Revelation 13:11) — later called the false prophet. He works lying signs, compels the world to worship the first beast, and enforces its mark, "the number of a man... Six hundred threescore and six" (13:18). The mark on the hand and forehead is a deliberate counterfeit of the seal of God on His servants.

## The Beast and Babylon
In Revelation 17 the beast carries the harlot [[Babylon]], the great city drunk with the blood of the saints; its seven heads and ten horns are interpreted as kings and kingdoms. The beast and its allies finally turn on the harlot, and then gather "to make war with the Lamb, and the Lamb shall overcome them: for he is Lord of lords, and King of kings" (Revelation 17:14).

## Counterfeit of Christ
The Beast is an anti-christ in the deepest sense — a parody of the real thing: a slain-and-revived head mimicking the resurrection, a mark counterfeiting the seal, demanded worship counterfeiting true worship, and a dragon "father" counterfeiting the Father. It offers the world a false lord. See [[The Word]] and [[The Resurrection]] for the realities it imitates, and [[The Church]] for the people it persecutes.

## Defeat
The Beast's reign is permitted but brief. At the return of Christ, "the beast was taken, and with him the false prophet... These both were cast alive into a lake of fire burning with brimstone" (Revelation 19:20). See [[Judgment]], [[The Day of the LORD]], [[The Second Coming]].

## Satan and His Agents
- [[Satan (the Accuser)]] / [[The Dragon]] — gives the beast its power, throne, and authority
- [[The Man of Sin]] — Paul's parallel lawless one, destroyed by Christ's coming
- [[The Antichrists]] — the present, many-membered spirit of the same rebellion

## Interpretive Views
- **Preterist** — the beast is first-century [[Rome]] and its emperor cult (e.g., Nero).
- **Historicist** — a succession of God-opposing powers across church history.
- **Futurist** — a final personal Antichrist and his world system.
- **Idealist** — the recurring pattern of idolatrous, persecuting empire in every age.

The text's deliberate fusion of personal and political imagery supports reading the beast as a power that takes concrete form in history and comes to a final head.

## Notes
The Beast is best understood not as an isolated villain but as the recurring biblical pattern of God-opposing empire brought to its final head: Babel, Egypt, Babylon, and Rome all prefigure it. Its mark, its demand for worship, and its blasphemous claims parody the seal of God and the worship due to Christ — counterfeit lordship that ends in the lake of fire. Interpreters divide over whether the beast is primarily a future individual, a system of imperial idolatry, or both; the text's fusion of personal and political imagery supports reading it as a power that takes concrete form in history.
