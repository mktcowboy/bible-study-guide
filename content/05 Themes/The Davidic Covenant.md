# The Davidic Covenant

## Overview
In 2 Samuel 7 God makes an unconditional promise to David that one of his sons will reign on an eternal throne — a promise that the Old Testament could not fully contain and the New Testament identifies as fulfilled in Jesus of Nazareth, the son of David who sits at the right hand of the Father.

## Canonical Development
The covenant originates in the divine oracle delivered through Nathan: "I will establish the throne of his kingdom for ever" (2 Samuel 7:13), and David's dynastic seed will be God's own son (7:14). The psalms meditate on these promises with growing messianic intensity — Psalm 2 hails the anointed king as God's son who inherits the nations, Psalm 89 rehearses the covenant's unconditional character even in the face of dynastic failure, and Psalm 110 elevates David's lord to a royal-priestly session at God's right hand. Isaiah 9:6-7 sees a child born to the house of David "upon whose shoulders the government shall be" and "of the increase of his government and peace there shall be no end." The New Testament opens by anchoring Jesus in this lineage ("the son of David," Matthew 1:1), and Gabriel tells Mary that "the Lord God shall give unto him the throne of his father David: and he shall reign over the house of Jacob for ever" (Luke 1:32-33). Acts 2:30-31 reads David's psalms as explicit prophecy of Christ's resurrection and enthronement. Revelation 5:5 crowns the fulfillment: Jesus is "the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David."

## Notes
The Davidic Covenant is the thread that holds together the OT monarchy and the NT proclamation: Israel's greatest king was not the fulfillment but the pointer. Every failed Davidic king — from Solomon's apostasy to the Babylonian exile — drove the hope further forward until it landed in the one David called his Lord. See also [[The Covenant]] for the broader covenantal framework within which the Davidic promise operates.
