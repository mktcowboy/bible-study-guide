# Judgment

## Overview
Judgment is not God's last resort but his essential attribute in action: because he is holy and just, he cannot permit wickedness to go unanswered. Scripture traces divine judgment from the first catastrophic intervention at the Flood through the prophetic warnings of a coming Day of Reckoning to its ultimate expression at the Great White Throne in Revelation 20.

## Canonical Development
The Flood established the archetype: universal human corruption meets universal divine judgment, with a remnant preserved through the one found righteous (Genesis 6–8). Babel scattered the nations when they united in rebellion (Genesis 11), and the ten plagues on Egypt displayed God's judgment on a pagan empire that oppressed his people (Exodus 7–12). The Old Testament prophets — Isaiah, Amos, Zephaniah, and others — universalized the pattern into the concept of the "Day of the LORD," a coming intervention in which both Israel and the nations would answer to God. In the New Testament Jesus himself becomes the appointed judge: "the Father judgeth no man, but hath committed all judgment unto the Son" (John 5:22), and in Matthew 25 he separates the sheep from the goats with eternal consequence. Paul insists that "we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ" (2 Corinthians 5:10), and Revelation brings history to its close with the Great White Throne from which none can flee (Revelation 20:11-15).

## Notes
Judgment in Scripture is never arbitrary wrath but the necessary consequence of God's character confronting human sin. The cross itself is the supreme act of divine judgment — borne by Christ in the place of his people — which is why Paul can declare "there is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus" (Romans 8:1). See also [[The Day of the LORD]] for the concentrated prophetic vision of God's day of reckoning.
