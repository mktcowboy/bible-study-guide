# The Covenant

## Overview
The covenant is the single most important organizing concept in Scripture. From Genesis to Revelation, God relates to his people not through bare command or abstract principle but through a series of self-binding commitments — solemn, oath-sealed agreements in which he pledges himself to a people and a purpose. Five major covenants structure the biblical narrative: the Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic (Sinai), Davidic, and New Covenant. Each covenant builds upon the last, narrowing in focus from all humanity to one family to one nation to one king to one man — Jesus Christ — in whom every covenant promise finds its Yes and Amen (2 Corinthians 1:20). To understand the covenants is to understand the architecture of salvation history.

## Theological Significance
The covenants reveal that God's relationship with humanity is not transactional but relational and progressive. Each covenant adds a layer of specificity to the central promise first whispered in Genesis 3:15 — that the seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head. The Noahic covenant secures the stage on which redemption will unfold; the Abrahamic covenant identifies the family line through which the Redeemer will come; the Mosaic covenant reveals the moral standard that makes a Redeemer necessary; the Davidic covenant identifies the royal office the Redeemer will hold; the New Covenant announces the terms on which the Redeemer's work will be applied to sinners.

A critical distinction runs through the covenant structure: unconditional versus conditional. The Mosaic covenant is bilateral — Israel's blessing depends on her obedience. The Abrahamic and Davidic covenants are unilateral — God swears by himself that he will accomplish what he promises, regardless of human failure. When Israel breaks the Mosaic covenant (repeatedly), the unconditional covenants do not collapse; they guarantee that God will find another way. That other way is the New Covenant, in which God supplies what he demands — the obedience Israel could not offer is provided by the perfectly obedient covenant mediator, Jesus Christ.

The [[Justification by Faith (Theme)]] theme flows directly from this structure. Paul's argument in Galatians and Romans is that the Abrahamic covenant came first (430 years before the law), is unconditional, and rests on faith — so the Mosaic law cannot overturn it. The New Covenant, in Hebrews' logic, renders the old Mosaic covenant "obsolete" (Hebrews 8:13), not because God has changed his mind, but because the shadow has given way to the reality it always pointed toward. Every covenant is ultimately a covenant of grace, administered differently across epochs but anchored in the same God who binds himself by oath to save.

The Lord's Supper functions as the covenant renewal ceremony of the New Covenant, just as circumcision was the sign of the Abrahamic and the Sabbath the sign of the Mosaic. When Jesus takes the cup and says "This is my blood of the covenant" (Matthew 26:28; Luke 22:20), he is inaugurating the new covenant Jeremiah promised — sealed not with the blood of animals but with his own.
