# Sacrifice and Atonement

## Overview
The red thread of blood runs from Genesis to Revelation. Sacrifice — the offering of something of value in the place of something else — is the Bible's primary vocabulary for what God does about sin. The concept is not peripheral; it is the mechanism through which the covenant relationship between God and humanity is maintained and restored. Every major sacrifice in the OT points forward to the once-for-all offering of Christ; every NT reference to the Cross reaches back to interpret it through the accumulated weight of the sacrificial system.

## Theological Significance
The logic of sacrifice runs through the entire Bible with one central argument: sin has a cost, and God provides the means to pay it. The Levitical system was not arbitrary religious ritual — it was a theological education, training Israel's conscience to understand that an offense against the holy God requires a corresponding price. The blood of animals could not actually remove sin (Hebrews 10:4: "it is not possible that the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins") — but it pointed to the One whose blood could.

The substitutionary principle appears first in Genesis 22:13, when God provides a ram in place of Isaac. The ram stands in the place of the son. This pattern reaches its ultimate expression at Calvary, where God provides himself as the sacrifice: "He that spared not his own Son, but delivered him up for us all" (Romans 8:32). The Passover pattern adds the corporate dimension — it is not just one life for one life, but one death that covers a family, then a nation, then ultimately the world. Paul calls Christ "our passover" (1 Corinthians 5:7).

The book of Hebrews draws all the threads together with precision: the old system was a shadow; Christ is the substance. The tabernacle was a copy of the heavenly reality. The Aaronic priests entered the Most Holy Place annually with other people's blood; Christ entered once, with his own blood, achieving eternal redemption. The once-for-all nature of Christ's sacrifice is Hebrews' great insistence — "by one offering he hath perfected for ever them that are sanctified" (10:14).

Revelation's vision of the Lamb standing in the throne room "as it had been slain" (5:6) completes the picture: the sacrifice that accomplished redemption is eternally present before the throne of God. The marks of the cross are not erased in glory but become the basis of all eternal praise: "Worthy is the Lamb that was slain to receive power, and riches, and wisdom, and strength, and honour, and glory, and blessing" (5:12).
