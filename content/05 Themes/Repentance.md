# Repentance

## Overview
Repentance is the God-ordained turning of the human heart away from sin and back toward the LORD — encompassing both outward change of conduct and inward transformation of will. It is among the most persistent demands of Scripture, from the wilderness wanderings to the apostolic kerygma.

## Canonical Development
In the Old Testament the call to repentance is carried by the Hebrew word *shub* ("turn, return"), sounded incessantly by the prophets: "Turn ye, turn ye from your evil ways; for why will ye die?" (Ezek. 33:11). Joel crystallizes it: "rend your heart, and not your garments, and turn unto the LORD your God" (Joel 2:13). [[Nineveh's Repentance]] stands as the supreme OT exemplar — an entire city from king to cattle turns at a prophet's cry. John the Baptist opens the New Testament era demanding "a baptism of repentance for the remission of sins" (Luke 3:3), and Jesus takes up the same note from his first public word: "Repent ye, and believe the gospel" (Mark 1:15). [[The Prodigal Son]] parable makes repentance pictorial — "he came to himself" and returned to the father — while Peter's Pentecost sermon makes it sacramental: "Repent, and be baptized every one of you" (Acts 2:38). Paul's second letter to Corinth adds the inward dimension: "godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of" (2 Cor. 7:10).

## Notes
Repentance in Scripture is never mere remorse but a comprehensive reorientation — intellectual (knowing the error), emotional (grieving it), and volitional (turning from it). The NT term *metanoia* ("change of mind") and the OT *shub* ("turn/return") together capture both the inward renewal and the outward change of direction that genuine repentance requires.
