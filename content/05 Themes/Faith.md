# Faith

## Overview
Faith is the foundational posture of the redeemed — trust in God's word and promises that is reckoned as righteousness. The canon traces a single thread from Abraham through the prophets to Christ, culminating in the apostolic definition that "faith without works is dead."

## Canonical Development
The theme begins in Genesis 15:6 when Abraham "believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness" — the prototype of saving faith that Paul later calls the pattern for all who believe (Romans 4:3; Galatians 3:6). Habakkuk 2:4 distills the principle for the prophetic era: "the just shall live by his faith," a verse Paul cites in both Romans 1:17 and Galatians 3:11 to establish that justification has always been by faith, not law. Hebrews 11 assembles the great Hall of Faith, demonstrating that every major figure from Abel to the Maccabean martyrs acted on things unseen, "all these having obtained a good report through faith" (Hebrews 11:39). James completes the picture not by contradicting Paul but by insisting that genuine faith necessarily produces works — "faith without works is dead" (James 2:26) — pressing against a presumptuous faith that remains inert.

## Notes
This note complements [[Justification by Faith (Theme)]], which focuses on the forensic and Pauline dimensions; the present theme traces the broader canon-wide portrait of faith itself as trust, obedience, and endurance. Together they show that faith is both the ground of right standing before God and the animating principle of the covenant life from Abraham onward.
