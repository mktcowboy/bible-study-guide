# The Excellent Wife

## Overview
The closing poem of the book of Proverbs (31:10-31) is a Hebrew acrostic — each of its 22 verses begins with a successive letter of the Hebrew alphabet — portraying a woman of noble character whose worth surpasses rubies. Known by its Hebrew opening as the Eshet Chayil ("woman of valor" or "excellent wife"), this portrait closes the entire book of Proverbs as its crowning practical illustration of wisdom embodied in daily life. The poem is both a celebration of a remarkable woman and a concrete picture of what personified Wisdom looks like when she takes flesh in household, marketplace, and community.

## Theological Significance
The Eshet Chayil poem is the culmination of the book of Proverbs and should be read in light of the entire book's framework. Proverbs opens with the call to pursue wisdom and fear the LORD; it closes with this portrait of a woman in whom wisdom and the fear of the LORD have become flesh in the rhythms of daily life. The poem is not primarily a domestic instruction list but a theological statement: this is what wisdom looks like when embodied in a human being. Her household management, her commerce, her generosity, and her dignity are not independent virtues but expressions of a unified character rooted in the fear of God.

The scope of her activity is remarkable by any standard. She rises before dawn to provide for her household (v. 15), considers a field and buys it from her own earnings (v. 16), girds herself with strength and makes her arms strong (v. 17), extends charity to the poor and needy (v. 20), makes fine linen and sells it (v. 24), speaks with wisdom and teaches kindness (v. 26). She is simultaneously a household manager, entrepreneur, employer, philanthropist, and educator. The poem does not confine her to domestic invisibility but celebrates her public competence and fruitfulness. Yet it frames all of this with the key line: "Favour is deceitful, and beauty is vain: but a woman that feareth the LORD, she shall be praised" (v. 30). Her fruitfulness flows not from natural gifts alone but from her orientation toward God.

Scholars have long noted the literary connection between the excellent wife of Proverbs 31 and Wisdom personified in Proverbs 8-9. Both are presented as desirable beyond price; both are active in the world with productive energy; both build households; both are praised at the city gates. The excellent wife is, in a real sense, Wisdom's human portrait — what it looks like when a person lives the life that Wisdom's invitation promised. This connection runs both directions: the young man urged throughout Proverbs to pursue wisdom, to love her and not forsake her, finds in the Eshet Chayil a picture of what that wisdom produces. The book that began with "fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" ends with a woman whose entire life demonstrates why this is so.
