# The Blood

## Overview
Blood is the covenant currency of Scripture — the price of sin, the mark of deliverance, and the seal of every binding agreement between God and his people. From the first Passover lamb to the Lamb slain before the foundation of the world, the shedding of blood is God's appointed means of redemption.

## Canonical Development
The Passover in Egypt established the pattern: a spotless lamb is slain, its blood applied to the doorposts, and death passes over the household sheltered beneath it (Exodus 12). The Levitical system expanded this grammar into a full sacrificial vocabulary culminating in the Day of Atonement, when the high priest sprinkled blood on the mercy seat to cover the sins of the entire nation (Leviticus 16). The prophetic word condensed the logic into a single principle — "without shedding of blood is no remission" (Hebrews 9:22) — and Christ ratified the new covenant at the Last Supper with the declaration "this is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" (Matthew 26:28). Revelation brings the theme to its eternal conclusion: the overcomers conquer "by the blood of the Lamb" (Revelation 12:11), and the Lamb bears his wounds before the throne of God forever.

## Notes
The theme of blood unifies the entire biblical narrative under one question: how can a holy God dwell among sinful people? Every blood rite in the Old Testament was a shadow of the answer; Christ's cross is the substance. See also [[Sacrifice and Atonement]] for the broader sacrificial system of which the blood motif is the central element.
