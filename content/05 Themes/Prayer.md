# Prayer

## Overview
Prayer in Scripture is not a technique for obtaining results but the natural breath of covenant relationship — the creature speaking to the Creator who has first spoken to them. From Abraham's bold intercession for Sodom to Jesus's agonized petition in Gethsemane, the Bible presents prayer as honest, persistent, sometimes anguished, and always oriented toward a God who genuinely hears. The full range of prayer is on display across the canon: petition, lament, intercession, confession, praise, thanksgiving, and silence. What unites every expression is the conviction that God is personal, attentive, and responsive — and that humans are invited, even commanded, to bring their whole selves before him.

## Theological Significance
The arc of biblical prayer reveals an escalating intimacy. In Genesis, humans hide from God after the Fall; by Abraham, they are bargaining with him; by Moses, they are laying down their lives for others before him; by David (in the Psalms), they are pouring out every emotion — rage, despair, exultation — directly to him. The Psalter, as Israel's prayer book, gives language to the full spectrum of human experience before God. Lament is not a failure of faith but one of its most honest expressions: you only cry out to God if you believe he is there and that it matters what he does. [[Psalm 22 - My God My God]] opens with abandonment and closes with praise — the same movement that will structure the Passion and Resurrection of Jesus.

Moses and Nehemiah bracket the Old Testament as models of intercessory and missional prayer. Moses's willingness to be "blotted out" if Israel is not forgiven (Exodus 32:32) foreshadows Paul's similar anguish over Israel in Romans 9:3, and ultimately points to the one Mediator who actually was "made to be sin" for his people (2 Corinthians 5:21). [[Nehemiah's Prayer]] introduces a pattern — prayer as preparation for action — that recurs throughout Scripture: Daniel prays before interpreting dreams; Paul prays before missionary journeys; Jesus prays before every major decision.

The Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13) is the kingdom's posture in miniature: it begins with God ("hallowed be your name"), passes through the world's need ("your kingdom come"), and arrives at human necessity ("give us today our daily bread") only after establishing the right orientation. It is corporate ("our Father"), eschatological ("your kingdom come"), and penitential ("forgive us our debts") — correcting every tendency to make prayer private, timeless, or self-righteous. Gethsemane is the Lord's Prayer prayed in its most extreme form: "your will be done" when the will of God is the cross.

John 17 — the high priestly prayer — stands as the greatest recorded prayer in Scripture. Here Jesus intercedes for his disciples across time ("not for these only, but also for those who will believe in me through their word," John 17:20), uniting his intercession with his imminent self-offering. The book of Hebrews later draws the implication: he "always lives to make intercession" for his people (Hebrews 7:25). Prayer, for the Christian, is participation in a conversation already in progress — the ongoing intercession of the Son before the Father, in which the Spirit also intercedes "with groanings too deep for words" (Romans 8:26).
