# I Am My Beloved's

## Overview
- **Testament:** Old
- **Book:** [[Song of Solomon]]
- **Chapters:** Song of Solomon 2:16; 6:3; 7:10
- **Summary:** The threefold declaration of mutual belonging — "I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine" — occurs at 2:16, 6:3, and 7:10, shifting slightly with each appearance. Together they form the poem's spine, tracing a movement from mutual possession toward the man's desire for and the woman's confident belonging in him. This refrain is the Song's summary of covenant love.

## Details

Song of Solomon's most famous refrain appears three times across the poem, and each appearance differs from the others in ways that carry theological weight. The small shifts across the three occurrences trace a spiritual progression that many readers across history have identified as the movement of a maturing covenant relationship.

**The first occurrence (2:16):** "My beloved is mine, and I am his: he feedeth among the lilies." The order is significant — she begins with what he is to her ("my beloved is mine") before affirming what she is to him ("and I am his"). This is the voice of desire, of fresh love that first reaches toward the object of affection and then discovers the reciprocity. The appended image — "he feedeth among the lilies" — places him in a pastoral scene of beauty and peace; he is present, gentle, and wholly absorbed in his own world, which includes her.

**The second occurrence (6:3):** "I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine: he feedeth among the lilies." The order reverses — now she begins with herself ("I am my beloved's") before naming what he is to her ("and my beloved is mine"). The priority has shifted from possession to surrender, from "he is mine" to "I am his." This is the voice of mature love: the security to give oneself first, before counting what is received. The image remains the same — he feedeth among the lilies — but the order in which she approaches the relationship has changed.

**The third occurrence (7:10):** "I am my beloved's, and his desire is toward me." Here the formula transforms. The second half — "my beloved is mine" — drops entirely, replaced by something richer: "his desire is toward me." She no longer names her claim on him; she names his desire for her. This is the voice of fully secure love: she needs no assertion of ownership because she rests in his desire. The word for "desire" (*teshuqah*) appears only three times in the Hebrew Bible — Genesis 3:16 (where the woman's desire is for her husband after the Fall), Genesis 4:7 (where sin crouches desiring Cain), and here. The Song deliberately echoes the Fall's language of disordered desire and reverses it: what was broken in Eden is restored in the covenant love of the Beloved and the Bride.

The theological movement traces the way covenant love matures: from grasping to giving, from asserting claim to resting in being desired. This is not merely romantic progression; it maps onto the biblical pattern of covenant relationship with God — the soul that begins by reaching for God ("my beloved is mine") is led into surrender ("I am my beloved's") and arrives at rest in God's prior desire for the soul ("his desire is toward me"). The Song's refrain becomes, in the allegorical reading, a map of the soul's journey into God.

## Cross-References
- **Genesis 3:16** — "Thy desire shall be to thy husband" — the Fall's disordering of desire; Song 7:10 reverses the curse by placing *his* desire toward her, suggesting the restoration of Eden's equality
- **Hosea 2:16–20** — "And it shall be at that day, saith the LORD, that thou shalt call me Ishi [my husband]" — the covenant re-betrothal of Israel using the same language of belonging
- **Ephesians 5:25–33** — "Husbands, love your wives, as Christ also loved the church" — the NT exposition of the mutual belonging the Song enacts, now grounded in Christ's self-giving

## Sequential Connections
- **2:16** — Follows the spring-love scene (2:10–15); precedes the threefold warning "Do not awaken love" (2:7)
- **6:3** — Follows the reunion after the bride's night search and her description of her beloved (5:2–6:2)
- **7:10** — Follows the groom's extended praise of the bride (7:1–9); precedes the bride's invitation to go to the vineyards (7:11–13)
