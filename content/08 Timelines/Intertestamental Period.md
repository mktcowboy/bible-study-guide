# Intertestamental Period

## Overview
The intertestamental period is the roughly four-century span between the close of the Old Testament prophetic era and the opening of the New Testament.

## Timeline
| Era | Approx. Dates | Orientation |
|-----|---------------|-------------|
| Persian period | 539-332 BC | Judah lives as a restored but politically dependent community after the return from exile. |
| Greek conquest | 332-323 BC | Alexander the Great conquers the Persian Empire, spreading Greek language and culture. |
| Hellenistic kingdoms | 323-167 BC | Judea is pulled between Ptolemaic Egypt and Seleucid Syria. |
| Maccabean revolt | 167-160 BC | Jewish resistance rises against Seleucid oppression and temple desecration. |
| Hasmonean rule | 140-63 BC | A Jewish dynasty controls Judea, joining priestly and political power. |
| Roman period begins | 63 BC onward | Rome takes control; Herodian rule sets the stage for the Gospels. |

## Biblical Connections
- [[Daniel]] - Visions of successive kingdoms frame the period theologically.
- [[Malachi]] - The final prophetic book anticipates the messenger and the day of the LORD.
- [[Herod the Great]] - New Testament Judea begins under Herodian power.
- [[Rome]] - Imperial setting of the New Testament world.
- [[Birth of Jesus]] - The New Testament opens after this period of waiting.

## Historical Significance
This period explains much of the New Testament setting: synagogues, scribes, Pharisees, Sadducees, Greek as a common language, Roman roads, Herodian politics, and heightened expectation for deliverance.

## Notes
The Bible does not narrate this era directly in the Protestant canon, but its historical developments form the world into which Jesus is born.
