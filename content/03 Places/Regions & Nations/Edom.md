# Edom

## Overview
- **Type:** Nation / Region
- **Testament:** Both
- **Modern Location:** Southern Jordan and the Negev desert; roughly the area around Petra

## Biblical Significance
Edom was the nation descended from Esau, Jacob's twin brother, and occupied the mountainous terrain south and southeast of the Dead Sea, stretching down toward the Gulf of Aqaba. The Edomites established a sophisticated kingdom in this rugged terrain, with their capital at Bozrah (later the region included the rock-cut city of Petra). The land was characterized by reddish sandstone mountains, which some scholars connect to Esau's red complexion and the name Edom meaning "red." From the very beginning, the tension between Esau and Jacob became the pattern for centuries of conflict between Edom and Israel.

The most defining episode in Edom's early relations with Israel came during the Exodus, when Israel requested permission to pass through Edomite territory on their way to Canaan. Edom refused, forcing Israel to take a longer route around the country. Despite this hostility, the Law commanded Israel not to "abhor an Edomite, for he is your brother" (Deuteronomy 23:7), acknowledging the kinship between the two peoples. In the period of the monarchy, Saul and David both fought against Edom, and Solomon controlled Edomite ports on the Red Sea, giving Israel access to maritime trade.

The prophets reserved some of their harshest oracles for Edom, particularly because of Edomite behavior during the Babylonian destruction of Jerusalem in 586 BC. Edom reportedly cheered the attackers, blocked escape routes for fleeing Judeans, and even participated in looting Jerusalem. The book of Obadiah is entirely devoted to judgment against Edom for this betrayal of brotherhood. Psalm 137 echoes this anguish. Ezekiel, Jeremiah, Amos, and Isaiah also contain oracles against Edom. The destruction of Edom became a symbol in prophetic literature of God's judgment on all nations that oppose his people, and "Edom" in later Jewish apocalyptic literature sometimes symbolized Rome.

## Notes
- The perpetual "sword" between Edom and Israel (Amos 1:11) reflects a theological pattern: the older-younger brother rivalry that begins with Esau and Jacob becomes a national and eschatological conflict
- Herod the Great, the Idumean (Edomite) king who ruled Judea at the time of Jesus' birth, represents the irony of an Edomite sitting on an Israelite throne
- The oracles against Edom in the prophets are among the most severe in the OT, perhaps because treachery against a brother was considered a greater sin than enmity from a stranger
