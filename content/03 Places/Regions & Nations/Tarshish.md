# Tarshish

## Overview
- **Type:** Distant port city / region
- **Testament:** Old
- **Modern Location:** Disputed — most likely Tartessus on the southwestern coast of Spain (near modern Cádiz or Huelva), though Sardinia, Carthage, and even Tarsus in Cilicia have been proposed

## Biblical Significance

Tarshish is the furthest named destination in the OT's geographical imagination — a distant port associated with trade in silver, iron, tin, and lead (Ezekiel 27:12), and with large ocean-going vessels called "ships of Tarshish" (1 Kings 10:22; Psalm 48:7; Isaiah 2:16). Its distance from Israel made it the natural symbol for the end of the known world. Solomon and Jehoshaphat both attempted to build fleets to reach Tarshish from the port of Ezion-Geber on the Red Sea (1 Kings 10:22; 22:48), the latter fleet being wrecked before it could sail. The ships of Tarshish in Psalm 48:7 are described as broken by the east wind, suggesting the symbolic weight of distant power destroyed before God.

In the book of [[Jonah]], Tarshish functions as the ultimate expression of flight from God's presence. God commissions [[Jonah]] to go northeast to Nineveh; Jonah goes to Joppa and boards a ship going to Tarshish — as far west as the ancient world extended (Jonah 1:3). The text repeats the destination with emphasis: Jonah goes "to flee unto Tarshish from the presence of the LORD" (1:3), and again the narrator underlines it — "to go with them unto Tarshish from the presence of the LORD." The repetition hammers the point: Tarshish is not merely a geographic destination but a theological one. It is wherever God is not, or wherever Jonah imagines God cannot follow. The subsequent storm demonstrates that the LORD who made "the sea and the dry land" (1:9) is sovereign over every route to Tarshish.

The identification of Tarshish with Tartessus in southwestern Spain has considerable ancient support. Herodotus mentions Tartessus as a wealthy trading kingdom at the western end of the Mediterranean. Tin and silver deposits in the Iberian Peninsula match the metals associated with Tarshish in Ezekiel. Phoenician trading networks — which operated the ships Jonah would have boarded at Joppa — reached as far as Spain by the 8th century BC. Isaiah 66:19 lists Tarshish among the distant nations to which God will send survivors to declare his glory, confirming its status as the western edge of the known world. The "ships of Tarshish" became a biblical idiom for powerful ocean-going vessels regardless of their actual destination (Isaiah 2:16; 23:1, 14; 60:9).
