# Achaia

## Overview
- **Type:** Roman province
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Southern and central Greece

## Biblical Significance
Achaia was the Roman province encompassing southern and central Greece, including the Peloponnese peninsula and the region around the Saronic and Corinthian Gulfs. It was reorganized as a separate province under Augustus and had Corinth as its capital after Caesar refounded Corinth as a Roman colony in 44 BC. The province included not only Corinth but also Athens, Cenchreae, and numerous smaller cities, making it one of the most culturally and intellectually significant regions in the empire. The proconsul Gallio, before whom Paul was brought in Corinth, was the Roman governor of Achaia, and his proconsulship has been dated by the Delphic inscription to approximately AD 51-52.

Paul ministered extensively in Achaia during his second and third missionary journeys. His extended 18-month stay in Corinth, his briefer ministry in Athens, and his visits to other communities in the region established a network of churches throughout southern Greece. Paul used the term "Achaia" collectively when discussing the churches he had founded in the region, distinguishing them from the Macedonian churches to the north. The generosity of the Achaian churches, along with the Macedonians, in contributing to the collection for Jerusalem believers is highlighted in Romans 15 and 2 Corinthians 8-9.

Achaia held symbolic importance as the heartland of Greek philosophical and cultural tradition. Athens — the city of Plato, Aristotle, and the Stoics and Epicureans — lay within its borders. Paul's encounter with Greek intellectual culture at the Areopagus, and his deliberate engagement with Athenian philosophy, took place in the heart of Achaia. The challenges the Corinthian church faced with Greek philosophical categories (wisdom, spiritual giftedness, bodily resurrection) reflect the broader cultural ethos of Achaia that Paul had to navigate throughout his ministry there.

## Notes
- Paul's letters to the Corinthians are addressed not just to Corinth but "to all the saints throughout Achaia" (2 Corinthians 1:1), indicating that the Corinthian congregation functioned as the regional hub for a broader network of Achaian believers
- The phrase "household of Stephanas" in 1 Corinthians 16:15 — called "the first converts in Achaia" — suggests that some of the very first Greek believers on European soil belonged to this family
- Achaia's proconsular status (rather than imperial province) meant its governor had more independence from direct imperial oversight — a fact relevant to Gallio's ability to dismiss the charges against Paul without imperial authorization
