# Kadesh-barnea

## Overview
- **Type:** Desert oasis / encampment
- **Testament:** Old
- **Modern Location:** Identified with Ein el-Qudeirat in the Sinai Peninsula near the border of modern Israel and Egypt

## Biblical Significance
Kadesh-barnea is the most significant stopping place in Israel's wilderness journey after [[Mount Sinai]]. It was the staging point for the spy mission into Canaan and the site of Israel's catastrophic decision to refuse to enter the promised land — a failure that condemned the first generation to die in the wilderness.

From Kadesh, twelve spies were sent to reconnoiter Canaan (Numbers 13). They returned after forty days with a divided report: ten spies declared the land was unconquerable because of its giant inhabitants; [[Joshua (Son of Nun)]] and [[Caleb]] urged immediate entry. The congregation sided with the ten, wept through the night, and proposed returning to Egypt. God's judgment was swift: the first generation (all those over 20 at the time) would not see the promised land — they would wander forty years, one year for each day of the spy mission, until the unbelieving generation died.

Kadesh also appears in Numbers 20, where [[Miriam]] died and was buried, and where [[Moses]] struck the rock twice in anger instead of speaking to it — an act that cost him his own entry into the land. From Kadesh, Moses sent messengers to the king of Edom requesting passage, which was denied.

## Notes
- The author of Hebrews cites Kadesh's rebellion (Psalm 95:7-11) as the defining warning against hardened hearts: "Today if ye will hear his voice, harden not your hearts."
- Kadesh encapsulates the tragic pattern of the wilderness generation: standing at the threshold of the promise and refusing it.
- Moses' sin at Kadesh (striking rather than speaking) illustrates that even the most faithful leader can forfeit something precious through a single moment of impatience and self-assertion.
