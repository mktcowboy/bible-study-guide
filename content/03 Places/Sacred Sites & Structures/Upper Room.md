# Upper Room

## Overview
- **Testament:** New
- **Region:** [[Jerusalem]], within the city walls; traditionally associated with the southwestern hill (Mount Zion)
- **Key Books:** [[Matthew]], [[Mark]], [[Luke]], [[John]], [[Acts]]

## Description

The Upper Room was a large furnished guest chamber in [[Jerusalem]] where [[Jesus Christ]] gathered with his disciples for the [[Last Supper]] on the night of his betrayal, and where the risen Christ appeared to his disciples, and where the early church prayed together before [[Pentecost and the Birth of the Church]]. It stands at the intersection of the passion narrative and the birth of the church, making it one of the most theologically laden locations in the New Testament.

The room is described with deliberate specificity in the synoptic accounts. When [[Jesus Christ]] sent [[Peter]] and [[John]] to prepare the Passover, he told them to follow a man carrying a pitcher of water, who would lead them to a householder; that householder would show them "a large upper room furnished and prepared: there make ready for us" (Mark 14:15). Luke records the same detail: "a large upper room furnished: there make ready" (Luke 22:12). The emphasis on its size and furnishing suggests a room set apart and prepared for a gathering of significance — the Twelve plus their Lord. The identity of the homeowner is not given in Scripture, though early tradition sometimes associates it with the family of [[Mark]] the Evangelist.

It was in this room that [[Jesus Christ]] instituted the Lord's Supper, washed the disciples' feet (John 13), and delivered the Farewell Discourse recorded in John 13-17. Following the [[Crucifixion]] and [[Resurrection]], the disciples gathered again in a room "where the doors were shut... for fear of the Jews" (John 20:19); the most natural reading is that this was the same upper room. There [[Jesus Christ]] appeared among them, showed his hands and side, and breathed on them the first breath of the Holy Spirit. Eight days later he appeared again in the same place, this time with [[Thomas]] present, drawing from him the confession "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28). A later post-resurrection appearance by the sea (John 21) followed, but the upper room remained the center of the disciples' gathered life in [[Jerusalem]].

After the ascension, the disciples returned to [[Jerusalem]] and went up "into an upper room" — likely the same chamber — where they "continued with one accord in prayer and supplication" (Acts 1:13-14). [[Peter]], [[James]], [[John]], [[Andrew]], [[Philip (Apostle)|Philip]], [[Thomas]], Bartholomew, [[Matthew]], James the son of Alphaeus, Simon Zelotes, and Judas the brother of James are all named as present. With the women and [[Mary (Mother of Jesus)]], this group of about one hundred twenty waited in prayer until the day of [[Pentecost and the Birth of the Church]], when the Spirit descended and the church was born. The Upper Room is thus the womb of the New Testament church.

## Key Events
- [[Last Supper]] — [[Jesus Christ]] celebrated the Passover here, instituted the Lord's Supper, washed the disciples' feet, and delivered the Farewell Discourse (Matthew 26:17-30; Mark 14:12-26; Luke 22:7-38; John 13-17)
- [[Resurrection]] — the risen [[Jesus Christ]] appeared to the gathered disciples here on the evening of the resurrection day; [[Thomas]] was absent (John 20:19-23)
- [[Resurrection]] — eight days later [[Jesus Christ]] appeared again with [[Thomas]] present; Thomas confessed "My Lord and my God" (John 20:24-29)
- [[Pentecost and the Birth of the Church]] — the Eleven and others prayed here before and during Pentecost; the Spirit descended and the church was born (Acts 1:13-14; Acts 2)

## Notes
- The Greek *anagaion* (upper room) in Mark 14:15 and Luke 22:12 denotes a room on an upper floor, typically accessed by an exterior stairway
- Whether the upper room of Acts 1:13 is the same room as the Last Supper is not stated explicitly but the proximity of events and the gathered company strongly suggest it
- The "Cenacle" (from Latin *cenaculum*, dining room) is the traditional name used in church history for this room; a site on Mount Zion in [[Jerusalem]] has been venerated as its location since at least the 4th century
- The room witnessed in sequence: institution of the Eucharist, the foot washing, the Farewell Discourse, the post-resurrection appearances, and the prayer vigil that preceded Pentecost — an unbroken chain of foundational Christian events
