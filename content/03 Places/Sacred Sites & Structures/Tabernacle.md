# Tabernacle

## Overview
- **Type:** Landmark / Portable Sanctuary
- **Testament:** Old (with NT references)
- **Modern Location:** N/A — portable structure, moved throughout wilderness

## Biblical Significance
The Tabernacle (Hebrew: mishkan, "dwelling place") was the portable sanctuary where God's presence dwelt among Israel during the wilderness wanderings and conquest. Designed by God Himself (Exodus 25–27), it contained the bronze altar, the laver, and the tent of meeting with the Holy Place (lampstand, table of showbread, golden altar of incense) and the Most Holy Place (Ark of the Covenant). The tabernacle was the center of Israelite worship, sacrifice, and divine encounter. It stood at the center of the camp, with the twelve tribes arranged around it. The glory of the Lord filled it (Exodus 40:34–35). It was eventually replaced by Solomon's Temple in Jerusalem.

## Notes
The tabernacle is the most theologically rich structure in the Old Testament. Every element — from the bronze altar to the mercy seat — points forward to Christ and the New Covenant. Jesus Himself is the true Tabernacle: "And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt [tabernacled] among us" (John 1:14).
