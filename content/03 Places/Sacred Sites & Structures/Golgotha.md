# Golgotha

## Overview
- **Type:** Hill / Execution site
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Traditionally identified with the site of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem, or alternatively Gordon's Calvary north of the Damascus Gate

## Biblical Significance
Golgotha — an Aramaic word meaning "Place of the Skull" (rendered in Latin as Calvaria, giving the English "Calvary") — was the site outside Jerusalem's walls where condemned criminals were executed by crucifixion. The Gospel accounts agree on its location outside the city walls, near a road where passersby could observe and mock, and close enough to a garden with a new tomb to allow a hasty burial before the Sabbath. The name's origin is uncertain: it may refer to the hill's skull-like shape, or to the skulls of executed criminals left there, or to a tradition that Adam's skull was buried there.

The crucifixion of Jesus at Golgotha is the central event of the Christian faith — the moment when the eternal Son of God, bearing the sins of the world, died the death of a criminal outside the holy city. Each Gospel records details that together paint a complete picture: the mocking of soldiers and bystanders, the darkness from noon to three, Jesus' cries from the cross including "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?", and his final declaration "It is finished." The tearing of the temple veil at the moment of his death signified the end of the old covenant barrier between God and humanity.

Golgotha's location "outside the camp" carries profound theological weight. The author of Hebrews draws on the Day of Atonement typology — where sin offerings were burned outside the camp — to argue that Jesus suffered outside the city gate to sanctify the people through his own blood. The invitation to "go to him outside the camp, bearing the disgrace he bore" (Hebrews 13:13) makes Golgotha not just a historical location but a posture of discipleship: willingness to share in Christ's reproach.

## Notes
- The Church of the Holy Sepulchre, which most historians and archaeologists favor as the authentic site, was outside the city walls in the first century despite being inside them today — the walls were extended northward in AD 41-44
- The three languages on the titulus — Hebrew, Greek, and Latin — reflect the tri-lingual, multicultural reality of Jerusalem under Roman rule, and perhaps the universal scope of Christ's work
- Golgotha's proximity to a road meant that crucifixion was designed as public spectacle and deterrent; Christ's death was not hidden but performed in maximum public view
