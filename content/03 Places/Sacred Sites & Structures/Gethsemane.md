# Gethsemane

## Overview
- **Type:** Olive grove / Garden
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** At the foot of the Mount of Olives, east of Jerusalem, across the Kidron Valley; the Church of All Nations marks the traditional site

## Biblical Significance
Gethsemane — a name meaning "oil press" in Aramaic, reflecting the olive grove's function — was a garden at the base of the Mount of Olives where Jesus and his disciples regularly went after evenings in Jerusalem. It was familiar enough that Judas knew where to bring the soldiers to make the arrest. The garden's location across the Kidron Valley from Jerusalem placed it just outside the city, accessible by a short walk over the valley and up the lower slopes of Olivet, yet removed from the crowds of the city proper.

The agony in Gethsemane is one of the most theologically profound and humanly moving passages in the entire Bible. Jesus withdrew from the disciples, fell on his face, and prayed with such intensity that Luke records his sweat fell like drops of blood — a recognized physiological response to extreme psychological stress. His prayer — "Father, if it is possible, let this cup pass from me; yet not as I will, but as you will" — reveals the full weight of what he was about to undertake. The "cup" he prayed to avoid was not merely physical death but the bearing of divine wrath on behalf of sinners, the experience of forsakenness by the Father that he cried out about from the cross. Gethsemane is where the obedience of the incarnation reached its most costly and conscious point.

The disciples' repeated failure to stay awake while Jesus agonized is a tender and sobering detail. Three times Jesus returned to find them sleeping, and his gentle rebuke — "Could you not watch with me one hour?" — has echoed through centuries of Christian reflection on prayerfulness. The contrast between Jesus' agonized wakefulness and the disciples' sleepy inability to pray previews their failure in the hours that followed: Peter's denial, their flight, the abandonment of their Lord. Gethsemane is the place where the gap between Jesus' obedience and human weakness is most starkly visible, and also the place where the foundation of salvation was laid in prayer and surrender.

## Notes
- Hebrews 5:7-9 interprets the Gethsemane prayer as the moment of Jesus' "learning obedience through suffering" — not that he was disobedient before, but that active, costly obedience in the face of agony constitutes a qualitatively different kind of faithfulness
- The garden setting echoes Eden: in a garden, the first Adam disobeyed in a moment of temptation; in a garden, the last Adam submitted in a moment of agony — the typological contrast is theologically rich
- Luke's detail of Jesus' sweating blood (hematidrosis) and the angelic strengthening (Luke 22:43-44) are unique to his account and emphasize the full humanity of Jesus' suffering in Gethsemane — he was not impervious to fear and anguish but endured them in full
