# Nazareth

## Overview
- **Type:** Town / Village
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Nazareth, Lower Galilee, Israel (same city, continuously inhabited)

## Biblical Significance
Nazareth was a small agricultural town in lower Galilee, nestled in a basin among the hills north of the Jezreel Valley. It receives no mention in the Old Testament, the Talmud, or any first-century non-Christian sources before it became associated with Jesus, suggesting it was an unremarkable settlement of perhaps a few hundred people. Its obscurity was reflected in Nathanael's skeptical question, "Can anything good come from Nazareth?" — and yet it was precisely here that the eternal Son of God spent approximately 30 of his 33 years on earth.

Joseph and Mary lived in Nazareth, and it was there that the angel Gabriel announced to Mary that she would conceive and bear the Son of God. After the birth in Bethlehem and the flight to Egypt, the holy family returned to Nazareth, and Luke records that Jesus "grew in wisdom and stature, and in favor with God and man" in this town. The ordinary rhythms of village life — carpentry, synagogue worship, Sabbath observance, family relationships — formed the human context in which the Son of God was nurtured. Jesus was so identified with the town that "Jesus of Nazareth" became his standard designation throughout the Gospels and Acts.

The rejection at Nazareth is one of the most theologically charged episodes in the Synoptic Gospels. When Jesus returned to his hometown and read from Isaiah 61 in the synagogue, declaring "Today this scripture is fulfilled in your hearing," the initial wonder turned to offense when he implicitly compared himself to Elijah and Elisha — prophets who bypassed Israel to serve Gentiles. The crowd drove him to a cliff to throw him off. Jesus' pronouncement that "no prophet is accepted in his hometown" became a summary principle of prophetic rejection, and the event foreshadowed the larger rejection he would face in Jerusalem.

## Notes
- Matthew 2:23's "He will be called a Nazarene" is theologically debated — no single OT text contains this phrase, and Matthew may be making a wordplay on "Netzer" (branch, Isaiah 11:1) or referencing the Nazirite tradition
- Recent archaeology at Nazareth has uncovered a first-century house that some scholars tentatively identify as possibly connected to Jesus' family, providing rare material evidence from the period
- The phrase "Jesus of Nazareth" appears throughout Acts as the standard christological designation in early preaching, indicating that the town's association with Jesus was not an embarrassment but became part of his identity in the apostolic proclamation
