# Troas

## Overview
- **Type:** Coastal city / Port
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Near Dalyan (ancient Alexandria Troas), Çanakkale Province, Turkey

## Biblical Significance
Troas, formally called Alexandria Troas, was a major port city on the northwestern coast of Asia Minor, strategically located near the ancient site of Troy. It was one of the most important ports on the Aegean Sea, serving as the primary crossing point between Asia Minor and the European mainland. Augustus elevated it to the status of a Roman colony, and it functioned as a gateway city for travel between east and west. Its natural harbor made it the standard embarkation point for travel from Asia to Macedonia, and conversely the landing point for those coming from Europe.

Troas features prominently at two pivotal moments in Paul's missionary career. During the second missionary journey, Paul received the vision of the Macedonian man at Troas — a vision that redirected the entire course of his mission and led to the gospel's first intentional entry into Europe. On the third journey, Paul stopped in Troas for seven days and gathered with the local believers on the first day of the week. It was during this extended Sunday gathering that Paul preached until midnight, Eutychus fell asleep in a window and dropped three stories to his death, and Paul restored him to life before continuing the teaching until dawn.

Troas also carries more personal significance in Paul's story. In 2 Corinthians 2, he describes arriving in Troas with an open door for gospel ministry but finding no rest because Titus had not arrived with news from Corinth — he moved on to Macedonia driven by anxiety. In 2 Timothy 4, written from his final imprisonment, Paul asks Timothy to bring the cloak he left at Troas with Carpus, and especially his books and parchments — a poignant detail suggesting a hasty and unplanned departure that left valuable items behind.

## Notes
- The "we" passages in Acts begin at Troas (Acts 16:10-17), indicating that Luke joined Paul's traveling party at this point — a shift from third-person to first-person narration that suggests eyewitness presence
- The Sunday evening gathering at Troas in Acts 20 is one of the earliest and most explicit references to the first day of the week as the Christian day of gathering and worship
- The request in 2 Timothy 4:13 for his cloak, books, and parchments reveals Paul's intellectual life even in final imprisonment — he continued studying and teaching to the very end
