# Tekoa

## Overview
- **Type:** City / village
- **Testament:** Old
- **Modern Location:** Khirbet Tequ', approximately 10 miles south of Bethlehem and 12 miles south of Jerusalem, in the Judean highlands

## Biblical Significance

Tekoa was a highland village perched on the edge of the Judean wilderness, sitting at roughly 2,800 feet elevation above the Judean desert that drops eastward toward the Dead Sea. Its position on the boundary between the cultivated highlands and the harsh wilderness gave it a dual agricultural character — the limestone hills above suited for grazing, the lower Shephelah accessible for dryland crops. This geography shaped Tekoa's most famous resident: [[Amos]], who describes himself as a herdsman and a gatherer of sycamore fruit, occupations that fit Tekoa's boundary situation exactly. The village is first mentioned in the OT as the home from which Joab summoned a wise woman to persuade David in the matter of Absalom (2 Samuel 14:2), suggesting it had a reputation for wisdom.

Amos is identified in the book's superscription as "Amos, who was among the herdmen of Tekoa" (Amos 1:1). From this Judean village he received his commission to prophesy not in Judah but in the northern kingdom of Israel. The geographical distance from Tekoa to Bethel — the royal sanctuary where Amos confronted [[Amaziah]] — is approximately 25 miles north, crossing the border between the kingdoms. This makes Amos a southerner sent north, a Judean intruding into Israelite religious space, which helps explain Amaziah's reaction: "O thou seer, go, flee thee away into the land of Judah, and there eat bread, and prophesy there: But prophesy not again any more at Bethel" (Amos 7:12–13). The implication is that Tekoa is where Amos belongs; he has no standing in the north.

Tekoa also appears in the books of Chronicles as a settlement rebuilt by Ashur, a descendant of Judah (1 Chronicles 2:24; 4:5), and in Nehemiah as the home of men who labored on the wall of Jerusalem under Nehemiah's direction (Nehemiah 3:5, 27), though Nehemiah 3:5 notes that their nobles "put not their necks to the work of their Lord." The prophet Jeremiah alludes to Tekoa in his lament over Judah, calling for the trumpet to be blown there as a warning sign (Jeremiah 6:1). The site's modern name, Khirbet Tequ', preserves the ancient name across more than two millennia.
