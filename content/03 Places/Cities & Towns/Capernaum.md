# Capernaum

## Overview
- **Type:** Fishing town / City
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Tell Hum on the northern shore of the Sea of Galilee, Israel

## Biblical Significance
Capernaum was a prosperous fishing town on the northwestern shore of the Sea of Galilee that became the operational headquarters of Jesus' Galilean ministry. After being rejected in Nazareth, Jesus moved to Capernaum, fulfilling the prophecy of Isaiah that light would come to "Galilee of the Gentiles." Matthew identifies Capernaum as "his own city," suggesting Jesus used it as a home base for the bulk of his public ministry. Peter and Andrew — the first disciples called — lived there, and the Gospels record Jesus staying in Peter's house, where he healed Peter's mother-in-law.

The synagogue at Capernaum was the site of some of Jesus' most authoritative teaching. Mark records that the crowds were "astonished at his teaching, for he taught them as one who had authority, and not as the scribes." In that same synagogue, Jesus cast out an unclean spirit, establishing immediately that his authority extended over demonic powers. The synagogue where he taught has been partially excavated; a later fourth-century synagogue was built on its foundations, likely in the same location.

Despite the abundance of miracles performed in Capernaum — healings, exorcisms, and the forgiveness of the paralyzed man lowered through the roof — the city ultimately rejected Jesus. In Matthew 11, Jesus pronounces a solemn judgment on Capernaum, comparing its fate to that of Sodom and declaring that if Sodom had seen such miracles it would have repented. The city was eventually abandoned and today lies in ruins, a sobering testimony to the consequences of rejecting the presence of Christ.

## Notes
- Capernaum was a customs post on a major trade route from Damascus to the Mediterranean, explaining the presence of tax collectors like Matthew and Roman military like the centurion
- The ruins of a later synagogue built over the original first-century one are well-preserved at the site today, one of the most excavated sites in Galilee
- Jesus' judgment on Capernaum (Matthew 11:23) serves as a warning that greater privilege brings greater accountability — the city that saw the most miracles faced the harshest condemnation for unbelief
