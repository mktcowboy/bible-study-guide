# Zoar

## Overview
- **Type:** City
- **Testament:** Old
- **Modern Location:** Uncertain; possibly near the southern end of the Dead Sea in modern Jordan

## Biblical Significance
Zoar was a small city in the region of the plain that included the infamous cities of [[Sodom]] and [[Gomorrah]]. It appears first in Genesis 14 as "Bela (that is, Zoar)" in the list of the five cities of the plain whose kings made war against the coalition of eastern kings. Its original name, Bela, may carry a connotation of destruction or swallowing, while Zoar means "little" or "small" — a name that becomes part of the narrative when Lot pleads for its preservation on the grounds of its smallness.

The city's most significant role in Scripture is as the refuge granted to Lot during the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah. When the angels urged Lot to flee to the mountains, Lot — whose faith was weak and attachment to comfort strong — begged instead to flee to the small nearby city, arguing that the mountain was too far and that he could not survive there. God granted this request and promised not to destroy Zoar, explicitly sparing the city on account of Lot's petition. This detail is remarkable: divine judgment was stayed for a city and its inhabitants because a weak-faithed believer asked for accommodation.

Lot's sojourn in Zoar proved brief. Despite the city being spared, Lot's fear drove him to leave Zoar and retreat to the mountain caves after all — the very place he had initially rejected. In the cave above Zoar, the sordid episode with his daughters occurred, resulting in the birth of Moab and Ammon. Zoar is mentioned later in Isaiah 15 and Jeremiah 48 in oracles against Moab, and the prophet Jeremiah uses it in a description of the lament over Moab's cities. The city's longevity beyond Sodom's destruction made it a landmark reference in later prophetic literature.

## Notes
- Zoar's sparing is a quiet example of divine accommodation to human weakness — God granted Lot's imperfect request even when faith in his provision would have led to the mountains
- The city's continued existence after Sodom's destruction made it a visible reminder of that judgment in later centuries; its appearance in Isaiah and Jeremiah shows it persisted as a known location for at least several centuries
- The irony of the Zoar narrative is that Lot's "safety" there was so tenuous that he immediately abandoned it — his flight to a lesser refuge rather than trusting God's full provision ultimately led him to the mountain caves he feared, and to the disasters that followed
