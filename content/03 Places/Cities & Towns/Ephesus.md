# Ephesus

## Overview
- **Type:** Major port city / Provincial capital
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Near Selçuk, Izmir Province, Turkey; well-excavated ruins

## Biblical Significance
Ephesus was the largest and most important city in the Roman province of Asia, serving as its de facto capital and a hub of trade, religion, and governance. It sat at the mouth of the Cayster River on the western coast of Asia Minor and was home to the Temple of Artemis, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The city's harbor, its well-paved marble streets, its grand library of Celsus, and its theater seating 25,000 made it one of the most impressive cities in the empire. Its religious culture was dominated by the cult of Artemis, whose temple employed thousands and generated enormous economic activity through the sale of silver shrines.

Paul spent more than two years in Ephesus during his third missionary journey (Acts 19), making it his longest and most productive sustained ministry. He began in the synagogue, then moved to the lecture hall of Tyrannus, from which "all the residents of Asia heard the word of the Lord, both Jews and Greeks." Extraordinary miracles followed — handkerchiefs from Paul's body healed the sick and cast out demons. The encounter with Sceva's sons exposed the difference between genuine apostolic power and magical imitation. The burning of magic books worth fifty thousand drachmas illustrated the transformative social impact of the gospel.

The riot of the silversmiths (Acts 19:23-41) reveals Ephesus's systemic resistance: the gospel's success had threatened the economy of idol-making, prompting a mob chant of "Great is Artemis of the Ephesians!" in the great theater. Paul later wrote an extraordinarily elevated letter to the Ephesian church — focusing on cosmic Christology, spiritual warfare, the mystery of the church as Christ's body, and the full armor of God. Revelation addresses the Ephesian church first among the seven churches of Asia, praising their perseverance but warning that they had abandoned their first love.

## Notes
- The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus was four times the size of the Parthenon and one of the largest buildings in the ancient world — the economic and religious center of the region
- The Ephesian letter's focus on spiritual warfare and the armor of God (Ephesians 6) may reflect the city's reputation for magic arts and occult practices
- The warning to Ephesus in Revelation 2 — "you have abandoned the love you had at first" — is sobering given the extraordinary level of apostolic investment the city received
