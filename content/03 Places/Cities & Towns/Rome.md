# Rome

## Overview
- **Type:** Capital city / Empire center
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Rome, Italy (same city, continuously inhabited; capital of Italy)

## Biblical Significance
Rome was the capital and nerve center of the Roman Empire — the city to which all roads literally led, the seat of imperial power, the largest city in the ancient world with a population estimated between half a million and one million people. The empire it governed encompassed virtually the entire Mediterranean world, stretching from Britain to Mesopotamia. For the apostolic mission, Rome represented both the ultimate destination and the ultimate challenge: if the gospel could take root there, it could spread from that hub to the ends of the earth.

Paul's letter to the Romans, written before he had visited the city, is his most systematic and theologically comprehensive letter. Addressed to a mixed Jewish-Gentile church he had not founded, it lays out the whole argument of the gospel: the universal need for righteousness, justification by faith alone, sanctification by the Spirit, the mystery of Israel's role in redemption, and the practical shape of Christian community. Paul's stated ambition was to use Rome as a launching pad for mission to Spain — the western edge of the known world — indicating that he saw Rome's strategic location as an asset for further expansion.

Paul arrived in Rome not as a free missionary but as a prisoner under Roman guard, the culmination of a dramatic journey through shipwreck and storm described in Acts 27-28. Yet even under house arrest, he taught all who came to him, and his imprisonment paradoxically advanced the gospel into the praetorian guard and Caesar's household (Philippians 1:12-13; 4:22). Church tradition holds that both Paul and Peter were martyred in Rome under Nero, making the city the ground where two pillars of the apostolic mission laid down their lives. Rome thus became, paradoxically, both the center of persecution and the seedbed of a church that would eventually transform the empire.

## Notes
- Rome's cosmopolitan Jewish population (estimated at 50,000) meant the church there likely arose through diaspora Jews who heard the gospel at Pentecost (Acts 2:10 lists Romans among those present) rather than through any single apostolic founder
- The expulsion of Jews from Rome under Claudius (Acts 18:2; ~AD 49) that affected Aquila and Priscilla corresponds to the Roman historian Suetonius's account of disturbances caused by "Chrestus" — possibly a reference to disputes about Christ
- Paul's strategy of using Rome as a mission base for Spain reflects his principle of working in strategic centers from which the gospel would radiate outward — a pattern consistent with his work in Antioch, Corinth, and Ephesus
