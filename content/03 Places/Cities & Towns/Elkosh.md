# Elkosh

## Overview
- **Type:** Village / hometown
- **Testament:** Old
- **Modern Location:** Unknown; debated among Galilee, Judah, and Mesopotamia

## Biblical Significance
Elkosh is mentioned only once in the Bible, as the hometown of the prophet Nahum: "The book of the vision of Nahum the Elkoshite" (Nahum 1:1). The word "Elkoshite" is the entirety of Nahum's biographical information outside of his name. No tribe, no family line, no king's reign, no other contextual details are given. As a result, scholars have proposed several locations, and none can be confirmed with certainty.

The most commonly discussed proposals are three. First, a Galilean location: Jerome in the 4th century AD reported visiting a village in Galilee called Elkesi or Helkesei, and identified it as Nahum's birthplace. This location, if accurate, would make Nahum a northern Israelite prophesying after the fall of Samaria in 722 BC. Second, a Judean location: some have proposed the village of Beit Jibrin (ancient Eleutheropolis) or another site in the Shephelah of Judah, which would make Nahum a southern Judean prophet in the tradition of Micah. Third, a Mesopotamian location: a village called Alqush exists near the ancient site of Calah (Nimrud) in modern Iraq, and some local Christian tradition identifies it as Nahum's hometown, which would be fitting poetically—a man from near Nineveh prophesying that city's doom. Alqush even contains a site traditionally identified as Nahum's tomb. However, this tradition is late and cannot be verified historically.

The uncertainty surrounding Elkosh does not diminish the power or precision of Nahum's oracle. Whatever his origin, he wrote as one who knew Assyrian power intimately—the imagery of charging horses, plundering soldiers, and fleeing populations in Nahum 2-3 is vivid and specific. The complete and irreversible destruction of Nineveh in 612 BC vindicated the vision given to "the Elkoshite," wherever that may have been.
