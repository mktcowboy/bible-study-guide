# Colossae

## Overview
- **Type:** City
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Near modern Honaz, Denizli Province, Turkey (unexcavated)

## Biblical Significance
Colossae was a city in the Lycus River valley in the Roman province of Asia Minor, situated about 100 miles east of Ephesus. Once a thriving commercial city on an important trade route, by Paul's era it had been eclipsed by the nearby cities of [[Laodicea]] and [[Hierapolis]] as the valley's dominant centers. Despite its diminished status, a significant Christian community existed there — one that Paul had never personally visited, though he had heard of their faith and love through Epaphras.

The church at Colossae was founded by Epaphras, likely a convert of Paul's ministry at Ephesus, who then carried the gospel eastward through the Lycus Valley. Paul wrote to the Colossians from imprisonment — either Rome, Caesarea, or Ephesus — to address a developing theological error sometimes called the "Colossian heresy." This syncretistic teaching appeared to combine Jewish ceremonial practices with proto-Gnostic mysticism, angelic veneration, and ascetic regulations, threatening to displace Christ from his central role in salvation.

Paul's response in Colossians is one of the most exalted Christological passages in Scripture. The hymn of Colossians 1:15-20 declares Christ as the image of the invisible God, firstborn over all creation, the one in whom all things hold together. By anchoring believers' identity and hope in Christ alone — "Christ in you, the hope of glory" — Paul dismantles any system that adds intermediaries or requirements beyond him. The letter also has personal dimensions, as Philemon the slave-owner and his slave Onesimus were both connected to the Colossian congregation.

## Notes
- Colossae is one of the few New Testament cities that has never been systematically excavated, leaving many historical details unknown
- The "Colossian heresy" is not explicitly named in the letter — scholars reconstruct it from Paul's responses, making it a mirror-reading exercise
- The household codes in Colossians 3-4 (wives/husbands, children/fathers, slaves/masters) are some of the most debated NT passages for understanding early Christian social ethics
