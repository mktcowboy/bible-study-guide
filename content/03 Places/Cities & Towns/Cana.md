# Cana

## Overview
- **Type:** Town
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Possibly Khirbet Qana or Kafr Kanna in Galilee, Israel

## Biblical Significance
Cana of Galilee is most famously the site of Jesus' first recorded miracle — the transformation of water into wine at a wedding feast. The Gospel of John situates this event at the very beginning of Jesus' public ministry, presenting it as the inaugural "sign" that revealed his glory and prompted his disciples to believe in him. The miracle is theologically rich: Jesus transforms six stone water jars used for Jewish purification rites, filling them with wine of superior quality — a symbolic gesture pointing to the new covenant's abundance superseding the old covenant's rituals.

The wedding setting is itself significant in biblical theology. The Old Testament prophets depicted the coming messianic age as a great wedding banquet, with God as the husband of Israel. Jesus' presence at a wedding and his provision of wine at its climax signals the inauguration of that eschatological celebration. His mother Mary's involvement — and her confidence in his ability to help — offers an early glimpse into the dynamic between them and foreshadows the deeper significance of the "hour" that has not yet come.

Cana is also the setting for a second sign in John's Gospel: the healing of a royal official's son who was near death in Capernaum. Jesus healed the boy from a distance, simply by speaking the word, illustrating that his authority transcends physical location. John explicitly numbers this as the second sign performed in Galilee after Jesus' return from Judea, reinforcing Cana's importance as a locus of his early Galilean ministry.

## Notes
- The six stone jars held 20-30 gallons each — the total volume of wine produced was staggering, a picture of messianic abundance
- John calls the water-to-wine event the "beginning of signs" (John 2:11), placing it as the first of seven signs that structure his Gospel
- Nathanael, one of the first disciples, was from Cana (John 21:2), giving the location personal resonance for at least one of the Twelve
