# Jerusalem

## Overview
- **Type:** City / royal capital
- **Testament:** Both
- **Modern Location:** Jerusalem in Israel

## Biblical Significance
Jerusalem becomes the political and spiritual center of David's kingdom in [[2 Samuel]]. After capturing the city (earlier called [[Jebus]]) from the Jebusites, [[David]] makes it his capital and brings the ark there, shaping it into the city of the king and eventually the city of the temple.

In the wider canon, Jerusalem becomes the city of worship, prophecy, reform, judgment, exile memory, and restoration hope. In the New Testament it is the city where Jesus is crucified and raised, where the Spirit is poured out at Pentecost, and where the early church first takes visible shape.

## Notes
- Jerusalem often carries a double symbolism: the place of God's name and worship, yet also a city capable of rebellion, hypocrisy, and judgment.
- The city gathers together throne, temple, sacrifice, prophecy, and kingdom expectation more densely than any other biblical place.
- Later biblical theology uses Jerusalem both historically and symbolically, looking back to its role in covenant history and forward to the hope of a renewed dwelling of God with His people.
