# Laodicea

## Overview
- **Type:** City
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Near Denizli, Turkey; ancient ruins at Eskihisar

## Biblical Significance
Laodicea was a prosperous commercial city in the Lycus Valley of Asia Minor, situated at the junction of major trade routes. Founded by the Seleucid king Antiochus II around 250 BC and named after his wife Laodice, the city became famous for three industries: banking and financial services, a distinctive black wool textile industry, and a medical school known for a Phrygian eye powder. These three industries — wealth, clothing, and eye medicine — become ironic points of reference in the letter to the Laodicean church in Revelation 3, where Christ calls the church poor, naked, and blind.

The church at Laodicea was connected to the network of Lycus Valley churches founded by [[Epaphras]] and overseen in connection with Paul's ministry from Ephesus. Paul's letter to the Colossians greets the Laodiceans and mentions a letter sent to Laodicea that Paul asks the Colossians to exchange and read. This "letter to the Laodiceans" is not in the New Testament canon, though some early manuscripts appended a short epistle by that name. The city also hosted a believer named [[Nympha]], in whose house the church met (Colossians 4:15).

The letter to the Laodicean church in Revelation 3 is among the most sobering passages in Scripture. Christ delivers no praise to this congregation — only rebuke. Their spiritual condition mirrors their city's water supply: Laodicea's water was piped in from a distant source and arrived lukewarm and mineral-laden, nauseating to drink. Hot springs from nearby [[Hierapolis]] and cold springs from Colossae provided useful extremes; Laodicea's tepid water was useful for nothing. Christ threatens to "spit them out of his mouth," yet the letter closes with one of the most tender invitations in Revelation: "Behold, I stand at the door and knock."

## Notes
- The Laodicean church's self-assessment — "I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing" — reflects the actual economic prosperity of the city, making it a case study in how material success can mask spiritual bankruptcy
- Christ's declaration "I stand at the door and knock" (Revelation 3:20) is addressed to a church, not to unbelievers, carrying the chilling implication that Christ can be excluded from a church that bears his name
- The city's famous bank and the "black wool" textile trade meant Laodiceans were clothed and wealthy by worldly standards — exactly the terms in which Christ contrasts their real poverty and nakedness
