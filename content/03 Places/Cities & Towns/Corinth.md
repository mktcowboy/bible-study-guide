# Corinth

## Overview
- **Type:** Major port city / Roman colony
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Corinth (Korinthos), Greece; ancient city near modern Corinth

## Biblical Significance
Corinth was one of the most strategically located and commercially powerful cities in the ancient Mediterranean world. Situated on the narrow isthmus connecting mainland Greece to the Peloponnese, it controlled two ports — Cenchreae on the east and Lechaion on the west — allowing it to serve trade flowing between the Aegean and Adriatic seas. The city was wealthy, cosmopolitan, ethnically diverse, and notorious for its moral permissiveness. The Greek term "to Corinthianize" had become a byword for sexual immorality before the city was destroyed by Rome in 146 BC and later rebuilt as a Roman colony in 44 BC.

Paul arrived in Corinth during his second missionary journey, likely around AD 50-51. He stayed 18 months — one of his longest stays in any single city — working as a tentmaker alongside Aquila and Priscilla, who had recently been expelled from Rome under Claudius's edict. He preached first in the synagogue, and when opposition arose, he pivoted to the house of Titius Justus next door, which became the church's meeting point. The proconsul Gallio's dismissal of charges against Paul provides one of the most useful chronological anchors for Pauline chronology.

The two Corinthian letters address a church deeply marked by the surrounding culture: factions forming around celebrity preachers, lawsuits between believers, sexual immorality including a case of incest, abuse of the Lord's Supper, confusion about spiritual gifts, and questions about the resurrection. Paul's letters to Corinth are therefore some of the most practically comprehensive in the New Testament, covering ecclesiology, ethics, eschatology, and the nature of Christian ministry. The soaring passage on love in 1 Corinthians 13 and the resurrection chapter of 1 Corinthians 15 emerged from this fractious, worldly congregation.

## Notes
- Corinth's two ports meant the city profited from both east-west land and sea trade, making it one of the wealthiest cities in the empire — a fact that shaped the church's struggles with status and wealth
- The "wisdom" controversies in 1 Corinthians reflect the Greek philosophical culture of Corinth, where rhetoric and sophistry were valued — Paul deliberately contrasted this with the "foolishness" of the cross
- The Gallio inscription from Delphi, dating his proconsulship to AD 51-52, is one of the most important archaeological anchors for fixing the chronology of Paul's missionary journeys
