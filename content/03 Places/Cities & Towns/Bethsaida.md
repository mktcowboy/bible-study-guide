# Bethsaida

## Overview
- **Type:** Town / Fishing village
- **Testament:** New
- **Modern Location:** Et-Tell or el-Araj, on the northern shore of the Sea of Galilee near the Jordan River inlet

## Biblical Significance
Bethsaida — meaning "house of fish" or "house of the fisherman" — was a fishing town on the northern shore of the Sea of Galilee. It holds a unique place in the Gospel narratives as both a home of apostles and a recipient of severe judgment for unbelief.

Philip, Andrew, and Peter were all from Bethsaida (John 1:44), which establishes it as the hometown of at least three of Jesus's twelve disciples. Despite this, Jesus pronounced one of his harshest woes against the city: "Woe unto thee, Chorazin! woe unto thee, Bethsaida! for if the mighty works had been done in Tyre and Sidon, which have been done in you, they had a great while ago repented, sitting in sackcloth and ashes" (Luke 10:13). The city witnessed extraordinary miracles and did not repent — a pattern that illustrated the hardening Jesus spoke about.

The feeding of the five thousand occurred near Bethsaida (Luke 9:10; Mark 6:45). In [[Mark]] 6:45, Jesus compelled the disciples to sail toward Bethsaida while he dismissed the crowd — placing the famous walking on water episode in its geographical context. Most distinctively, [[Mark]] records the healing of a blind man at Bethsaida (Mark 8:22-26) — the [[Healing at Bethsaida]] — which is the only two-stage miracle in the Gospels and is found only in Mark. Jesus led the man outside the village, healed him in stages, and instructed him not to go back into the village — an interesting command given the woe pronounced over Bethsaida.
